Why the Texas Volts Called Stanford to Help NiJaree Canady

By Sarah Mitchell | I Love Softball | July 16, 2026

NiJaree Canady finished her college career as one of the most statistically dominant pitchers in the history of the sport. She entered the 2026 AUSL season as the No. 2 overall draft pick, one of the most anticipated professional debuts in women's softball history, and a name on Time Magazine's list of the 100 Most Influential Athletes of 2026.

Through the current AUSL season, she is 0-3 with a 6.87 ERA.

According to Sports Illustrated, the Texas Volts reached out to former Stanford pitching coach Tori Nyberg to work with Canady. This is what that gap looks like — and what the organization is doing about it.

The College Numbers

To understand the gap, start with what Canady did in college.

Across her career at Stanford and Texas Tech, she went 104-24 with 13 saves, a 1.09 ERA, and 1,127 strikeouts in 796 innings. In 2025 at Texas Tech, she posted a 34-7 record and a 1.11 ERA. In her final college season in 2026, she went 29-7 with a 1.87 ERA.

Those are numbers that make voters put a pitcher's name on national awards ballots. They're the reason the Volts used their top-two pick on her in the AUSL Draft. They're the numbers that seemed to guarantee a professional career.

What Changed

Sports Illustrated's reporting points to injuries Canady sustained at Texas Tech that altered her mechanics and body positioning. The changes were subtle enough that they didn't always show at the college level, where her stuff was so overpowering — velocity, movement, command — that minor mechanical inconsistencies didn't cost her outcomes.

Professional hitters are a different problem. They have more experience, better pitch recognition, and longer film rooms. The margin between a well-executed pitch and a hittable one is smaller. And when mechanics shift even slightly, that margin disappears.

Canady has allowed 17 earned runs and 7 home runs through her first three professional appearances. The Texas Volts, at approximately 6-16, are out of playoff contention.

Why Tori Nyberg

The Volts' decision to contact former Stanford pitching coach Tori Nyberg reflects what they believe about Canady: that the talent is real, that the mechanics are fixable, and that the organization's job is to invest in the adjustment rather than retreat from it.

Nyberg worked with pitchers at Stanford who made professional transitions. The Volts are hoping her perspective — on mechanics, on the difference between college and professional execution, on the specific physical patterns that can erode under the pressure of an injury — can accelerate a timeline that might otherwise take a full season to find its footing.

The Adjustment Is the Story

This isn't the first time a dominant college pitcher has struggled in a new professional context. The translation from amateur to professional softball — like the translation in almost every professional sport — is rarely automatic. College success is a strong predictor of professional potential, not professional results.

Canady was a Time Magazine Top 100 athlete before she threw a professional pitch. She remains one of the most talented pitchers in the sport by any measure of her college body of work. The adjustment is part of the story, not the end of it.

The Volts believe that. That's why they called Stanford.
