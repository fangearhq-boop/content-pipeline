Two Titles in a Row Proves Texas Softball Has Built Something That Lasts

By Emily Rawlings June 5, 2026

Say the Word

Dynasty. Two consecutive Women's College World Series championships earned the right to use it. Texas softball swept Texas Tech in the 2026 championship series — 7-3 in Game 1, 4-1 in Game 2 — and in doing so joined the five-member club of programs that have won back-to-back national titles. The word fits.

Dynasties are not always obvious while they are happening. It is only when the titles accumulate that the picture becomes clear. Texas has won two in a row, swept two championship series in two years, and outscored every opponent they faced at Devon Park in both tournaments. The picture is clear.

The People Behind the Dynasty

Teagan Kavan is the most decorated pitcher in this era of college softball. Two national championships. Two Most Outstanding Player awards — the first back-to-back in WCWS history. She carried this program in every elimination game that mattered and closed out two title-clinching wins in consecutive years.

Katie Stewart made WCWS history with her bat this tournament, becoming the first player ever to homer in four consecutive WCWS games. Her combination of power and durability at the plate gave Texas an offensive dimension that opponents could never fully account for when Kavan was still pitching.

Kayden Henry hit the home run that put Game 2 out of reach Thursday night — a seventh-inning shot to the opposite field that extended the lead to 3-1 and effectively ended Texas Tech's night. That is the kind of moment that championship lineups produce.

Coach Mike White made the gamble in Game 2 that cemented everything: starting Citlaly Gutierrez to protect Kavan, deploying freshman Hannah Wells as a bridge, and deploying his ace for the final two innings when the stakes were highest. Every decision was right. That is what coaching at a dynasty-level looks like.

Depth Built This

What separates programs that win once from programs that win twice is depth. One transcendent player can carry a team to a title. Two titles in a row require everyone. Texas's 2026 championship run featured contributions from three pitchers in the clinching game, a home run from Henry that won the decisive moment, and a lineup that collected hits against elite pitching staffs for two straight weeks.

This is what sustainable success in college softball looks like — a program where the next player steps up exactly when she is needed.

What's Next

Texas enters the offseason as the program every coach in the country measures themselves against. Kavan, Stewart, and other contributors will face decisions about their next chapters, and some roster turnover is inevitable. But the standard has been set. The Longhorns go into 2027 as the defending national champion — again — with a dynasty to defend and the country's best recruiting pitch in the form of two trophies in two years. Whatever comes next, this program has earned the right to be discussed among the all-time greats in college softball history.
