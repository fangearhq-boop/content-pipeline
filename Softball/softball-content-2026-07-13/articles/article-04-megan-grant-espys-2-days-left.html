Why Grant Belongs in This Category

Grant is nominated for Best Record-Breaking Performance. That category is exactly what it sounds like: the best single performance in the calendar year that broke a record that had stood before.

Grant hit 42 home runs at UCLA in the 2026 season. The previous NCAA Division I single-season home run record was 37, set by Arizona's Laura Espinoza in 1995. Espinoza's record stood for 31 years. It survived the power surge of the post-Title IX era, the rise of elite training programs, the improvement of bats and conditioning science, and three decades of elite hitters trying to reach that mark.

Grant exceeded it by five. In one season. At UCLA. In 63 games.

When a record lasts 31 years and gets broken by five, that is not a narrow victory at the margin of athletic performance. That is a statement.

The Competition

Grant's competitors in the Best Record-Breaking Performance category are Myles Garrett, Johannes Høsflot Klæbo, and Sabastian Sawe. Each represents an extraordinary athletic achievement in their sport. Garrett's edge-rushing record reflects years of elite NFL performance. Klæbo's Winter Olympic gold haul is historically significant. Sawe's sub-two-hour marathon is a human performance milestone.

Softball fans: the only category that matters here is whether Grant's record belongs in that conversation. She broke a 31-year record by five home runs in a power sport that demands elite hand-eye coordination, bat speed, and the mental discipline to stay consistent over an entire season. She belongs.

Her Professional Career

Grant did not stop setting marks when her college career ended. She entered the inaugural AUSL season as the No. 4 overall pick for the Portland Cascade. In her debut week in the league, she hit what became the first officially measured AUSL home run — 313 feet. The league's home run distance record belongs to her, and she set it in her first week on the job.

She finished her UCLA career with 91 total home runs, the all-time school record.
