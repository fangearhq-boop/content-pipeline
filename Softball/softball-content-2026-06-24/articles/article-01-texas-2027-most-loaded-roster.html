Texas 2027: Is This the Most Loaded Roster in College Softball History?

By Sarah Mitchell | I Love Softball | June 24, 2026

The 2026 transfer portal window closed on June 22. The dust has settled. And when you step back and look at what Mike White has assembled in Austin for the 2027 season, a question that once seemed hyperbolic now feels genuinely reasonable:

Is Texas building the most dangerous roster in the history of college softball?

Consider what the Longhorns are bringing back. Teagan Kavan, the first two-time Women's College World Series Most Outstanding Pitcher, went 4-1 with a 1.47 ERA and 30 strikeouts across 33.1 innings in the 2026 tournament alone. Katie Stewart hit 31 home runs during the 2026 season — a Texas program record — and became the first player in WCWS history to homer in four consecutive games. Samantha Swan, who signed with Texas from Arizona State in June, brings a .383 batting average, 12 home runs, and 55 RBIs as the new catcher. Swan is, improbably, also a Georgetown, Texas native — the same hometown as the Longhorns' newest arrival.

Torres Changes Everything

Isa Torres committed to Texas on June 19, and in doing so she transformed an already elite roster into something genuinely unprecedented.

In her final season at Florida State, Torres hit .530 with a .978 slugging percentage, a .591 on-base percentage, 16 home runs, 56 RBIs, and 78 runs scored. She was named D1Softball Player of the Year, ACC Player of the Year, and ACC Defensive Player of the Year. She set an NCAA record by recording a hit in 16 consecutive at-bats. And she is a Georgetown, Texas native — the same community that produced Swan — coming home to Austin.

Torres had one year of eligibility remaining. She used it on the program that just won back-to-back national championships. For Texas fans, the move felt almost surreal. For everyone else, it felt like the final piece of a roster that no one asked to be this good.

Adding to the Foundation

What makes the 2027 Texas roster genuinely special is not any single piece — it is how the pieces connect.

Torres brings speed and contact at the top of the lineup. Stewart provides middle-of-the-order thunder from left. Swan gives the team an elite receiver behind the plate who can also hit for power. Kavan anchors a pitching staff as the ace of a program that has won two straight titles. And the 2027 recruiting class — ranked among the nation's top four by D1Softball — adds depth that includes incoming freshman Madi Doty, one of the top pitching prospects in the country.

Texas does not have a single obvious weakness on paper. The defense is elite. The power hitting is elite. The starting pitching is elite. The depth, reinforced by a top national recruiting class, is elite.

The Three-Peat Argument

No program in NCAA softball history has won three consecutive national championships. It is the sport's most elusive achievement. Texas won in 2025 and 2026. The 2027 season now begins with the Longhorns as not just the overwhelming favorite — but potentially the most decorated roster the sport has ever seen heading into a single season.

The competition is real. Texas Tech has assembled its own historic portal class, adding three All-Americans in Kenna Polar (.555 BA), Jayden Heavener, and Kasidi Pickering (.370 BA / 20 HR). Florida added Jaysoni Beachum, the No. 1 transfer in the portal. Nebraska figures to reload after its remarkable 51-6 run. The SEC programs will push back.

But the starting point for any three-peat conversation runs through Austin. When the 2027 season tips off, it will be worth remembering this moment — when the most complete roster in college softball history was quietly assembled during an offseason that most fans spent grieving their own program's portal losses.

What's Next

Texas opens fall practice in August, giving this roster its first chance to play together as a unit. The coaching staff changes — Earleywine and Zaleski both departing — add one layer of uncertainty that Mike White will need to resolve quickly. New hires will be announced before fall camp. The three-peat bid begins in earnest the moment those players take the field.

Watch the preseason polls when they come out in January. If Texas does not start at No. 1 — unanimously — the voters will have some explaining to do.
