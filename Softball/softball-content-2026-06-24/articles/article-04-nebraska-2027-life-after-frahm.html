Nebraska 2027: Can the Cornhuskers Get Back to OKC Without Jordy Frahm?

By Sarah Mitchell | I Love Softball | June 24, 2026

When Nebraska took the field in Oklahoma City in May 2026, it had been 13 years since the Cornhuskers last appeared at the Women's College World Series. The 2013 team felt like a high-water mark the program would spend years trying to match.

The 2026 Cornhuskers didn't just match it. They surpassed it.

Nebraska went 51-6, won 27 consecutive games, and fought all the way to the WCWS semifinals before falling to Tennessee 2-1 in nine innings. They beat Alabama during the tournament. They won a walk-off thriller over Tennessee in pool play with a home run from catcher Emma Kuszak. They were, by any reasonable measure, one of the four best teams in the country — and one of the great Cinderella stories in recent NCAA softball history.

At the center of it all was Jordy Frahm.

What Frahm Was

Frahm was named USA Softball Collegiate Player of the Year for the 2026 season — the first Cornhusker ever to earn the honor. She hit 19 home runs with a 1.338 OPS at the plate. On the mound, she went 20-4. She threw 133 pitches in a single WCWS game against Alabama. She was, in every sense, a two-way phenomenon at the center of a program's revival.

Her college career is over. She was the engine of this run. And she cannot be replaced directly.

The Case for Nebraska in 2027

Here is what makes this question genuinely interesting: Nebraska's 51-6 record was not built on Frahm alone. It was built on depth, on defense, on an offense that could score without relying entirely on its two-way star.

Kuszak returns. The walk-off home run she hit against Tennessee in pool play demonstrated the kind of big-moment capability that defines championship runs. Others from the 2026 roster figure to return. Head coach Rhonda Revelle-Deifel has built this program carefully over years — she is not a one-player coach.

Nebraska also has a track record worth respecting. Revelle-Deifel has been building this program toward a return to national relevance for years. The 2026 season was not a fluke. The infrastructure — the culture, the recruiting pipeline, the development model — produced a 51-6 team. That does not disappear with one player's departure.

The Hard Question

Replacing Frahm's pitching production is the challenge that has no clean answer. She went 20-4. She was the ace who gave Nebraska confidence in every big game. Without a pitcher of comparable quality, the Cornhuskers will need to win differently — more runs, more bullpen management, more margin for error in the offense.

The transfer portal was one avenue to address the pitching depth. How Nebraska used the 2026 window, and what they find in their existing roster, will define whether this is a program that sustains or a program that retreats after its best season in a generation.

Why This Story Matters

Nebraska's 2027 season is one of the most compelling storylines in college softball going into the fall. Not because Nebraska is a favorite — they are not, not with Texas and Texas Tech and Florida all loaded — but because the question itself is fascinating. Can a team that ran as hot as Nebraska did in 2026 sustain that level after losing the player who defined its identity?

College softball needs great stories. This is one of them.

What's Next

Watch Nebraska's portal activity and fall camp roster announcements through July and August. Revelle-Deifel and her staff will need to identify the 2027 ace. The Cornhuskers open fall practice in August with more questions than they had in any of the last few years — but also with more credibility as a program than they have carried since the early 2010s. That is not nothing.
