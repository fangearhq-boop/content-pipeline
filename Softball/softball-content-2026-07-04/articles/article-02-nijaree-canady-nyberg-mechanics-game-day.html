NiJaree Canady's Turnaround Attempt Hits Game Day: Nyberg, Mechanics, and the Bandits

By Jordan Reeves

July 4, 2026

NiJaree Canady takes the circle this afternoon at 4:30 PM ET in Rosemont, Illinois, and it may be the most important start of her early professional career.

The Texas Volts ace — No. 2 overall pick in the 2026 AUSL College Draft — is 0-3 with a 6.87 ERA through her first professional outings. She has allowed 17 earned runs and seven home runs. It is not the debut anyone expected from the most decorated collegiate pitcher of her generation.

But something has changed in the Volts' preparation this week: they brought in Tori Nyberg.

The Stanford Coach Who Knows Her Best

Nyberg was Canady's pitching coach at Stanford, where he originally recruited her. He watched her post ERAs of 0.57 and 0.73 across two elite seasons with the Cardinal. He knows exactly what her mechanics looked like at peak form, and he knows exactly what they look like now.

His diagnosis, shared publicly, is specific: injuries Canady sustained during her time at Texas Tech changed her body positioning. Her arm path, release point, and physical sequencing have all drifted from the version of her mechanics that made her nearly unhittable.

Nyberg, Canady, and Volts assistant coach Josh Trevino have been working together on a plan. The stated goal, in Nyberg's own words: get back to the "OG NiJa."

The Context Behind the Struggles

Canady's path to the AUSL included a brief contract holdout before she signed with the Texas Volts on June 12, missing the first two games of the season. When she arrived, she was already behind in reps — and pitching in a professional league is not the same as arriving at a campus in August and building toward a February opener.

At Texas Tech in 2026, she finished 29-7 with a 1.87 ERA and 253 strikeouts despite the physical wear of a 36-week season. That mileage matters. Nyberg's suggestion that injuries altered her mechanics is not a minor diagnostic note — it's a structural explanation for why a pitcher who dominated Division I is currently struggling against professional competition.

The AUSL features professionals who track pitching mechanics closely. They are not seeing what opposing hitters at Texas Tech saw. The seven home runs allowed reflect a pitcher whose pitch movement, location, and deception are all slightly misaligned.

What to Watch Today

Today's matchup against the Chicago Bandits at the Stadium at Parkway Bank Sports Complex is the first in-game test of the Nyberg-led mechanical reset. The Bandits are a competitive AUSL franchise. This is not a cupcake test.

The metrics to watch are not just ERA: pitch movement on the riseball, location consistency on the changeup, and whether opponents are squaring up fastballs as easily as they have in her first three outings. Those are the tells that indicate whether the mechanical work is translating to on-field results.

The AUSL regular season runs through July 20. The Championship Series is July 25-27. Canady has time — but not unlimited time.

What's Next

The immediate marker is simple: did today's outing look different? A strong performance against the Bandits would be the first data point suggesting the Nyberg collaboration is working. A second consecutive strong start would confirm a real trend. The AUSL championship window is open through late July, and the Volts need their No. 2 pick pitching like the player they drafted. The "OG NiJa" is in there. Whether she arrives in time for a championship run is the question worth tracking through the rest of July.
