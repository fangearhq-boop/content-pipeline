Isa Torres Commits to Texas — 'I'm Coming Home'

By Sarah Mitchell | I Love Softball | June 19, 2026

Three words. A photo in burnt orange. The loudest moment of the 2026 transfer portal window.

Isa Torres, the 2026 D1 Softball Player of the Year, has committed to the University of Texas — posting the announcement to Instagram Tuesday evening with the caption "I'm coming home." With those three words, the most coveted player in the transfer portal made her decision, and she made it personal.

Torres grew up in Georgetown, Texas, roughly 30 minutes from Red and Charline McCombs Field in Austin. She left home to play at Florida State, where she spent three seasons becoming one of the most decorated players in Seminole history. Now she is coming back to the state that raised her — to the two-time defending national champions who have spent this portal window building toward something even bigger.

Who Isa Torres Is

The numbers from Torres's 2026 season at Florida State are not subtle. A .530 batting average. A .978 slugging percentage. A .591 on-base percentage. Seventy-eight runs scored. She broke multiple single-season program records at FSU — batting average, runs, slugging, and OBP among them.

She also holds the NCAA record for most consecutive hits, having strung together 16 in a row — a mark that reflects not just contact ability but a level of disciplined, consistent approach at the plate that separates elite hitters from historic ones.

Defensively, she was named the ACC Defensive Player of the Year in 2026, an honor that matches the ACC Player of the Year she received on the offensive side. D1 Softball named her the national Player of the Year. She earned First-Team All-American recognition. There was no individual award available to a college softball player in 2026 that Torres did not win.

She is a shortstop by position, a leader by nature, and a native Texan by identity. All three factors shaped where she ended up.

What Texas Already Had

Torres does not walk into a project. She walks into the best program in college softball.

Texas won the national championship in back-to-back years, cementing head coach Mike White's program as the standard-bearer in the sport. The Longhorns have the infrastructure, the facilities, the recruiting history, and the culture of a program that expects to compete for championships every single season.

White had already been active in this portal window before Torres announced. Catcher Samantha Swan arrived from Arizona State, bringing defensive reliability and offensive capability behind the plate. Pitcher Samantha Lincoln came over from Texas Tech, adding depth to a rotation that already profiles as one of the deepest in the sport.

Torres makes the class elite. Swan and Lincoln were strong additions. Torres is the statement.

What This Means for 2027

Texas was already the team to beat in 2027. That was true before the portal opened. It is emphatically true now.

Adding the D1 Player of the Year — a shortstop who posted a .530 batting average and won every individual honor available to her — to a two-time defending championship program is the kind of move that reframes an entire season's conversation before it begins.

Every team in college softball will open their 2027 schedule knowing that Torres is in the Texas lineup. Every pitcher will have to account for what she does to baseballs. Every opposing coach will have to build a gameplan around a player who, in 2026, could not be stopped.

There is also the rivalry element. Texas Tech assembled its own historic portal class this window — Moriah Polar, Jayden Heavener, and Kasidi Pickering arriving to load a Red Raiders program that was already 61-8 and in the WCWS championship game in 2026. Texas vs. Texas Tech is already the marquee matchup on the 2027 calendar. It just became something more.

What's Next

Torres's commitment closes Texas's portal business. The Longhorns are done. They have their class — Torres, Swan, and Lincoln — and now the work shifts to integration, preparation, and the long runway toward Oklahoma City.

For Torres personally, this is the final chapter of her college career playing in the state she grew up in, in front of fans who have watched her from afar and now get to watch her in burnt orange. That is not a small thing.

The portal window closes June 22. Three days remain, and the remaining story is Jaysoni Beachum. But the Torres chapter? That one is written. She is coming home — and Texas softball is better for it.
