Texas Portal Class Is Complete — Torres, Swan and Lincoln Join Two-Time Champions

By Jordan Reeves | I Love Softball | June 19, 2026

Texas head coach Mike White set out to make this portal window matter. He accomplished that with the most important class of his tenure.

With Isa Torres now committed to the Longhorns, Texas closes its 2026 transfer portal class at three players: Torres, catcher Samantha Swan from Arizona State, and pitcher Samantha Lincoln from Texas Tech. Three additions to the two-time defending national championship program. Three players who bring verified production into an already elite environment.

The class is complete. It is also, by any reasonable measure, the best portal class Texas has assembled since the sport's transfer window became the dominant story of every college softball offseason.

Player by Player

Torres is the headliner, and her credentials require no qualification. The 2026 D1 Softball Player of the Year posted a .530 batting average, a .978 slugging percentage, a .591 OBP, and 78 runs scored at Florida State. She won the ACC Player of the Year and Defensive Player of the Year. She earned First-Team All-American recognition. She holds the NCAA record for consecutive hits at 16. She is a shortstop who can play the position at the highest level, from Tallahassee to Austin, without a gap in quality of play.

Swan brings depth and dimension to Texas's roster at catcher — a premium position that demands both defensive skill and offensive contribution. Her time at Arizona State in a competitive Pac-12 environment prepared her for the expectations of a program that competes every year in Oklahoma City. Catching is where championship teams are often built from, and Swan gives Texas a proven presence at the position.

Lincoln is a pitcher who developed at Texas Tech — a program that was 61-8 in 2026 and reached the WCWS championship game. She is moving from a program that will compete for national titles to the program that just won two of them. The experience she brings from pitching in those environments translates directly to what Texas needs: a reliable arm who has already seen the biggest stages in the sport.

What Texas Was Before the Portal

Texas did not need the portal to be good. They were already the class of the sport.

Back-to-back national championships. A program infrastructure that attracts elite recruiting classes before portal windows open. Mike White's tenure at Texas has produced consistent, dominant results — the kind that make a program a destination, not just a contender.

The players who won those championships largely return for 2027. The portal class does not replace what Texas had. It adds to it — specifically in areas where White identified gaps and moved to fill them with proven, high-production transfers.

That is the intelligent use of the portal: not replacement, but reinforcement.

The Rivalry Context

Texas's class will be immediately compared to what Texas Tech assembled this portal window, and the comparison is warranted. The Red Raiders landed three All-Americans of their own: Moriah Polar (nation's top hitter at .555 BA), Jayden Heavener (No. 1 ranked portal pitcher), and Kasidi Pickering (50 career home runs from Oklahoma).

Softball America called Texas Tech's class No. 1 nationally. The same organization's portal rankings had Torres as the top overall available player. The two programs effectively split the elite of the 2026 portal between them.

Texas and Texas Tech were already in a rivalry. They played in the 2026 WCWS championship game. Now they will open 2027 as arguably the two best teams in the country, each measurably better than they were last year, each heading to the same destination the sport points them toward: October in Oklahoma City.

What's Next

Texas is done in the portal. The class is set. The work now is integration — installing Torres into a lineup that is already dangerous, building chemistry with Swan behind the plate, and finding the role where Lincoln is most effective.

White has built something in Austin that gives his program a genuine argument as the best-positioned program to win a third consecutive national title. That is not a small thing. Dynasties in college softball are difficult to sustain; three-peats are rarer still.

But the 2027 Longhorns will be built around the D1 Player of the Year playing shortstop in her home state, behind a proven catching tandem, with a pitching staff deeper than it was when they won it all. That is the class Mike White built. It is complete.
