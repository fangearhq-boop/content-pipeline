Teagan Kavan Won the WCWS MOP Award Twice. No One Had Ever Done That Before.

By Sarah Mitchell June 30, 2026

History Earned Twice

The Women's College World Series has been played for decades, and its Most Outstanding Player award has been earned by some of the greatest pitchers and players the sport has produced. Every year, one player — the one who dominated when the games mattered most — takes home that recognition.

Until this month, no player had taken it home twice.

Texas pitcher Teagan Kavan was named WCWS Most Outstanding Player in 2025. She was named Most Outstanding Player again in 2026. The achievement is unprecedented. In the history of the tournament, spanning every program and every great player who has competed on that stage in Oklahoma City, Kavan is the first to earn the award twice.

What Texas Built Around Her

Kavan's back-to-back MOPs arrived alongside back-to-back national championships. Texas defeated Texas Tech in the 2026 championship series, sweeping the Red Raiders 2-0 to claim the title. That sweep made the Longhorns the 5th program in Division I history to win consecutive national championships — a short list that establishes Texas as one of the sport's great dynasties in the making.

The road to the championship in 2026 was not a wire-to-wire statement. Texas dropped their opening game of the WCWS — a loss that typically signals early exit for programs without the depth and mental durability to flip a switch and win every game from that point forward. Texas won every game after that loss. They are the first team since Oklahoma in 2021 to win the national championship after losing their first tournament game. That path is more impressive, not less, than a clean run through the bracket.

What Makes Kavan's Achievement Historic

WCWS Most Outstanding Player is decided by committee — the most valuable player across the entire championship, not just the winning team's best performer. Earning it once requires pitching at a historically dominant level when every team remaining is playing its best softball and the margin for error is zero.

Earning it twice means doing that in consecutive years, against programs with a full calendar of scouting data on how you pitch and what you throw. Hitters adjust. Programs prepare. The second MOP is harder than the first, not easier, because the entire field of remaining teams has been building their approach around how to beat you.

Kavan pitched through all of it, twice, and both times walked away as the most dominant player in the country's most competitive stage.

What the Dynasty Means Going Forward

Texas is no longer a program building toward sustained success. They have sustained it. Two consecutive national championships, with a core returning and new portal additions like Isa Torres — who broke multiple Florida State records before transferring in — suggest the Longhorns are positioned to compete at the highest level again in 2027.

The target on Texas's back is now the largest in college softball. Every program recruiting toward 2027 is making its pitch around the idea of beating the Longhorns. Every coaching staff studying Texas is looking for something they can exploit. The standard the sport now measures against runs through Austin, and through Kavan specifically.

What's Next

Kavan will enter 2027 as the most accomplished active player in college softball. The question is not whether she can pitch at a high level — she has done that in every environment imaginable. The question is whether Texas can build a third consecutive national championship team around her. If history is any guide, the Longhorns will be the hardest program in the sport to dislodge from the top of the preseason rankings.
