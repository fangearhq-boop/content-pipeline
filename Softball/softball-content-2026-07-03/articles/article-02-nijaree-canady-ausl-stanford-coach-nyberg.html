The Call to Stanford: Why the Texas Volts Brought Back the Coach Who Made Canady Great

By Emily Rawlings July 3, 2026

The Numbers Tell the Story

NiJaree Canady's ERA at Stanford in Year 1 was 0.57. Year 2 was 0.73. Her first year at Texas Tech, she posted 1.11. Her second year at Texas Tech, 1.87. In her professional debut with the Texas Volts, pitching in the AUSL, her ERA is 6.87. She is 0-3. She has allowed 17 earned runs and 7 home runs.

The arc is hard to look at, but it tells something specific rather than something vague. The line from 0.57 to 6.87 is not a line of talent declining. It is a line of mechanics drifting. Those are different problems with different solutions, and the Texas Volts have moved toward a solution.

The Call to Palo Alto

Texas Volts general manager Cat Osterman — herself one of the most decorated pitchers in college softball history — made the decision to bring in Tori Nyberg, Canady's pitching coach during her two seasons at Stanford. Nyberg recruited Canady out of high school, was on the staff when she posted back-to-back sub-1.00 ERAs, and has a relationship with Canady that predates her college career.

What Nyberg identified when he arrived in Texas was a body positioning problem. Injuries at Texas Tech had altered the mechanics Canady developed at Stanford — the mechanics that produced a 0.57 ERA and made her the consensus best college pitcher in the country. The movement patterns had changed enough that her results were no longer matching her stuff. Nyberg's assessment: the original Canady, what he calls the "OG NiJa," is still in there. The work is mechanical, not mental.

Canady's first AUSL appearance lasted 2.1 innings. She allowed six hits and posted a 15.00 ERA in that outing. That was the moment the Volts recognized that the adjustment was more significant than a typical professional learning curve.

The Pro Adjustment Is Real

The college-to-pro transition in softball does not get discussed enough. The pitchers who face Canady in the AUSL are different from anyone she faced in the Pac-12 or the Big 12. Professional hitters have seen more pitching, make faster adjustments, and do not have the approach vulnerabilities that college lineups carry. A pitcher relying on mechanics that have drifted — even a pitcher with Canady's raw talent — will get exposed faster in the AUSL than she would in any college environment.

The other factor is workload. Texas Tech's 2026 season ended with Canady pitching deep into the Women's College World Series on short rest, carrying a program that leaned on her heavily throughout a deep tournament run. The mechanical habits formed under that kind of load are exactly the patterns that get hardened into place. Nyberg's job is to untangle what months of competition compressed.

Still the Draw

None of Canady's early professional struggles change her standing as the sport's biggest name. She will be featured in the MLBx All-Star 3-on-3 at Citizens Bank Park on July 12, part of All-Star Weekend in Philadelphia, as one of four AUSL standouts anchoring the showcase event. The AUSL's highest-profile player is still its highest-profile player. Her ERA is a data point in an ongoing story, not the conclusion of one.

Cat Osterman's public posture has been encouraging throughout. The Volts knew they were getting a player in the first moments of a professional career, and they built their response to her early results around development rather than roster decisions. That is what a GM does when the core belief in a player remains intact. The call to Stanford was the action that matched the belief.

What's Next

Canady's results in the next several AUSL starts will tell whether Nyberg's mechanical corrections are taking hold. The AUSL regular season runs through July 20. Postseason for the Texas Volts begins July 23. There is still time within this season for the adjustment to show in the numbers. If the original mechanics return — if the "OG NiJa" Nyberg is describing becomes visible again on the mound — the professional version of NiJaree Canady will be something new entirely. The college record proved the talent. The next few starts will begin to answer what comes after it.
