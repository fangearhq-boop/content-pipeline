Megan Grant Shatters 31-Year NCAA Single-Season Home Run Record With Her 38th

By Emily Rawlings | I Love Softball | May 10, 2026

UCLA's Megan Grant broke Laura Espinoza's all-time NCAA record during the Big Ten Championship game against Nebraska — hitting her 38th home run of the season in just 55 games.

The record had stood since 1995. For 31 years, through generations of elite softball sluggers and entire eras of the sport's evolution, nobody reached 37 home runs in a single season.

On Saturday, in the 2026 Big Ten Softball Tournament championship game at Maryland Softball Stadium in College Park, UCLA's Megan Grant stepped to the plate against Nebraska pitcher Jordy Frahm and changed the record books forever.

Her 38th home run of the season — a solo shot to left-center field in the third inning — surpassed Laura Espinoza's mark of 37 set at Arizona in 1995. The NCAA single-season home run record, one of the sport's most celebrated achievements, belonged to Grant.

Setting the Stage

Grant had been chasing history all week at the Big Ten Tournament. She hit home runs in each of UCLA's three tournament games, and in the semifinal victory over Wisconsin, she reached 37 — tying Espinoza's 31-year-old record. The softball world held its breath heading into the championship.

One swing later, it exhaled in amazement.

The record-breaking homer came in the third inning, off an 0-2 count from Frahm. Grant connected and sent it to left-center — the same pure, explosive power she has shown all season, now attached to a number that will live in the sport's history forever.

A Record for the Ages

The numbers tell the full story of Grant's extraordinary season. Laura Espinoza set her mark of 37 home runs in 1995 across 72 games. Grant broke it in just 55 appearances — a pace that has no historical parallel in Division I softball.

Think about what that means: every 1.4 games this season, Grant was hitting a home run. She was not chasing a record the way a runner chases a lap time — she was obliterating the underlying assumption about what a power hitter could do in a college softball season.

Thirty-one years is a long time for any sports record to hold. That it fell to a player who is doing it faster, more efficiently, and with more total production than the original record-holder makes Grant's achievement even more remarkable.

A Bittersweet Moment

Grant's record-breaking shot gave UCLA a 2-0 lead in the third inning — and then Nebraska came back. The Huskers won 7-2, with Jordy Frahm throwing a complete game with 8 strikeouts. Nebraska claimed the Big Ten Tournament championship on the same afternoon Grant made history.

It was a genuinely bittersweet moment: the greatest individual achievement in single-season college softball power hitting, delivered in a game her team ultimately lost. But records don't come with consolation asterisks. What Grant did on Saturday belongs to her, and to her alone, regardless of the final score.

What's Next

UCLA will participate in the 2026 NCAA Softball Tournament, with the bracket being revealed Sunday night at 7 PM ET on ESPN2. Despite the loss in the Big Ten championship, the Bruins have plenty of ammunition to earn an at-large bid, and Grant's record-setting season will follow her regardless of how the postseason unfolds.

The record is 38. It belongs to Megan Grant. And the sport of college softball has a new landmark that future generations of power hitters will spend careers trying to approach.
