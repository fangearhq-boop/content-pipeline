Kendall Wells Is Six Home Runs from Rewriting College Softball History

By Emily Rawlings | April 17, 2026 | I Love Softball

Lauren Espinoza hit her 37th home run of the 1995 season for Arizona, and nobody has come close to touching it since. Thirty-one years, hundreds of elite college softball hitters, and that number has stood as the high-water mark for power in the sport's history. Kendall Wells now needs six.

The Oklahoma freshman enters this weekend's Arkansas series with 31 home runs on the season, batting .383 with 60 RBIs. She already broke the NCAA freshman single-season home run record earlier this month, surpassing the shared mark of 30 held by Jocelyn Alo, Lauren Chamberlain, and Kelly Majam with a third-inning blast against Texas on April 12. That was a milestone. This next one would be a different category of achievement entirely.

The all-time single-season record is not the freshman record. It is not the SEC record. It is the number that defines what a college softball player can do with a bat in a single season — one number across all eras, all eras of equipment, all levels of competition. Espinoza set it in a different world, before the growth of club softball pipelines, before the NIL era, before the sport's national broadcast footprint. The fact that it has survived three decades makes it one of the most durable records in collegiate athletics.

The Math Favors Wells

Oklahoma has three games against Arkansas this weekend at Love's Field, then additional SEC series and postseason play still to come. If Wells produces at even a reduced pace over the final stretch — say, one home run every two or three games — she reaches 37 before the regular season ends. At her current rate of roughly 1.5 home runs per series, she could break the record in the coming weeks and still have games remaining.

The record is not a foregone conclusion. It never is. Arkansas will bring an approach designed to neutralize Wells — pitchers do not survive in the SEC by simply throwing strikes to the sport's best power hitter. But Oklahoma has protected her in the lineup effectively all season, and Wells has shown the ability to do damage against any type of pitching she has seen this year.

Tonight's ESPN2 broadcast puts her pursuit directly in front of the country. Matt Schumacker and Amanda Scarborough will be calling every at-bat, and every ball Wells puts in the air will generate crowd noise. That is not pressure she has seemed to struggle with — she hit No. 31 against Texas in a nationally televised Red River Rivalry game, under maximum scrutiny, in the eighth inning of a tied ballgame.

Historical Context

For perspective on just how dominant Wells' season has been: the list of players who hit 30 or more home runs in a single Division I season is extraordinarily short. Most elite power hitters in college softball peak in the mid-20s. Wells has done it as a freshman, against a schedule that includes the best pitchers in the SEC and Big 12.

Espinoza's record has felt untouchable because it requires not just sustained excellence but historic consistency over 60-plus games. Wells has that consistency. She has not gone more than a handful of games without recording extra-base power, and her .383 batting average ensures she is getting on base even in the at-bats that do not end with a home run.

If she breaks the record, it will not happen quietly. It will happen in front of television cameras, with Oklahoma's home crowd at Love's Field or a stadium full of witnesses at whatever venue hosts the final milestone. That is fitting for a record this significant.

What's Next

Wells and the Sooners face Arkansas in Game 1 tonight at 5:30 p.m. CT on ESPN2, Game 2 Saturday, and Game 3 Sunday. After Arkansas, Oklahoma still has SEC games and the postseason before the regular season closes. Six home runs over that span is very achievable at Wells' current pace. Watch her at-bat totals this weekend — the record watch is entering its most intense phase yet.
