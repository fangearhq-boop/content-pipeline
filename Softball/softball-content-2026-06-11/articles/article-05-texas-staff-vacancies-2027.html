Texas Has Two Coaching Vacancies — The Dynasty's Three-Peat Bid Requires Getting This Right

By Emily Rawlings | June 11, 2026 | I Love Softball

The scoreboard says back-to-back national champions. The roster says Teagan Kavan and Katie Stewart are coming back. But the coaching staff looks different today than it did when Texas hoisted its second consecutive national championship trophy in Oklahoma City, and head coach Mike White has work to do before fall practices begin.

Within the span of a single week, Texas lost two of its most important non-head-coaching positions. Ehren Earleywine, the offensive coordinator who engineered the Longhorns' program-record 99 home runs in 2026, departed for Tennessee, where he will serve as associate head coach under Kim Weekly. Kristen Zaleski, who served as associate head coach, also departed. Two of the three most prominent names on Texas's coaching staff — and both roles that carry enormous responsibility for player development and program culture — are now open.

What Those Roles Did

Earleywine's contribution to Texas's 2026 offense is quantifiable. He arrived in Austin and, in one season, produced the most powerful lineup in college softball history. The Longhorns hit 99 home runs — a program record. Katie Stewart, who emerged as one of the most decorated players in the country, set a school record with 31 home runs and won SEC Player of the Year. The offense was the engine of back-to-back championships, and Earleywine was the architect.

That level of offensive output doesn't happen by accident. It comes from coaching — from sequencing at-bats, from mechanical adjustments in the cage, from reading how opposing pitchers are setting up hitters and making real-time adjustments. Whoever replaces Earleywine inherits a talented lineup but also inherits the expectation that Texas will remain the most dangerous offensive team in the sport.

Zaleski's role as associate head coach carried its own weight — player development, culture management, and the daily work of running a program at the highest level. Both positions matter. Both need to be filled with people who can perform at a championship level.

White's Dual Challenge

What makes this offseason particularly challenging is the simultaneity of everything Mike White is managing. The 15-day transfer portal window opened June 8 and runs through June 22. During that same window, White is conducting a coaching search. He is recruiting players, facilitating visits, having scholarship conversations, and managing a program in the middle of its biggest roster turnover in years — all at the same time he needs to identify and hire two coaches.

Fall practices typically begin in August. That gives White approximately two months to hire, onboard, and integrate new coaches before the program begins preparing for 2027. The timeline is manageable but not comfortable. These are not the kinds of hires that benefit from rushing.

What's Next

White has not announced any hirings as of June 11. The expectation is that he is simultaneously conducting the coaching search alongside the portal recruitment process. When names emerge — whether through announcements or reports — they will be scrutinized heavily. Texas's ability to maintain its standard at both the coaching and player levels this offseason will define whether a three-peat is a realistic expectation or an ambitious hope. The players are there. Now comes the work of building the staff that will coach them.
