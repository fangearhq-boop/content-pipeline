Canady Finds the Final Strike: Texas Tech Ousts UCLA 8-7 in Nine Innings

By Emily Rawlings | June 1, 2026

NiJaree Canady had not been herself at this WCWS. Her 7.16 ERA in Oklahoma City stood in stark contrast to the 1.62 mark she carried into the tournament, and Sunday's elimination game against UCLA continued the struggle. But in the bottom of the ninth inning, with Texas Tech's season on the line and Bri Alejandre at the plate, Canady delivered the pitch that ended it.

Strikeout. Game over. Texas Tech 8, UCLA 7 in nine innings.

The No. 11 seed is in the WCWS Final Four.

The Ninth-Inning Breakthrough

Texas Tech had tied the game and gone back and forth with UCLA through eight innings before the Red Raiders took control in the top of the ninth. Taylor Pannell led off with a single and eventually came home to score on Kaitlyn Terry's RBI double to left-center field — giving Tech the lead for good at 8-7.

Mihyia Davis scored on a UCLA error to add an insurance run, and Canady's job was to preserve the two-run advantage. After a rough night on the mound, she did it with the game-ending strikeout of Alejandre, pumping her fist as the stadium erupted.

It was the kind of moment that defines a career and a tournament run. Even when Canady was not at her best, she found a way to be there when it mattered.

Megan Grant's Season Ends

On the other side of the field, Megan Grant's career at UCLA came to a close without the national title she had chased all season.

Grant finished her senior year with 42 home runs — the new NCAA single-season record she had already broken before arriving in Oklahoma City — and 91 career home runs, a new UCLA program record surpassing Stacy Nuveman's mark set in 2002. The numbers are historic. The season was one of the most individually dominant in college softball history.

Her elimination at the WCWS means the record stands for now — 42 home runs in a single season — without a national championship to accompany it. That will be the lasting complexity of Grant's remarkable final year: the statistics were all-time, but the Bruins ran out of games in Oklahoma City.

The Cinderella Run Continues

Texas Tech entered this WCWS as the No. 11 seed — an unseeded double-digit team that most bracketologists expected to exit early. Instead, the Red Raiders have reached the Final Four. They beat Mississippi State in their opener, fell to Tennessee 2-1, then survived back-to-back elimination games to reach the national semifinal.

Canady has been the centerpiece of it all, even in the games where her ERA climbed. Her competitive resolve — staying in the game, making the big pitch when required — has been the throughline of Tech's run.

The Red Raiders' coaching staff and players have spoken throughout the tournament about believing in themselves despite the seed. Sunday's nine-inning survival against a UCLA program that rarely loses close games is the best evidence yet that the belief is justified.

What's Next

Texas Tech faces No. 1 Alabama tonight at 7 PM ET on ESPN in one of the two WCWS semifinals. Jocelyn Briski (25-3, SEC Pitcher of the Year) is the opposing ace — a different challenge than Canady has faced in any prior round. Alabama is 2-0 at the WCWS and has not trailed in either game.

If Canady can find the form she had before the WCWS, the Cinderella story could continue. If Alabama's machine-like consistency prevails, at minimum Texas Tech leaves Oklahoma City as the feel-good program of the 2026 tournament — a No. 11 seed that shocked everyone and earned a national semifinal berth before the season was over.
