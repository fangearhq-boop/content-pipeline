After Pickens, Morgan, and Plumlee: Tennessee Faces Its Steepest Rebuild in Years

By Sarah Mitchell June 8, 2026

The Pickens Era Is Over

For the last several years, college softball had a shared understanding: when you played Tennessee, you faced Karlyn Pickens. The fastest pitcher in college softball history — her top speed of 79.4 miles per hour has never been matched — was the identity, the threat, and the constant that made the Lady Vols one of the most dangerous teams in any bracket they entered.

Pickens graduated after the 2026 season. Three-time NFCA First-Team All-American. Two-time SEC Pitcher of the Year. The kind of player who cannot be replaced from a roster and cannot be replicated through recruiting. She simply existed once at Tennessee, and now she is gone. The program must figure out what it is without her.

The Portal Departures Compound the Challenge

If the Pickens graduation were the only thing Tennessee was managing this offseason, the task would be large but singular. But the transfer portal window opened today, and with it came the confirmation of two additional departures that deepen the rebuild.

Sophomore outfielder Saviya Morgan, who had been developing in the Tennessee lineup, has entered the portal. Freshman pitcher Kailey Plumlee, a young arm the program had been counting on to help fill the gap left by the graduation of Pickens, followed her. Both players announced their intent before the portal window officially opened; their entries today are the formal beginning of their search for new programs.

The combination matters. Pickens was an irreplaceable talent. Morgan was a developmental piece in the outfield who was still growing. Plumlee was the most direct answer to the pitching question that Pickens' departure created. Losing both of them in the same week as the portal opens leaves Tennessee with significant work across multiple positions simultaneously.

What Tennessee Still Has

Context matters here: Tennessee is not a program in crisis. Karen Weekly has built the Lady Vols into one of the most respected softball programs in the country over her tenure, and the 2026 season was evidence that the program remains capable of competing at the highest level. The WCWS appearance — one of many in the program's history — speaks to the culture, the recruiting infrastructure, and the coaching staff's ability to win consistently.

Tennessee still has returning players on the roster. The brand and the fanbase remain among the best in the sport. Weekly's track record of recruiting and developing talent is not diminished by a difficult offseason. Programs absorb losses like this more easily when the coaching staff has already demonstrated the ability to rebuild, and Weekly has that track record.

The 15 Days That Define the Rebuild Timeline

The next two weeks are disproportionately important for Tennessee. The portal window runs through June 22, and every program in the country is competing for the same pool of available talent. Tennessee's ability to use this window — to find pitching, to add offensive pieces, to signal to remaining roster players that the program is actively investing in the 2027 season — will set the tone for what the rebuild looks like.

Programs that move quickly and decisively in portal windows tend to compress their rebuild timelines. Programs that hesitate or are underprepared can find themselves in a hole that takes a full recruiting cycle to exit. Weekly has navigated significant offseasons before. The advantage of experience is that it prepares a coaching staff for exactly this kind of moment.

What's Next

Tennessee will be among the most active programs in the portal over the next 15 days. Watch for pitching additions first — that is the most pressing and most difficult position to fill after Pickens' departure. Any commitment that comes to Knoxville before June 22 will begin to answer the question of how the 2027 Lady Vols take shape. The rebuild has officially begun.
