Megan Grant Hits Record 42nd HR as UCLA Blasts Arkansas 11-0 at the WCWS

By Sarah Mitchell

May 30, 2026

In a nine-run second inning, Grant broke both the NCAA single-season home run record and the UCLA career record. Four Bruins homered, tying a WCWS record.

This is what a record season looks like at its highest moment.

Megan Grant came to the plate in the second inning Friday night with the Women's College World Series scoreboard already turning in UCLA's favor. What followed was the kind of swing that defines careers — a three-run home run, her 42nd of the season, extending her own NCAA single-season record and adding her 91st career home run, the new UCLA all-time record past Stacey Nuveman's mark of 90 that had stood since 2002.

UCLA 11, Arkansas 0. Run rule. Five innings. Devon Park went home early Friday night — but for all the right reasons if you root for the Bruins.

The Second Inning That Changed Everything

The second inning produced nine runs — tied for the second-most in a single WCWS frame in history (per NCAA records) — and it unfolded in a way that felt almost scripted. Aleena Garcia homered on the first pitch she saw. Then came the rally. Then Grant stepped in and delivered the blow that made the record books.

After Grant's three-run shot, Soo-Jin Berry hit a three-run homer of her own to left field. The damage was done before Arkansas could do anything about it. In the fifth inning, freshman utility player Jolyna Lamar homered to stretch the lead further, giving UCLA four different home run hitters in the game — tying the single-game WCWS record.

The numbers are staggering. Grant entered the 2026 WCWS with 41 home runs — already the NCAA single-season record. She added the 42nd on Friday. She also entered with 90 career home runs — already tied for the UCLA record with Nuveman. The 91st put her alone atop the UCLA record books (per UCLA Athletics).

Grant's Two Records in One Night

It is difficult to overstate the significance of what Grant accomplished Friday. Breaking the NCAA single-season home run record is one thing. Doing it during the Women's College World Series, on a stage where every moment is magnified, while simultaneously setting a new career record for her program, is something else entirely.

Nuveman's UCLA record had been in place since 2002 — 24 years. It belonged to one of the most celebrated players in program history. Grant erased it with a swing in an elimination game at Devon Park.

Grant's 68 home runs over the past two seasons ties the all-time Division I record for a two-year span (per reporting from the Associated Press). She was already one of the most prolific power hitters in the history of college softball. Friday's performance put the exclamation point on a case that needed no additional argument.

Arkansas Goes Home With Its Head Up

Arkansas came to the 2026 WCWS for the first time in program history, and their tournament ends at 0-2. The Razorbacks went 47-13 this season — a genuine achievement for a program that had never reached this stage before. Coach Courtney Deifel's team pushed Nebraska to 10 innings in Game 4 on Thursday before falling 5-3. On Friday, they ran into UCLA's best night of the season.

There is no shame in that. The Bruins scored 11 runs in five innings against an Arkansas staff that had held its own for much of the season. This was a performance that would have overwhelmed most teams in the country. Arkansas simply ran into the wrong team on the wrong night — a UCLA offense that was operating at a level few college softball lineups ever reach.

What's Next

UCLA advances to an elimination game Sunday, where they will face the loser of today's Game 7 between Texas Tech and Tennessee. If Texas Tech loses, UCLA faces NiJaree Canady. If Tennessee loses, UCLA faces a program looking for redemption after falling to the Bruins in Game 3 of the opening round.

Grant is still playing. The most prolific home run hitter in college softball history — now definitively — still has games ahead. For Arkansas, the 2026 season ends with a program-first WCWS appearance and 47 wins. That is something to build on.

But Friday night belonged to Grant, to Garcia, to Berry, to Lamar, and to a UCLA team that just delivered one of the most historic offensive performances in Women's College World Series history.
