Oklahoma's Kendall Wells Made History — Now She's Building the Sooners' Future

By Emily Rawlings | I Love Softball | June 25, 2026

In a summer dominated by Texas transfer announcements and coaching staff hirings, a piece of quiet history was made at Oklahoma on June 9.

Freshman catcher Kendall Wells won the 2026 Johnny Bench Award — given annually to the top catcher in college softball. In doing so, she became the first freshman in the award's history to claim the honor. She is only the second Oklahoma player ever to win it.

History made. In year one.

The Award and Why It Matters

The Johnny Bench Award is named for the Hall of Fame catcher widely regarded as the greatest at his position in baseball history. In college softball, the award goes to the top catcher in the country — a player who excels both behind the plate and at the bat, who leads her team, and who demonstrates the complete catcher's skillset that the award was designed to honor.

Freshmen almost never win it. The demands of the catcher position — reading hitters, framing pitches, calling a game, managing a pitching staff — typically require experience that most first-year players simply don't have. The players who have won this award are overwhelmingly upperclassmen, players who have spent three or four years refining their craft at the college level.

Wells did it as a freshman. And she did it for a program that didn't reach the Women's College World Series in 2026.

Oklahoma's 2026 Season and What Comes Next

The Sooners exited in the Super Regional in 2026 — a disappointing end for a program that has made deep WCWS runs under head coach Patty Gasso for much of the last two decades. Oklahoma has won six national championships. They have built one of the most storied programs in the sport's history. But 2026 was a year of transition, and the Super Regional exit reflected the difficulty of rebuilding while still trying to compete at the highest level.

What changes the narrative heading into 2027 is Wells. A generational catcher — one who produced historic individual results in her very first college season — is exactly the kind of cornerstone piece that allows a program to rebuild around a clear identity.

Gasso has done this before. After previous transitions, Oklahoma found its way back to national contention. The infrastructure at Norman is elite: facilities, support staff, name recognition, and a coaching staff that has produced first-round professional players at every position. A program with those foundations and a freshman year's Johnny Bench Award winner behind the plate is not a rebuilding program. It is a program in the early stages of its next run.

The Franchise Piece Is Already In Place

The most expensive thing in college softball roster construction is finding a catcher who can anchor a team for multiple years. Most programs cycle through catchers without ever landing one who wins individual awards. Oklahoma landed one in their first year of the new recruiting class — and she has multiple seasons of eligibility ahead of her.

What the Sooners need to build around Wells: pitching depth, portal additions where needed, and the kind of continuity that comes from a healthy team culture. Gasso has built that before at OU. Wells gives her a clear starting point to do it again.

What's Next

Oklahoma enters the 2026 offseason as a watching-and-waiting program rather than a dominant one — but the long-term trajectory looks stronger than the 2026 results suggest. Kendall Wells enters the summer as the most decorated freshman catcher in college softball history. Watch for Oklahoma's 2027 preseason ranking in January. If the Sooners are in the top 20 — which seems likely given Wells's presence and Gasso's track record — it will signal that the program's return to national contender status is already underway.
