Karlyn Pickens Is Leading AUSL All-Star Voting — and It's Not a Surprise

By Sarah Mitchell

July 5, 2026

Two starts. Two wins. A 1.47 ERA. And now the lead in AUSL All-Star voting.

Karlyn Pickens, the No. 1 overall pick in the inaugural AUSL College Draft, is having the kind of professional debut that No. 1 picks are supposed to have. The Carolina Blaze's ace has not only performed on the field — she's capturing the attention of the sport's growing fan base as well, leading All-Star Cup voting alongside catcher Reese Atwood.

For a league in its first season, Pickens has become one of its defining faces.

The Numbers Behind the Votes

Pickens opened her professional career with a complete-game run-rule win over the Portland Cascade (11-2, five strikeouts) — the kind of dominant, efficient start that immediately establishes authority. She followed that with a 9-1 win in six innings over the Texas Volts.

Her college production translated cleanly. In 2026, Pickens went 15-8 with a 1.70 ERA and 194 strikeouts in 148 innings for Tennessee — numbers that held up in the physical demands of a full SEC schedule. She came into the AUSL healthy and with her mechanics intact. That matters more than the raw talent, which everyone already knew she had.

She also holds the Blaze's fastest-pitch record in the AUSL, adding an element of pure velocity that keeps the sport's conversation about elite pitching centered on her name.

The Contrast That Defines the Early Season

Pickens' success is impossible to discuss without mentioning NiJaree Canady — the No. 2 overall pick, who is 0-3 with a 6.87 ERA through the early AUSL season. Both were college pitching legends. Both entered the same professional league in the same debut season. The gap in their early results has become the defining storyline of professional softball's inaugural year.

The explanation isn't talent. Canady is one of the greatest pitchers in the history of college softball. The explanation, per the Texas Volts' own assessment, is mechanics: injuries at Texas Tech altered her arm path, release point, and body positioning. The team brought in her former Stanford coach Tori Nyberg to rebuild what made Canady dominant in Palo Alto.

Pickens, by contrast, arrived healthy and translated immediately. The All-Star vote reflects that difference in real time.

What's Next

Pickens and the Blaze have legitimate championship aspirations with the AUSL regular season running through July 20. All-Star Cup voting will conclude before the Championship Series (July 25-27), meaning Pickens could headline the midseason showcase and then carry the Blaze into the postseason. If she maintains this trajectory, her first professional season will serve as the benchmark for what the AUSL's No. 1 pick is supposed to deliver — and will raise the standard for every top selection that follows in future drafts.
