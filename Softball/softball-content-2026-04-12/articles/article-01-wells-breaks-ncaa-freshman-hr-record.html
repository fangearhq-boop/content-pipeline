History Made: Kendall Wells Breaks NCAA Freshman Home Run Record With No. 31

By Jordan Reeves | April 12, 2026

Thirty-one years. That's how long the standard sat, untouched, as one of college softball's most durable benchmarks. On Saturday night in Austin, Kendall Wells ended the wait.

The Oklahoma freshman hit HR No. 31 in the fifth inning of the Sooners' 4-3 win over Texas — a three-run shot that broke the NCAA all-time freshman single-season home run record and sent a jolt through college softball's national conversation. The previous record of 30, shared by Lauren Chamberlain, Janie Reed Espinoza, and Dejah Mulipola, is no longer the standard. Wells is.

The location and moment couldn't have been scripted any better. The Red River Showdown. Game 2 of a back-and-forth series at McCombs Field. Texas holding a 2-1 lead going into the fifth. Wells stepped up, and the record fell.

What No. 31 Means

The freshman home run record had been considered one of the most difficult individual marks in college softball. Reaching 30 as a first-year player requires an elite combination of raw power, pitch recognition, and the mental ability to perform at a high level on the biggest stages — things that typically take years to develop.

Wells has done all of it at a pace that genuinely defies comparison. She hit HR No. 27 on March 31 to break the SEC single-season home run record. She tied the freshman record at 30 on April 4 against Kentucky. Now, just one week later, she owns it outright.

For context on the pace: most players who have reached 30 freshman home runs did so over full seasons that extended into the 60s in games played. Wells reached the milestone in 42 games. The efficiency alone makes this season something the sport hasn't seen before.

The Three-Run Shot That Clinched a Series

The record-breaking home run didn't come in a meaningless moment. Oklahoma trailed Texas 2-1 entering the fifth inning, and the game's momentum was very much in play. Wells came up with runners on base and delivered — a three-run shot that gave the Sooners a 4-2 lead they would protect for the series-clinching win.

Oklahoma had already taken Game 1 on Friday, 3-0, with Audrey Lowry throwing a complete-game shutout. Back-to-back wins in Austin — against the defending national champions — represent exactly the kind of road performance that validates a team's national title credentials. Wells' record-breaking swing was the exclamation point on a dominant weekend.

Six More: The All-Time Record Is Still Out There

Breaking the freshman record is remarkable. But there's another number on Wells' horizon that makes this story even more compelling: 37.

The all-time NCAA single-season home run record — held by Espinoza, set at Arizona in 1995 — stands at 37. Wells now needs six more home runs to surpass it. With the regular season continuing into May and the postseason to follow, the games are there. The question is whether the at-bats produce the results.

No freshman has ever broken both records in the same season. Wells is now in position to do exactly that.

What's Next

Oklahoma and Texas play Game 3 Sunday at 1 p.m. CT on ESPN, with the series already clinched for the Sooners. Wells will take every at-bat with No. 32 somewhere in the back of her mind — though the Sooners' goal for Sunday is simply adding another win to a season that is now clearly historic.

The all-time record chase now becomes the defining storyline of the final stretch of the college softball season. Every Wells at-bat will carry extra weight. Every home run swing will be followed by a scoreboard check. Thirty-one down, six to go — and the most exciting record chase in college softball history has just entered its final chapter.
