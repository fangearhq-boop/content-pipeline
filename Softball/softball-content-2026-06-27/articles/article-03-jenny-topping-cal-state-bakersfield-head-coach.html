Jenny Topping Gets the Bakersfield Job — and Brings Olympic Gold to Her First Coaching Post

By Jordan Reeves | I Love Softball | June 27, 2026

Cal State Bakersfield hired Jenny Topping as head coach on June 24. For anyone who followed USA Softball in Athens in 2004, the name requires no introduction. Topping was part of the United States team that won the Olympic gold medal that summer. She is a champion at the highest level the sport has ever reached.

Now she is a first-time Division I head coach, and Bakersfield has made a very loud statement about what kind of program they intend to build.

What an Olympic Credential Does for a Program

Cal State Bakersfield competes in the Western Athletic Conference. The WAC is not the SEC or the Pac-10, but it is a conference where a transformative hire can change a program's recruiting trajectory quickly. What Topping brings to Bakersfield is not just coaching knowledge — it is credibility that cannot be manufactured.

When a coach walks into a living room for a recruiting visit and can say they won an Olympic gold medal, the conversation changes. The prospect and her family are talking to someone who has been to the place the player is trying to reach. That is not a credential that most coaches have, and its recruiting value is real.

For a high school player evaluating options between a mid-major and a mid-major, the coach who played at the highest level becomes a meaningful differentiator. Topping's career does not guarantee Bakersfield will immediately compete for WAC titles. But it raises the floor of what recruiting looks like within the first few cycles of her tenure.

The Broader Pattern

Topping's hire is part of a broader pattern in women's team sports: elite players staying in the sport after their playing careers end and choosing coaching as the vehicle for their continued involvement. When players of Topping's caliber stay, the sport's coaching pool improves. The knowledge she carries — what it takes to compete under Olympic pressure, how elite programs prepare, what separates good players from great ones — enters the development pipeline for the next generation of players.

The coaching carousel this week produced several notable moves beyond Topping. Frank Reed retired after 25 years at Chattanooga. Multiple assistant coaches were elevated or made lateral moves at programs across the country. The sport is turning over its coaching base with experienced players who bring firsthand knowledge of what it takes to compete at the highest levels.

Bakersfield was not a program most people had on their radar heading into the 2026-27 offseason. They are now. That is not a trivial outcome from a single hire.

What's Next

Topping will begin assembling her staff and building her recruiting class over the coming months. The 2026-27 roster is largely in place, giving her a first year to establish systems and culture before the roster reflects her own personnel decisions. The 2027 recruiting class — and specifically how she deploys her Olympic credential in recruiting conversations — will be the first real test of what her tenure at Bakersfield can become. The sport will be watching.
