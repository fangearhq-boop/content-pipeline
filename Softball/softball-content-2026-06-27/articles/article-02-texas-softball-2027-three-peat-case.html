Texas Softball 2027: The Specific Case for a Three-Peat

By Emily Rawlings | I Love Softball | June 27, 2026

The abstract version of the Texas argument — that they are loaded, that they won the portal, that they are the defending national champions — has been told. It is accurate. It is also incomplete.

The case for a three-peat is specific, not general. It lives in the details of who is playing where, who is coaching what, and whether the pieces actually connect. Those details have been coming into focus, and the picture they form is more compelling than the headline version.

The Lineup Shift That Changes Everything

Katie Stewart hit 31 home runs in 2026 — a Texas single-season school record. She did it as an outfielder. For 2027, she is moving to first base.

That position change is not a demotion. It is a roster puzzle solved. Isa Torres, who committed to Texas after winning the D1 Softball Player of the Year award, needs to play somewhere. The outfield is her natural home. But with Stewart already occupying that space as one of the most productive hitters in the country, the math required someone to adjust.

Stewart's willingness to move — and the offensive production she brings regardless of the position she plays — is the kind of selfless roster flexibility that championship teams require. The result is a lineup that begins with Stewart at first, Torres in the outfield, and Teagan Kavan at her natural position. Any pitching staff preparing to face that combination in 2027 will not have a comparable lineup in their database.

The Staff Additions

Texas hired Amber Freeman as an assistant coach. Freeman comes from Arizona, where she worked with hitters and catchers. Head coach Mike White has been explicit about wanting offensive infrastructure that matches the talent level on the field. Freeman's background gives him that.

Texas was also recognized as the 2026 ATEC/NFCA Division I National Coaching Staff of the Year. The award acknowledges what the sport has come to understand through results: the staff in Austin is operating at the top of the profession. Adding Freeman does not replace anything — it adds a specific skillset to a staff that was already recognized as the best in the country.

The Three-Peat Context

Three-peats in college softball are not impossible, but they are genuinely rare. Oklahoma built the sport's most recognizable sustained dynasty, winning multiple national championships across a half-decade run. That required continuous roster renewal, pitching development, and a staff capable of making adjustments every year.

Texas's situation is structurally similar. The Longhorns are not winning on one player or one cycle of recruiting. They have a coaching staff that earned national recognition, a portal strategy that produced the No. 1 class in the country, and a returning core that knows what winning at the highest level feels like.

The programs most likely to interrupt a three-peat — Texas Tech, Florida, Tennessee, Nebraska — are all building. None of them will be passive. But Texas enters 2027 with more specific advantages than any of them, and those advantages are not likely to disappear by February.

What's Next

Texas's fall practice begins in August. The roster, as currently constructed, gives White and his staff enough pieces to work through lineup construction before the spring season arrives. The questions that remain — how Torres integrates with a new program, how Stewart adjusts to first base, how Freeman's offensive system fits with the existing approach — are the right questions to have. They are about optimization, not survival. Texas goes into 2027 with a problem every program in the country would trade for: too many good players for the positions they currently play.
