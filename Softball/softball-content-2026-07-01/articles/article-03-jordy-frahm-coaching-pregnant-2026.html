Jordy Frahm Pitched the Whole 2026 Season While Pregnant. Then She Joined Nebraska's Coaching Staff.

By Jordan Reeves July 1, 2026

The Revelation

When Nebraska's 2026 season ended — 52 wins, 8 losses, a Women's College World Series appearance that the program had not made in 13 years, and Jordy Frahm winning NFCA National Player of the Year for the second consecutive season — the story felt complete. The most decorated pitching performance in recent college softball history, by a program that had rebuilt itself into a national contender around a single player who could win a game at the plate and in the circle on the same afternoon.

Now the full story has been told. Frahm revealed that she competed throughout the entire 2026 season while pregnant. She is due in December 2026. She joined the Nebraska coaching staff on June 5.

Every number from Nebraska's 2026 season now carries a different weight.

What the Numbers Mean Now

Frahm's 2026 pitching line: 21-6 record, 1.37 ERA, 251 strikeouts, 12 saves. The 12 saves led all of Division I. She won the NFCA National Player of the Year for the second consecutive year — the first player to win back-to-back since the award was established in 2016.

Those statistics were historically elite before this revelation. A 1.37 ERA and 251 strikeouts are numbers that define a generation-level pitching season. Twelve saves as a starting pitcher capable of closing games in relief reflects a versatility that stretched Nebraska's coaching staff's ability to deploy her. She was the rare player who made a staff better by simply being available in multiple roles on consecutive days.

Understanding that she did it while pregnant — managing physical changes across a spring season that runs from February into June — transforms what Nebraska accomplished from a great season into something harder to categorize. There is no obvious comparison. The athletic and competitive demands of Division I softball pitching at an elite level are severe under ordinary circumstances. The circumstances Frahm was managing were not ordinary.

The Transition to Coaching

Frahm joined the Nebraska coaching staff on June 5, less than two weeks after the 2026 season concluded. The move is not surprising. She spent four years as the most important player in the program's recent history, working closely with a coaching staff that built a system around her abilities while developing her as a complete player. The institutional knowledge she carries — about what Nebraska's pitchers need, how the staff thinks about preparation, how a program at that level approaches the WCWS — is the kind of knowledge you do not find on the open market.

What is notable is the timing. Frahm is not stepping away from the game to process a remarkable career before deciding what comes next. She is joining the staff immediately, with a December due date ahead of her. The transition is not a pause. It is a continuation, with a new role and new responsibilities added to an already remarkable set of circumstances.

What This Means for Nebraska's Legacy

Nebraska's 2026 season was already the best in program history. The WCWS appearance ended a 13-year drought. The 52-8 record was the program's best in modern softball. Frahm's individual awards — two consecutive NFCA National Player of the Year honors, the nation-leading saves total, the ERA that no starter in Division I could match — gave the season a singular identity.

That identity is now more complex. The program that got Jordy Frahm to perform at that level across a full season while she was pregnant did not know it was asking that of her. The question of what the 2026 Nebraska season was, and what it represents, has changed in ways that cannot be reduced to a single sentence. What is clear is that the answer to that question is larger than it was the day the season ended.

What's Next

Frahm will spend the summer on Nebraska's staff while preparing for December. The Huskers begin 2027 without their anchor in the circle, building a pitching staff and a lineup that will need to find its identity without the player who defined the program for four years. The coaching staff now includes the person who knows what that standard looks like from the inside. What Nebraska does with that knowledge in 2027 is the next chapter in a story that — as of today — is still being written.
