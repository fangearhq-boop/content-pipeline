Home Is Where the Trophy Is: Texas Brings WCWS Title Back to a City Ready to Celebrate

By Sarah Mitchell June 6, 2026

Austin Turns Out for Its Champions

They came in the rain. They came in burnt orange. Thousands of Texas softball fans packed into McCombs Field on Friday night for a free public celebration welcoming home the back-to-back national champion Longhorns, and the turnout was exactly what you'd expect from a city that has watched two consecutive championships come through their program.

The Longhorn Band played. Bevo XV made an appearance. Smokey the Cannon fired. Texas Athletics set up photo opportunities for fans and gave away championship posters to the first 500 through the gates. For one Friday evening, McCombs Field became the epicenter of the best run in current college softball.

A Year That Will Be Remembered at UT

The celebration capped an academic year that no Texas Athletics department would want to improve upon. The Longhorns claimed three national championships in the 2025-26 school year. Men's swimming and diving won in March. Rowing brought a title home on May 31. And now softball has made it three — back-to-back at Devon Park in Oklahoma City.

Three team national championships in a single academic year is an extraordinary feat for any program. At Texas, it was treated the way all great things should be: with music, with fans, with a crowd willing to stand in the rain for the chance to say thank you.

The Champions Came Back to Their People

For Teagan Kavan, Katie Stewart, and the rest of the Longhorns, Friday night was the exhale. The tournament that began at Devon Park on May 28 had taken everything out of them — a championship series sweep of Texas Tech, a most outstanding player honor for Kavan for the second straight year, and a place in the history books as one of only five programs to win consecutive national titles.

Coming home to a celebration like this is part of what makes championships mean something at the college level. These players aren't earning salaries. They play for the program, the school, and the community that fills the stands. Friday night, Austin gave something back.

What's Next

The party is over, but the work is not. Both Teagan Kavan and Katie Stewart are returning juniors, which means Texas softball heads into the 2027 season with its best player and its best position bat returning for one more run. The three-peat conversation has already begun. For a program that has built the standard in college softball, the only question left is whether history is about to get even bigger in Austin.
