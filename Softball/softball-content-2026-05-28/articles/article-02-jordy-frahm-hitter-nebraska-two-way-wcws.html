Nebraska's Secret Weapon — Jordy Frahm Hits 19 Home Runs Too

By Emily Rawlings

May 28, 2026

The 2026 USA Softball Collegiate Player of the Year brought a 20-4 record to Devon Park. She also brought 19 home runs and a 1.338 OPS. Arkansas has to stop both versions of her tonight.

By now, the pitching résumé is well-documented. Jordy Frahm is 20-4. She carries a 1.14 ERA and 234 strikeouts in 171.2 innings pitched. She is the 2026 USA Softball Collegiate Player of the Year, announced just yesterday — the sport's highest individual honor, earned the day before she takes the mound at the Women's College World Series for the first time.

That version of Jordy Frahm — the pitcher — is the one opposing teams spend their preparation time worrying about. It is the version that helped Nebraska hold opponents to just three total runs in five NCAA postseason games.

But when Nebraska's nightcap against Arkansas begins tonight at 9:30 PM ET, Frahm will also walk to the plate. And the numbers she carries there are just as formidable.

The Hitting Numbers No One Is Talking About

Jordy Frahm hit 19 home runs in 2026. She posted a 1.338 OPS — a number that would stand out in any lineup in the country, regardless of what she did in the circle. For context, an OPS above 1.000 in college softball is elite. A 1.338 OPS from a starting pitcher is something different entirely.

Nebraska finished the regular season 51-6 with a 26-game winning streak — the longest active streak in Division I softball and the longest in program history. That run did not happen by pitching alone. The offense averaged 7.0 runs per game and hit .334 as a team. Frahm was a core reason why.

The combination forces opposing coaches into decisions that have no clean answer. You cannot gear your game plan entirely toward neutralizing Frahm's bat without opening up space for the rest of Nebraska's lineup. And you cannot concentrate entirely on not scoring against Frahm the pitcher without neglecting the reality that she may also be the hitter who beats you.

What Two-Way Production Does to a Roster

The practical impact of a two-way ace goes beyond any single stat. Nebraska head coach Rhonda Revelle has described her program's identity as "nameless and faceless" — a collective over individual recognition. It is a philosophy built around the idea that no single player is bigger than the team's mission.

That identity has held through a 51-win season and a postseason run that has allowed just three runs in five games. But Jordy Frahm now has a name. She has the Player of the Year award. And tonight, when she steps into the box against Arkansas pitcher Robyn Herron, the "nameless, faceless" philosophy will go right out the window — because everyone in the stadium will be watching exactly what she does.

That attention, if anything, might benefit Nebraska. The Huskers have been competing in relative quiet for most of this season. The WCWS stage changes that. And Frahm, by all available evidence, is built for exactly this kind of moment.

The Test She Has Not Faced

Nebraska's last WCWS appearance was in 2013. Most of the players on this roster were in elementary school. They have not experienced Devon Park's specific pressure before — the television spotlights, the crowd noise, the elimination urgency of double elimination when the first loss changes everything.

Frahm has been spectacular in five NCAA postseason games, but those games came at regional and super regional sites. Devon Park is a different category. A 0.42 ERA in five postseason games is remarkable context. Tonight she shows whether it translates to the biggest stage in the sport.

Arkansas brings a .345 team batting average and averages 7.7 runs per game. They scored enough to earn a No. 2 seed and survive to the WCWS for the first time in program history. They are not a team that will simply yield.

What's Next

Tonight's game is the final slot of opening day — 9:30 PM ET on ESPN2, the nightcap of a twelve-hour WCWS marathon. The late start benefits a pitcher who can lock in during a long wait. Whether Frahm takes the mound, the batter's box, or both tonight, Nebraska's identity as a team enters Devon Park with a two-way weapon that no scouting report fully captures.

Arkansas has a plan. Every team here has a plan. But Jordy Frahm — pitcher, hitter, Player of the Year — is a problem that does not fit neatly into any one game plan.
