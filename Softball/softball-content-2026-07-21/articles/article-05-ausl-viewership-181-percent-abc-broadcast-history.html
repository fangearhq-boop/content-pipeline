Pro Softball Didn't Get a Network Broadcast. It Earned One.

By Jordan Reeves | I Love Softball | July 21, 2026

On July 25, Game 1 of the AUSL Championship Series will air on ABC. It will be the first professional softball game ever broadcast on a U.S. broadcast television network — not cable, not streaming, but network television. When people ask how that happened, there is a clear answer: the numbers.

The ABC broadcast is not a promotional gesture. It is not a favor from a network that shares a parent company with the league's primary broadcaster. It is the direct result of viewership data that justified the placement, from a league in only its second season of existence.

The Numbers

AUSL viewership is up 181 percent over this same point in the 2025 season. That is not a typical year-over-year growth figure for a professional sports league. Established leagues in their second decade may track viewership trends in the single digits, positive or negative. A league in its second year posting 181 percent growth over its first represents something different: an audience that found the product and came back at a substantially higher rate.

The single most-watched regular-season game in AUSL history came on June 21, when the Oklahoma City Spark faced the Portland Cascade. That game drew 404,000 average viewers — a league record. For context, the Cascade was eliminated from the playoffs on Tuesday. The game that set the viewership record involved one of the three teams that made the postseason and one that didn't. That is what the audience showed up for regardless of bracket implications.

The 2026 season has been distributed across ESPN, ESPN2, ESPNU, ESPN Deportes, CBS Sports Network, and MLB Network. Each platform has contributed to a collective audience that, according to viewership data, represents the single fastest year-over-year growth in professional softball history.

What the ABC Broadcast Represents

Four years ago, the idea of a sustainable professional softball league in the United States was still an open question. The Athletes Unlimited model — built on individual player scoring alongside team results — was new, untested at the professional level, and drawing skepticism from parts of the softball world that had watched previous leagues fail. Two years ago, the AUSL played its first game.

On Saturday, July 25, the Championship Series begins on ABC.

Network television reaches audiences that cable and streaming don't. It reaches households that don't have cable subscriptions. It reaches casual sports fans who scroll past a game they'd never seek out on ESPN+ but will pause on a network broadcast in primetime. The ABC placement gives the AUSL Championship the kind of visibility that cable sports networks have never provided for professional softball.

That visibility matters not just for the championship, but for what comes next. A sport that can demonstrate its audience on a network broadcast carries that into future rights negotiations, future sponsorship conversations, and future expansion discussions. The July 25 broadcast is a document that will be cited for years.

How It Got Here

The simplest explanation: the audience told the networks that professional softball was worth the placement. Viewership up 181 percent over year one. A record-setting regular-season game. Distribution across six platforms. The data made the case that the ABC broadcast team didn't have to. When a property doubles its audience in one year, the question isn't whether to put it on network television. The question is when.

The when is July 25. This Saturday. Davis Diamond in College Station, Texas.

The sport earned this moment. The viewers who showed up in 2025, and then showed up in far larger numbers in 2026, made it possible. The ABC broadcast is the result of what happens when a sport with a dedicated audience finally gets the product — and the league — that it deserved.

What's Next

AUSL Championship Series Game 1 airs on ABC on Saturday, July 25, at Davis Diamond in College Station, Texas.
