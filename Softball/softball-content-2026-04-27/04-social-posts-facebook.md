### STORY 2: Kendall Wells One Homer From History

**Long-Form Post**
Window: 11:00 AM ET

🏠 ONE HOME RUN FROM HISTORY

Lauren Espinoza set the all-time NCAA single-season home run record with 37 home runs for Arizona in 1995 — 31 years ago.

No one has touched it since.

Until now, maybe.

Oklahoma freshman Kendall Wells hit her 36th home run of the season Sunday in the Sooners' 3-1 win over No. 11 Georgia. Just one home run separates her from the most celebrated record in college softball history.

What makes this even more remarkable: Wells already owns the NCAA freshman home run record (HR No. 31, April 11 vs. Texas). She's doing things no first-year player has ever done, in a season that has No. 1 Oklahoma at 45-6 and firmly in control of their national championship destiny.

If Wells hits No. 37, she'll join Espinoza as the only player in Division I softball history to hit that many in a single season. And she'll do it as a freshman — someone who hadn't played a single college game before February.

Oklahoma still has five regular-season games remaining, plus conference and postseason play. Wells has plenty of chances.

The record is right there.

Will she get it?

### STORY 3: Katie Stewart Texas Program Record

**Long-Form Post**
Window: 1:00 PM ET

🤘 KATIE STEWART MAKES LONGHORN HISTORY

Junior first baseman Katie Stewart had a weekend for the record books at Kentucky — and we mean that literally.

On Saturday, Stewart crushed a go-ahead grand slam in the seventh inning to tie the Texas program's single-season home run record at 23, helping the Longhorns win 6-1.

Then on Sunday, she went one better — hitting home run No. 24 to break Reese Atwood's Texas program record outright. Texas finished the game 11-2 and swept the series from Kentucky.

What makes Stewart's feat even sweeter: she broke a record that was set just last year (Atwood hit 23 in 2025). And she did it with postseason games still ahead — giving her the chance to push the record even higher before her junior season is done.

No. 4 Texas (approximately 40-9 overall, 14-7 SEC) is firmly in national seed contention, and Stewart's power surge has been a major reason why. She leads the SEC in home runs among juniors and is one of the most feared hitters in the country right now.

We said after March that Stewart was someone to watch. She's delivering.
