Texas vs. Texas Tech: Breaking Down Who Actually Won the 2026 Transfer Portal

By Sarah Mitchell | I Love Softball | June 20, 2026

The 2026 NCAA softball transfer portal window produced two headline-making classes — one from the two-time defending national champions, and one from the program that finished as the WCWS runner-up. With the window closing June 22, it's worth settling the debate that's been running across softball circles all week: Texas or Texas Tech — who actually won the portal?

The honest answer is that both arguments are legitimate. But the reasons are different, and the implications for 2027 are distinct enough to be worth unpacking carefully.

What Texas Built

Texas entered the portal as two-time defending national champions and exited it with the most impactful single addition of any program in the country.

Isa Torres is the 2026 D1 Softball Player of the Year. She hit .530 with First-Team All-American recognition, ACC Player of the Year honors, and a defensive résumé that won her the ACC Defensive Player of the Year award as well. She also holds the NCAA record for consecutive hits. Texas landed her — a Texas native, a homecoming story, and the best player in college softball — in the same portal window where they also added catcher Samantha Swan from Arizona State and pitcher Samantha Lincoln from Texas Tech.

The Texas class, including Samantha Altmeyer, gives Mike White's program a retooled roster around a championship core. Swan addresses the catching position with proven reliability. Lincoln adds arm depth to an already formidable rotation. Torres transforms the lineup into something that opposing pitchers will lose sleep over.

No single player moved in this portal window carries more weight than Torres. That's what Texas wins: the narrative, the headline, the player who defines the entire offseason conversation.

What Texas Tech Built

Texas Tech finished 61-8 in 2026 and played for the national championship. Then they opened the portal and assembled what Softball America immediately ranked the No. 1 class in the country.

Moriah Polar arrived from Purdue as the nation's top hitter by batting average — .555, with 101 hits on the season. To put that in perspective: she hit the ball at a rate that statistically outperformed Torres by 25 points. Polar is a contact and consistency machine at a level that strains comprehension.

Jayden Heavener came in as the No. 1 pitcher available in the portal, posting a 2.81 ERA and 128 strikeouts. Adding the top available arm to a rotation that already carried the Red Raiders to a WCWS final is the kind of move that shifts a program from contender to favorite.

Kasidi Pickering arrived from Oklahoma with 50 career home runs, a .370 batting average, and 20 home runs in 2026 alone. She is a former Sooner power hitter who lands in Lubbock with a point to prove and a lineup around her that makes her more dangerous, not less.

Three All-American-caliber players. Three positions of need addressed. Softball America's No. 1 ranking — not self-assigned, not manufactured, but earned through the weight of the additions.

The Rest of the Field

Texas and Texas Tech were not the only programs to make meaningful moves, and context requires noting what else happened in this window.

Arkansas landed Katie Wetteland — the Mountain West Freshman of the Year with a 1.95 ERA — alongside Kendall Yarnell from UCF, who hit .344 with 15 home runs and 46 RBI. That's a legitimate two-player haul that makes the Razorbacks a more complete team.

Tennessee added Karley Shelton from South Carolina, a .345 hitter with 40 RBI, and returns a proven senior in Mardjetko. The Vols were already dangerous. They got more so.

Florida State, starting from the worst position of any major program — having lost Torres and potentially Beachum — committed No. 10 portal player Ella Dodge. Given the circumstances, getting a top-10 player with two years of eligibility into the fold is a reasonable start. It is not a match for what FSU lost.

Oklahoma added depth with Adi Hansen. Texas A&M brought in Makena Azua. Alabama added Kailey Plumlee from Tennessee. The portal's secondary moves filled real roster needs at programs that needed to stay competitive without reinventing themselves.

So Who Won?

Texas wins the narrative. There is no player available in the 2026 portal who carries more weight than Isa Torres, and landing her at home — to the defending champions, in the state where she grew up — is a story with emotional, historical, and competitive dimensions that no other transaction this cycle can match. If you're judging on the single highest-impact move, Texas wins.

Texas Tech wins the class ranking. Softball America's No. 1 designation is not a minor honor. Three legitimate All-American additions — the nation's top hitter, the nation's top pitcher, and a 50-career-home-run power bat — assembled onto a roster that went 61-8 and nearly won a national championship in 2026 is a depth-and-volume argument that holds up under scrutiny. If you're judging on total class value, Texas Tech wins.

Both arguments are legitimate because they are measuring different things. Texas made the most impactful individual addition. Texas Tech made the most comprehensive class addition. Both results are real. The sport is big enough to hold both answers at once.

The only honest tiebreaker is the 2027 season. When these two programs play each other — and they will — the portal receipts come due on the field. That is the only scoreboard that actually settles it.

What's Next

The portal closes June 22. With 120 players having entered the window and more than 79 committed — a rate above 66 percent — the volume of this cycle underscores how central the portal has become to offseason roster construction at every level.

The remaining significant uncommitted name is Jaysoni Beachum, the Softball America No. 1 player with a .417 batting average, 16 home runs, and a .549 on-base percentage in 2026. Where she lands before Sunday's deadline will be the final major news event of this portal cycle and could shift the team-level analysis depending on her destination.

After the portal closes, the next chapter of the Texas vs. Texas Tech conversation plays out in fall practice, in the preseason rankings, and eventually on a softball diamond in 2027. Whichever program performs when it matters most will own the final word.
