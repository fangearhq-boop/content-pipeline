FSU's Next Era Starts With Ella Dodge — and a Lot of Work Still Left to Do

By Jordan Reeves | I Love Softball | June 20, 2026

On June 19, Florida State announced the commitment of Ella Dodge — a top-10 transfer portal player, a Florida native, and someone who gives the Seminoles a real piece to build around heading into 2027. It's a solid move. It is not a solution. And no one at FSU should pretend otherwise.

The reality of where Florida State sits right now is stark. Isa Torres — the 2026 D1 Softball Player of the Year, ACC Player of the Year, and owner of a .530 batting average that broke program records and redefined what excellence at the plate looks like — is gone to Texas. Jaysoni Beachum, ranked No. 1 in the transfer portal by Softball America, is still uncommitted and may well leave the Seminole circle for good. The portal closes June 22. Two days remain.

What FSU does in those two days will shape the program's trajectory far more than any single commitment already made.

What Ella Dodge Brings

Dodge arrives with legitimate credentials. Ranked No. 10 in the transfer portal, she posted a .285 batting average with 11 home runs and 46 RBI — numbers that profile as a productive, multi-dimensional contributor in any lineup. She's a Florida native, which matters for recruiting relationships and program culture. She's a redshirt junior with two years of eligibility remaining, meaning FSU isn't adding a one-year rental. They're adding someone who can become part of the program's identity through 2028.

That long-term value matters. The portal era rewards programs that find contributors who stick around, not just names that look good in a press release. Dodge has the eligibility and the profile to develop into something more than a stopgap.

But context is everything. Torres was hitting .530. Dodge is at .285. Torres was an All-American first baseman who won every individual award available in college softball. Dodge is a top-10 portal player — a different tier, on a different level of the sport's hierarchy. Acknowledging that gap isn't disrespect. It's math.

The Challenge of Rebuilding in the Portal Era

Florida State's situation is a case study in the new reality of college softball. The transfer portal giveth and the transfer portal taketh away — often simultaneously and often from the same program.

The Seminoles watched Torres become the best player in college softball over three seasons, then watched her enter the portal and commit to Texas in a matter of days. That's the deal now. Programs develop elite players. Elite players leave. The cycle is relentless.

What separates programs that stay competitive from those that spiral is the ability to replenish quickly. FSU has done part of that work with Dodge. A top-10 portal player committed within the first two weeks of the window is a real achievement — plenty of programs spend the entire 15 days chasing names and landing nothing of substance.

But the Beachum situation hangs over everything. She batted .417 with 16 home runs and a .549 on-base percentage in 2026. Her career numbers — 37 home runs, 180 RBI, 202 hits — represent the kind of sustained production that doesn't just walk back through the door. If she leaves FSU, the Seminoles will have lost two of their three most productive players from the 2026 roster in a single portal window. That is a significant hole that no single commitment fully fills.

Head coach Lonni Alameda has rebuilt the FSU program before. She knows what it takes. The question is whether this portal cycle — with two days remaining — gives her the pieces to do it again.

What FSU's Approach Signals

Adding Dodge signals that Florida State is not panicking. They found a credentialed, young player with multiple years of eligibility and made the commitment happen. That's how you build in the portal era — not by reaching for names that don't fit, but by identifying players who genuinely project into your system.

The two-year eligibility factor is particularly telling. FSU could have chased a one-year player with bigger numbers and a shorter runway. Instead they went younger, went deeper, and went home — Dodge is a Florida native choosing to stay in state, which carries its own recruiting narrative heading into future cycles.

The approach is sound. The results, so far, are modest relative to what was lost. Both of those things can be true at the same time.

What's Next

The portal closes June 22. Florida State has 48 hours to add to what Dodge started. Whether Beachum stays or goes will define the ceiling of this roster more than any other single decision — and right now, that decision remains unresolved.

If Beachum commits elsewhere, FSU will need to find another high-impact name before Sunday. If she stays, the picture changes substantially — a .417 hitter and a No. 10 portal recruit in the same lineup is a foundation worth building on, even if it's nowhere near what FSU had in 2026.

Watch the final 48 hours closely. The Seminoles' rebuild is underway. Whether it gains real momentum before the window shuts is still an open question.
