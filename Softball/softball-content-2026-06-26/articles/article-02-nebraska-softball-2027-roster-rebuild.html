Nebraska's Historic WCWS Return: Inside the Roster Being Built for 2027

By Emily Rawlings | I Love Softball | June 26, 2026

Thirteen years is a long time to wait. It is long enough for an entire generation of Nebraska fans to grow up having never seen their program compete in Oklahoma City.

The 2026 Cornhuskers ended that drought. Nebraska went 52-8, recorded a .867 winning percentage — the best in program history — and finished 5th at the Women's College World Series. It was the first WCWS appearance for the program since 2013, and it announced something that the college softball world needed to hear clearly: Nebraska is back.

The question that arrives immediately after every successful season is whether it was a one-year miracle or the beginning of something sustained. The evidence, in Nebraska's case, points toward sustained.

The Transfer Core That Built the Run

The 2026 season was made possible by a transfer portal strategy that head coach Rhonda Revelle executed with precision. The Cornhuskers brought in Jordy Frahm and Hannah Coor from Oklahoma, Lauren Camenzind and Hannah Camenzind from Arkansas, Jesse Farrell from UNLV, and Ava Kuszak from Wisconsin.

These were not random portal additions. They were targeted players selected to fill specific positional needs and bring program experience from established programs. The Camenzind sisters brought SEC pedigree from Arkansas. Frahm and Coor arrived from one of the program's most direct competitors in the Big 12 landscape. Farrell added experience from a Mountain West program comfortable competing under pressure. Kuszak brought Big Ten familiarity.

The core of that class returns for 2027. Nebraska is not rebuilding again — they are returning the pieces that made the historic 2026 run possible and adding to them.

The Addition That Changes the Ceiling

The most notable offseason move has been the addition of pitcher Lorin Boutté from Ohio State. Boutté earned All-Big Ten Freshman Team honors and Freshman All-American recognition in 2025, establishing herself as one of the most promising young arms to emerge from the Big Ten.

Pitching is the area where programs that want to sustain WCWS-level performance cannot afford gaps. Nebraska's 2026 run was built on competitive pitching within their rotation, but adding a pitcher with Boutté's accolades at this early stage of her career gives the Cornhuskers something they did not have entering 2026: a proven high-ceiling arm who has yet to reach her prime.

A freshman All-American pitcher who now competes for a WCWS program changes the ceiling of what Nebraska's rotation can become.

The Program Infrastructure Is Real

It would be easy to dismiss the 2026 Nebraska season as a transfer portal mirage — a good team assembled quickly from other programs' players, without the institutional foundation to sustain it. That reading is wrong.

Revelle's program has the facilities, the recruiting footprint in the Midwest, and the staff continuity to build something lasting. The portal accelerated a timeline that was already trending upward. The returners from 2026 are the proof: these players chose Nebraska knowing what they were committing to, competed at the WCWS, and are coming back.

When players return to a program after its best season in 13 years, it says something about what it is like to be inside that program. Nebraska has something real.

What's Next

Nebraska enters fall practice with a roster that is deeper and more experienced than anything the program has had in at least a decade. The question for 2027 is not whether the Cornhuskers can reach the WCWS — it is whether they can compete once they get there. The 5th-place finish in 2026 was historic. The 2027 goal is to go further. With the returning transfer core, Boutté on the mound, and a program infrastructure that is now proven at the highest level, that is not an unreasonable expectation.
