The 2027 Challengers: Florida and Tennessee Are Coming for Texas

By Sarah Mitchell | I Love Softball | June 26, 2026

Texas is No. 1. That will be true in January when the preseason polls come out, and it will likely remain true in February when the season opens. The Longhorns are the defending back-to-back national champions with the most loaded roster in the history of college softball and a complete coaching staff in place.

But two programs entered the 2026-27 offseason with rosters deep enough in All-SEC talent to make a legitimate challenge — and neither of them is Texas Tech.

Florida and Tennessee are coming.

Florida: Three First-Team All-SEC Returners

Florida returns three first-team All-SEC players for 2027: Keagan Rothrock, Taylor Shumaker, and Townsen Thomas. Three first-team All-SEC players at the same program in the same season is a remarkable concentration of talent — it means Florida's starting lineup has multiple players whom the SEC coaches themselves selected as the conference's best at their positions.

And then there is the addition. Tim Walton landed Jaysoni Beachum — the player On3 ranked as the No. 1 transfer in the entire portal — in this offseason's window. Beachum posted a .417 batting average with 16 home runs, 66 RBI, and a .549 on-base percentage. She is a transformative offensive presence joining a roster that was already producing three first-team All-SEC performers.

The combination of Beachum's offensive production with the existing All-SEC core gives Florida an offensive ceiling that rivals any non-Texas program in the country. If Florida's pitching staff can match that offensive firepower, the Gators have everything they need to reach Oklahoma City and compete once they get there.

Tennessee: Two First-Team All-SEC Returners and Unfinished Business

Tennessee returns two first-team All-SEC players in Alannah Leach and Sage Mardjetko. The Volunteers were one of the most athletically dynamic programs in the country in 2026 — a team that could beat opponents with speed, with power, and with pitching on different nights depending on the matchup.

The difference between Tennessee reaching the WCWS level in 2026 and finishing as a national title contender in 2027 comes down to execution. The talent is already there. Leach and Mardjetko are two of the best players in the SEC. The supporting cast that surrounds them has the athleticism to compete at the highest level.

Tennessee's coaching staff hired veteran experience this offseason — including a high-profile addition from the Texas program's previous coaching staff. The investment in coaching continuity signals that the program believes the roster it has is ready to compete, and that the remaining variable is in-game management and player development.

The Common Thread

Florida and Tennessee share the same fundamental challenge: neither program plays Texas before the postseason in a normal season structure, which means the first real test of these rosters against the national champions comes in a tournament environment where single-game elimination amplifies every decision.

Both programs have been there before. Both have coaching staffs with WCWS experience. Both have enough talent returning — and added — to genuinely compete on the biggest stage.

Texas is the favorite. Florida and Tennessee are the programs most likely to make that conversation interesting.

What's Next

Fall practice begins in August, giving Florida and Tennessee their first opportunities to integrate new additions and evaluate whether the offseason construction translates into on-field cohesion. Early preseason polls in January will formalize the national rankings picture. The 2027 season officially begins in February — and for both Florida and Tennessee, the goal is the same: reach Oklahoma City and make Texas prove it is as good as everyone expects.
