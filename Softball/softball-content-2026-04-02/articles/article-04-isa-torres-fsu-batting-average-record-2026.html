Isa Torres Is Batting .615 — The Greatest Offensive Season in Recent College Softball Memory

By Sarah Mitchell April 2, 2026

A Number That Demands Attention

When a player bats .400, she gets mentioned in Player of the Year conversations. When she bats .500, she becomes the story of the season. Florida State's Isa Torres is batting .615. There's no established framework for what that number means because it's the kind of batting average that Division I softball almost never produces.

Torres leads all of Division I softball in batting average. Her .664 on-base percentage is second in the country. Over her last 15 games since returning from injury in mid-March, she has gone 37-for-52 — a .712 clip — with five home runs and 22 RBI. She is hitting in nearly every game, reaching base in nearly every plate appearance, and doing it against ACC pitching that includes some of the best arms in college softball.

The season has produced multiple milestones. From March 8 through March 21, Torres went 16 consecutive at-bats with a hit — setting the NCAA record for consecutive hits. She also reached base in 23 straight plate appearances, the third-longest streak in NCAA history. Both records were set without Torres appearing to be in pursuit of them; she was simply doing what she does.

The Injury That Didn't Break the Streak

Context matters when evaluating this season. Torres missed eight games earlier in the year due to injury. She returned to the lineup in mid-March and immediately picked up where she left off. In the games she's played, there has been almost no period of adjustment, no rustiness, no sign that time away from the lineup affected her approach or her results.

Her hit total for the season — 59 through the most recent games — ranks fourth in the country, despite the missed games. If she had played all of Florida State's games, her hit total would likely be top three or higher. The rate at which she accumulates hits is simply extraordinary.

The per-game production during her streak is the kind of number that analysts use to contextualize great historical seasons. Thirty-seven hits in 52 at-bats over 15 games, with five extra-base hits among the home runs — this isn't just a hot streak. It's a sustained demonstration of the highest level of offensive performance the sport offers.

2025 Was Already Special. 2026 Is Something Else.

Torres's 2025 season would have been the defining performance of most players' careers. She hit .436 — the highest batting average in Florida State program history — and was a unanimous first-team All-American. Every major award she was eligible for, she was in the conversation. She entered 2026 as the prohibitive favorite for national Player of the Year.

What she's produced in 2026 makes 2025 look like the warm-up act. The .615 average, the .664 OBP, the consecutive-hit NCAA record, the 23-consecutive-PA reaching-base streak — these are the kinds of numbers that get referenced when discussing the greatest individual offensive seasons in college softball history. Torres is 59 hits into a season that still has a full conference stretch and postseason run ahead.

Florida State head coach Lonni Alameda is in her 18th season leading the program and has earned 1,000 career wins. She has coached All-Americans, national champions, and generational talent. What she has in Torres right now is something that doesn't come along often.

What's Next

Torres and the Seminoles open an ACC series against Notre Dame tonight at JoAnne Graf Field (6 PM ET, ACC Network Extra). Game 3 on Saturday airs nationally on ACC Network at 2 PM ET. With the ACC title race effectively in hand at 9-0, Torres's next milestone is the Player of the Year award — which will be announced at the end of the season. At the current pace, the case for her winning it unanimously is already well-established. Every game she plays between now and June adds to the record she's building.
