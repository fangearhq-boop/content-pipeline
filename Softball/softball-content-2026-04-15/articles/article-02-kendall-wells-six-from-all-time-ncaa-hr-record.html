Six From History: Kendall Wells and the All-Time NCAA Home Run Record

By Emily Rawlings April 15, 2026

The Record That Has Stood for 31 Years

Lauren Espinoza hit 37 home runs for Arizona in 1995. That was the Clinton administration, two years before Women's College World Series attendance started drawing the kind of numbers people take for granted now. The record has survived 31 seasons, the transfer portal era, the explosion of bat technology, and every power hitter the sport has produced in between.

Kendall Wells needs six more. The Oklahoma freshman sits at 31 home runs heading into tonight's Bedlam game at Devon Park, batting .383 with 60 RBIs, leading the entire nation in home runs. She broke the NCAA freshman single-season record earlier this month when she launched her 31st against Texas. That mark had been shared by Lauren Chamberlain, Jocelyn Alo, and Kelly Majam. Wells didn't stop there. Six home runs separate her from a record most people assumed would last another 31 years.

The Pace Is Unprecedented

Wells reached 31 home runs in approximately 44 games. That pace, sustained across a full season, would project to somewhere above 40. But projections miss the point. What matters is the remaining schedule. Oklahoma has 12-plus regular season games left, followed by the SEC Tournament and, almost certainly, an NCAA Tournament run. Wells has multiple opportunities per game and faces conference pitching that has already adjusted to her presence in the lineup.

The adjustment factor is real. Pitchers do not give Wells anything easy anymore. She is drawing more walks, seeing more breaking balls away, and being pitched around in high-leverage situations. Her .383 average says she is handling it. The fact that she still leads the nation despite that adjustment makes the home run total more impressive, not less.

A Freshman Doing It First

Context matters here. Chamberlain and Alo, two of the prior co-holders of the freshman record, were both multi-year stars who continued to put up big numbers throughout their careers. Wells is doing this in her first year of college softball, against SEC competition, on the nation's No. 1 team. She has not had the benefit of experience or the comfort of knowing what D1 pitching looks like at its best.

Georgia, the state Wells calls home, has produced big hitters before. But nothing quite like this. The Wells story has become the sport's signature narrative in 2026, and with every game, especially tonight's nationally televised Bedlam matchup, the audience watching grows.

What's Next

Wells steps into the batter's box tonight at Devon Park with ESPN2 cameras rolling. Then come three games against No. 6 Arkansas at Love's Field April 17-19. Six home runs in 15-plus games is not a guaranteed outcome, but it is achievable at the pace she has been going. Watch the game. Watch the at-bats. This is what a living record chase looks like.
