Seven From History: Kendall Wells Is Chasing the All-Time NCAA Home Run Record

By Sarah Mitchell | April 11, 2026

The number is 37. It has stood since 1995. And Kendall Wells is seven away.

The Oklahoma freshman enters the Red River series against No. 4 Texas with 30 home runs on the season — a total that has already broken the SEC single-season record and tied the NCAA freshman single-season record. Now she's chasing something bigger: the all-time NCAA single-season home run record of 37, set by Janie Reed Espinoza of Arizona 31 years ago.

Seven home runs sounds like a lot. Consider who's hitting them. Wells is averaging better than a home run per every 1.5 games this season. She reached 30 in just 40 games — faster than any previous record-holder. And she has the rest of April and all of the postseason ahead of her.

The Records She's Already Broken

It's worth stepping back to appreciate what Wells has already accomplished before she chases the ultimate mark.

On March 31, in a win over Wichita State, Wells hit her 27th home run of the season — breaking the SEC single-season home run record previously held by 2025 USA Player of the Year Bri Ellis. She did it in just her 37th game of her college career.

Then on April 4, against Kentucky, she homered for the 30th time — tying the NCAA freshman single-season record shared by Lauren Chamberlain, Janie Reed Espinoza, and Dejah Mulipola. Wells reached that milestone in fewer games than any of the three previous record-holders.

She's batting .383 with more than 60 RBIs. She leads the nation in home runs by a significant margin. She was named both D1Softball Player of the Week and SEC Player of the Week for the week ending April 7. She's a freshman. All of this is happening in her first college season.

The Record That Hasn't Moved Since 1995

Janie Reed Espinoza's 37 home runs in a single season at Arizona in 1995 is one of college softball's most enduring records. It has survived 31 seasons of increasingly powerful lineups, deeper talent pools, and advances in sports science and training. No one has come within striking distance in recent memory.

Wells is not only striking distance — she's seven away in April, with games remaining to get there.

The all-time mark is historic because the landscape of college softball has changed dramatically since 1995. Pitching has evolved. Scouting is more sophisticated. Defenses know who the dangerous hitters are and game-plan accordingly. For a freshman — in her first season of college softball — to be chasing a 31-year-old power record speaks to just how extraordinary Wells' talent is.

The Texas Series as the Stage

Wells could not have scripted a better stage for the record chase than the Texas series. The Longhorns are the defending national champions, hosting a prime-time ESPN audience at McCombs Field in Austin. If Wells goes deep in front of that crowd, the video will be everywhere.

Texas pitching is also among the best in the SEC. If Wells can hit home runs against the Longhorns, it removes all doubt about the record-setting pace.

Oklahoma's coaching staff and Wells herself have handled the attention around the record with professionalism. There's no indication she's pressing or changing her approach. She's 19 years old, playing the game the way she always has, and the results keep coming.

What's Next

Wells and the Sooners play Game 2 of the Texas series Saturday night at 7:00 PM ET on ESPN and Game 3 on Sunday at 1:00 PM ET on ESPN. Every Wells at-bat this weekend is appointment television for anyone following the record chase.

The all-time NCAA single-season home run record is seven away. The biggest series of the year is here. Kendall Wells is ready — and college softball history is watching.
