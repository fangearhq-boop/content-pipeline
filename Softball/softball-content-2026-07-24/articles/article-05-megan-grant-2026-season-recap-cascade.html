Megan Grant's 2026 Season Is Over. Here's What She Did.

By Emily Rawlings | I Love Softball | July 24, 2026

Megan Grant grounded out in the eleventh inning. Ailana Agbayani singled. Portland Cascade lost 6-5. The season was over.

Grant walked off the Davis Diamond field Thursday night with 10 professional home runs, a 17-game on-base streak — the longest active streak in the AUSL all season — and a résumé that most professional softball players spend multiple years building. She spent one season doing it.

What She Built

The numbers start at UCLA. In the spring of 2026, Grant set the NCAA single-season home run record with 42. Not career total — single season. She hit 42 home runs in one college season and broke a record that had stood as one of the most watched benchmarks in the sport.

When she turned professional with the expansion Portland Cascade, she hit a 313-foot home run in her professional debut. That was the longest blast in AUSL history at the time. In subsequent games, she added nine more, finishing with 10 for the season — the second-most in AUSL history behind Maya Brady's new record of 11.

She also never stopped getting on base. That 17-game on-base streak was the longest run by any AUSL player during the 2026 season. It ended Thursday night — not because she was struggling or declining, but because the season itself ended.

The Record She Almost Broke

Maya Brady — Grant's former UCLA teammate — set the AUSL single-season home run record at 11 on July 22, hitting it in the Oklahoma City Spark's elimination loss. Grant entered Thursday night's play-in with 10. She needed two home runs in one game to tie it.

She did not get there. But the juxtaposition is worth dwelling on: two former UCLA teammates, in professional softball's first full postseason, chasing the same record in the same week. Brady set it in a loss. Grant had the chance to break it and her season ended before she could. The record sits at 11. Grant's career sits at 10.

She has years ahead of her to change that number.

What Portland Cascade Meant

Portland Cascade was an expansion team. They were not supposed to be in the postseason. There is no franchise history to lean on, no prior AUSL championship culture, no established core that had been through a postseason before.

They went 14-11 in the regular season, beat both the Bandits and the defending champion Utah Talons, and made it to the first play-in game in AUSL history. They took Chicago to eleven innings in that play-in before walking off the field.

Grant was the engine. Her presence in the lineup forced opposing pitching staffs to make adjustments that opened opportunities for Sierra Sacco-Ferrie and the rest of the Cascade's lineup. When Grant was on base — which was most of the time — Portland scored. The team finished its inaugural season having beaten the two best teams in the league, which is exactly the kind of thing an expansion franchise needs in Year 1 to build credibility.

What Comes Next

Grant is 22 years old. She will be back next year, either with Portland Cascade or with a different roster depending on how the AUSL off-season shakes out. She enters that off-season as one of the two or three best hitters in professional softball.

The AUSL single-season home run record is 11. Grant's career professional home run total is 10.

That will not stay the case for long.
