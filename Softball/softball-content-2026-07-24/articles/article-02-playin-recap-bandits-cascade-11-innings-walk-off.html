Chicago Wins 6-5 in 11 Innings. Kilfoyl Saves It. Agbayani Walks It Off.

By Emily Rawlings | I Love Softball | July 24, 2026

The first play-in game in AUSL history lasted eleven innings and ended just before midnight at Davis Diamond in College Station, Texas. It was the longest game in league history. It ended on a walk-off single.

Chicago Bandits 6, Portland Cascade 5.

The Bandits are going to the Championship.

How It Ended

Bottom of the eleventh. Two outs. Ailana Agbayani stepped into the batter's box and drilled a single down the first-base line. Taryn Kern — who had doubled earlier in the inning — came around to score. Chicago erupted. Portland's season was over.

Both extra-inning runs for Chicago came on unearned runs, which meant Portland's defense had been what kept the Cascade alive as long as it did. When that defense broke down in the eleventh, there was nothing left to hold the Bandits back.

The Pitcher Who Saved the Season

Kat Sandercock started for Chicago and pitched four innings, giving up five hits and three earned runs. Portland's Carley Hoover — who entered the game at 9-3 on the season with a 3.57 ERA — matched her and kept the Bandits from pulling away early.

But the performance of the night belonged to Lexi Kilfoyl.

Kilfoyl entered the game as a reliever — a role she has not been asked to fill during her 6-0 regular season. She came in with the game in the balance, with Portland capable of scoring at any moment, with the Bandits' entire postseason hanging on every pitch. She responded with 4 strikeouts and just 2 hits allowed through the late innings and extra frames. She shut Portland's lineup down when it mattered most.

Kilfoyl's 6-0 record survives. More importantly, so do the Bandits' Championship hopes.

Portland Fought Until the End

Megan Grant entered Thursday night's game with 10 home runs and a 17-game on-base streak. She did not add to the home run total, but she was at the center of everything the Cascade did to stay alive. Portland's offense — which had beaten both the Bandits and the No. 1 seed Utah Talons during the regular season — kept finding ways to manufacture pressure.

In the end, it was not enough. But Portland Cascade — an expansion team in its first AUSL season — pushed the Bandits to eleven innings in a single-elimination game. That is not an asterisk. That is a statement about what this franchise built in its first year.

What This Game Means

Chicago came in as the No. 2 seed and survived the hardest possible first-round draw: a single game, sudden death, against the most dangerous No. 3 seed the AUSL had seen. They won 6-5 in eleven innings by using their ace out of the bullpen and walking it off in the dark.

That is not the way a team wants to enter a Championship Series. But it is also a team that knows how to win ugly, how to trust their best pitcher in any role, and how to survive.

Utah Talons have been watching film. They saw all eleven innings.
