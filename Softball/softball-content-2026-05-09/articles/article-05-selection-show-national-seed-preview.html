Selection Show Sunday: The National Seed Race Is Wide Open — Here's What to Watch

By Emily Rawlings | I Love Softball | May 9, 2026

With Oklahoma eliminated and conference championships decided Saturday, the 2026 NCAA Tournament's national seed picture is anyone's guess. Here's how tomorrow night's Selection Show sets the table for the WCWS.

For months, Oklahoma was the team everyone expected to anchor the No. 1 national seed in the 2026 NCAA Softball Tournament. Then No. 9 seed Georgia happened. The Sooners' stunning 10-5 elimination in the SEC Tournament quarterfinals reshuffled the entire national seed picture, and now — on the eve of the Selection Show — there is no clear frontrunner.

Sunday night at 7 PM ET on ESPN2, the NCAA will reveal the full 64-team bracket, assign 16 national seeds, and set the path to the Women's College World Series. It will be one of the most compelling Selection Shows in recent memory, because almost anyone can make a credible case for the top four spots.

The Frontrunners for National Seeds

Nebraska has done everything right. The Huskers are Big Ten Tournament champions, sporting a 21-game win streak — the longest active in D-I. They've won north of 47 games, beat some of the country's top programs in non-conference play, and own one of the best records in the country. If Nebraska doesn't land a top-4 seed, the committee has some explaining to do.

Alabama or Texas — whichever team wins today's SEC Championship — will leave Lexington as one of college softball's premier credentials. The Crimson Tide (49-6) are coming off a dominant run-rule victory over Florida and have been one of the country's best teams all season. Texas, meanwhile, has shown championship-caliber grit all tournament. SEC champion status carries enormous weight with the selection committee.

Texas Tech has been historically dominant. The Red Raiders were the first team to reach 50 wins this season, threw a perfect game in May, and blasted Kansas 14-0 in the Big 12 semis Friday. A Big 12 championship today would be the final piece of a résumé argument that's already very strong.

Florida State, if it wins the ACC Championship today over Virginia Tech, adds a conference tournament title to a season of sustained excellence. The Seminoles have been a consistent top-10 presence and would be a natural national seed candidate.

What the Selection Show Means

The 16 national seeds have first-round hosting privileges for their regional (May 15-18) and, if they advance, hosting rights for the super regional (May 22-24). Hosting means playing in front of home fans, in familiar conditions, against teams seeded below you. It's a massive competitive advantage.

The top two seeds — the No. 1 and No. 2 national seeds — typically receive the most favorable paths to the WCWS. This year, with so many elite programs fighting for position, the gap between the first and fourth national seed could be smaller than in any recent season.

The WCWS and the Road Ahead

This season's Women's College World Series is scheduled for May 28 through June 3 at Devon Park in Oklahoma City. The same venue that's been hosting some of this week's Big 12 action will become the sport's biggest stage in three weeks.

Regionals run May 15-18. Super Regionals are May 22-24. The bracket reveal tomorrow sets all of that in motion. Sixteen teams will host. Thirty-two others will travel. Every road game becomes harder, every home game more precious.

What's Next

Set your alarm. The Selection Show airs tomorrow — Sunday, May 10 — at 7 PM ET on ESPN2. The 64-team bracket will be revealed live, with national seeds announced and regional hosts identified.

Before then, today's championship games — SEC (Alabama vs. Texas), Big 12 (Texas Tech), and ACC (FSU vs. Virginia Tech) — will shape the committee's final decisions. Every championship win today matters. Every loss matters too.

The road to the 2026 Women's College World Series officially begins tomorrow night.
