Alabama Kept Everyone. Not One Player Entered the Transfer Portal. That Is Extraordinary.

By Emily Rawlings June 29, 2026

The Number Nobody Is Talking About

The transfer portal window opened June 8 and closed June 22. When it was over, the sport had been reshuffled in every direction: Florida added Jaysoni Beachum and Tori Edwards. Oklahoma lost the Zache sisters. Tennessee lost two coordinators and saw players enter the portal. FSU lost Isa Torres and Beachum in the same offseason. Program after program was processing departures while simultaneously adding through the portal.

Alabama processed nothing. Not one player from Patrick Murphy's roster entered the transfer portal. Zero. In a sport where high-profile departures have become expected from every program — no matter how successful — the Crimson Tide's complete retention is an achievement that deserves far more attention than it has received.

Context Makes the Number More Impressive

Alabama did not retain everyone because their season was disappointing and there was nowhere better to go. The Crimson Tide went 56-0 in their win column in 2026. They earned the No. 1 overall national seed in the NCAA Tournament. They made the Women's College World Series semifinals. This is a program that performs at the highest level of the sport, and every player on its roster had legitimate options if they wanted to explore them.

The portal is not a trap for unhappy players. It is an open door that every Division I player can walk through at any time during the designated window. Players leave programs that are winning at high levels all the time — because of playing time, fit, family situations, or simply the pull of something new. Alabama's returnable players, to a person, decided they did not want that door. They chose to stay in Tuscaloosa. That is a statement about the program, and Murphy deserves the credit for creating an environment where elite players with options chose to remain.

What It Means for 2027

Alabama will enter the 2027 season with one of the most experienced rosters in the country. There will be no integration period for a group of new transfers learning each other's tendencies. There will be no early-season awkwardness as a remade lineup tries to find its rhythm. The Tide will return the same core that won 56 games, and they will do it with the continuity that most programs cannot offer.

The seven seniors who graduated were the only departures — players whose eligibility was simply exhausted, not players who chose to leave. That distinction matters. Alabama did not hold on to everyone because the portal bypassed them. They held on to everyone because their players made a deliberate choice, and that choice becomes a recruiting advantage in itself. Prospects watching from the outside see a program where players stay, where culture is genuine, and where winning does not come at the cost of individual satisfaction.

The Broader Picture

College softball has been navigating the portal era with varying degrees of success. Some programs — historically dominant ones — have seen their rosters rebuilt season by season, with familiar names appearing at new schools every June. The continuity that defined the sport for decades has become something of a rarity. What Alabama did this offseason is a reminder that it is not impossible. It requires coaching, culture, communication, and a program identity strong enough that players believe they are better off staying than leaving.

Murphy has been building toward this kind of program stability for years. Zero portal losses in a cycle where the sport saw more movement than ever is not a coincidence. It is a result.

What's Next

Alabama will add to their 2027 roster through traditional recruiting — the high school class and any graduate transfers who might fit specific needs. But the foundation they are building on is already in place. When fall camp opens, Murphy will walk out to a group that has been together, knows the system, and has unfinished business from a WCWS semifinal exit. That combination is genuinely dangerous heading into 2027.
