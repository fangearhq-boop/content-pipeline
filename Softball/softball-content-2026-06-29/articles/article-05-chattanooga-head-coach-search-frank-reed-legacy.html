Chattanooga Softball Has Never Had a Different Head Coach. Now They Need One.

By Emily Rawlings June 29, 2026

What Frank Reed Built

Frank Reed announced his retirement from Chattanooga softball on June 22, ending a 25-year tenure that is one of the most complete single-program coaching careers in Division I history. His record at UTC: 824-581-1. His Southern Conference record: 300-169. He led the Mocs to 10 regular season championships and 11 NCAA tournament appearances. On May 9 of this season, a win over Wofford gave him his 1,300th career victory, counting his decade at Chattanooga State Community College before he arrived at UTC in 2002.

Reed did not build a dynasty in the Power Four sense. Chattanooga is not Oklahoma or Texas. It does not have the same recruiting pull, the same facilities budget, or the same national visibility. What Reed built was something more durable in its own way: a sustained, consistent, competitive program at the mid-major level that showed up in the NCAA tournament year after year and won its conference at a rate most coaches at any level would envy. Ten SoCon regular season titles in 25 years is not an accident. It is the product of a coach who understood his environment and maximized it completely.

The Unprecedented Search

Chattanooga has never done this before. That is not hyperbole — Frank Reed is the only Division I head coach this program has had. Every player who has worn a Mocs uniform in the modern era of the program was recruited by Reed, coached by Reed, and sent into the world by Reed. The institutional knowledge that exists in this program is his knowledge. The culture that has sustained it is his culture.

The search for his replacement is therefore the most consequential hire in program history. Not because the situation is dire — it is not. Reed leaves behind a functioning, tournament-caliber program. But whoever comes next is not inheriting a blank slate. They are inheriting 25 years of identity, and the challenge is to honor what was built while establishing their own voice in a way that keeps the program competitive in the Southern Conference and relevant in the national conversation about mid-major softball.

What the Mocs Need

The profile of Chattanooga's next head coach will generate significant speculation over the coming weeks. A few considerations are worth framing clearly.

The program does not need to reach outside its level to succeed. Reed proved that an excellent mid-major coach with the right fit can build something lasting without a Power Four résumé. An assistant from a Power Four program who has never run their own staff faces a learning curve that smaller programs can ill afford. An experienced mid-major head coach who has proven they can recruit, develop, and win at a similar level — but wants a better-resourced situation — might be the natural target.

Internal candidates deserve serious consideration. Reed's assistants have been inside this program and understand its culture firsthand. If one of them has the full skill set to be a head coach, elevating from within preserves continuity and gives the new coach an immediate credibility with returning players. Programs that have undergone smooth transitions historically have often made internal or near-internal hires after a long-tenured coach steps down.

What's Next

Chattanooga's search is now underway, and the athletic department will move through the summer to have a hire in place before fall workouts begin. The timeline matters — a coach who arrives before August can begin recruiting the 2027 high school class at PGF Nationals and build relationships with the current roster before they are tested in the fall. Watch for the Mocs to conduct a deliberate process rather than a rushed one. Frank Reed served this program for 25 years. Finding the right next voice deserves the time it takes.
