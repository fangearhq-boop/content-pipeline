Hamilton Abandons Simulator Before Canada — The Radical Gamble After Miami Frustrations

By Elena Voss | F1 Fanrecap

May 12, 2026

Lewis Hamilton is one of the most analytically prepared drivers in Formula 1 history. His approach to race weekends — simulator sessions, deep engineering briefings, relentless preparation — helped him win seven World Championships over a career that transformed the sport. It is part of the reason why, at 41, he remains one of the most scrutinized and talked-about figures in F1.

Which makes his announcement this week so striking. Hamilton has confirmed he will not use the simulator at all in the period between the Miami Grand Prix and his arrival in Montreal for the Canadian GP. For a driver who made meticulous preparation his superpower, it is a striking departure — and a telling signal about where Ferrari's season stands.

What Happened in Miami

Miami was supposed to be a statement weekend for Ferrari. They arrived with 11 aero upgrades — the team's first significant package of the 2026 season — and optimism that the SF-26 had turned a corner. Instead, it started badly on lap one and never fully recovered.

Hamilton was caught up in a collision with Cadillac driver Franco Colapinto at the first corner, damaging the floor and right sidepod of his car. Running with compromised aerodynamics for the rest of the race, he managed a sixth-place finish — only aided by a 20-second penalty applied to teammate Charles Leclerc after a final-lap spin at Turn 3.

Leclerc's own race told a similar story. Despite the upgrades and high expectations, Ferrari left Miami with sixth and seventh place finishes. McLaren's Norris took second and Piastri third. Ferrari's constructors' championship lead over McLaren fell from 44 points after the Japanese Grand Prix to just 16 after Miami.

Going Back to Instinct

Hamilton's decision to skip simulator work is an acknowledgment that something needs to change — and that the answer might not come from more data. The simulator can refine a setup, sharpen reaction time for specific circuit features, and validate strategic scenarios. What it cannot do is recapture the feel of a driver who needs to reconnect with the car in a more fundamental way.

For Hamilton, who has spoken openly this season about the challenges of learning the SF-26 and the differences between Ferrari's approach and Mercedes' culture, going into Canada with fresh eyes and heightened physical instincts may be a deliberate attempt to unlock something that data-driven preparation has not yet found.

Montreal as the Reset

Circuit Gilles-Villeneuve is a circuit that rewards feel and bravery as much as raw pace. The bumpy Montreal surface, the heavy braking demands, and the constant threat of the barriers require a driver to be flowing and confident. Hamilton has historically performed well at tracks like this — his record at Montreal is strong, and the circuit's demands align with his strengths.

If abandoning the simulator leads to a freer, more confident Hamilton in Montreal, the gamble will look inspired in hindsight. If it results in a disconnected opening practice session, the narrative will write itself.

What's Next

Hamilton and Ferrari arrive in Montreal on May 22 for practice sessions that will tell the paddock quickly whether the no-simulator approach has produced something different. Ferrari needs a result at the Canadian GP — not just for Hamilton's campaign, but to halt McLaren's momentum in the constructors' standings. The sprint format adds urgency to every session from the opening lap.

Sometimes the boldest preparation is no preparation at all. Lewis Hamilton is betting his Canadian Grand Prix weekend on it.
