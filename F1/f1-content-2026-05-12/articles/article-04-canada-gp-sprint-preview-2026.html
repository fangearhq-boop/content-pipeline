Canada GP Preview: Sprint Debut, Upgrades and a Chance to Finally Challenge Antonelli

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap

May 12, 2026

Formula 1 returns in ten days. The Canadian Grand Prix — May 22-24 at Circuit Gilles-Villeneuve in Montreal — is the next stop on what has been a remarkable 2026 season. And for the first time this year, it will be a sprint format weekend, which means more racing, more strategy, and more opportunities for the championship picture to shift.

Kimi Antonelli arrives in Canada having won every race of the season. Nobody has come close to him on a Sunday. The question now is whether a new circuit, new upgrade packages from the chasing teams, and the added pressure of a sprint race can combine to produce something different.

What Sprint Means for the Championship

The sprint race adds up to eight points to the winner, with smaller points parcels for the next several finishers. In championship terms, it means a maximum of around 34 total points available across the weekend — significantly more than a standard grand prix weekend.

For the drivers chasing Antonelli, that extra opportunity is significant. George Russell trails by 20 points. Charles Leclerc is 41 behind. Lando Norris and Lewis Hamilton are both 49 adrift. A perfect sprint-and-race performance from a rival, combined with a rough weekend from Antonelli, could meaningfully compress the standings heading into the summer stretch.

The Upgrade Battle

McLaren is bringing a significant upgrade package to Montreal, having identified areas of development after a strong Miami showing that saw Norris finish second. Mercedes is targeting a major aero package of their own. Ferrari, still recovering from a disappointing Miami race despite running 11 upgrades, faces pressure to halt their slide down the constructors' standings — their lead over McLaren has shrunk from 44 points after Japan to just 16 after Miami.

Canada's circuit characteristics offer a different kind of challenge than the smooth Miami tarmac. Circuit Gilles-Villeneuve is famous for its hard braking zones, traction demands out of slow corners, and the unforgiving concrete walls that line the track. Low-drag setups typically perform well there, which could suit McLaren's recent direction of development.

The Circuit's History

Circuit Gilles-Villeneuve has produced some of Formula 1's most dramatic races. Safety car deployments, wall-licking overtakes, and sudden weather changes are all part of its character. It is a place where results that seemed inevitable during practice can unravel in the race itself.

The 70-lap race around the 4.361-kilometer circuit demands precision under pressure. Every driver who arrives in Montreal knows the walls are only inches away.

What's Next

The Canadian Grand Prix weekend begins Friday, May 22 with practice sessions and sprint qualifying. The sprint race runs Saturday before full qualifying, and the main race takes place Sunday, May 24. It is the fifth round of the 2026 season.

After months of Antonelli perfection, Montreal might be the weekend that finally gives us a different result — or it might cement his dominance even further. Either way, F1's return to one of its most beloved circuits cannot come soon enough.
