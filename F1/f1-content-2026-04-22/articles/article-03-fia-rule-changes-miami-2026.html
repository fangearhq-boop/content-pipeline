FIA Agrees 2026 Rule Changes for Miami: Super Clipping Boost, Energy Limits, and a New Start System

By Elena Voss | F1 Fanrecap | April 22, 2026

Formula 1's 2026 regulations have already been modified once — and the season is four races old.

Following an online meeting on April 21 between the FIA, all team principals, power unit manufacturers, and Formula One Management, all stakeholders agreed to a package of regulation refinements to be implemented from the Miami Grand Prix onward. The agreement was unanimous, a significant signal that the changes address concerns shared across the paddock rather than representing a controversial ruling from the sport's governing body.

The Key Changes

The most headline-grabbing adjustment is the increase in super clipping from 250 kilowatts to the maximum 350 kilowatts, effective from Miami. Super clipping is the process by which the MGU-K (motor generator unit-kinetic) is allowed to deploy electrical power to assist the internal combustion engine. Raising the ceiling allows drivers and engineers to deploy significantly more power during key sections of the lap, altering overtaking zones, race strategy, and how different power unit architectures compare against one another.

Simultaneously, the maximum permitted energy recharge per lap has been reduced from 8MJ to 7MJ. The FIA's stated rationale is explicit: excessive harvesting under the previous limit was creating phases of driving that felt artificially restrained and inconsistent. The reduction is intended to keep cars at fuller power output for more of the lap, improving the racing product and making the driving experience more predictable for the drivers themselves.

MGU-K deployment parameters also shift slightly, limited to 250kW outside of key acceleration zones where full deployment of 350kW is still permitted. The net effect is a more nuanced power profile across each lap, requiring car and strategy teams to recalibrate their approaches from the Miami round onward.

The Start Detection System

Perhaps the most novel element of the Miami changes is the introduction of a "low power start detection system" to be trialed across the race weekend. The system is designed to identify cars that show abnormally low acceleration in the moments immediately following clutch release at the start of a race or Sprint — addressing a concern raised publicly by drivers including Max Verstappen.

Verstappen and others noted that the 2026 cars' energy deployment systems create moments of unpredictable power fluctuation at starts, making the opening seconds of a race particularly chaotic and difficult to manage. The detection system aims to flag these anomalies in real time, giving the FIA and teams better data and, over time, a mechanism to prevent dangerous or unfair power loss events at starts.

The FIA has noted that the start changes are still in the "trial" phase at Miami, with full adoption pending feedback and analysis from the race weekend.

What's Next

The regulatory changes go live at Miami International Autodrome across the Sprint weekend of May 1-3. Every team's engineers will be recalibrating their car setups, energy strategies, and race simulations in the days before Sprint Qualifying to account for the new parameters.

The critical question is whether the changes disproportionately benefit or disadvantage any particular power unit architecture. Mercedes has been dominant through the first three races — but the sport's self-regulation in real time is a reminder that 2026 is still a season in progress, and no technical advantage is guaranteed to persist for long.
