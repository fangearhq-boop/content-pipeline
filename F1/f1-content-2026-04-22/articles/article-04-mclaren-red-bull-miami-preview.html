McLaren's Miami Charge and Red Bull's Technical Rebuild: The Midfield Story of 2026

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap | April 22, 2026

While Mercedes has dominated the headlines through the opening three rounds of the 2026 Formula 1 season, the story of who will challenge them — and how quickly — may be the more compelling thread heading into Miami.

Two teams in very different circumstances are both hoping the Florida race represents a turning point: McLaren, currently the closest viable challenger to Mercedes, and Red Bull, whose 2026 campaign has been genuinely alarming given the heights they reached in the previous regulations.

McLaren: Upgrades Incoming

McLaren's 2026 season began with a string of misfortunes that masked the true potential of their MCL40. Oscar Piastri crashed on his way to the starting grid in Melbourne, failing to start the race entirely. Both cars then retired in China with electrical issues, leaving the team with no constructors' points from two of the three opening rounds. Piastri's second-place finish in Japan — behind Antonelli and ahead of Leclerc — offered a more accurate window into what the MCL40 can do.

Team principal Andrea Stella confirmed McLaren used the five-week April break productively, with extensive simulator sessions and engineering meetings focused on better understanding the MCL40's behavior under the new 2026 regulations. The first significant upgrade package is planned to arrive at Miami and Montreal in a phased aerodynamic rollout, with Stella expecting it to push the car toward "more important positions" once the season resumes.

McLaren currently sits third in the Constructors' Championship with 56 points — 79 behind Mercedes, but well clear of the teams behind them. A successful Miami upgrade could make them the closest challenger to the Silver Arrows for the balance of the season.

Red Bull: An Organization Under Pressure

The scale of Red Bull's difficulties in 2026 is hard to overstate. After four consecutive Constructors' Championships under the previous regulatory framework, the team arrived in the new era and found themselves sixth after three races with just 16 points — a staggering 119 points behind Mercedes.

The team's response has been structural. Red Bull announced this week that Ben Waterhouse has been promoted to the newly defined role of chief performance and design engineer, with cross-functional responsibility spanning both design and vehicle performance areas. The aim is to break down the siloing between these departments that has contributed to the RB22's lack of competitiveness. A technical hire from sister team Racing Bulls is also reinforcing the engineering department.

Whether these organizational changes can translate into on-track improvement quickly enough to save their 2026 season is an open question. Miami will be the first real test of any tangible progress.

What's Next

Miami is a Sprint weekend, meaning there are points to be earned on Saturday before the main event on Sunday. For McLaren, it's an opportunity to demonstrate their upgrade works and begin converting raw pace into consistent championship points. For Red Bull, it's simply about finding the baseline competitiveness that has been missing all season.

Both teams will need to factor in the FIA's newly agreed rule changes simultaneously — the super clipping increase, the recharge limit reduction, and the new start detection system all debut in Miami. If McLaren's engineers have modeled the changes effectively through the April break, they may arrive in Florida with a genuine edge in understanding the new parameters.
