Ferrari's Miami Upgrade Gamble: New Floor, Macarena Wing, and a Championship Reset

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap | April 22, 2026

Formula 1's five-week break before Miami has given Ferrari time to do something far more significant than rest. The Scuderia has been developing, refining, and privately testing the most comprehensive upgrade package of their 2026 campaign — a technical evolution they believe could fundamentally alter their competitive position against Mercedes.

The package, evaluated by Lewis Hamilton and Charles Leclerc during a private filming day at Monza before the break, centers on two headline pieces. The first is a new floor originally developed for the cancelled Sakhir Grand Prix — an update designed to increase overall downforce and improve the airflow management beneath the SF-26 through high-speed corners. The second is the return of Ferrari's so-called Macarena rear wing.

The Macarena Wing

Named by team principal Frédéric Vasseur for the way its upper flap rotates completely upside down during certain aerodynamic phases, the Macarena wing generated significant attention when it briefly appeared during the China Grand Prix weekend. Stability issues when braking while turning led to its removal mid-weekend, but Ferrari's engineers have spent the intervening weeks on a revised specification they believe addresses those concerns.

The potential efficiency gains are meaningful. Combining the new floor's downforce increase with the Macarena wing's aerodynamic flexibility could give Hamilton and Leclerc a significantly different machine in Miami than the one they raced in Japan.

A "Reset" Moment

Vasseur has been clear in his internal messaging: Miami is the reset point. His expectation is that all teams will arrive in Florida with significantly upgraded machinery, disrupting the current order that has seen Mercedes win every race. Ferrari is confident their package is the most impactful of any team's Miami upgrades, and Vasseur believes it will close — and potentially eliminate — their current deficit to the Silver Arrows.

The numbers give context. Ferrari sits 45 points behind Mercedes in the Constructors' Championship (90 to 135). Leclerc sits 23 points behind championship leader Antonelli in the Drivers' standings. Neither gap is insurmountable with 17 rounds remaining, but both require an SF-26 that can match or beat the Mercedes W17 on raw pace — something that hasn't happened yet in 2026.

Hamilton's presence adds a specific story thread. This is his first season at Ferrari, and while his third-place finish in China was encouraging — a maiden podium with the Scuderia — he has yet to challenge at the front of the field. A successful Miami upgrade would give both Ferrari drivers their clearest opportunity to demonstrate what this partnership can achieve.

What's Next

The verdict on Ferrari's upgrade package will come across Sprint Qualifying, the Sprint race, and ultimately the Grand Prix itself at Miami International Autodrome (May 1-3). Given that Miami also coincides with the debut of the FIA's revised 2026 regulations, the data gathered across five sessions in three days will be invaluable for both Ferrari's development curve and their championship aspirations.

If Vasseur's "reset" narrative proves accurate and Ferrari emerges from Miami as genuine pace rivals to Mercedes, the 2026 title fight becomes a completely different story. If the package underdelivers, the gap will become increasingly difficult to close as the season progresses.
