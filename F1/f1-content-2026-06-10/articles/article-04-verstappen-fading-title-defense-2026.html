Max Verstappen's 2026 Season: From Four-Time Champion to Seventh in the Standings

By Elena Voss | June 10, 2026 | F1 Fanrecap

When 2026 began, Max Verstappen was the benchmark. Four consecutive world championships. The most dominant driver of his era. The man every other team built their strategy around defeating.

Six races into the new season, he is seventh in the drivers' championship with 43 points. He has not won a race. He has not started from pole position. And in Monaco, his race ended before it began — a power unit failure stalling his car off the line and sending him into a retirement that added nothing to his points total.

This is not the Verstappen anyone expected at the start of the year.

What Happened

The roots of Verstappen's difficult 2026 campaign go back to the regulation change that reshaped Formula 1 this year. The new technical rules — which Verstappen himself was reportedly so uncertain about that he negotiated a performance-based exit clause into his Red Bull contract — have not favored the Red Bull chassis.

Where Mercedes has thrived in the new formula, Red Bull has struggled to find the downforce and balance that made their car so dominant in previous years. The team that gave Verstappen a car capable of winning 17 races in a single season cannot currently match what Kimi Antonelli has at his disposal every race weekend.

The result is a season where Verstappen's extraordinary talent is being suppressed by machinery that cannot deliver what he needs. It is the same dynamic that frustrated him in his early years at Red Bull — and that he eventually escaped by winning. Escaping it again will require Red Bull to find development pace that has so far eluded them.

Monaco and the Numbers

The Monaco Grand Prix summed up the season in a few seconds. A power unit failure stalled Verstappen's Red Bull as the race started, dropping him to the back of the field before he eventually parked. Zero points. A retirement that cost him a chance to at least limit the damage to a troubling championship deficit.

After six rounds: 43 points. Seventh in the standings. No wins, one podium, no pole positions. Verstappen trails championship leader Antonelli by 113 points — a margin that, barring a catastrophic collapse from Mercedes, is effectively insurmountable.

The championship is not realistically in play for Verstappen in 2026. The real battle now is restoring Red Bull's competitive position before the summer, and seeing whether the Austrian Grand Prix — the team's home race on June 26-28 — can serve as a turning point.

What Comes Next

Barcelona this weekend is another circuit where Red Bull needs answers. The Circuit de Barcelona-Catalunya is the sport's primary technical reference point — a track that shows, cleanly and without distortion, where teams stand relative to each other. If Red Bull cannot find pace there, the message from the data will be clear: the gap to Mercedes is structural and will not close quickly.

The Austrian Grand Prix looms as the more emotionally charged test. Red Bull Racing is an Austrian team. The Red Bull Ring is their home circuit. If there is going to be a breakthrough moment in their 2026 season, Spielberg is the most natural place for it.

What's Next

Free practice for the Spanish Grand Prix begins Friday, June 12 at Circuit de Barcelona-Catalunya. The race runs Sunday, June 14 at 9:00 AM ET on ESPN.

For Verstappen, Barcelona is another opportunity to show that the car, despite its limitations, can deliver at least a podium result in the right conditions. That won't change the championship picture. But it would be evidence that Red Bull's 2026 season has something worth building on — and that the most decorated active driver in the sport still belongs among the leaders on race day.
