Hamilton Eyes First Ferrari Win at Monaco: "Power Is Not King Here"

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap | June 2, 2026

Lewis Hamilton has been waiting for a moment like this since the day he signed for Ferrari.

The Canadian Grand Prix gave him the closest thing to it yet. Starting from fifth on the grid, Hamilton worked his way through the field on merit, hunted down Max Verstappen in a wheel-to-wheel battle the paddock stopped to watch, and crossed the line in second place behind his Mercedes teammate Kimi Antonelli. After the race, he called it "the happiest day at Ferrari so far." His mother was in the grandstands. The joy was unfiltered.

Now comes Monaco — and Hamilton believes this is where his first Ferrari win will happen.

"Power Is Not King There"

The quote that defined Hamilton's Monaco outlook this week was direct: "Power is not king there. I think our car could be really strong."

He is correct on the technical analysis. The SF-26 suffers from a power unit deficit to Mercedes at circuits where high-speed straights and active aerodynamics come into play. That is why Ferrari has not yet won a race in 2026, despite carrying what many paddock observers believe is the stronger chassis in slow and medium speed conditions.

At Monaco, that power deficit evaporates. The FIA's ban on active aerodynamics removes the primary mechanical advantage Mercedes has exploited all season. And the circuit itself — where no straight exceeds the 3-second activation threshold — is built entirely around the slow-corner, mechanical-grip performance that the SF-26 excels at.

A Career Chapter Waiting to Close

Hamilton has seven World Championships and 104 Grand Prix victories. He has won everywhere. But a Monaco win for Ferrari would be something different — the first chapter written in red in the book of one of the sport's greatest careers.

He has identified Monaco as Ferrari's golden opportunity, and the paddock agrees with his assessment. The FIA's active aero ban is described across the F1 media as a significant event that could reorder the season. If there was ever a circuit where Hamilton's Ferrari debut could shift from "promising but incomplete" to "winner," it is this one.

What's Next

Hamilton faces one complication: teammate Charles Leclerc. Leclerc is Monaco's defending champion, has three Monaco pole positions to his name, and is the home-race driver with the deepest knowledge of these streets. Both Ferrari drivers will be fast here. Whether they race each other to a clean finish, or whether Monaco's unpredictability takes a hand, is the most compelling question of the weekend. Friday practice will tell us where the pace is, and Saturday qualifying at 15:00 local will set the stage.
