Red Bull's Miami Gamble: Can the RB22 Relaunch Save Verstappen's Season?

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap

April 12, 2026

The team that won four consecutive Constructors' World Championships is sitting sixth with 16 points after three races. Max Verstappen, a four-time world champion, is ninth in the Drivers' standings with 12 points — behind drivers from Williams, Alpine, and Haas in the early-season count. The RB22's performance deficit has been quantified at approximately one second per lap versus the field-leading Mercedes W17. The breakdown is stark: 0.8 seconds from the chassis, 0.2 seconds from the Ford power unit making its Formula 1 debut.

Red Bull knows this. The drivers have not been diplomatic. Isack Hadjar, Verstappen's teammate, described the car as "terrible." The engineers have identified wind tunnel aging — a degradation in the accuracy of their aero simulation tools — as a primary contributor to the correlation errors that produced an RB22 that doesn't match its predicted performance. Suzuka only deepened the crisis: an upgrade package introduced there made things worse before Red Bull reverted to an earlier specification.

The Miami Plan

The five-week break has become Red Bull's most urgent engineering period since the first year of the hybrid era. The team is developing what is described as a comprehensive upgrade package for the Miami Grand Prix: a full revision of the sidepod configuration and a reworking of the aerodynamic load distribution across the floor and rear of the car. This is not an iterative update — it is a fundamental rethink of the RB22's concept.

Whether it will close the one-second gap in a single stroke is unlikely. Closing 0.8 seconds on a rival's chassis deficit in five weeks would be extraordinary by any standard. More realistically, Red Bull aims to make Verstappen and Hadjar competitive in the midfield battles and capable of points finishes — something that has eluded them in Japan, where Verstappen qualified ninth and finished out of the points regularly.

The Stakes Behind the Update

Verstappen's contract with Red Bull Racing contains an exit clause exercisable between August and October 2026. The condition attached to that clause relates to the team's standing in the championship — reportedly requiring Red Bull to be within a certain range of the top positions. If Miami does not deliver improvement, the possibility of Verstappen invoking that clause before the clause window opens becomes a live storyline rather than a background one.

The human dimension adds weight to what is already a high-stakes engineering challenge. Red Bull's future as a competitive entity in the grid's current form depends on retaining Verstappen. Verstappen's future at Red Bull depends on the RB22 becoming a competitive car. Both of those things hinge on what arrives at Miami.

What's Next

Red Bull will bring its upgrade to Miami for the May 1-3 Grand Prix weekend. Verstappen will also be racing at the 24H Nurburgring Qualifiers on April 18-19 — a different arena where the RB22 is not the variable. The team expects the Miami package to produce a measurable performance step. Whether "measurable" means closer to Mercedes or closer to the podium is the question Formula 1 fans will be watching to answer in 19 days.
