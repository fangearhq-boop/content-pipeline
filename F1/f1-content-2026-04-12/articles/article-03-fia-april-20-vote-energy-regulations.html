The April 20 Vote: What F1's Biggest Regulation Meeting Could Change

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap

April 12, 2026

In eight days, the FIA will hold the most consequential regulatory meeting in the young 2026 Formula 1 season. The April 20 binding Commission vote could change how the cars are built, how they are driven, and how teams approach the Miami Grand Prix just 11 days later. Understanding what is being proposed — and why — matters for anyone following the sport.

The FIA confirmed "constructive dialogue" following a series of meetings in early April, and outlined a clear decision timeline: a Sporting Regulations meeting on April 15, a Technical session on April 16, and the binding Commission vote on April 20. The changes under consideration are not minor tweaks — they address the fundamental energy management architecture of the 2026 power unit formula.

What Is Being Proposed

Two technical proposals are at the center of the April 20 agenda.

The first addresses the "super clip" — the maximum rate of ERS deployment per lap. Under current 2026 regulations, the limit is 250kW. The proposal would raise that ceiling to 350kW. In practical terms, this means drivers could use the hybrid motor's power more aggressively through certain corners and deployment zones, making overtaking more decisive and reducing the sensitivity of energy management strategies that have made some races unpredictable.

The second proposal reduces the energy recovery cap from 9MJ to 6MJ per lap. Energy regeneration has been one of the most controversial aspects of the 2026 rules. The higher recovery rate creates sudden, abrupt torque fluctuations — particularly under high-speed braking — that have contributed to car instability. Reducing recovery makes the cars more predictable and potentially safer.

The Safety Catalyst

The urgency behind these discussions was sharpened by Esteban Ocon's crash at the Japanese Grand Prix, which subjected the driver to an estimated 50G impact. The investigation pointed to energy management behavior as a contributing factor to the loss of control at the speeds involved. The FIA's safety mandate drives these proposals as much as any performance consideration.

The ADUO — Additional Development and Upgrade Opportunities — framework is also being refined in parallel. This mechanism allows power unit manufacturers whose ICE Performance Index falls more than 2-4% below the best-performing unit to access additional development tokens. It is designed to ensure no team is locked into a structural disadvantage in the opening years of the new formula.

What's Next

If the April 20 vote passes, the changes would require a subsequent electronic vote among FIA World Motor Sport Council members before formal ratification. The target window for implementation is the Miami Grand Prix on May 1-3 — giving teams less than two weeks to adapt their development and setup philosophy. Teams are currently developing upgrades under the assumption that current rules apply, while simultaneously modeling for the proposed changes. The April 20 vote resolves that uncertainty — one way or the other.
