Five Weeks to Miami: Every F1 Team Is Racing the Clock

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap

April 12, 2026

There are no races this weekend. There were no races last weekend. There will be no races next weekend either. Formula 1 is experiencing its most unusual interlude in recent memory — a five-week gap created by the cancellation of the Bahrain and Saudi Arabian Grands Prix, which were removed from the calendar following the escalation of conflict in Iran.

The teams are not on holiday. If anything, the enforced break has created the most intense factory period of the 2026 season, with every constructor pouring resources into development ahead of the Miami Grand Prix on May 1-3. What emerges from that development window could determine whether the first three races represent a sustainable advantage for Mercedes — or a temporary one.

The Teams and Their Missions

Mercedes enters the break in a commanding position. With 135 points in the Constructors' Championship — 45 more than second-placed Ferrari — they are not so much in a development race as they are working to make their cars bulletproof for the rest of the season. Kimi Antonelli leads the Drivers' Championship; George Russell is nine points behind him. The mission at Brackley is not reinvention but refinement.

Ferrari's situation is more urgent. The team has scheduled a filming day at Monza on April 22, with both Lewis Hamilton and Charles Leclerc taking seat time in the SF-26. The objective is clear: get the Miami aero package onto the car and learn from it before arriving in Florida. A revised floor, cooling management components, and the much-discussed "Macarena" rear wing configuration are all under evaluation. Ferrari is third in Constructors but 45 points behind Mercedes — a gap that demands improvement, not just consolidation.

Red Bull is in the most precarious position. The RB22 has a quantified deficit of approximately one second per lap compared to Mercedes — 0.8 seconds from the chassis and 0.2 seconds from the Ford power unit. The team is developing an aggressive upgrade package for Miami, with a focus on the sidepod configuration and overall aerodynamic platform. For a team that won four consecutive Constructors' Championships, sitting sixth with 16 points after three races is not simply a setback — it is a crisis.

The Regulatory Variable

Layered on top of the development race is an unprecedented regulatory wrinkle. The FIA is holding a binding vote on April 20 — just 11 days before Miami — on proposed changes to the 2026 energy management rules. If passed, the super clip (maximum ERS deployment per lap) would increase from 250kW to 350kW, and the energy recovery cap would fall from 9MJ to 6MJ per lap. These are not cosmetic adjustments: they could meaningfully alter how teams configure their cars for both qualifying and race trim.

Teams are essentially developing upgrade packages in parallel with regulatory uncertainty. A floor designed for current rules may need recalibration if the April 20 vote reshapes the energy landscape. It adds complexity to what is already the busiest five-week period in recent F1 history.

What's Next

The FIA holds its binding Commission vote on April 20. Ferrari runs at Monza on April 22. Verstappen races in the 24H Nurburgring Qualifiers on April 18-19. The Miami Grand Prix begins on May 1. By the time the field assembles in Florida, the 2026 F1 season will have been partially rewritten during a period when no races were run. That kind of context is what makes the Miami opener feel like the real start of the championship.
