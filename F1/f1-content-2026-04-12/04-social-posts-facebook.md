### STORY 3: FIA April 20 Vote

#### Long-Form Post — 12:00 PM ET

Eight days from now, the FIA will vote on changes that could fundamentally reshape the 2026 Formula 1 season.

Here is what is on the table for the April 20 binding commission vote:

ENERGY RULE CHANGES:
• The "super clip" — the maximum ERS deployment rate — would increase from 250kW to 350kW per lap. This is designed to allow drivers to use the electric motor more aggressively through corners and in certain attack phases.
• The "recovery" cap would decrease from 9MJ to 6MJ, reducing the energy each car can regenerate per lap. This addresses the current issue where regeneration is causing sudden torque shifts that are making the cars unpredictable and dangerous at high speed.

WHAT TRIGGERED IT:
Esteban Ocon's approximately 50G crash during the Japanese Grand Prix was a key catalyst. The energy management rules in their current form were contributing to handling difficulties at extreme speeds.

ADUO:
The Additional Development and Upgrade Opportunities framework — a safety net for power unit manufacturers whose ICE performance index falls more than 2-4% below the best-performing unit — is also being refined.

TIMELINE:
• April 15: Sporting regulations meeting
• April 16: Technical session
• April 20: Binding FIA Commission vote
• After April 20: e-vote, then WMSC ratification
• Target: Changes active for Miami GP May 1-3

The FIA called the discussions "constructive" and confirmed "commitment to making tweaks." A yes vote in eight days could deliver changed rules to Miami's paddock in less than three weeks.

### STORY 4: Ferrari Monza Test

#### Long-Form Post — 12:30 PM ET

On April 22 — ten days from now — Lewis Hamilton and Charles Leclerc will take turns behind the wheel of the SF-26 at Monza.

It is a filming day (limited to 200km), but Ferrari's ambitions for it are anything but promotional. This is the team's most important pre-Miami preparation session, and what they learn in those laps could shape whether Ferrari can challenge Mercedes in Florida.

The primary upgrade being evaluated: a revised aerodynamic floor that was originally intended for the Bahrain Grand Prix (before it was cancelled). Ferrari's engineers believe it adds meaningful downforce, and Monza — with its high-speed demands and cooling challenges — provides useful correlation data.

Also under evaluation: cooling management components (crucial for Miami's Florida heat) and the "Macarena" rear wing configuration, which the team has been working to finalize. A version of the Macarena wing appeared in China practice but was removed when compliance issues with the material regulations were discovered. Ferrari is working against the clock to have a race-legal version ready for Miami.

The deadline is real. The Constructors gap to Mercedes stands at 45 points through three races. Ferrari cannot afford to arrive in Miami without their development path firing on all cylinders.

### STORY 5: Red Bull Miami Upgrade

#### Long-Form Post — 2:00 PM ET

The numbers are brutal. After three races, Red Bull Racing sits sixth in the Constructors' Championship with just 16 points. Max Verstappen is ninth in the Drivers' standings.

The performance deficit has been quantified internally at approximately one second per lap versus Mercedes. The breakdown: 0.8 seconds comes from the chassis — the RB22's aerodynamic and mechanical package — while 0.2 seconds is attributed to the Ford power unit, making its Formula 1 debut in 2026.

Red Bull drivers have not been subtle about the car's problems. Isack Hadjar called the RB22 "terrible" after testing. Verstappen himself has described it as difficult to drive at the limit. Technical analysis points to the wind tunnel aging as a contributing factor to the aerodynamic miscorrelation between development environments and real-world performance.

What Red Bull is planning for Miami: an aggressive, comprehensive upgrade to the RB22. The sidepod configuration is being completely revised, and aero loads across the floor and rear are being re-engineered. This is not a small update — it is a relaunch.

The stakes extend beyond Miami. Verstappen holds an exit clause in his contract, exercisable between August and October 2026, that is contingent on the team's championship standing. For Red Bull to retain their greatest driver, Miami cannot be another disappointment.

### STORY 6: Verstappen 24H Qualifiers

#### Long-Form Post — 5:00 PM ET

Formula 1 might not be going well for Max Verstappen in 2026. But the Nurburgring? That's different.

After winning NLS2 at the Nordschleife in March with the #3 Winward Racing Mercedes-AMG GT3, Verstappen was disqualified — the team had used seven tyre sets when only six were permitted. The win went to the #99 Rowe Racing BMW of Dan Harper and Jordan Pepper. A painful outcome for a race he appeared to win by nearly a minute.

Then came the NLS3 on April 11. Verstappen's team was committed to the GT World Challenge Europe round at Paul Ricard. He didn't race at the Nurburgring. Another week without the Nordschleife closure he wanted.

Next up: the 24H Nurburgring Qualifiers on April 18-19. This is not just another warm-up race — it is the primary qualifying event for the full 24-hour main race, typically held in May. Verstappen has spoken publicly about how much the Green Hell means to him: he described competing there as something that "brings a big smile" to his face, a contrast to the frustrations of the current F1 season.

Whether he wins in F1 or not, Verstappen has found another home at the Nordschleife. April 18 is his next chance to do something about the NLS2 disqualification.
