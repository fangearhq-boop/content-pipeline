Cadillac's Austria Upgrade: Can America's F1 Team Finally Score Their First Points?

By Elena Voss June 25, 2026

Seven Races, Zero Points

Cadillac F1 arrived on the 2026 Formula 1 grid as the sport's 11th constructor and its first new American team in decades. Seven races into their debut season, they have not scored a single championship point. Sergio Perez finished 16th at the Miami Grand Prix. Valtteri Bottas collected a drive-through penalty at the same event. Across every round of 2026, the combination of experience and pedigree — more than 500 combined career race starts, 16 grand prix victories between Perez and Bottas — has not translated into results.

The gap between prior career performance and debut-season output is not unusual for new constructors. What Cadillac have done is identify precisely why it is happening and prepare a technical response. That response arrives in Austria this weekend.

The Tire Cliff Problem

Cadillac's technical team identified a consistent pattern across their race stints: a dramatic pace loss that begins at approximately lap 15 of any given stint. The team described it as a tire degradation cliff — a point at which the aerodynamic wake characteristics of their car cause the rear tires to overheat, lose grip, and fall off the performance curve faster than any strategy can compensate for.

In practice, this means Cadillac is competitive for the first portion of each stint and then drops sharply out of the points-paying positions before their drivers can string together enough laps to hold position. The problem is not the drivers — it is the aerodynamic package generating excess heat in the underbody airflow that reaches the tires.

The Austria Package

For the Austrian GP, Cadillac are bringing the most significant upgrade package they have introduced since the start of the 2026 season. It targets the aerodynamic sources of the tire overheating — changes designed to reduce the underbody wake characteristics that create the lap 15 cliff. Whether those modifications are enough to keep both cars within points contention past the halfway mark of their stints will determine whether Austria becomes the race where Cadillac's record changes.

The Red Bull Ring circuit — a high-downforce, medium-speed layout with long, flowing sections and a significant elevation change — places particular demands on mechanical grip in the later stages of a stint. It is not the easiest circuit at which to debut a new aerodynamic solution, but it is the race where the team has chosen to introduce it.

Context for a Debut Season

Haas F1, the last new team to join Formula 1 before Cadillac, scored points in their very first race in 2016 — a result that surprised the entire paddock and set a benchmark that few new entries have approached since. Cadillac have not matched it. But the historical difficulty of debut seasons, and the specific technical challenge of building a competitive underbody package from a standing start, provides some framing for a zero-point record through seven rounds.

Their first championship point, whenever it arrives, will carry significance beyond the raw standings number — for the team's 300-plus employees, for Cadillac's parent General Motors, and for F1's expanding American presence.

What's Next

Friday's practice sessions will give Cadillac the first live data on how their upgraded package behaves at race pace and across stint length. The critical number to watch is not their lap time in FP1 — it is how their long-run pace compares to their short-run pace, and whether the lap 15 degradation cliff has been flattened. If the data looks promising, they will arrive at Sunday's race with a genuine case for their first points. If the cliff persists despite the new parts, the wait continues beyond Austria.
