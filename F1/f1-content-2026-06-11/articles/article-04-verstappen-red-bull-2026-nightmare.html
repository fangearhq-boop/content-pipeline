Zero Wins in Eight Rounds: Inside Verstappen's 2026 Nightmare at Red Bull

By Marcus Cole | June 11, 2026 | F1 Fanrecap

In 2023, Max Verstappen won 19 races in a single season. He won the championship by a margin so large that the title was decided before the Belgian Grand Prix in late July. He was, by the evidence of results, the most dominant driver in Formula 1 history at that moment — the possessor of a skill set that seemed independent of the car beneath him.

Through eight rounds of the 2026 Formula 1 World Championship, Verstappen has zero wins. He sits seventh in the standings with 43 points. His best finish all season is a single third-place podium in Canada.

The Regulations Reset Everything

The 2026 season began under a completely new technical ruleset — one that Verstappen himself had reportedly been apprehensive about for years. Reports revealed that his Red Bull contract included an exit clause specifically linked to his concerns about the new regulations, allowing him to leave if he was not in the top two of the championship at a defined point in the season. (Red Bull's Helmut Marko later confirmed the clause had been renegotiated.)

Those concerns turned out to be well-founded — though not in the way anyone anticipated. The issue is not Verstappen's driving. By most paddock assessments, he is extracting everything the Red Bull RB22 has to offer. The problem is that the RB22 is not a competitive car under the new regulations. Red Bull's design philosophy — which produced the most efficient cars of the 2022-2025 era — did not translate cleanly into the 2026 framework.

Monaco and the Depths of the Season

If there is a single moment that encapsulates the gap between where Verstappen was and where he is, it is Monaco. He retired on lap 1 after the car stalled on the formation lap — a power unit issue that cut his race before it started. Around him, Kimi Antonelli was winning for the fifth consecutive time. The contrast was complete.

Verstappen did not have the car to win in Monaco. He probably does not have the car to win in Barcelona either. His 43 points — while not zero — represent a gulf from championship relevance that is difficult to bridge without a fundamental improvement in the RB22's underlying pace.

Red Bull's Path Forward

Formula 1 teams do not stay in the same place. Red Bull built the dominant machinery of 2022 through 2025; they know how to develop fast cars. The question for 2026 is whether the team's engineering group can identify the technical direction that closes the gap to Mercedes within this season, or whether the gains come in 2027 with a new development cycle.

For Verstappen, the waiting is the hardest part. He is 28 years old. He has four world championships. He is still, by most assessments, one of the two or three best drivers in the world. He is simply driving a car that cannot win races right now.

What's Next

Verstappen will be on track this weekend at Circuit de Barcelona-Catalunya, where the technical demands of the circuit tend to amplify the gaps between the leading and trailing teams. A strong result here — points, perhaps a podium — would be encouraging. A win would be genuinely surprising.

The 2026 season is only at its midpoint. There is time for Red Bull to find answers. But time, in Formula 1, has a way of running out faster than the standings suggest.
