Verstappen's Monaco Redemption: Two-Time Winner, 2026's Ninth-Place Driver

By Ryan Calloway | May 28, 2026 | F1 Fanrecap

The context is almost absurd. Max Verstappen — four-time Formula 1 world champion, the driver who spent several seasons making the championship feel foregone — currently sits ninth in the 2026 drivers' standings. He is more than 60 points behind Kimi Antonelli, who was not yet a teenager when Verstappen was winning his first title.

And yet: Verstappen has won the Monaco Grand Prix twice. In 2021 and 2023, he turned the streets of Monte Carlo into his private proving ground. Monaco rewards a particular kind of driving — precise, committed, brilliant at the barrier-scraping limits — and Verstappen has delivered at that circuit consistently enough that no preview of Monaco 2026 is complete without his name at the center of the conversation.

The 2026 Problem

Verstappen's poor 2026 championship position is a function of the Red Bull RB22's struggles, not his driving. The car has been burdened by weight — approximately 10 kilograms over the minimum requirement — which translates to roughly three to four tenths per lap of lost pace. In a field where Mercedes has been dominant and Ferrari has been competitive, that deficit has pushed Verstappen into regular midfield finishes at circuits where he was previously untouchable.

His best result of 2026 came in Canada, where he finished third — 11.2 seconds behind Antonelli. The podium was a meaningful result for Red Bull and represented genuine progress, but it also illustrated the scale of the gap between the top two cars and the machinery Verstappen is working with.

Red Bull has acknowledged the weight issue and has been pursuing solutions throughout the season. The RBPT-Ford power unit — the product of Red Bull's own engine program in partnership with Ford — is widely regarded as one of the top two engines in the field alongside Mercedes. If the car's structural problems can be addressed, the raw speed is there to compete.

Why Monaco is Different

Monaco does not reward the same performance variables as a high-speed circuit. At Monza or Spa, raw straight-line pace and aerodynamic efficiency dominate the lap time. At Monaco, the race comes down to precision through narrow gaps, smooth tyre management across an extended stint, and the courage to commit to racing lines that would be dangerous on any other circuit.

Verstappen's two Monaco victories showed what he can produce when the circuit context removes some of the car performance advantages his rivals might normally exploit. In 2021, he led from start to finish. In 2023, he managed the tyres masterfully to hold position against late-race pressure. His understanding of the circuit's rhythms — the feel through Portier, the reference points in the Casino section, the exit at Rascasse — is as deep as any active driver.

The RB22's weight issue is less critical in Monaco's low-speed environment than it would be elsewhere. The gap to the front runners may narrow naturally simply because the circuit plays to different car strengths. Combined with a power unit that is genuinely competitive, Monaco represents Verstappen's best opportunity to challenge for a race win in 2026.

The Wild Card Factor

Beyond the technical analysis, Verstappen at Monaco carries an intangible weight that pure data cannot fully capture. He has won there. He knows what a winning Monaco weekend looks like from the inside. That experience — knowing the correct tyre conservation points, knowing when to push and when to manage, knowing how to respond when the safety car comes out in the closing laps — is not something that shows up in any metric but shapes his decision-making throughout the race weekend.

Red Bull's Christian Horner has spoken previously about "rolling the dice" with Monaco strategy. The team is willing to be bold in Monte Carlo in a way they might not be at a conventional circuit. That boldness, applied to a car with a strong power unit and a driver who has proven he can win there, makes Red Bull worth watching carefully in the Monaco GP classification every year.

What's Next

The Monaco Grand Prix weekend runs June 5-7. Verstappen's qualifying lap on Saturday — in the unforgiving millimetre-precision environment of Monte Carlo — will be one of the most revealing moments of his 2026 season. A front-row start would give him real race options. Even from row two or three, his racecraft through the opening lap could position him for a challenge.

In a season that has been humbling by Verstappen's standards, Monaco offers the possibility of a reminder. The circuit has favored him before. It could favor him again.
