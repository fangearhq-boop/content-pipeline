The Two-Stop Rule is Gone: Monaco Returns to Free Strategy in 2026

By Elena Voss | May 28, 2026 | F1 Fanrecap

The FIA tried an experiment at Monaco in 2025. It did not work. For 2026, the governing body has reversed course — and the decision to scrap the mandatory two-stop rule is one of the most consequential regulatory changes for this year's Monaco Grand Prix.

Understanding why the rule was introduced, why it failed, and what free strategy means for June 7 requires a brief look at what made Monaco racing feel broken in recent years — and how the FIA's attempted fix made things considerably worse before making them better.

Why the Two-Stop Rule Existed

Monaco's fundamental racing problem is structural: the circuit is too narrow for overtaking. Once the field settles into its running order after the opening lap, position changes become almost exclusively dependent on pit stop strategy and safety car periods. The processional character of Monaco races has drawn fan criticism for years, with laps of uncontested running providing little of the wheel-to-wheel drama that makes Formula 1 compelling at other venues.

The FIA's response for 2025 was to mandate the use of three different tyre compounds, forcing at least two pit stops per car. The theory: more stops create more opportunities for overtaking in the pit lane and through the strategic windows they open. More varied tyre strategies would create different on-track speeds at different points in the race, enabling drivers to close gaps and make passes.

How Teams Broke the Rule

The reality of the 2025 Monaco GP was a textbook demonstration of unintended consequences. Teams quickly identified that the mandatory stop structure created a vulnerability they could exploit: by controlling the pace of a train of cars at a strategic moment, one team could create a pit window that advantaged their own leading car.

Williams and Racing Bulls were among the teams that deployed a two-car strategy — one car driving slowly to bunch the field and create a free pit stop window for the car running ahead. Drivers lapped as much as four seconds per lap below the circuit's potential during these phases. The effect on the race was the opposite of exciting: it introduced deliberate, managed slowness into what was supposed to be a sporting competition.

Fans were not impressed. Media criticism was universal. The FIA's own single-seater director, Nikolas Tombazis, acknowledged the regulation had not achieved its intended purpose.

The Reversal and What It Means

The World Motor Sport Council ratified the deletion of all Monaco-specific tyre usage clauses from the 2026 Formula 1 sporting regulations. The mandatory two-stop requirement is gone. The 2026 Monaco Grand Prix operates under the same standard tyre rules as every other race: use at least two different compounds, make at least one pit stop, manage the race however you choose.

Free strategy restores genuine decision-making to the teams. An early pit stop from an unexpected position — betting on track position before others pit — becomes viable again. An overcut from a team that reads tyre degradation data better than the competition can swing the race order by several places. A late safety car that reshuffles a field of cars on different aged tyres creates genuine jeopardy rather than manufactured variation.

None of this guarantees Monaco 2026 will be a dramatically different race spectacle than its recent predecessors. The circuit's fundamental character does not change because the tyre rules changed. But removing the artificial manipulation that defined 2025 and returning strategic authority to the teams is unambiguously the right call.

What's Next

The Monaco Grand Prix runs June 5-7. Under free strategy, the pre-race tactical planning will be more complex and more interesting than it was in 2025. Teams will analyze tyre degradation data from the practice sessions carefully, knowing that their strategy call in qualifying may define their race approach as much as their grid position does.

For drivers who benefit from strategic unpredictability — Verstappen, who won Monaco with masterful tyre management in 2023, comes immediately to mind — free strategy is a genuine asset. For teams that prefer to control the race from the front through pace, it is simply the return to normal.

The worst rule Monaco has seen in recent memory is gone. The chess match, for better or worse, is back.
