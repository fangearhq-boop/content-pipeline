### STORY 2: Antonelli's Race Start Problem Could Cost Him at Monaco

#### Long-Form Post

Kimi Antonelli is the dominant force in Formula 1 this season — four consecutive wins, 43 points clear in the championship, and a driving style that has made him the talk of the paddock. But former F1 driver Jolyon Palmer has identified one weakness that Monaco could ruthlessly expose.

Antonelli's race starts.

In Miami, Antonelli started from pole position but was beaten into Turn 1 by Charles Leclerc, who converted the lead into a race win. In Canada, his battle with Russell off the line led to what Toto Wolff described as a fight that was "a bit close." The pattern is there: Antonelli struggles to translate pole position into a clean Turn 1 lead.

At Monaco, that matters more than anywhere else. The Circuit de Monaco has one of the shortest distances from the start line to the first braking point of any circuit on the calendar. There is almost no room to recover from a bad start. If Antonelli sits on pole and hesitates even slightly, the driver behind him will be in the lead before the field has even reached the first corner.

Palmer's assessment was direct: if Antonelli cannot improve his launch, even a pole position at Monaco will not guarantee him a race win.

With 43 points of breathing room in the championship, a Monaco loss would not be catastrophic. But in a sport of habits, this particular one is worth watching very closely.

### STORY 5: The Two-Stop Rule Is Dead — Monaco Strategy Is Free Again

#### Long-Form Post

One of the most disliked rules in recent Formula 1 history is gone.

For Monaco 2025, the FIA introduced a mandatory two-stop requirement — drivers had to use three different tyre sets, forcing a minimum of two pit stops. The idea was to unlock strategic variety at a circuit notorious for its processional racing once the field settles into its running order.

It backfired spectacularly.

Teams that ran two cars in close proximity — like Racing Bulls and Williams — deployed one car to deliberately slow the pack, creating an artificial pit window to benefit the car running ahead. Drivers were lapping up to four seconds per lap slower than the circuit was capable of, creating a stilted spectacle that pleased no one. The FIA's own single-seater director, Nikolas Tombazis, acknowledged the regulation had not delivered its intended outcome.

The response for 2026: the FIA's World Motor Sport Council ratified the removal of all Monaco-specific tyre usage clauses from the sporting regulations. Monaco goes back to the same rules as every other circuit — use at least two different tyre compounds, make at least one pit stop, manage the rest yourself.

Free strategy means different teams will make different calls. An undercut could be worth everything. An overcut could be brilliant or disastrous. The gap between a smart call and a costly one could decide the race winner.

The 2025 Monaco GP was a cautionary tale in unintended consequences. The 2026 edition should be a return to the strategic tension that makes Formula 1 at Monaco genuinely compelling.
