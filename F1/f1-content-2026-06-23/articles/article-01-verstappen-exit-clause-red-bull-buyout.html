Verstappen Exit Clause: Red Bull Eyes Eight-Figure Buyout as Shareholders Split Over Dutchman's Future

By Ryan Calloway | June 23, 2026

The Max Verstappen situation at Red Bull Racing has reached a decisive moment. With the summer break — and its contractual deadline — looming after the Hungarian Grand Prix on July 26, Red Bull is reportedly exploring an eight-figure buyout to prevent Verstappen from triggering his performance exit clause. The plan is complicated by a divided shareholder group, a Dutchman who cannot mathematically reach the top two in the standings, and the shadow of Mercedes waiting in the wings.

The Clause That Changes Everything

Verstappen's Red Bull contract contains an exit clause that activates if he is not in the top two of the drivers' championship by the summer break. He currently sits seventh, with 60 points separating him from Lewis Hamilton in second place. The mathematics are unambiguous: even a victory at this weekend's Austrian Grand Prix cannot move him into the top two by July 26.

The clause does not require immediate action — it can be triggered as late as October — but the summer-break assessment is the first concrete evaluation point. Red Bull's management knows that if Verstappen leaves the summer break in seventh place, the situation becomes significantly harder to manage.

The Buyout — and Why It's Complicated

Reports from Germany and multiple motorsport outlets suggest Red Bull is prepared to offer Verstappen a payment in the "low double millions" to waive the exit clause. That would effectively compensate him for giving up the right to leave early — a right that his manager Raymond Vermeulen confirmed exists and is legitimate.

Vermeulen's public stance is direct: "He should have the chance to win. If the team cannot provide that, he needs options." The message is clear — Verstappen is not threatening to leave out of ego, but out of a genuine assessment that the 2026 RB22 has not given him competitive machinery.

The obstacle is internal. Red Bull's shareholder structure means the decision is not unilateral. Thai stakeholder Chalerm Yoovidhya supports paying to remove the clause. Co-owners Mark Mateschitz and Oliver Mintzlaff are reportedly opposed. The disagreement has slowed negotiations and created uncertainty at a moment when the team can least afford it.

Mercedes Waits

If Verstappen triggers his exit clause, Mercedes is considered the frontrunner destination. The Silver Arrows have produced the fastest car on the 2026 grid — and championship leader Andrea Kimi Antonelli is proof of how competitive their package is. For a four-time champion accustomed to winning machinery, the prospect of pairing with the best car on the grid is a powerful argument for walking away from Red Bull.

An F1 insider told GPFans the clause is now "impossible to block" if Verstappen chooses to trigger it. The buyout is Red Bull's only real avenue to retain their most decorated driver through the remainder of the season and beyond.

What's Next

The Austrian Grand Prix this weekend — at the Red Bull Ring, effectively Verstappen's home circuit — adds an emotional dimension to an already tense situation. Red Bull arrives with their second major upgrade of the season, designed to eliminate the final six kilograms of excess weight from the RB22. Team principal Laurent Mekies has been honest: the upgrade alone will not win races. But a strong home result — even a podium — would buy goodwill and demonstrate progress. The shareholder discussions are expected to continue through the Austrian race weekend. A resolution, in either direction, is likely before the summer break.
