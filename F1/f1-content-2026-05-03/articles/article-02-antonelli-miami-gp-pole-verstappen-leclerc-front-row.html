Antonelli Takes Miami GP Pole as Verstappen Lines Up Second and Leclerc Third

By Marcus Cole | May 3, 2026 | F1 Fanrecap

Kimi Antonelli could have been forgiven for letting Saturday evening's sprint penalty linger in his mind when he climbed back into his Mercedes for main qualifying. Instead, the 19-year-old went out and reminded everyone exactly why he leads the Drivers' Championship.

Antonelli claimed pole position for the Miami Grand Prix with a lap time of 1m27.798s in a qualifying session where the entire top five was covered by less than four-tenths of a second. Max Verstappen qualified second, Charles Leclerc third, Lando Norris fourth, and George Russell fifth — a front-five grid that features virtually every major championship storyline of the 2026 season compressed into one volatile starting order.

Antonelli Bounces Back

There was something almost defiant about the pole lap. Antonelli had been dropped to sixth in the sprint result after a penalty, and with McLaren showing frightening pace all weekend, there were genuine questions about whether Mercedes had the raw speed to stay ahead. Saturday's qualifying answered those questions clearly: not just yes, but by enough of a margin to claim the fastest time outright.

His 1m27.798s was 0.166 seconds clear of Verstappen and 0.345 seconds ahead of Leclerc — a gap that is not enormous in modern Formula 1 terms but is meaningful on a tight street-style circuit where starting position translates directly into strategic advantage. Antonelli will have clean air at the front, free to manage his race and respond to whatever the midfield throws up.

Verstappen's Best Grid Slot of 2026

Max Verstappen's second-place qualifying result deserves attention because it represents the Dutch driver's strongest grid position in what has been an extremely difficult 2026 campaign for Red Bull Racing. Verstappen, currently ninth in the championship with just 12 points, has been vocal about his frustrations with the RB22 and the new technical regulations — but on Saturday in Miami, the car found something.

Starting from the front row gives Verstappen the best possible chance to convert lap time into race positions and, finally, into championship points that reflect his actual pace. The question is whether the race pace matches the qualifying pace, and whether he can hold off Leclerc's Ferrari at the first corner when the lights go out.

Leclerc and Norris Threaten from Row Two

Charles Leclerc in third and Lando Norris in fourth give Ferrari and McLaren excellent starting positions to challenge for the podium and potentially the race win. Leclerc has been consistent throughout the Miami weekend, taking a sprint podium in third and now qualifying directly behind the two championship combatants at the front.

Norris, carrying momentum from his sprint win and his team's upgraded machinery, starts from the second row in a position that could translate into a race lead if the Safety Car appears in the early stages. George Russell in fifth gives Mercedes a second car well-positioned to score significant points for the constructors.

What's Next

Today's race starts at 1 PM ET — moved three hours earlier to avoid the afternoon thunderstorm threat that has been the backdrop to the entire Miami weekend. Fifty-seven laps around the Miami International Autodrome await, and with championship implications riding on every position, what unfolds from the front row could define the direction of the 2026 title fight.
