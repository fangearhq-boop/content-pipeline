Leclerc's Miami Disaster: A Final-Lap Crash, a 20-Second Penalty, and a Ferrari Crisis

By Elena Voss | F1 Fanrecap | May 20, 2026

There have been bad final laps in Formula 1. And then there was Charles Leclerc's final lap at the 2026 Miami Grand Prix.

Leclerc was in position for a podium finish — a result that would have helped stabilize Ferrari's championship position — when he spun into the wall at Turn 3 on the final lap. The impact damaged his Ferrari SF-26. He continued, fighting through the corners, and then lost further positions heading to the flag. He crossed the line in sixth place.

Then the stewards met. Leclerc received a 20-second post-race time penalty for repeatedly leaving the track limits during the race — a penalty that had been noted but deferred while the race was running. When it was applied, Leclerc dropped from sixth to eighth, behind Lewis Hamilton and Franco Colapinto.

His response afterward was simple: "It was all on me."

What Happened and Why It Matters

The crash itself was a moment of driver error under pressure. Leclerc pushed too hard through Turn 3 on a lap when he was already aware his race was not going to deliver the result Ferrari needed. Whether that pressure contributed to the mistake or whether it was a simple misjudgment is a question Leclerc will have examined at length before arriving in Montreal.

The 20-second penalty compounded the damage. Track limits violations are an ongoing issue in F1, and Leclerc's case in Miami involved repeated infractions that, in total, warranted the stewards' intervention. The penalty was not controversial in its application — but its timing, falling after an already terrible final lap, amplified the damage to Ferrari's constructors' standing and Leclerc's personal championship position.

He sits third in the Drivers' Championship with 59 points — 41 behind Antonelli. In fourth, Lewis Hamilton has 51 points. Behind him, Lando Norris also has 51. The gap between a strong and weak result at Canada is enormous for Leclerc and Ferrari.

Ferrari's Upgrade Response

Ferrari arrived at Miami with 11 new aero elements on the SF-26, one of the largest single-race upgrade packages the team has introduced in recent memory. Despite the upgrade, the race result underlined that raw aero performance is not the only variable at play. Driver execution and operational precision — pit timing, track limits management, tire strategy — remain areas where Ferrari has been hurt in 2026.

The team is expected to bring further upgrades to Canada. What form those upgrades take, and whether they provide a meaningful lap-time benefit around a circuit with a very different character to Miami, will be central to Ferrari's weekend strategy.

What's Next

Leclerc has historically been strong at street circuits — Monaco, Baku, and Singapore have been venues where his precision and commitment have delivered some of his best results. Circuit Gilles Villeneuve shares characteristics with those tracks — tight corners, walls close to the racing surface, and a circuit that rewards commitment and punishes mistakes. Canada is a genuine opportunity for Leclerc to reset the narrative after Miami's disaster. The championship still has 18 rounds remaining. But Ferrari and Leclerc cannot afford to continue dropping points at this rate.
