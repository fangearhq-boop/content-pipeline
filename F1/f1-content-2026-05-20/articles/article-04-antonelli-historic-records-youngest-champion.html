Antonelli at 19: The Record Books Say This Has Never Happened Before

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap | May 20, 2026

Kimi Antonelli is 19 years old. He is in his first season of Formula 1. He drives for Mercedes. And he is currently doing things that no driver in the history of the sport has ever done before.

After four rounds of the 2026 Formula 1 World Championship — including the most technologically complex regulatory overhaul the sport has seen in decades — Antonelli leads the Drivers' Championship with 100 points. He is the youngest driver in Formula 1 history to hold that position.

The Records

The full list of records Antonelli holds at this stage of his career is extraordinary in its scope. He is the first driver in Formula 1 history to convert each of his first three pole positions into race victories — three consecutive weekends where he started from the front and led from the first lap to the last. No driver in the sport's 75-year history had previously achieved that sequence.

He is also the youngest driver to lead a Formula 1 race lap, and the youngest to set a Grand Prix fastest lap. These are not minor historical footnotes — they are markers of a driver who arrived in the sport's highest category and immediately performed at its highest level.

In his four starts, Antonelli has accumulated three wins, three pole positions, five fastest laps, and seven podium finishes. The sprint weekend format means that the race count includes Sprint Races, but the pole-to-win record applies specifically to the three full Grands Prix in which he has competed.

Context and Comparison

The names that inevitably arise when Antonelli's records are discussed are the sport's greatest: Ayrton Senna, who arrived in Formula 1 in 1984 and immediately showed the combination of qualifying brilliance and race instinct that defines a generational talent. Max Verstappen, who became the youngest race winner in F1 history at 18 when he won the 2016 Spanish Grand Prix on debut at Red Bull.

Antonelli's situation has an added dimension of complexity. He replaced Lewis Hamilton — the seven-time world champion — at Mercedes. The weight of expectation that came with that role would have been immense for any driver. For a 19-year-old making his Formula 1 debut, it was a test of psychological resilience as much as driving ability. He has passed it.

The 2026 regulation changes — a major overhaul of the hybrid power unit system and the introduction of active aerodynamics — theoretically leveled the playing field and gave every team a chance to lead the competitive order. Mercedes, with Antonelli, has emerged dominant. How much of that is car, and how much is driver, will be debated throughout the season. The records suggest the driver is carrying more than his share.

What's Next

Canada is Antonelli's next test. Circuit Gilles Villeneuve is a circuit he has never competed at in Formula 1, and the Sprint format means there is no room to use the Friday sessions to build up gradually. He will need to deliver immediately in Sprint Qualifying on Friday evening.

If Antonelli wins the Canadian Grand Prix, he will become the first driver in F1 history to win each of his first four Grands Prix. That record, if it falls, will place his season among the most extraordinary in the sport's history — at any age, let alone at 19.
