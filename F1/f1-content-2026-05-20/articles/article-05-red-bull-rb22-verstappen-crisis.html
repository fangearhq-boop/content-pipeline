From Dominant to Desperate: How Red Bull's RB22 Became Verstappen's Biggest Problem

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap | May 20, 2026

Four years ago, Red Bull Racing was the most dominant team in Formula 1. Max Verstappen won four consecutive World Drivers' Championships from 2021 to 2024. Red Bull's RB19 was described by rivals as the greatest Formula 1 car ever built. The team appeared untouchable.

In 2026, Max Verstappen sits seventh in the Drivers' Championship with 26 points — 74 behind leader Kimi Antonelli. Red Bull is sixth in the constructors' standings. And Verstappen has described his current car as "incredibly tough to drive — every lap is a fight."

The fall has been swift, severe, and, according to people inside the team, potentially long-lasting.

What Went Wrong with the RB22

The problems with the RB22 were apparent from the moment the 2026 technical regulations were introduced. The overhaul was the most extensive in Formula 1 history — revised hybrid power units with a significantly higher proportion of electrical energy, new active aerodynamic systems, and different tire compounds all changed the fundamental design requirements of a competitive car.

Red Bull's response — the RB22 — has structural balance issues that engineers have described as systemic rather than surface-level. The car harvests energy far less efficiently than the Mercedes in high-speed corners, where the 2026 regulations place the highest power demands. It also suffers from tire degradation that forces Verstappen to manage his pace well below the limits of what he would prefer to push to.

Red Bull admitted to "significant shortcomings" in the car's design as early as the Chinese Grand Prix — an unusually candid acknowledgment from a team that typically prefers to keep internal assessments private. Reports have cited the team's development infrastructure — specifically its wind tunnel, which has not been upgraded at the rate of its rivals — as a contributing factor to the gap in aerodynamic understanding that the 2026 regulations have exposed.

Verstappen's Exit Clause

Verstappen's contract with Red Bull contains an exit clause — a provision that allows him to leave the team if certain conditions are met. The clause is tied to his championship position at the Hungarian Grand Prix, scheduled for late July. If Verstappen is not within the top two of the Drivers' Championship at that point, the clause can be activated.

Currently, he is 74 points behind Antonelli and 54 points behind Russell in second. The Hungarian Grand Prix is twelve rounds away, and twelve rounds of points are enough to mathematically close significant gaps — but only if the RB22 becomes dramatically more competitive. The team has acknowledged that a fundamental fix may require a new chassis that would not arrive until the 2027 season.

If the exit clause is exercised, it would represent one of the most seismic driver moves in the sport's history — a four-time champion departing the team that defined his dominance because they can no longer give him a car capable of winning. Verstappen has not publicly commented on the exit clause status, but the gap in the championship makes the timeline significant.

What's Next

Canada offers Red Bull a partial test of their competitive position. Circuit Gilles Villeneuve — with its long straights, chicanes, and medium-speed corners — will expose different characteristics of the RB22 than the high-speed sections that have been most punishing for the team in 2026. If the team can show improvement in Montreal, it will provide evidence that the fix is closer than feared. If not, the timeline around Verstappen's future will accelerate toward a decision point.
