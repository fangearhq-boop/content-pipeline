Revealed: The Immediate Changes F1 Can Make to Fix the 2026 Power Unit Crisis

By F1 Fanrecap Staff March 31, 2026

The Problem in Plain English

The 2026 Formula 1 power unit regulations introduced the most significant technical overhaul in over a decade, raising the share of electrical power in the drivetrain to a far higher proportion than the previous hybrid era. The goal was to align Formula 1 with road car electrification trends and position the sport as a technology leader. The unintended consequence — now a safety crisis — is a phenomenon called "super clipping."

"Super clipping" occurs when a car's power unit reduces its engine output in order to harvest energy into the battery. The car's speed drops suddenly, sometimes by a significant margin, without any braking input from the driver. On a racing circuit operating at 300-plus km/h, a following car approaching at full speed has limited time and distance to react. Oliver Bearman's 50G crash at Suzuka's Spoon Curve during the Japanese Grand Prix provided the clearest demonstration yet of what happens when the system fails in a race environment. Franco Colapinto's Alpine entered harvesting mode on the racing line, creating a 200kW power disparity. Bearman hit the slowing car before he could react.

The Options on the Table

Analysis published by RacingNews365.com on Tuesday outlined several interventions that could be implemented relatively quickly — without requiring a fundamental redesign of the power unit architecture. The most straightforward option is a zone-based approach: banning energy harvesting deployment in certain designated sections of each circuit, particularly high-speed sections where sudden deceleration creates the highest risk. This would be implemented through software parameters and could theoretically be ready for Miami.

A second approach involves adjusting the threshold parameters that trigger "super clipping" — essentially limiting how aggressively a car can drop power in a single electronic step. The current regulation allows large and sudden power reductions; a revised threshold would smooth the transition, reducing the speed differential with following cars. A third option — more technically complex — involves developing warning systems that notify following drivers via dashboard display when a car ahead has entered harvesting mode, giving them a fraction more reaction time.

The FIA's Timeline

The FIA has confirmed it will hold high-level meetings in April to review the regulations, with the Miami Grand Prix on May 1-3 as the implicit deadline. The five-week break between Japan and Miami is the longest gap in the 2026 calendar — and it gives the governing body its best window for rapid action. Under the FIA's sporting regulations, technical directives can be issued between races on safety grounds, bypassing the normal rule-change timeline that would otherwise require months of consultation.

The political dimension is equally important. Multiple teams have invested billions in the 2026 power unit architecture and are not keen to see the regulations fundamentally altered this early in the cycle. Any intervention will need to balance genuine safety improvement with competitive fairness — ensuring that changes do not disproportionately benefit or disadvantage any one power unit supplier.

What's Next

The FIA's April meetings will produce one of three outcomes: immediate regulatory changes implemented via technical directive before Miami, a framework for longer-term changes to be introduced at a later race, or — the worst case — no meaningful action, leaving the status quo intact heading into a street circuit in Miami where speed differentials could be even more consequential. The F1 community, from drivers to designers to fans, will be watching closely. The sport's credibility on safety depends on getting this right.
