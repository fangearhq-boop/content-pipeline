Ex-Designer Warns 2026 F1 Energy Rules Risk 'Potentially Fatal' Crash as FIA Schedules April Review

By F1 Fanrecap Staff March 31, 2026

The Warning From Inside the Sport

Three races into the 2026 Formula 1 season, the sport's new power unit regulations have produced their most serious crisis yet. An ex-Formula 1 designer has publicly warned that the 2026 energy harvesting framework risks a "potentially fatal" crash if left unchanged — language that is remarkable for a sport that has spent decades building one of the strongest safety cultures in professional motorsport.

The warning came in a March 31 round-up published by Racefans.net and follows Oliver Bearman's 50G impact at Suzuka's Spoon Curve during the Japanese Grand Prix. Bearman's crash was caused when Franco Colapinto's Alpine entered energy harvesting mode on the racing line, creating a 200kW power disparity that dramatically slowed the car. The resulting closing speed gap — approximately 45 km/h — gave Bearman no time to react. He escaped with bruising, but the severity of the impact has focused the entire paddock on the underlying regulation.

Understanding "Super Clipping"

The technical culprit is a phenomenon engineers are calling "super clipping" — an electronic system built into the 2026 power units that reduces engine power output in order to harvest and store electrical energy in the battery. In the 2026 regulations, the proportion of electrical power in the drivetrain is significantly higher than in previous seasons, reflecting Formula 1's push to remain relevant to road car electrification technology.

In theory, "super clipping" is simply energy management — teams and drivers balancing deployment and recovery across a lap to optimize their electrical reserves. In practice, the speed drops it creates can be sudden, significant, and — critically — unpredictable to following drivers. At 300-plus km/h on a racing circuit, a car losing meaningful power output can appear to brake without warning. Fernando Alonso and multiple other drivers raised concerns about closing speeds during the Japanese Grand Prix weekend. With a prominent designer now using the word "fatal," the conversation has moved well beyond paddock grumbling.

The FIA's Response and What Comes Next

The FIA issued a statement acknowledging the role of "high closing speeds" in the Bearman incident and confirmed that high-level meetings have been scheduled for April to review the 2026 power unit regulations. The question for the sport is whether those meetings can produce actionable rule changes in time for the Miami Grand Prix on May 1-3 — the next race following a five-week break.

Analysis published on Tuesday outlined several immediate interventions under consideration: banning energy harvesting mode in designated circuit zones, adjusting the thresholds that trigger "super clipping," and requiring visual or electronic warning systems that notify following drivers when a car enters harvesting mode. None of these are structural changes to the power unit itself — they are regulatory and software interventions that could theoretically be implemented on short notice if the FIA determines they are necessary for safety.

What's Next

The FIA's April meetings will be the most closely watched regulatory sessions since the 2022 ground effect regulations were debated. Teams, drivers, and designers will be watching for whether the governing body has the speed and decisiveness to address the crisis before Miami. For F1 fans, the immediate ask is simple: trust that the sport which has made remarkable safety strides in recent decades will apply that same commitment to a regulation that — by the admission of those inside the industry — needs urgent attention.
