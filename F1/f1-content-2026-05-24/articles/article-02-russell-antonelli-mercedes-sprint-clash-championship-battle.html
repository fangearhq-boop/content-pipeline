Russell Pushes Antonelli Wide, Wins Sprint, Then Wins the Race: The Mercedes Battle That Defined Canada

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap | May 24, 2026

The 2026 Formula 1 season now has its defining subplot. And it lives entirely inside the Mercedes garage.

On Saturday at Circuit Gilles-Villeneuve, George Russell and Kimi Antonelli raced each other — their own teammate — with the kind of intensity that usually emerges only in the final stages of a championship. Russell pushed Antonelli onto the grass in their battle for Sprint positions. Antonelli went to the radio immediately, demanding a penalty. Toto Wolff's voice came back just as quickly, telling his young driver to end the conversation. Russell finished first. Antonelli settled for third.

Then, on Sunday, Russell went out and won the Grand Prix.

A Rivalry in Plain Sight

The dynamic between these two Mercedes drivers has been building since the season opened. Antonelli — 19 years old, racing in his first full Formula 1 season — arrived with three wins from his first three races, each converted from pole position. He led the championship by 20 points heading into Canada and had the look of a driver who could not be beaten.

Russell, his experienced teammate, has been watching, measuring, and waiting. In Montreal, on one of the sport's most demanding circuits, he executed perfectly at every phase of the weekend: the fastest in qualifying, dominant in the Sprint, and then in control of the race from lights to flag.

When they made contact in the Sprint — Russell's Mercedes briefly touching Antonelli's car as he pushed his teammate onto the grass — the physical reality of their competition was exposed. This is not a cordial arrangement between two drivers content to play No. 1 and No. 2. Both want the championship. Both believe they deserve to lead the team's effort. And Wolff, who has navigated intra-team wars before, is managing the most consequential version of that dynamic he has faced since he oversaw the Hamilton-Rosberg battles of the mid-2010s.

Wolff's Containment Strategy

The team principal's response to Antonelli's radio complaint was swift and unambiguous. Wolff did not engage with the substance of Antonelli's frustration — he simply shut the conversation down. The message delivered in that moment was: whatever happened on track is between you and the race director, not something that gets aired in public. It is exactly the kind of message that maintains team cohesion in the short term while leaving unresolved tension for another day.

That day could come in Monaco, or Spain, or Silverstone. But for now, Russell holds the momentum. He has reduced Antonelli's championship lead significantly through Canada's sprint and race points. Whether that shift in the championship changes the dynamic within the team — and whether Wolff's management of the situation holds — is the question the paddock is watching most closely heading into Monaco.

What's Next

Russell arrives with two wins in two days, championship momentum, and questions swirling about how his relationship with Antonelli will develop.
