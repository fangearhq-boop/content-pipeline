### STORY 2: Verstappen Exit Clause Confirmed

**Long-Form Post (10:00 AM ET)**
This is now official — and it could reshape Formula 1 for the next several years.

For the first time, Max Verstappen's manager publicly confirmed on Thursday that the Red Bull exit clause is officially active. The clause was triggered when it became mathematically impossible for Verstappen to finish in the top two of the championship, a threshold built into his contract to allow him to explore options elsewhere despite being under contract through 2028.

Multiple sources describe McLaren negotiations as being in the "closing stages." Red Bull reportedly attempted to buy out the exit clause from Verstappen's side, but he declined. The clause represents Verstappen's leverage — and he appears willing to use it.

One scenario circulating in the paddock involves a Verstappen-Piastri swap: Max to McLaren, Oscar to Red Bull. Journalist Mat Coch has characterized that specific scenario as "hot air" for now, but the underlying negotiations are real and advanced.

The timing creates an interesting backdrop as Verstappen enters the Belgian GP looking strong — P2 in FP1 and P3 in FP2 — while potentially racing for a different team next season.

### STORY 3: Championship Three-Way Fight

**Long-Form Post (12:00 PM ET)**
The 2026 Formula 1 championship is no longer Kimi Antonelli's to lose. It is genuinely open.

The Mercedes teenager entered the season on a blistering run — five wins, five poles, and a lead that ballooned to 66 points after nine rounds. But the last three races have seen that margin collapse. Mechanical failure at Silverstone, Ferrari's resurgence, and consistent pressure from both teammates in the standings have combined to trim his lead to just 25 points over George Russell, with Lewis Hamilton a further seven behind.

Hamilton was candid at this race week: "The Mercedes duo should be a lot further ahead." He is right. Ferrari has won two of the last three races. McLaren won both practice sessions on Friday. The car that dominated the opening nine rounds suddenly looks like one of three or four genuine contenders rather than the outright class of the field.

Belgium is the last race before the summer break. A swing of 25 points in a single race — entirely plausible at Spa — could change everything about how the second half of the season is framed.

### STORY 4: Aston Martin Upgrades at Spa

**Long-Form Post (2:00 PM ET)**
Aston Martin had a good Friday at Spa-Francorchamps — and it could mean something for the race.

The team brought a new upgrade package to Belgium, and in FP1 both Lance Stroll and Fernando Alonso broke into the top 10, finishing ahead of Racing Bulls' Isack Hadjar. After a difficult mid-season stretch, any positive data point for Aston Martin counts.

Spa suits their car in certain ways — the long Kemmel Straight rewards straight-line speed, and the upgrade package appears to have helped in that department. If the team can carry Friday's pace into qualifying and the race, this weekend could represent a meaningful step forward in the constructors' standings.

Alonso, two-time world champion, never wastes an opportunity at a circuit he knows well. With the upgrade package arriving, keep an eye on the No. 14 car this weekend.

### STORY 5: Belgian GP Qualifying Preview

**Long-Form Post (5:00 PM ET)**
Friday told us one story at Spa. Saturday qualifying might tell us a completely different one.

With a 65 percent chance of rain forecast for qualifying at Spa-Francorchamps, the dry pace McLaren showed in FP1 and FP2 could become irrelevant very quickly. Spa is notorious for unpredictable weather — a thunderstorm that arrives on one part of the circuit might not be visible from the other end.

Lewis Hamilton is the kind of driver who thrives in that environment. He has six wins at Spa, a remarkable record at what is widely considered the sport's greatest circuit. In a rain-affected qualifying session, his ability to read conditions in the moment becomes as important as raw pace. Hamilton needs points, and a wet session could be his opportunity.

Charles Leclerc is another factor. The Ferrari driver took pole position at Spa in 2019, 2023, and 2024 — three of the last five qualifying sessions here. In FP2, Leclerc was fourth. Ferrari has the pace to threaten at the front.

McLaren will want to put both cars on the front row and control the race from the front. Whether the weather cooperates is another matter entirely.
