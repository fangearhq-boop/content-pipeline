Norris and McLaren's Suzuka Comeback: Can the Reigning Champion Rescue His Season?

By F1 Fanrecap Staff | March 28, 2026

Before the 2026 season began, Lando Norris said McLaren had built the best car on the grid. The reigning Formula 1 World Champion was confident, his team was confident, and the paddock whispered that McLaren's winter programme had produced something special.

Two races later, Norris sits sixth in the championship with 15 points. The team has collected 0 points in one of those two races. Oliver Bearman in a Haas has more points than Norris. And Japan — the third round of the season — has become a must-deliver race for a team whose potential has been obscured almost entirely by catastrophic reliability.

The China Disaster

The Chinese Grand Prix should have been the race where McLaren's underlying pace translated into points. Instead, both Norris and Oscar Piastri suffered separate electrical power unit failures before the race had even begun. The double DNS — two drivers from the same team failing to start due to independent mechanical failures — was the kind of catastrophe that typically takes years to recover from psychologically.

McLaren reacted quickly. The team ordered an immediate joint investigation with Mercedes HPP, the power unit supplier they share with the works team. Norris, to his credit, maintained his optimism publicly. "Things in the pipeline," he said ahead of Japan. The paddock watched to see if the words would be backed by results.

Why Suzuka Matters for McLaren's Season Trajectory

Norris trails championship leader Russell by 36 points with 22 races remaining. That gap is recoverable — but only if McLaren starts delivering the results their engineering resources suggest they should be capable of producing. A strong qualifying and race at Suzuka would immediately shift the championship mathematics back toward something manageable.

Suzuka also provides a genuine technical test. The circuit's high-speed flowing sections reward aerodynamic efficiency — precisely the kind of performance characteristic that McLaren claimed their 2026 car delivered in testing. If the pace Norris predicted is real, it should show up at a circuit this demanding.

The psychological dimension is equally important. Norris is a driver who feeds on momentum. Getting a clean, competitive weekend at Japan — regardless of whether it ends in a podium or a top-5 — would restore the team's belief in their car and their own ability to execute.

What's Next

Norris and McLaren face qualifying at Suzuka on March 28 knowing that anything outside the top five feels like a setback given their stated ambitions. The race on Sunday March 29 offers the first real opportunity to reclaim ground on Russell. With the next race not until Miami on May 3, Suzuka's points will be the only ones available for five weeks — making every position gained or lost here carry unusual weight.
