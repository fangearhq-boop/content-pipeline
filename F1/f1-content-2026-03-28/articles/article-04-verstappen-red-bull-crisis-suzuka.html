Verstappen and Red Bull's 2026 Crisis: Suzuka as the First Real Test

By F1 Fanrecap Staff | March 28, 2026

Something remarkable is happening in Formula 1, and Max Verstappen is at the centre of it.

The four-time world champion — who won the drivers' championship in each of the past four seasons in dominant, often uncontested fashion — sits without a 2026 race win, without a podium, and with a retirement on his record after just two rounds. More pointedly, Red Bull Racing is P4 in the constructors' championship. Behind Haas.

This is not a statistical anomaly or a result of circumstances. The 2026 regulations — which introduced sweeping changes to both the power unit architecture and the aerodynamic philosophy — appear to have caught Red Bull at a disadvantage that their prodigious car development capabilities have not yet overcome.

How Bad Is It?

In Australia, Verstappen qualified last on the grid and used his formidable racecraft to recover to P6. It was a reminder of his talent — but also a stark illustration of how far behind his car was at a circuit that should have been relatively straightforward. In China, he was running behind Oliver Bearman's Haas before an ERS coolant failure ended his race on lap 45.

Red Bull's situation is made more complex by the departure of Adrian Newey — the legendary aerodynamicist who joined Aston Martin for 2026 — at a moment when the team most needed their best technical minds to adapt to new rules. The combination of new regulations, a new technical structure, and a power unit that appears not to match the outright performance of Mercedes and Ferrari has created a perfect storm of underperformance.

Why Suzuka Could Be Different

There is cautious optimism in the Red Bull camp heading into Japan. Suzuka's demanding layout — high-speed corners, technical chicanes, a challenging final sector — has historically rewarded cars with precise mechanical grip and strong aerodynamic downforce. These are characteristics that Red Bull cars have excelled at in previous regulation eras.

If any circuit on the 2026 calendar is likely to expose the relative strengths of Verstappen's package compared to the Suzuka-specific weaknesses of rivals, it is this one. A genuine qualifying battle at the front, or a strong race result, would signal that Red Bull's development trajectory is heading in the right direction.

What's Next

Verstappen and Red Bull face qualifying on March 28 knowing that any improvement on their Australia and China performances will be welcomed as a positive signal. The Japanese GP race on Sunday could provide the first genuine indication of whether Red Bull's 2026 season is a temporary adjustment period or a longer structural challenge. With Miami not until May 3, Suzuka's result will define the team's narrative for the entire inter-race development window.
