Verstappen's Double Whammy: Red Bull's Silverstone Problem Goes Deeper Than a Bad Day

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap | July 5, 2026

Max Verstappen is not in the habit of making excuses. When the four-time world champion stands in front of cameras and describes his car's performance at Silverstone as a "double whammy" — and adds the phrase "it's extra painful" — it is worth paying close attention.

Verstappen qualified seventh for Sunday's British Grand Prix, a result made more uncomfortable by the fact that Racing Bulls junior Isack Hadjar outqualified him by two positions in fifth. In the sprint race, Verstappen finished P6, scoring three points. It was not the result Red Bull needed, and the explanation Verstappen provided suggests the concern extends beyond a single track or a single weekend.

The Double Problem

The first layer of Verstappen's "double whammy" is familiar: the RB22 lacks the straight-line speed to compete with Mercedes and Ferrari on Silverstone's long straights. The Hangar Straight, which decides pass attempts in sprints and sets the tone for every lap, exposed that deficit clearly. When Antonelli deployed his Boost to pass Hamilton on Lap 8 of the sprint, Verstappen in sixth had no corresponding tool to improve his position.

The second layer is what makes it compound. Because the car is slower on the straights, it burns more battery energy trying to maintain competitive speeds. That battery depletion then carries through into Silverstone's demanding high-speed corner complexes — Copse, Maggotts, Becketts — where the car needs that stored energy for aerodynamic stability and peak performance. The result is a car that is compromised across the entire lap, not just on the straights.

In Q2 of main event qualifying, Verstappen reported an additional concern: the engine was "not responding normally." Whether that represents a connected issue or a separate mechanical problem, it added another layer of difficulty to an already challenging session.

Hadjar Above Verstappen

The most striking element of the qualifying result was the positioning of Isack Hadjar. The Red Bull junior driver, competing for Racing Bulls, qualified fifth — two positions ahead of Verstappen in seventh. On a circuit where Red Bull has historically been competitive, seeing a junior team car outperform the main team's lead driver highlights the scale of the challenge the RB22 is facing at this type of circuit.

Championship Concerns

Verstappen sits seventh in the Drivers' Championship with 76 points — 103 behind Antonelli. The deficit is substantial. At circuits where the Red Bull's strengths align with the track characteristics, Verstappen remains competitive. But Silverstone has exposed a pattern that will concern the team heading into the remainder of the season.

What's Next

Red Bull will start Sunday's British Grand Prix with Verstappen from P7. The race covers 52 laps of the 5.891-kilometre Silverstone circuit, and the team will look to strategy to compensate for the car's qualifying deficit. Tyre management and pit stop timing could provide opportunities that raw pace cannot. Whether Sunday yields a significant improvement in position will tell the team — and the paddock — whether the "double whammy" at Silverstone is a circuit-specific issue or something more structural that needs addressing before the season's next phase.
