FIA's April 20 Vote Could Change the 2026 F1 Season as Technical Meetings Continue

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap

April 10, 2026

The most consequential week in the 2026 Formula 1 season may not involve a single racing lap. It involves meeting rooms, technical proposals, and a binding vote on rules changes that could reshape the competitive order before the first of the remaining 22 races is run.

The FIA's two-day technical regulation meeting — convened specifically to address issues created by the new 2026 power unit regulations — continues today, April 10, with its second session of technical representatives. Yesterday's Day 1 gathering featured team technical staff in a discussion-only format; no binding decisions were made. Today's session is designed to finalize the agenda for the April 20 team principals meeting, where a formal vote on rule changes is expected. Any changes agreed by team principals must also receive World Motor Sport Council (WMSC) approval before taking effect.

What's on the Table

The proposals center on two specific modifications to the 2026 power unit regulations. First: raising the super-clipping limit from 250kW to 350kW. Second: reducing the energy recovery from 9MJ to 6MJ per lap. Both changes are aimed at addressing what has become one of the defining safety concerns of the new generation of cars.

The problem was made vivid at the Japanese Grand Prix, when Haas driver Oliver Bearman suffered a 50G crash at the Spoon Curve after losing control of his car. Engineers subsequently identified that Bearman's incident was connected to an approximately 50km/h speed differential between the car's full-boost mode and its energy-saving mode. That kind of sudden, unpredictable power drop in high-speed corners represents a meaningful safety hazard — and it has driven the urgency of this week's meetings.

The Competitive Implications

Safety is the stated motivation, but the competitive implications are impossible to ignore. The current regulations have produced a clear hierarchy: Mercedes have optimized most effectively for the 2026 power unit architecture, with Kimi Antonelli and George Russell running first and second in the Drivers' Championship. Teams that have best exploited the current super-clipping window and energy recovery systems have a competitive advantage.

If the rules change — if the super-clipping limit rises to 350kW or energy recovery drops — teams that have not yet maximized under the current parameters may find themselves more competitive. Conversely, teams who have built their entire 2026 car philosophy around the current numbers may lose an advantage they spent an entire winter developing. Red Bull, the team most visibly struggling, could theoretically benefit from regulation changes that shift the playing field.

The Timeline

The FIA's stated goal is to implement any approved changes by the Miami Grand Prix, which runs May 1-3. That gives teams approximately three weeks between the April 20 vote and the Miami race weekend to adapt hardware and software. For power unit specifications, three weeks is an extremely tight window for physical changes; software modifications are faster. The proposals would need to thread the needle between meaningful safety improvement and practical implementation timeline.

What's Next

Today's technical session (Day 2) will set the specific agenda items for the April 20 team principals meeting. The binding vote on April 20 will determine whether and how the 2026 regulations change before Miami. Fans should expect significant coverage of the April 20 meeting as teams react to the proposals — Mercedes, who stand most to lose from regulation changes, and Red Bull, who stand most to gain, will define the battlelines. If changes pass, Miami on May 1-3 becomes the first race under modified rules.
