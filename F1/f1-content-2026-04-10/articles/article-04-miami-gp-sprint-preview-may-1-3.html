Five Weeks Away: What to Expect at the Miami GP Sprint Weekend on May 1-3

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap

April 10, 2026

Formula 1 fans are in the middle of the longest wait of the 2026 season — and what they get at the end of it is a sprint weekend.

Following the cancellations of both the 2026 Bahrain Grand Prix (originally April 10-12) and the Saudi Arabian Grand Prix (April 17-19) due to regional instability, the next race on the calendar is the Miami Grand Prix at the Miami International Autodrome. The date: May 1-3. The format: a sprint weekend, which compresses F1's usual three-day structure into a format where every session has competitive consequences.

What Is a Sprint Weekend?

Sprint weekends have evolved significantly since their introduction, and the 2026 format is as follows. Friday hosts sprint qualifying — a shootout that sets the grid for Saturday's sprint race. The sprint is a shorter grand prix (roughly one-third the distance) run without mandatory pit stops, with points available for the top eight finishers. Sunday is the full Grand Prix — 57 laps around the 5.412km Miami International Autodrome, with race start at 16:00 EDT (21:00 BST).

Sprint weekends put an additional premium on car setup and driver adaptability, since teams have minimal practice time before committing to a competitive configuration. Teams that struggle to dial in their cars quickly — Red Bull has been one such team this season — face added jeopardy.

What the Five-Week Break Changes

For Kimi Antonelli and Mercedes, the break is welcome breathing room. The championship leader enters Miami with two wins and 72 points; the only team activity of note during the break involves refining an already dominant package. Mercedes should arrive in Miami as favorites, with or without regulation changes from the April 20 FIA vote.

For Ferrari, the break is a critical development window. Lewis Hamilton and Charles Leclerc share the upgraded SF-26 at Monza on April 22, testing a revised floor, ADUO power unit software, and a redesigned rear wing. If the package delivers, Ferrari could arrive in Miami as genuine race-winning threats for the first time this season.

For Red Bull, the break represents the team's best opportunity to address the RB22's fundamental aerodynamic shortcomings. The wind tunnel limitations are not easily solved in five weeks, but any incremental improvement would be significant given how far behind Verstappen currently sits.

Key Storylines to Watch in Miami

The FIA's April 20 vote on regulation changes adds an additional variable. If the super-clipping limit rises or energy recovery rules change before Miami, the competitive landscape could shift between now and the race weekend. Teams will be monitoring the situation carefully; the sprint weekend format means there is even less time to adapt to late-breaking rule changes.

Verstappen and Red Bull's Miami performance will be closely watched as the first data point after the regulation crisis. If the car is still fundamentally uncompetitive — still in "the midfield battle" as Verstappen described — his August-October exit clause discussion will grow considerably louder.

What's Next

The 2026 Miami Grand Prix runs May 1-3 at the Miami International Autodrome. Sprint qualifying takes place Friday, May 1; the sprint race on Saturday, May 2; the full Grand Prix on Sunday, May 3 at 16:00 EDT. The April 20 FIA team principals meeting precedes the race and will determine whether any regulation changes take effect in Miami. Coverage begins Friday evening for European audiences and starts afternoon for fans in the Americas.
