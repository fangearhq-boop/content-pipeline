Verstappen Admits Red Bull Are in "The Midfield Battle" as Crisis Deepens After Japan

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap

April 10, 2026

Four world championships. Four consecutive drivers' titles. A team that once dominated Formula 1 so thoroughly that rivals spent hundreds of millions of dollars simply trying to understand how they did it.

And now, Max Verstappen is fighting in "the midfield battle."

Those were his own words after the 2026 Japanese Grand Prix at Suzuka, where Red Bull's season of misery deepened further. Verstappen sits ninth in the Drivers' Championship with 16 points through three races — the worst start for both driver and team since 2015. Red Bull themselves admitted to "significant shortcomings" with the RB22 following the Japanese weekend, a bluntness rarely seen from an organization that spent years carefully managing its public image.

The Wind Tunnel Problem

Multiple technical analyses have identified the same root cause for Red Bull's struggles: their wind tunnel. The facility that generated four consecutive championship-winning cars was built for a different regulatory era. Under the sweeping 2026 regulations — which introduced active aerodynamics, a fundamentally different power unit architecture, and the removal of DRS — the team's ability to develop and optimize the RB22 is severely hampered by infrastructure that cannot replicate how the new cars actually behave at speed.

The numbers are damning. During the Chinese Grand Prix, Verstappen was averaging 1.7 seconds per lap slower than the race winner. In Formula 1, where margins are measured in hundredths of a second, 1.7 seconds represents a chasm. The car is not simply in need of fine-tuning — it has a fundamental performance deficit that Red Bull's current tools cannot easily solve.

A Personnel Crisis Alongside the Technical One

The car problems would be challenging enough in isolation. But they are unfolding against the backdrop of a personnel exodus that has stripped Red Bull of institutional knowledge and leadership depth. Christian Horner, the team principal who oversaw every championship victory, departed. So did Helmut Marko, the talent scout who helped identify Verstappen as a future champion. Courtenay, the lead strategist. Skinner, the chief designer. And most recently, Ole Schack — Verstappen's front-end mechanic for over 20 years and the only trackside staff member to have attended every Grand Prix since the 2005 Australian.

The combination of technical failure and leadership departure has created a team that looks very different from the one that dominated 2023 so completely.

The Exit Clause and the Sabbatical

Verstappen's contract contains an exit clause that can be triggered between August and October 2026 if the team's performance does not improve to meet specified criteria. ESPN sources have reported that Verstappen is leaning toward a sabbatical from Formula 1 in 2027 rather than an outright retirement — stepping away while waiting for either Red Bull to fix their car or the regulations to change in a direction that suits him.

He has been vocal in his criticism of the 2026 regulations, calling the cars "terrible" and comparing them to "Mario Kart." The FIA's technical meetings this week may produce rule changes for Miami — but whether they come soon enough to meaningfully change Red Bull's standing remains to be seen.

What's Next

Red Bull will use the five-week break before the Miami Grand Prix (May 1-3) to address the RB22's aerodynamic weaknesses. Meanwhile, Verstappen has been cleared to pursue sportscar racing activities at the Nurburgring — NLS Round 3 on April 11 — as a competitive outlet during the extended break. The April 20 FIA team principals meeting, where binding votes on regulation changes are expected, may provide some relief. But for a team that once made everyone else look slow, the road back to the front of the grid will be long.
