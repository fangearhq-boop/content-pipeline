Four Drivers, Four Penalties: The Belgian GP Grid That Grid Penalties Built

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap | July 19, 2026

The 2026 Belgian Grand Prix starting grid looks almost nothing like the qualifying classification. Four separate grid penalties — totaling 50 places of combined drops — have reshuffled the order in ways that change the race's character entirely.

Here is what happened, and what it means for Sunday's race.

Lando Norris: 10-Place Drop

The most prominent penalty belongs to the defending world champion. McLaren fitted Norris with a fourth control electronics unit at Belgium — one beyond the season's permitted allocation. Qualifying third on raw pace, Norris drops to thirteenth on the grid. McLaren made the deliberate choice to take this penalty at Spa rather than at a later circuit, banking on the Belgian track's overtaking opportunities to minimize the damage. Norris goes into the race with a "wait and see" mindset.

Fernando Alonso: 20-Place Drop (Last on Grid)

The most severe penalty of the weekend belongs to Fernando Alonso. The Aston Martin driver has exceeded his permitted season allocation of Energy Store, Control Electronics, and Ancillary Component units — hitting all three thresholds in one go. That triggers a 20-place grid penalty, sending the two-time world champion to the back of the grid in P22. Last. Starting behind every car on the grid.

For Alonso, who has maintained throughout his Aston Martin tenure that he believes the team can still give him a car capable of competing, today is a painful reminder of the reliability struggles the 2026 regulations have caused for the Silverstone-based outfit. Both Aston Martin cars — Alonso and Stroll — start near the back of the grid.

Lance Stroll and Isack Hadjar: 10 Places Each

Stroll's 10-place drop places him P20, one ahead of his future teammate Alonso (who takes the bigger hit). Isack Hadjar, the Racing Bulls driver who showed strong pace in qualifying, drops to P21 after his own 10-place penalty. The combined Aston Martin and Red Bull junior team penalties mean the tail of the grid is a mix of talent operating well below their natural order.

Who Benefits

The penalties provide unexpected opportunity for drivers who might otherwise be swallowed by the midfield. Gabriel Bortoleto in the Audi starts P8 — a position that reflects both his qualifying performance and the grid reshuffle working in his favour. The Audi program has been quietly impressive in 2026, and P8 on a grid at Spa represents the kind of starting position that can produce a top-six finish.

The opening lap through La Source and up Raidillon typically determines a significant portion of the race's outcome. With so many drivers starting away from their natural positions — some looking to charge forward, others looking to survive — chaos at Turn One is almost guaranteed.

What's Next

The Belgian Grand Prix represents the final race before the summer break, meaning whatever chaos the grid penalties create today will be the final story before teams disperse for August. After the break, the penalty picture will reset for many teams as new component allocations become available. Today, however, the grid is what it is — and the race will be the richer for it.
