F1 2027 Engine Revolution — All Stakeholders Agree to Boost ICE Power and Reduce ERS

By Elena Voss | May 9, 2026 | F1 Fanrecap

Formula 1 is walking back one of the most controversial aspects of its 2026 regulations — and it's doing so with complete unanimity from every corner of the paddock. Following an online meeting convened by the FIA and attended by all team principals, Formula One Management, and representatives of all five power unit manufacturers, stakeholders agreed in principle to a significant shift in engine philosophy for the 2027 season.

The core of the change: Internal Combustion Engine power will increase by approximately 50 kilowatts, while Energy Recovery System deployment power will decrease by approximately 50kW. What was a near-50:50 split between combustion and electrical power in 2026 will shift toward a 60:40 ratio in favor of the traditional combustion engine.

Why This Matters — The Problem With 2026

The 2026 regulations were designed to make Formula 1 more environmentally aligned, with a dramatic increase in the role of electrical power. In theory, this created a new era of cutting-edge hybrid racing. In practice, it created something else: drivers complaining about energy harvesting requirements that fundamentally changed how the cars could be driven.

"Super clipping" — a technique where drivers lift off throttle at the end of straights to recharge the battery — became a defining feature of 2026 racing. Alongside lifting and coasting in corners, drivers were spending significant mental bandwidth managing an energy budget rather than attacking circuits at their limits. It was a legitimate concern, and the sport's leadership heard it.

The proposed 2027 changes address the root cause directly. Less ERS deployment power means less energy needs to be harvested. Less energy to harvest means less super clipping and lifting. More ICE power means more combustion-based performance — the kind that feels natural to drivers, looks natural to fans, and sounds natural to everyone watching.

Unprecedented Consensus

What makes this announcement remarkable isn't just the content — it's the unanimity. All five power unit manufacturers in Formula 1 (Mercedes, Ferrari, Honda, Renault, and Audi) agreed to these changes alongside every team and Formula One Management itself. In a sport where technical regulations are frequently contested, this level of consensus is exceptional.

The unanimous agreement reflects a shared recognition that the 2026 regulations — while technically impressive — created unintended racing consequences that needed addressing. The meeting also reviewed refinements introduced at the Miami Grand Prix (focused on safety and reducing excessive harvesting), which were deemed successful and have now informed the longer-term 2027 approach.

What Still Needs to Happen

The changes agreed in principle are not yet confirmed. Further detailed discussion in technical working groups comprising teams and power unit manufacturers is required before a final package is decided. Once manufacturers vote on the final agreed package, it proceeds to the World Motor Sport Council for formal rubber-stamping.

That process takes time, but the hard part — getting every stakeholder in the room and walking away with agreement — is done. The technical details are refinements to an agreed direction, not the direction itself.

What's Next

For 2026, the season continues as planned — the current regulations remain in place through the end of the year. The first opportunity to see the impact of the 2027 direction will come during winter testing at the end of the year, when teams will begin presenting concepts aligned with the new balance.

For fans, the promise is straightforward: more combustion power, less energy management, and racing that looks and sounds more like the sport at its most thrilling. Formula 1 is not abandoning hybrid power — electrical energy remains a significant component. But the balance is shifting, and the sport is all the better for admitting it needed to.
