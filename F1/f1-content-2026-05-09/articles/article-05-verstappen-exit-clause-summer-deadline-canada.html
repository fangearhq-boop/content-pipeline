Verstappen's Exit Clause — The Summer Deadline Is Approaching and the Numbers Don't Lie

By Ryan Calloway | May 9, 2026 | F1 Fanrecap

Max Verstappen has won four Formula 1 World Championships. He has been, at various points in his career, the most dominant driver on the planet. In 2026, he is sitting seventh in the championship with 26 points, 74 behind the leader — a young Mercedes protégé named Kimi Antonelli who was born in 2005.

The numbers are one thing. The calendar is something else entirely. Verstappen's contract with Red Bull is widely reported to include an exit clause that would allow him to leave if he is not within the top three of the Drivers' Championship standings at the summer break — traditionally scheduled after the Hungarian Grand Prix in late July. He currently sits 5 places outside that threshold.

How Red Bull Got Here

The Red Bull RB22's 2026 struggles trace back to a fundamental issue that became apparent very early in the season: the car was significantly overweight. F1's new 2026 regulations introduced cars with a minimum weight limit of 768 kilograms. The RB22 reportedly came in at approximately 30 kilograms above that threshold — excess mass that negated much of the car's aerodynamic and mechanical engineering.

Red Bull's Miami upgrade package delivered meaningful progress. The excess weight was reportedly halved, dropping from approximately 30kg over minimum to approximately 15kg over. Team staff have been described as "smiling again" — a tell-tale sign that people who work closely with the technical situation believe they're on the right track. A more comprehensive solution, potentially involving a new chassis, is targeted for the summer break.

But 15kg of excess weight is still 15kg of excess weight. It's better. It's not solved.

The Canada Opportunity

The Canadian Grand Prix's sprint weekend format gives Verstappen double the scoring opportunities in a single event. Maximum points from a sprint weekend (sprint win + race win) are 33 — compared to 26 for a standard race weekend. If Verstappen can find a way to challenge the front runners in Montreal, the sprint structure rewards that ambition with more return than a normal race.

The Circuit Gilles Villeneuve also suits Red Bull's historical strengths. Verstappen has been exceptional at Montreal in the past, and the circuit's stop-start character — where car balance through chicanes matters as much as straight-line speed — has occasionally allowed Red Bull to outperform expectations. The weight reduction from Miami's upgrades might be enough to let Verstappen compete for meaningful points positions.

What "Top Three" Actually Requires

After four races, the standings read: Antonelli 100, Russell 80, Leclerc 59, Norris 51, Hamilton 51, Verstappen 26. Third place — the threshold widely reported in Verstappen's exit clause — is currently held by Charles Leclerc with 59 points. Verstappen would need to close a 33-point gap to reach third, while also preventing Leclerc, Norris, and Hamilton from extending their current leads.

The summer break typically occurs after the Hungarian Grand Prix, which falls in late July. That's approximately 10-11 race weekends from now, including one sprint weekend (Canada). In that span, a healthy car can move through the standings quickly. The question is whether the RB22 — even with its upgrade — will be healthy enough.

What's Next

Canada is the immediate next step, and it arrives at exactly the right moment for Verstappen to demonstrate what the upgraded Red Bull can do. A strong sprint weekend — podiums in both events, maximum points — would reduce his deficit and start the conversation about a genuine championship comeback.

The summer fix remains the bigger variable. If Red Bull delivers a comprehensive solution at the summer break, Verstappen could suddenly become a factor in the second half of the season. If the fix is delayed, or insufficient, the exit clause discussion will intensify regardless of how things go in Canada.

Verstappen has never been a driver who accepts a losing position quietly. His four championships were all built on relentless pressure, mechanical advantage, and an unwillingness to concede when the mathematics still offered a path. Right now, that path is narrow — but it exists. Whether he uses it in time is the most compelling subplot in Formula 1's 2026 season.
