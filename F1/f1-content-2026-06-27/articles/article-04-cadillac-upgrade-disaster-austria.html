Cadillac's Ten-Part Upgrade Arrives in Austria — and Goes Up in Smoke

By Elena Voss

June 27, 2026

F1 Fanrecap

Cadillac arrived at the Red Bull Ring with something to prove. Seven races into the 2026 Formula 1 season, the American constructor was the only team without a single championship point — a fact that had generated steadily growing scrutiny from within and outside the paddock. Their answer was ten new components, their largest upgrade package of the year, specifically targeted at the lap-15 tire degradation cliff that had cost them race time in every event so far.

By the end of Free Practice 2 on Friday, they had been unable to evaluate any of them.

Perez's Electrical Nightmare

The troubles started with Sergio Perez in Free Practice 1. Electrical failure struck the MAC-26 midway through the session, stranding the car on circuit after mechanics replaced the electronic control unit. When the replacement unit failed to resolve the issue, the car triggered a red flag — erasing practice time for every team on track and confirming that Cadillac's Friday was beginning in the worst possible way.

FP2 brought no improvement on Perez's side of the garage. The electrical issues that caused the red flag in the first session returned in the second, this time forcing a Virtual Safety Car period. The team spent the full day chasing a fault they could not isolate and fix quickly enough to put meaningful laps on the upgrade components they had shipped to Austria.

Bottas's Fire

Valtteri Bottas's Friday was a different kind of bad. Sparks flew from beneath his car during FP2 — an aerodynamic issue at floor level that was visible on the broadcast and alarming enough to prompt the team to recall him. In the garage, mechanics discovered smoke filling the cockpit, followed by a small fire under the rear of the car. The fire was extinguished. Then it reignited.

Bottas has experienced difficult seasons before — he spent years as Lewis Hamilton's teammate at Mercedes before transitioning to smaller teams. But a cockpit fire and a day spent entirely in the garage, unable to run the upgrade package that was supposed to represent the team's breakthrough, is the kind of Friday that tests resilience in any paddock environment.

"We've got a whole load of work tonight," the team acknowledged in a statement after the session. That was, by any measure, an understatement.

What the Upgrade Was Supposed to Do

Cadillac's ten-component package was designed to address the rear instability that had been the primary limiting factor in their race performance. The MAC-26 reaches lap 15 of any given grand prix and begins losing pace at a rate that prevents effective tire management, forcing either an early stop that wastes track position or a long stint that destroys lap times. The floor geometry and rear suspension changes in the upgrade package were specifically targeted at that degradation pattern.

Sergio Perez had previously claimed that Cadillac was the fastest unscored team in the field, outperforming Aston Martin by more than a second in outright pace at Barcelona. Whether that claim is accurate or not, the gap between pace and results has been the defining frustration of their 2026 campaign. Austria was the moment they had flagged as an opportunity to close it.

They left Friday unable to confirm whether the upgrade worked at all.

What's Next

Saturday morning brought the final practice session, followed by qualifying. Cadillac's engineers worked through the night to resolve the electrical issue on Perez's car and address the fire damage on Bottas's, giving the drivers limited but usable track time to at least gather some data on the upgrade package before Sunday's race.

The longer arc of Cadillac's situation remains unchanged. Valtteri Bottas has spoken publicly about uncertainty around the team's future, and the gap to the points-scoring teams has not been as narrow as the team's own assessments suggest. Austria was supposed to be a turning point. Whether the weekend ultimately provides any useful data on the upgrade — and whether that data translates into a different kind of performance on Sunday — is the question Cadillac's engineers will be working to answer through the rest of the weekend.

The clock toward their first Formula 1 championship point is still running. Zero points, seven races, and a ten-part upgrade that spent most of Friday unable to turn a wheel.
