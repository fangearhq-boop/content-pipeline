The Math Is Done: Verstappen's Red Bull Exit Clause Is Now Officially Triggered

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap | July 13, 2026

F1 Driver Market | Max Verstappen | Red Bull | Exit Clause

Numbers do not lie, and right now the numbers are telling Max Verstappen something very significant about his future at Red Bull.

Verstappen sits seventh in the 2026 Formula 1 Drivers Championship with 76 points. George Russell holds second place with 154. That gap — 78 points — cannot be closed before the summer break. The Belgian Grand Prix (July 17-19) and the Hungarian Grand Prix (July 24-26) are the only two races remaining, offering a maximum haul of 50 combined points even if Verstappen wins both. Seventy-six plus fifty equals 126. Russell's current total alone is 154, and he will continue scoring his own points in those races.

There is no mathematical path. Verstappen cannot reach second place in the standings before summer. His exit clause — the contractual mechanism that allows him to leave Red Bull for 2027 if he is outside the top two when the summer break begins — is now triggered.

What the Clause Actually Means

Verstappen's Red Bull contract runs through the end of the 2028 season. But embedded within it is a performance-linked exit clause: if he is not inside the top two in the championship standings at the start of the summer break, he has the right to terminate his deal early and leave for a rival team in 2027.

Triggering the clause and formally activating it are two different things. Verstappen has until approximately October 2026 to make a formal decision — to officially notify Red Bull of his intention to exercise the clause. That deadline gives him the Belgian GP weekend, the summer break itself, and several additional races to assess his situation before committing to a path.

Red Bull attempted to remove or buy out the clause entirely. Multiple reports confirm that effort failed. The clause stands.

Red Bull's Complicated Summer

For Red Bull, Verstappen's situation creates a profound planning problem. They cannot build a 2027 driver lineup without knowing whether their best driver will be part of it. Reports indicate ownership is internally divided on how to respond — Chalerm Yoovidhya's name has been mentioned in connection with the internal dispute. Meanwhile, Verstappen's longtime race engineer GianPiero Lambiase has already signed with McLaren, effective no later than 2028, further clouding the picture.

Verstappen has continued to perform professionally. A podium at the Austrian Grand Prix showed Red Bull that the car is capable of competing in specific conditions. But the team has not challenged for wins consistently in 2026, and Verstappen — a four-time world champion — has not hidden his frustration with the situation.

What Comes Next

Three possible futures remain in play. First, Verstappen could activate the clause and move to a new team for 2027 — McLaren remains the most discussed option despite official denials from CEO Zak Brown. Second, Verstappen could announce his retirement from Formula 1, a possibility that Yahoo Sports reported is increasingly taken seriously in paddock circles, particularly given his stated concerns about the sport's current driving style demands. Third, Verstappen and Red Bull could negotiate a new arrangement that renders the clause moot — though this seems least likely given the public trajectory of events.

The formal decision is expected before October. In the meantime, the Belgian and Hungarian Grand Prix weekends will serve as a backdrop to the most gripping off-track story of the 2026 season.

What's Next

Watch for any statements from Verstappen, his management team, or Red Bull in the coming days as the Belgian GP weekend approaches. The October deadline for formally triggering the clause means this story will continue developing through the summer break and into the second half of the season.
