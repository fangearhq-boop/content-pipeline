Max Wants to Stay: Verstappen's Manager Fires Back at Exit Clause Speculation

By Ryan Calloway | July 24, 2026

Raymond Vermeulen issued the clearest "stay" signal yet from Max Verstappen's camp, insisting the exit clause in his Red Bull contract does not mean he will leave. Whether that holds through October is a different question entirely.

For weeks, the assumption in Formula 1 has been that Max Verstappen's exit clause from Red Bull Racing would be activated — that by October, the most decorated active driver in the sport would announce a departure and begin a new chapter elsewhere. His manager wants you to know that is not the settled matter it appeared to be.

Raymond Vermeulen released a statement that represents the clearest signal yet from Verstappen's camp in favour of staying: "The fact that this clause exists doesn't mean we'll activate it. Max wants to finish his career here. He has a contract through 2028 and wants to see it out."

Formula 1 host Tom Clarkson separately reported this week that he has heard Verstappen "is going to stay for 2027." Red Bull, according to reports ahead of the Hungarian GP, has reportedly attempted to financially buy out the exit clause to remove the question entirely.

How the Clause Was Triggered

Verstappen's exit clause was triggered by performance conditions, not by his own choice. His DNF at the British Grand Prix mathematically eliminated any possibility of finishing in the top two of the Drivers' Championship — the threshold his contract identified. The clause has been active since Silverstone, giving Verstappen and his management team until October to decide whether to exercise it.

He currently sits seventh in the standings. At the Hungaroring on Friday, he was P8 in FP1 before dropping further back in FP2 — while both Aston Martin drivers posted P4 and P5 in the session.

The comparison writes itself, whether or not Verstappen's team is actually writing it.

The Aston Martin Variable

Andrew Newey's arrival at Aston Martin has been the defining off-track story of 2026. The 16-part B-spec that debuted at the Hungaroring this weekend — new nose, floor, suspension, and a chassis 30 kilograms lighter — is the first full design expression of what Newey's influence looks like on track. Stroll went P4 in FP2. Alonso, recovering from a back injury that kept him out of FP1, went P5.

Verstappen saw those times from his Red Bull. He knows what Newey has done to cars before. The RB20 that Verstappen drove to four consecutive titles was, in part, Newey's work. The car he is watching from the other side of the garage this weekend is what Newey is building when he controls the full design brief.

Whether that picture changes Verstappen's calculation is something only Verstappen knows. His manager's statement is unambiguous. The October deadline is not.

Is Vermeulen Sincere?

It is impossible to know from the outside. What is clear is that Red Bull believes retaining Verstappen is worth significant financial effort — the reported buyout attempt is itself a signal of how seriously the team takes the possibility of losing him. Teams do not attempt to buy out exit clauses on contracts they believe drivers intend to honour voluntarily.

Vermeulen's statement can be read two ways. It may be sincere — Verstappen has spoken before about Red Bull being his home in the sport, and his personal relationship with the team runs deeper than championship standings. Or it may be negotiating language, designed to reduce leverage for rival teams trying to recruit him and extract maximum financial terms from Red Bull's reported buyout approach.

Either reading is consistent with the statement he released. The Hungarian GP weekend gives Verstappen one last competitive read before the summer break — his car against the Aston Martin on the same track, in the same conditions. Whatever he sees this weekend becomes part of the decision that October will finalize.
