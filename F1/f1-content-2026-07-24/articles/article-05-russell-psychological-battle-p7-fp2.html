George Russell's Toughest Year: Software Fixed, Still Climbing — The Mental Load of Chasing a Teammate in His Prime

By Marcus Cole | July 24, 2026

George Russell said 2026 has been the "toughest psychological battle" of his career. Mercedes applied a software fix for Hungary. He finished FP2 in seventh. The car is better. The gap to his own teammate is 50 points.

George Russell used the word "battle" this week. Not to describe a race or a rival driver. To describe his own mind.

"2026 has been the toughest psychological battle of my career," Russell told reporters at the Hungarian Grand Prix.

It is not difficult to understand why. Russell enters the Hungarian GP third in the Drivers' Championship with 154 points — a position that, in almost any other season, would represent a genuine title contender. But his teammate Kimi Antonelli leads the championship with 204 points. Antonelli is 19 years old. He is in his first full Formula 1 season. He is driving the same car. And he is pulling away.

The Spa Problem and Its Fix

The gap between Russell and Antonelli widened at the Belgian Grand Prix in a particularly damaging way. Russell retired from a race he had been competitive in due to a power unit software failure. The result cost him potentially significant points and extended Antonelli's lead at exactly the moment Russell needed to close it.

Mercedes spent the weeks between Spa and Hungary diagnosing the problem. What they found: an algorithm was front-loading energy deployment from the hybrid system, depleting the battery before the car reached its main acceleration zones. The car was effectively exhausting its energy recovery benefit in the wrong parts of the lap, which compromised both outright pace and reliability. The issue had been present since the British Grand Prix at Silverstone — several races earlier than the Belgian GP failure — and was initially misdiagnosed as a driving-style problem. Russell had been told the car's deficit in certain sectors was a product of how he was driving, when in fact it was a software error creating a false picture of the data.

For Hungary, Mercedes applied a patch. The fix was confirmed to be working ahead of the weekend.

What P7 Means and Doesn't Mean

Russell finished FP2 in seventh place. That is ahead of Lewis Hamilton and Max Verstappen. It is behind both McLarens, both Aston Martins, and Charles Leclerc's Ferrari.

One day of practice with a patched software configuration does not tell you everything about where the car truly sits in the competitive order. Teams run different fuel loads, tyre compounds, and setup directions on Fridays. Russell's P7 may understate or overstate where Mercedes genuinely is once qualifying simulations begin Saturday afternoon.

What it does confirm is that the car completed both sessions without the energy harvest anomaly that caused the Spa failure. The fix, at minimum, holds up in practice conditions. Whether it translates to a pace improvement sufficient to close the gap to the McLarens and the new Aston Martin is a harder question — one that Saturday will begin to answer.

The Larger Picture

The Hungaroring is the final race before the Formula 1 summer break. If Russell cannot close the gap to Antonelli this weekend, his teammate could arrive at Zandvoort in September with a commanding points lead, a healthy psychological advantage, and two months of summer break to consolidate both.

Russell, for what it is worth, said he entered the weekend with a "positive mindset." He has been direct about the mental difficulty of 2026 without letting that directness become an excuse. He is competing. He is still in the championship conversation. And the problem that cost him points at Spa — a problem that turned out to be the car's, not his — has been addressed.

The mathematical picture is uncomfortable. Eight races remain after Hungary, with 200 points available. Antonelli leads by 50. Russell needs good weekends from here and needs Antonelli to stop having them. Hungary is the next chance to start changing the numbers. P7 in FP2 is not the answer he needed. It may, at least, be the platform to find one.
