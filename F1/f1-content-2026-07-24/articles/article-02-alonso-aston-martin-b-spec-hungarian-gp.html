Alonso Returns from Injury, Newey's B-Spec Delivers: Aston Martin's Big Gamble Pays Off on Day One

By Marcus Cole | July 24, 2026

Fernando Alonso missed FP1 with a back injury and returned to put the brand-new AMR26-B in P5 at the Hungaroring. Lance Stroll was P4. The 16-part Newey upgrade worked — on the first day it ever ran at a race weekend.

The Friday at the Hungaroring started with a scare for Aston Martin and ended with a statement.

Fernando Alonso, who has been managing a muscular back injury since the Belgian Grand Prix, was ruled out of FP1 on Friday morning. Reserve driver Felipe Drugovich was called in to take his AMR26 for the session. The team said Alonso would be assessed throughout the morning for a possible FP2 return. Nobody at Aston Martin would say with certainty what that assessment would find.

The medical team cleared him. Alonso returned for FP2, stepped into Andrew Newey's brand-new B-spec Aston Martin for the first time in a race weekend environment — and finished P5. His teammate Lance Stroll was P4. Together, they put the AMR26-B ahead of Lewis Hamilton, George Russell, and every car on the grid that wasn't named Norris, Piastri, or Leclerc.

What the B-Spec Actually Is

The upgrade package Aston Martin has brought to Hungary is not a traditional development step. It is a near-complete redesign under the regulatory structure that the team has classified as a B-spec — the same terminology used historically for season-defining mid-year overhauls.

The 16 separate changes include a new nose and front wing, reshaped brake ducts, four floor updates, revised rear suspension geometry, and a complete chassis re-homologation after approximately 30 kilograms were removed from the car's overall weight. The package represents the first full expression of Andrew Newey's influence on the Aston Martin concept since he joined the team. For most of the first ten rounds of the season, the AMR26 had run without significant upgrades while Newey's designs moved through the production pipeline.

Newey described the Hungarian GP weekend as the team's "absolute top priority" in terms of correlating real-world telemetry with his wind tunnel data. What he sees this weekend — the delta between simulation and track — will shape how the car develops for the rest of 2026.

The Numbers on Track

In FP1, Stroll ran the B-spec on its race weekend debut and ended the session P9. That result understated what the car was doing — FP1 included five mandatory rookie substitutions across the field, which shuffled the regular lineup and made comparative pace harder to read. The important thing from FP1 was that the car came back to the garage without drama and gave engineers the data they needed for FP2 setup.

FP2 told a different story. Stroll improved to P4. Alonso, having missed the first session entirely while being evaluated by the medical team, returned and went P5. Both results came ahead of both Mercedes drivers and were separated from the McLarens and Leclerc's Ferrari by what the engineers will now measure in precise detail.

One practice session is not a verdict. The Hungaroring's tight, technical layout rewards cars with high mechanical grip — the same characteristic that tends to flatter cars in Friday long-run simulations before qualifying removes the fuel load effect. Aston Martin may look different on Saturday when teams move into single-lap performance mode.

But the initial signal is what it is.

What This Means Beyond the Weekend

The timing of the B-spec's arrival is significant in ways that go beyond the Hungarian GP result. Max Verstappen's exit clause from Red Bull Racing is active following his DNF at the British Grand Prix. He was P8 in FP1 at the Hungaroring — in a Red Bull, watching Stroll and Alonso clock P4 and P5 in the new Aston Martin.

Whether Verstappen will activate his clause by October's deadline remains uncertain. His manager Raymond Vermeulen said this week that Verstappen "wants to finish his career" at Red Bull. But the data from Hungary is now part of whatever calculation Verstappen and his team are making. If Newey's B-spec continues to develop at this pace through the second half of the season, the car he might move to could look very different by 2027 than the one that has been midfield for most of 2026.

Alonso, for his part, is 44 years old, managing a back injury, and still P5 in a car he had never driven in race conditions before this afternoon. Whatever the rest of the weekend brings, Friday at the Hungaroring was the best evidence yet that the team Newey is building is worth watching.

What's Next

Alonso's fitness will be monitored overnight ahead of FP3 and qualifying on Saturday. The team expects him to be available for the full weekend. The bigger question is whether the pace Stroll and Alonso showed in FP2 holds up when McLaren, Ferrari, and Mercedes move into qualifying simulation on Saturday afternoon — and what the longer-run data from Friday tells the engineers about tyre degradation over a race distance.

If the AMR26-B can stay in contention through Saturday, Aston Martin could be looking at its strongest qualifying result of the season.
