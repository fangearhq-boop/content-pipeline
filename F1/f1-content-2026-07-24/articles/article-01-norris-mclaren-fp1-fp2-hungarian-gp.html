Norris Dominates Hungarian GP Friday — The Pre-Event Favourites Are in Trouble

By Ryan Calloway | July 24, 2026

Lando Norris topped both FP1 and FP2 at the Hungaroring, leading a McLaren 1-2 in every session on the day Ferrari was supposed to be uncatchable.

Before the Hungarian Grand Prix weekend began, Ferrari was the name on everyone's lips. The SF-26 suits the Hungaroring's medium-speed, high-downforce demands. Charles Leclerc and Lewis Hamilton needed a strong result before the summer break to close the gap to championship leader Kimi Antonelli.

By the end of Friday, McLaren had made sure none of those plans felt as secure as they had on Thursday.

Lando Norris set the fastest time in FP1 — a 1:16.052, just 0.019 seconds ahead of Oscar Piastri — and then did it again in FP2, posting a 1:15.624 with Piastri 0.291 seconds behind him. McLaren went 1-2 in both sessions. Ferrari's Leclerc was P3 in each, but the gap to the McLarens was clear.

The Session by Session Picture

FP1 at the Hungaroring was run with the five rookie slots the FIA mandates per team for the season, which shuffled the regular lineup for the opening hour. Kimi Antonelli's seat went to Frederik Vesti at Mercedes; Racing Bulls ran Paul Aron; Haas fielded Ryo Hirakawa; Cadillac gave Colton Herta his second FP1 appearance of the year; and McLaren ran Leonardo Fornaroli — the reigning Formula 2 champion. Aston Martin was without Fernando Alonso entirely, after he was ruled out with a muscular back injury managed since the Belgian GP. Reserve driver Felipe Drugovich drove the AMR26.

Despite the altered lineup, the headline from FP1 was unambiguous: Norris and Piastri were fastest, with a 0.019-second gap separating them. Leclerc was P3. Lewis Hamilton was P5. Antonelli's Mercedes — when he returned for the regular session — was P7. Max Verstappen was P8. Lance Stroll was P9 in the Aston Martin B-spec on its debut.

FP2 told a similar story. Norris led again. Piastri was second again. Leclerc recovered to maintain P3. The significant changes in the second session: Stroll jumped to P4 and Alonso — medically cleared and returning from his FP1 absence — posted P5. Hamilton was P6. Russell P7. Both title contenders behind the new Aston Martin.

What Went Right for McLaren

McLaren team chiefs have been open this season about their development trajectory. At the Belgian GP, they acknowledged being approximately two to three months behind in their aero development programme — a gap they attributed to the revised technical regulations affecting their 2026 car concept. The upgrade package they brought to Hungary appears to have changed that picture materially, at least on a single-day basis.

Norris and Piastri were fast in every phase of both sessions — short runs and long runs, early exits and late exits. The Hungaroring rewards cars with strong mechanical grip and consistent aero balance through the complex middle sector. McLaren appeared to have both on Friday.

One day of practice is not a pole position or a race win. Teams typically hold back performance in practice — running fuel loads, tyre programs, and setups that don't reflect qualifying pace. Ferrari may well look entirely different when it matters. But the signal from Friday is real.

Championship Implications

Antonelli's position in the championship — 204 points, 45 ahead of Hamilton — is comfortable but not impregnable. Eight races remain after Hungary, with 200 maximum points available. If the Mercedes is off the pace this weekend and McLaren takes the top positions, Antonelli's lead could narrow without him doing anything wrong.

More pressing: Russell, who sits third in the championship at 154 points, needs this weekend. His Belgian GP retirement for a power unit software failure was the kind of result that turns a title fight into a management exercise. He was P7 in FP2 with a fix applied. He has not been near the McLarens' pace. Hamilton, at P6 in FP2, is closer but still a step back.

Ferrari will have data to work with overnight. The gap is there to close on Saturday if they find what they're looking for in the simulator and on the tyres. But right now, this is McLaren's track.

What's Next

FP3 runs Saturday morning before qualifying in the afternoon. Tyre strategy becomes critical on the softest Pirelli compounds of the year — C4, C5, and C6. The Hungaroring is notoriously difficult to overtake on, which means qualifying position carries unusual weight for Sunday's race. Whoever takes pole position at the Hungaroring goes into Sunday with a significant strategic advantage.

Norris has made the clear statement of intent. Whether Ferrari, Mercedes, or even the improving Aston Martin can respond will define how the final race before the summer break plays out.
