FIA Stewards Admit They Should Have Penalized Leclerc at Spa — A Rare Admission With Consequences

By Elena Voss | July 24, 2026

At a formally requested drivers' meeting in Hungary on Thursday night, FIA stewards acknowledged on the record that Charles Leclerc should have been penalized for his contact with Oscar Piastri at the Belgian Grand Prix. Leclerc himself agreed.

The FIA rarely says it got something wrong. On Thursday night at the Hungaroring, they did.

At a formally requested meeting between members of the driver grid and FIA stewards — held at the circuit ahead of the Hungarian GP weekend — the stewards acknowledged that they should have penalized Charles Leclerc for his incident with Oscar Piastri at the Belgian Grand Prix. The admission was made on the record, in a room with enough of the grid present to give it weight.

It is a rare thing. And the context around it matters.

What Happened at Spa

On Lap 8 of the Belgian Grand Prix, Leclerc squeezed Piastri into the barrier approaching Les Combes. The contact caused floor damage to Piastri's McLaren that engineers estimated cost approximately two to three tenths of a second per lap — the kind of performance loss that changes an entire race strategy for a car trying to compete for a win. Piastri managed the damage throughout the race and finished fifth. Leclerc finished second.

Stewards investigated the incident and cleared Leclerc. McLaren, whose car absorbed most of the consequences, said little publicly. The decision stood.

At Thursday's meeting in Hungary, Leclerc himself said he "went a bit too far." The stewards said they agreed — and confirmed that, in retrospect, a penalty should have been issued.

The Double Standard That Triggered the Meeting

The Leclerc-Piastri decision did not exist in isolation. At the same Belgian GP, Lewis Hamilton received a five-second time penalty for contact with George Russell exiting Turn 3. Two incidents in the same race. Two different outcomes. The Hamilton penalty involved contact that affected Russell; the Leclerc incident involved contact that damaged a rival's car for the remainder of the race.

The contrast was noticed. Drivers, team principals, and broadcasters spent the weeks between Spa and Hungary questioning the consistency of how the stewards were applying the rules. The grid-level frustration was what led to Thursday's meeting in the first place — a formally requested session between the drivers and the officiating panel, which is itself an unusual step.

Hamilton did not attend. He told reporters afterward that he "knew nothing about it." Max Verstappen was also absent. The meeting drew enough of the grid to carry weight regardless.

What an Admission Actually Changes

In practical terms, the acknowledgment changes nothing about the Belgian GP result. Piastri finished fifth with a damaged car. Leclerc finished second. Those standings are permanent.

What may change is forward-looking. Stewards are human, and racing incidents involve judgment calls that reasonable people can evaluate differently. But an on-the-record admission — delivered directly to the driver community at a formally convened meeting — carries a different kind of weight than a private reconsideration. It signals that the panel has re-examined the standard it applied at Spa and found it wanting.

For the second half of 2026, that recalibration may show up in how similar incidents are judged. The test will come the next time a driver is squeezed into a barrier wall while fighting for position — and whether the decision that follows is more consistent with the outcome the stewards now say they should have reached at Spa.

What's Next

Leclerc is at the Hungaroring this weekend trying to recover the narrative after Friday's practice sessions went to McLaren rather than Ferrari. He was P3 in both FP1 and FP2, a meaningful gap behind Norris and Piastri. Saturday qualifying will tell a great deal more. The FIA's admission from Thursday is backdrop — the on-track weekend ahead is what either confirms or complicates Ferrari's standing as a genuine contender for the final race before the summer break.
