### STORY 1: McLaren 1-2 in Both FP1 and FP2

#### Long-Form Post — 8:00 AM ET

Ferrari was the pre-event favourite for the Hungarian Grand Prix.

After Friday at the Hungaroring, you'd never know it.

Lando Norris posted the fastest time in FP1 (1:16.052) and FP2 (1:15.624), leading Oscar Piastri to back-to-back McLaren 1-2 finishes across both practice sessions. In FP1, Piastri was just 0.019 seconds behind Norris — effectively the same lap. In FP2, the gap stretched to 0.291 seconds, but nobody came close to either McLaren.

Charles Leclerc was the closest Ferrari could manage: P3 in both sessions, 0.399 seconds off Norris in FP2. Lewis Hamilton was P5 in FP1 and P6 in FP2.

Championship leader Kimi Antonelli had a difficult Friday. He was P7 in FP1 — two thousandths behind Oliver Bearman's Haas — and did not crack the top six in FP2 either, where George Russell and his recovered Mercedes was ahead. Max Verstappen was P8 in FP1 before dropping further down in FP2.

Context matters here. McLaren had been publicly struggling with their aero direction at Spa, where team chiefs acknowledged being "two to three months behind" in development. They brought a package of updates for Hungary — and immediately it looks like something clicked.

Ferrari will need a response on Saturday if they want to make good on their pre-event favourite billing.

### STORY 2: Aston Martin B-Spec / Alonso Return

#### Long-Form Post — 10:00 AM ET

The Friday at the Hungaroring started with a scare for Aston Martin. It ended with a statement.

Fernando Alonso — who has been managing a muscular back injury since the Belgian GP — was ruled out of FP1 on Friday morning. Reserve driver Felipe Drugovich took his AMR26 for the session. The team said Alonso would be assessed throughout the morning for a possible FP2 return.

The medical team cleared him.

Alonso returned for FP2, climbed into Andrew Newey's brand-new B-spec Aston Martin for the first time in a race weekend environment — and he finished P5. His teammate Lance Stroll was P4. Together, they put the AMR26-B ahead of Lewis Hamilton, George Russell, and every other car on the grid except three.

The upgrade package is enormous: 16 separate changes that include a new nose and front wing, reshaped brake ducts, four floor updates, revised rear suspension, and a complete chassis re-homologation after approximately 30 kilograms were removed from the car's weight. Newey described the weekend's "absolute top priority" as calibrating real-world telemetry against his wind tunnel simulations.

One practice session is not a verdict. The Hungaroring's tight, technical layout suits cars with mechanical grip — and it's possible Aston Martin performs differently once the other teams get into qualifying simulation mode.

But Stroll P4 and Alonso P5 on a new car, with Alonso recovering from injury, on day one? That's the result Aston Martin needed.

Max Verstappen was P8 in FP1. His exit clause is active. He'll have seen those Aston Martin times.

### STORY 3: FIA Admits Leclerc-Piastri Stewarding Error

#### Long-Form Post — 12:00 PM ET

The FIA rarely admits it got something wrong. On Thursday night at the Hungaroring, they did.

At a formally requested meeting between the driver grid and FIA stewards — held at the circuit ahead of the Hungarian GP weekend — the stewards acknowledged that they should have penalized Charles Leclerc for his incident with Oscar Piastri at the Belgian Grand Prix.

Here is what happened at Spa: On Lap 8, Leclerc squeezed Piastri into the barrier approaching Les Combes. The contact caused floor damage to Piastri's McLaren estimated at 2-3 tenths per lap — the kind of deficit that affects entire race strategy for a car trying to win. Stewards investigated and cleared Leclerc. Piastri managed the damage for the rest of the race and finished fifth. Leclerc finished second.

At Thursday's meeting, Leclerc himself said he "went a bit too far." The stewards agreed, on record.

This is the backdrop that had the entire driver grid in a room together in the first place. In a separate Spa incident, Lewis Hamilton received a 5-second time penalty for contact with George Russell exiting Turn 3. Two incidents. Two outcomes. One consistent complaint from drivers, team principals, and commentators about the application of the rules.

Notably: Hamilton did not attend Thursday's meeting. He told reporters he "knew nothing about it." Verstappen was also absent. The meeting drew enough of the grid to carry weight nonetheless.

What changes? Stewards are human and interpretations of racing incidents vary. But an admission of error — on the record, to the drivers — may signal a recalibrated standard for the second half of 2026.

### STORY 4: Verstappen "Wants to Stay" — Manager Fires Back at Exit Clause Speculation

#### Long-Form Post — 2:00 PM ET

For weeks, the assumption in Formula 1 has been that Max Verstappen's exit clause from Red Bull Racing would be activated — that by October, the most decorated active driver in the sport would announce his departure to Aston Martin, Ferrari, or wherever the competitive wind takes him.

His manager wants you to know that's not a settled matter.

Raymond Vermeulen released a statement that represents the clearest "stay" signal yet from Verstappen's camp: "The fact that this clause exists doesn't mean we'll activate it. Max wants to finish his career here. He has a contract through 2028 and wants to see it out."

Formula 1 host Tom Clarkson separately reported that he has heard Verstappen "is going to stay for 2027." Red Bull, according to reports ahead of the Hungarian GP, has reportedly attempted to financially buy out the clause.

Here is the context: The exit clause was triggered after Verstappen's DNF at the British Grand Prix mathematically eliminated any chance of finishing in the top two of the championship. He currently sits seventh in the standings. He was P8 in FP1 at Hungary on Friday — comfortably behind both Aston Martin drivers in FP2.

Is Vermeulen sincere? It is impossible to know from the outside. What is clear is that Red Bull believes retaining Verstappen is worth significant effort — financial or otherwise — and that the October deadline the clause references has not yet closed.

The Hungaroring this weekend gives Verstappen the last look before the summer break at the car Newey is building at Aston Martin. Stroll P4, Alonso P5 in FP2. The data point is on the board.

### STORY 5: Russell's Mental Battle — P7 After Software Fix

#### Long-Form Post — 8:00 PM ET

George Russell used the word "battle" this week. Not to describe a race or a rival. To describe his own mind.

"2026 has been the toughest psychological battle of my career," Russell told reporters at the Hungarian Grand Prix.

It is not difficult to understand why. Russell enters the Hungarian GP third in the Drivers' Championship — a position that, in any other season, would feel like a success. But his teammate Kimi Antonelli leads by 50 points. Antonelli — 19 years old, in his first full Formula 1 season, driving the same car — is pulling away in the standings.

The gap widened at the Belgian GP, where Russell retired from a race he had been competitive in, due to a power unit software failure. Mercedes identified the root cause: an algorithm was front-loading energy deployment, depleting the battery before the car reached its main acceleration zones. The issue had been present since Silverstone and was initially misdiagnosed as a driving-style problem.

For Hungary, Mercedes applied a software patch.

Russell finished FP2 in seventh place. That's ahead of Lewis Hamilton's Ferrari and Max Verstappen, but behind both McLarens, both Aston Martins, and Leclerc's Ferrari. The car is fixed — or at least improved. Whether it's enough to get Russell back in a genuine title fight is a harder question.

The Hungaroring is the final race before the summer break. If Russell cannot close the gap this weekend, Antonelli could arrive at Zandvoort in September with a commanding lead and the psychological advantage of a teammate who was in their corner mentally all along.

Russell, for what it's worth, entered the weekend with a "positive mindset." That, at this stage, is what he has to work with.
