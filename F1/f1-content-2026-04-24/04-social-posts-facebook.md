### STORY 1: FIA Extends Miami FP1 to 90 Minutes — First Live Test of New Rules

**Long-Form Post**
Window: 8:00 AM ET

Formula 1 fans got an important scheduling update this week: the sole Free Practice session at the Miami Grand Prix has been extended from 60 to 90 minutes, running from 12:00 to 13:30 local time on Friday May 1.

The FIA confirmed three reasons for the extension. First, teams haven't had competitive track time since the Japanese Grand Prix in late March — a five-week gap that leaves everyone a step behind on setup. Second, the revised 2026 technical regulations take effect in Miami, requiring teams to re-map their energy management software under real race conditions. Third, because Miami operates under the Sprint weekend format, there's no FP2 — meaning the 90-minute FP1 session is essentially teams' only extended practice window before Sprint qualifying kicks off Saturday.

The regulatory changes themselves are significant: the maximum energy recharge over a lap has been reduced (from 8MJ to 7MJ in qualifying), while peak super-clip power has been increased, allowing drivers to maintain full throttle on straights for longer. The tweaks were designed to produce more consistent "flat-out driving" — but every team will need to optimize their software before it shows up in lap times.

Miami could be the session that reshuffles the entire competitive order. Set your alarms.

### STORY 2: Red Bull RB22 "Terrible" — Verstappen's Worst Start Since 2015

**Long-Form Post**
Window: 10:00 AM ET

When your own driver calls your car "terrible," it's hard to spin that as a positive.

Red Bull's Isack Hadjar — who shares the RB22 with four-time world champion Max Verstappen — used exactly that word to describe the chassis after Japan. His verdict: the engine is competitive, but the car lurches from understeer to oversteer in a way that's fundamentally difficult to manage. Red Bull's own acknowledgment of "significant shortcomings" with the RB22 isn't far from Hadjar's assessment.

For Verstappen, the numbers paint an equally bleak picture. He failed to reach Q3 at Suzuka — the first time that's happened since his 2015 rookie season. Through three rounds, Red Bull sit sixth in the constructors' championship with just 16 points and zero race wins. The team's analysis points to a fundamental concept error: the RB22 was designed with an extreme focus on drag reduction at the expense of downforce, which has proven catastrophic in 2026's revised aerodynamic environment.

New Red Bull team principal Laurent Mekies faces his biggest test since taking over from Christian Horner. The team conducted extensive filming-day work at Silverstone in April, introducing a new floor, redesigned sidepods, and adjusted wings. Whether those upgrades are enough to close the gap to Mercedes and Ferrari will begin to show in Miami.

### STORY 3: Cadillac After 3 Races — Zero Points, One Crash, Full Commitment

**Long-Form Post**
Window: 12:00 PM ET

Formula 1's newest team has completed three races without a championship point to show for it. But listening to Sergio Perez and Valtteri Bottas talk about Cadillac's debut season, you wouldn't know it.

Through Australia, China, and Japan, the American-backed 11th team has faced every challenge a debut squad can encounter. No cars have reached Q2 in qualifying. Bottas' P13 in China represents their best race finish. In that same race, Perez and Bottas collided — a moment that could have turned ugly, but the stewards ruled it a racing incident and both drivers were quick to apologize to each other. Bottas also retired in Australia with a fuel systems issue, though both cars crossed the finish line in China and Japan.

Yet the broader picture from both drivers is genuinely optimistic. Perez said the team has "made a lot of progress in a short space of time." Bottas noted that Japan was "much cleaner" than the bumpy races before it. Both have publicly committed to putting the team's development ahead of any personal ambitions — something that's notable given Perez's long career as a race winner and Bottas' experience as a multiple champion-in-waiting.

Upgrades are planned for Miami and beyond. Cadillac's debut season was never going to be easy. The question now is how quickly Graeme Lowdon's team can turn rate of development into actual pace. Miami is the first real test of that.

### STORY 4: Miami Grand Prix Preview — Sprint Weekend May 1-3

**Long-Form Post**
Window: 2:00 PM ET

After five weeks away, Formula 1 returns in Miami — and there's a lot to look forward to.

The Miami Grand Prix runs May 1-3 at Miami International Autodrome. It's a Sprint weekend, meaning a compressed schedule with Sprint qualifying on Saturday, the Sprint race, then full qualifying, then Sunday's Grand Prix. Practice time is limited — which is why the FIA extended FP1 to 90 minutes.

The 2026 regulatory changes take effect in Miami: more peak power in the super-clip zones, less energy recovery in qualifying. Teams have been spending the April break re-mapping their software, tuning their setups, and bringing new parts to the track. Ferrari tested their new floor and the Macarena wing at Monza. McLaren's first proper aero upgrades arrive in Miami and Montreal. Red Bull hopes their Silverstone filming-day revisions translate to lap time.

On the championship side, Andrea Kimi Antonelli continues to lead as the youngest driver in history to hold the top spot in the standings. The 19-year-old Italian has a nine-point cushion over Mercedes teammate George Russell heading into Miami. Ferrari's Charles Leclerc and Lewis Hamilton sit third and fourth.

Five weeks is a long time in Formula 1. Miami will tell us who used the break most wisely.

### STORY 5: Williams "Slimming Program" — The Heaviest Car in F1

**Long-Form Post**
Window: 5:00 PM ET

Formula 1 has had its share of technical oddities in the 2026 season, but perhaps none more unusual than the situation inside Williams Racing.

The FW48 is reportedly carrying significantly more weight than its competitors — multiple industry reports estimate the car is somewhere between 26 and 30 kilograms above the FIA's 768kg minimum weight limit. For context, Mercedes' W17 is understood to be just 0.4kg over the limit, and Ferrari's SF-26 sits around 2kg over. The gap between Williams and the field in this category is extraordinary.

The excess weight traces back to late design changes: last-minute structural reinforcements added to the FW48 before the season began made the car heavier than originally planned. Williams team principal James Vowles has described an ongoing "slimming program" — a technical drive during the April break to strip kilograms wherever possible without compromising safety or structural integrity. Former F1 world champion Ralf Schumacher was less diplomatic, calling Williams the "biggest flop of 2026."

Williams and Aston Martin are both pointless through three races. For Vowles and his team, Miami represents a chance to show the development direction is real — even if the points haven't come yet. In a season where regulations have upended the entire grid order, nothing is permanent.
