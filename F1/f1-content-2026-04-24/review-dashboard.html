FIA Extends Miami FP1 to 90 Minutes — First Chance to Test New Rules in Race Conditions
NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post Window: 8:00 AM ET Formula 1 fans got an important scheduling update this week: the sole Free Practice session at the Miami Grand Prix has been extended from 60 to 90 minutes, running from 12:00 to 13:30 local time on Friday May 1. The FIA confirmed three reasons for the extension. First, teams haven't had competitive track time since the Japanese Grand Prix in late March — a five-week gap that leaves everyone a step behind on setup. Second, the revised 2026 technical regulations take effect in Miami, requiring teams to re-map their energy management software under real race conditions. Third, because Miami operates under the Sprint weekend format, there's no FP2 — meaning the 90-minute FP1 session is essentially teams' only extended practice window before Sprint qualifying kicks off Saturday. The regulatory changes themselves are significant: the maximum energy recharge over a lap has been reduced (from 8MJ to 7MJ in qualifying), while peak super-clip power has been increased, allowing drivers to maintain full throttle on straights for longer. The tweaks were designed to produce more consistent "flat-out driving" — but every team will need to optimize their software before it shows up in lap times. Miami could be the session that reshuffles the entire competitive order. Set your alarms. #F1Fanrecap | #F1 | #MiamiGP

Red Bull's own acknowledgment of "significant shortcomings" with the RB22 isn't far from Hadjar's assessment. For Verstappen, the numbers paint an equally bleak picture. New Red Bull team principal Laurent Mekies faces his biggest test since taking over from Christian Horner. The team conducted extensive filming-day work at Silverstone in April, introducing a new floor, redesigned sidepods, and adjusted wings. ### Long-Form Post Window: 5:00 PM ET Formula 1 has had its share of technical oddities in the 2026 season, but perhaps none more unusual than the situation inside Williams Racing. The FW48 is reportedly carrying significantly more weight than its competitors — multiple industry reports estimate the car is somewhere between 26 and 30 kilograms above the FIA's 768kg minimum weight limit. For context, Mercedes' W17 is understood to be just 0.4kg over the limit, and Ferrari's SF-26 sits around 2kg over. The gap between Williams and the field in this category is extraordinary. The excess weight traces back to late design changes: last-minute structural reinforcements added to the FW48 before the season began made the car heavier than originally planned. Williams team principal James Vowles has described an ongoing "slimming program" — a technical drive during the April break to strip kilograms wherever possible without compromising safety or structural integrity. Former F1 world champion Ralf Schumacher was less diplomatic, calling Williams the "biggest flop of 2026." Williams and Aston Martin are both pointless through three races. For Vowles and his team, Miami represents a chance to show the development direction is real — even if the points haven't come yet.
