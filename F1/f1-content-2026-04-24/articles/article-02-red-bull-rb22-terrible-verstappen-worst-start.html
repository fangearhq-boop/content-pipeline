Hadjar Calls RB22 "Terrible" — Verstappen's Worst Start to a Season Since His Rookie Year

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap | April 24, 2026

The word came from inside the team — and it was blunt.

Red Bull's Isack Hadjar, sharing the RB22 with four-time world champion Max Verstappen in 2026, described the car's chassis as "terrible" following the Japanese Grand Prix. His specific critique: the car lurches from understeer to oversteer in a way that makes it almost impossible to manage consistently. Hadjar's verdict on the engine was more positive — the power unit, he said, is competitive. The problem is the car around it.

Red Bull themselves acknowledged "significant shortcomings" with the RB22 after struggling through the first three rounds. The admission that a conceptual error may have been made — analysts have pointed to the car being designed with extreme drag reduction at the expense of downforce — underlines just how serious the challenge is for a team that dominated Formula 1 for four consecutive seasons.

Verstappen's Historically Bad Start

For Max Verstappen personally, the 2026 season represents the worst start of his career since joining the grid as a 17-year-old in 2015. He failed to reach Q3 at Suzuka — a qualifying round he has typically dominated — for the first time in more than a decade. Red Bull sit sixth in the constructors' championship with just 16 points, and the four-time champion has yet to see the top step of the podium this season.

It is a jarring reality for a driver who was synonymous with dominance for much of the last four years. But Verstappen's talent has never been in question — the RB22's fundamental issues are well documented, and his performances in the circumstances have been professional. The problem is structural, and structural problems take time to fix.

What Red Bull Did During the April Break

During the five-week gap between Japan and Miami, Red Bull conducted an extensive filming day at Silverstone, running Verstappen as their sole driver through a package that included a new floor design, redesigned sidepods with a more acute kink, and revised front and rear wing configurations. Team principal Laurent Mekies described Miami as a "second season launch" — reflecting the significance of what the team hopes will be a substantial step forward in performance.

Whether those changes translate into meaningful lap time improvement is the biggest question heading into the Miami GP weekend. Red Bull's wind tunnel and simulation work suggested direction — but simulation and reality have diverged before, and a sprint format gives limited room for iteration if the updates don't work as expected.

What's Next

Miami represents a pivotal moment for Red Bull in 2026. If the revised RB22 performs closer to its potential, the team can begin to mount a genuine challenge for race wins and constructor points. If the updates fall short, the deficit — already substantial in only three races — risks becoming unmanageable over the course of a long season. Laurent Mekies has spoken of Miami as a reset. For Verstappen and Red Bull, it needs to be exactly that.
