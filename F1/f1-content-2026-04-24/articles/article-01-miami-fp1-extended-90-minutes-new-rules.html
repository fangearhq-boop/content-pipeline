Miami FP1 Extended to 90 Minutes: How F1's New Rules Make This the Most Important Practice Session of 2026

By Elena Voss | F1 Fanrecap | April 24, 2026

Formula 1 teams just got a significant gift heading into the Miami Grand Prix — and they're going to need every minute of it.

The FIA has confirmed that Free Practice 1 at Miami International Autodrome, taking place on Friday May 1, will be extended to 90 minutes — up from the standard 60. The session runs from 12:00 to 13:30 local time, with all preceding track sessions shifted forward by 30 minutes to accommodate the change. It is a relatively rare adjustment to the standard weekend format, and the reasons behind it are genuinely significant for the championship.

The FIA cited three factors in the decision: the five-week gap since the Japanese Grand Prix in late March, the revised 2026 technical regulations that come into full effect in Miami, and the fact that Miami operates under the Sprint format — which means there is no Free Practice 2. The 90-minute FP1 session is effectively the only extended practice window teams will have before Sprint qualifying fires up Saturday morning.

Why the New Rules Make This Session Critical

The 2026 regulatory tweaks are not minor adjustments. The maximum energy recharge permitted over a qualifying lap has been reduced from 8MJ to 7MJ, while the peak super-clip power has been increased — designed to allow drivers to maintain full throttle on straights for longer and produce more consistent flat-out racing. The MGU-K deployment outside key power zones has also been addressed.

In practical terms, these changes require every team to re-map their energy management software before the session that truly matters. No amount of simulator work fully replicates the complexity of running in live traffic, on a real circuit, under race temperature conditions. FP1 is where the data becomes real — and 90 minutes instead of 60 gives engineers a meaningful extra window to refine that mapping before the Sprint weekend escalates.

The tweaks were themselves a product of data gathered from the first three races in Australia, China, and Japan. F1's all-new power unit regulations have produced genuinely novel energy management challenges, and the FIA's refinements reflect a governing body responding quickly to early-season feedback from teams and drivers.

The Championship Picture at Stake

Miami also marks the first competitive action since Mercedes' Andrea Kimi Antonelli won the Japanese Grand Prix, extending his championship lead over teammate George Russell to nine points — 72 to 63. Ferrari's Charles Leclerc and Lewis Hamilton sit third and fourth. The extended five-week break has allowed every team to bring developments to Miami that they couldn't race in the Middle East after the Bahrain and Saudi Arabian Grands Prix were cancelled.

Ferrari tested an upgraded floor and aerodynamic package at Monza in late April. McLaren's first significant aero updates are arriving in Miami and Montreal. Red Bull's filming-day revisions at Silverstone were extensive. With new hardware, new regulations, and a Sprint weekend format, the 90-minute FP1 session could produce the clearest picture yet of where the competitive order truly stands in 2026.

What's Next

The full Miami Grand Prix weekend schedule begins May 1. Following the extended FP1, Sprint qualifying takes place on Saturday, followed by the Sprint race. Full qualifying runs Saturday as well, with the Grand Prix on Sunday May 3 at 4 PM ET. For fans who have been waiting five weeks for F1 to return, Miami promises a packed and consequential weekend — with FP1 setting the tone for everything that follows.
