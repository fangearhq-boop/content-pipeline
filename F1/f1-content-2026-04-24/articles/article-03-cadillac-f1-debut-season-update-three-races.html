Cadillac's F1 Debut After Three Races: Zero Points, One Teammate Clash, and Genuine Progress

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap | April 24, 2026

Formula 1's newest team walked into the 2026 season knowing the first year would be difficult. Cadillac had no illusions about competing for race wins from Race 1. But three grands prix into their debut campaign, the full story of where the American-backed outfit stands is more nuanced than the zero in the points column suggests.

Sergio Perez and Valtteri Bottas have completed three rounds — Australia, China, and Japan — without scoring a championship point. No Cadillac car has reached Q2 in qualifying. Bottas' P13 in China represents the team's best race finish. These are difficult facts, and they reflect the genuine pace deficit that a debut team faces in a field of established giants running highly developed machinery.

China: The Crash That Could Have Been Worse

The low point of the season so far came during the Chinese Grand Prix, when Perez and Bottas made contact on track in what the stewards ruled a racing incident. Both drivers were quick to apologize to each other, and the tone from both was measured — neither driver blamed the other publicly. It was an uncomfortable moment for a team still learning how its two experienced veterans fit together competitively, but one that appears to have strengthened rather than fractured the partnership.

Bottas retired in Australia with a fuel systems issue, but both cars reached the finish line in China and Japan. Reliability through the most difficult phase of a debut season is itself an achievement.

What the Drivers Are Saying

The language from Perez and Bottas has been consistently team-first throughout the season opener. Perez noted that the team had "made a lot of progress in a short space of time" after Japan, while Bottas called the Japanese Grand Prix "much cleaner" than what preceded it. Both have framed their role as foundational — building the infrastructure and culture of a new team in real time, through the grinding reality of a full F1 calendar.

For Perez, that represents a genuine philosophical shift. His years at McLaren, Racing Point, and Red Bull were about competing for results. At Cadillac, the result is the team itself — and both he and Bottas appear to understand that clearly.

What's Next

Cadillac is bringing upgrades to Miami and the races beyond. The April break gave the engineering team time to analyze data from the first three events and implement the early lessons into development priorities. A debut season's first upgrades are rarely transformational — the gaps between a new team and established operations are large and don't close overnight — but they represent the beginning of the process. For Cadillac, Miami is not about scoring their first point. It is about showing a development trajectory that gives the team — and its drivers — reason to believe the second half of 2026 will look different from the first.
