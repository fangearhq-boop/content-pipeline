Williams' "Slimming Program": How the Heaviest Car on the F1 Grid Is Fighting to Stay Relevant

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap | April 24, 2026

In Formula 1, every kilogram costs lap time. And Williams Racing's FW48 is reportedly carrying far more kilograms than any other car on the 2026 grid.

Multiple industry reports estimate that the FW48 sits somewhere between 26 and 30 kilograms above the FIA's 768kg minimum weight limit. To put that in perspective: Mercedes' W17 is understood to be approximately 0.4 kilograms over the minimum. Ferrari's SF-26 sits around 2 kilograms above. Williams' excess weight, if the estimates are accurate, is in a different category entirely — a structural penalty that has compounded the team's already difficult 2026 season before a wheel has been turned in anger.

How Did This Happen?

The weight problem traces back to the final phase of the FW48's development. Last-minute structural reinforcements — added in the weeks before the season began to address safety and integrity concerns — increased the car's mass beyond what the original design intended. It is a painful irony: doing the right thing from a safety standpoint created a technical burden the team has been carrying since Australia.

Former Formula 1 champion Ralf Schumacher was not subtle in his assessment, describing Williams as the "biggest flop of 2026." Team principal James Vowles has been more measured in his public communications, framing the team's April break work as a "slimming program" — a systematic effort to strip weight from the car without compromising the structural work already done. In a sport measured in hundredths of a second, shedding even a few kilograms can meaningfully change a car's braking performance, cornering balance, and tyre management.

Where Williams Stands

Williams has no championship points after three races, joining Aston Martin, Cadillac, and several other teams in a zero-point group that reflects how dramatically the 2026 regulations have reshuffled the competitive order. Last season, Williams showed genuine midfield competitiveness. This season, the FW48's weight has limited their ability to demonstrate the car's other strengths.

The broader context is important: 2026 is a regulation reset year, and teams that build on their development trajectories through the season tend to close gaps faster than the standings suggest. Williams' weight issue is real and impactful — but it is also a fixable technical problem, not an architectural failure. Vowles has built a competent technical organization. The slimming program is an engineering challenge, not a crisis.

What's Next

Miami represents Williams' best opportunity so far to demonstrate the effectiveness of their April break work. If the weight reduction program has made measurable progress, the FW48's other attributes — including its aerodynamic concept — may finally get a fair evaluation. If the excess weight remains as severe as early-season reports suggested, Vowles and his team face a long road to relevance in 2026. For a team that came into the year with genuine ambitions for the points zone, the next few weeks are critical.
