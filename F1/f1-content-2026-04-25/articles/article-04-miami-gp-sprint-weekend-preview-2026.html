Miami GP Sprint Weekend Preview: Can Anyone Stop Mercedes as F1 Returns to Florida?

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap | April 25, 2026

Formula 1 returns from its longest break of the season this weekend with a race that feels, in every meaningful sense, like the start of something new. The Miami Grand Prix — Round 5 of 2026, a Sprint weekend — arrives with the sport transformed by a five-week pause during which teams have torn their cars apart, rebuilt them, and are now preparing to find out whether their April work delivers on its ambition. If the first three races of the season felt like one chapter, Miami is the opening page of the next.

The championship stakes alone would make Miami significant. Kimi Antonelli leads the drivers' standings with 72 points. George Russell sits second with 63. Both drive for Mercedes, and their nine-point gap heading into Florida reflects a team with total dominance of the 2026 season so far — three races, three race wins, including two for the 19-year-old Antonelli who became the youngest championship leader in Formula 1 history after his back-to-back victories in China and Japan.

The Upgrade Arms Race

What makes Miami genuinely unpredictable is the volume and ambition of the upgrades arriving in Florida. Red Bull have brought what appears to be their most comprehensive single-event development package in years — a new floor, new sidepods, new front wing, and the Ferrari-inspired Macarena rear wing, all tested by Max Verstappen at Silverstone last week. Ferrari are introducing what their technical team describes as their biggest overhaul of the SF-26 to date, with roughly 50 per cent of the car's aerodynamic components revised and the full competitive debut of their own Macarena wing concept. McLaren, too, are bringing new components that they hope will extend the competitive momentum shown by Oscar Piastri in Japan.

Mercedes, for their part, are not standing still. The team that has set the benchmark with three consecutive victories will be watching the upgrade data from their rivals with careful attention, aware that the five-week pause may have given the field time to close some of the gap. Toto Wolff has consistently deflected expectations, and he is right to — competitive cycles in Formula 1 can shift dramatically, and no advantage is permanent.

New Rules, New Variables

Miami also marks the first race weekend under revised FIA technical regulations that take effect here. The super-clipping limit — the point at which drivers can harvest energy while at full throttle — has been raised from 250 kilowatts to 350 kilowatts. Separately, the energy harvesting allowance during qualifying has been reduced from 8 megajoules to 7 megajoules. These changes will affect every team, but their impact may not fall equally. Teams whose power unit and energy deployment strategies were built around the old numbers will face a recalibration period, and the Sprint format offers only limited time to optimise before the grid order is locked in for Sunday's race.

Free practice will be 90 minutes rather than the usual 60 — an extension the FIA approved to account for the length of the break and the volume of new components on the grid. Teams will need every minute of it.

What's Next

The Sprint weekend schedule begins May 1 with extended FP1, followed by Sprint Qualifying on Friday afternoon. The Sprint race itself takes place Saturday, with the main qualifying session Saturday evening and the Grand Prix on Sunday May 3 at 4 p.m. ET. For Antonelli, a strong weekend could put the championship well beyond Russell's reach. For Russell, Ferrari, Red Bull, and McLaren, Miami represents the first chance to establish that this title fight is not already decided. The answers begin in less than a week.
