Verstappen's Exit Clause, Lambiase's McLaren Move: Miami Has Never Mattered More for Red Bull

By Elena Voss | F1 Fanrecap | April 25, 2026

In the span of a few days during Formula 1's April break, the story around Red Bull Racing and Max Verstappen shifted from concerning to genuinely alarming. The team's four-time world champion sits ninth in the drivers' standings. His closest ally in the paddock — race engineer Gianpiero Lambiase, the man who has been alongside Verstappen for all four of his championship campaigns — has signed a contract to join McLaren. McLaren are now trying to take him early. And Dutch F1 insider Giedo van der Garde has issued the stunning prediction that Verstappen will leave Red Bull before Lambiase's 2028 departure date.

At the centre of it all is a contract clause that, according to multiple reports, could trigger a Verstappen exit from Red Bull as soon as this summer — if he is not sitting within the top two of the drivers' championship standings. He is currently ninth. The Miami Grand Prix on May 3 is the first race where the upgraded RB22 will have a genuine opportunity to demonstrate that the team's situation can be reversed.

The Lambiase Departure and Its Implications

When Gianpiero Lambiase's move to McLaren was announced, the Formula 1 paddock understood immediately what it meant. Lambiase has been Verstappen's race engineer since 2016 — a partnership spanning 71 Grand Prix victories and four world championships. Their relationship is closer than almost any driver-engineer pairing in the sport's modern history. Verstappen himself has said, more than once, that he would stop racing in Formula 1 when Lambiase leaves.

McLaren's Zak Brown confirmed during the break that his team are in talks with Red Bull about securing Lambiase's services ahead of his contracted 2028 start date. Whether those talks succeed or not, the message they send is clear: McLaren believe Lambiase's presence will make them a better team, and they are willing to test the boundaries of Red Bull's tolerance to get him sooner.

For Verstappen, the calculus is stark. His closest ally is leaving. His car is currently uncompetitive. His exit clause is reportedly approaching its activation point. And Miami — the first race after a five-week break and the first race with the team's biggest upgrade package of the season — is the event that will define which direction the story goes next.

The Championship Math

To return to the top two in the championship from ninth place, Verstappen would need a significant points swing over multiple races. Kimi Antonelli leads the standings with 72 points; Russell is second with 63. Verstappen has 12. That is a 51-point gap to the championship lead from a driver who has won four titles. The scale of the turnaround required is not impossible — championships have been swung by major performance shifts — but it requires the RB22 to become competitive, and quickly.

Van der Garde's prediction — that Verstappen leaves before Lambiase — has generated significant discussion in the Dutch media. Whether it reflects a settled plan or simply Verstappen's frustration with the current situation is unclear. What is clear is that the pressure on Red Bull heading into Miami is unlike anything the team has experienced in the modern era.

What's Next

The Miami Sprint weekend begins May 1. Verstappen will drive the upgraded RB22 in free practice for the first time in competitive conditions. The team, the paddock, and Verstappen himself will be watching every data point with unusual intensity. A strong result — even a podium — could change the narrative. A continuation of the struggles that have defined the first three races would bring the exit clause conversation from the background to the foreground. The sport's most successful driver in the current era is at a crossroads, and Miami is the first step on whichever road he ends up taking.
