Red Bull's Miami Macarena: Sweeping RB22 Upgrade Could Define Verstappen's Season

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap | April 25, 2026

When Laurent Mekies chose his words carefully at Red Bull's Silverstone filming day this week, he was not being diplomatic. He was being realistic. The team principal knows the RB22 has problems that cannot be papered over, and he knows that his team's upgrade package — while significant — is not a magic solution. "What we would like to see," Mekies said, "is to have a car where our drivers can push again." He was sure to add that Miami would not be a miracle.

The upgrade itself, however, is nothing short of sweeping. Max Verstappen drove a heavily revised RB22 at Silverstone on April 22, the team using their allocated filming day to trial a package that touches virtually every major aerodynamic surface. A new floor, redesigned sidepods with a more aggressively angled profile, a revised front wing, and — most significantly — a Ferrari-inspired "Macarena" rear wing have all been incorporated into the Miami specification. Technical analysts estimate the Macarena rear wing alone could unlock five to ten kilometres per hour of additional top speed on the straights at the Miami International Autodrome.

The Macarana Philosophy

The Macarena wing concept — so named for its resemblance to the arm movements in the famous dance — has been a talking point in the F1 paddock since Ferrari introduced their version earlier in the season. The rotating aerodynamic device is designed to reduce drag on straights while maintaining the downforce needed for cornering stability. Red Bull's version, tested at Silverstone, represents their interpretation of the same philosophy — and their hope that it can address one of the RB22's most persistent weaknesses: the car's tendency to be unstable at high speed.

The full package is designed to work in concert, with the sidepod redesign intended to improve airflow to the floor and rear diffuser, the new front wing providing better balance, and the Macarena rear wing reducing the drag penalty that has cost Verstappen and his team-mates time on straights throughout the first three races of the season.

Context: Three Races of Struggle

The scale of Red Bull's predicament cannot be overstated. After four consecutive constructors' championships — and four straight drivers' titles for Verstappen — the team entered 2026 with the new technical regulations and found themselves comprehensively outclassed. The RB22 earned the word "terrible" from its own driver, Isack Hadjar, who described the car's unpredictable handling as lurching between understeer and oversteer depending on the corner. Verstappen, diplomatic but clearly frustrated, failed to progress beyond Q2 in Suzuka — the first time since his rookie year in 2015 that he had missed Q3.

After three rounds, Red Bull sit sixth in the constructors' standings with 16 points. Mercedes have won every race. The gap is not just competitive; it is structural. Mekies acknowledged as much when he described the car as having "significant shortcomings" that the team needed to address at a fundamental level.

What's Next

Miami's Sprint weekend begins May 1, with the main race on May 3. The upgrade will face its first competitive test in free practice, and the team will be watching the data closely. If the RB22 shows meaningful improvement — if Verstappen is able to push the car and fight at the front of the midfield, or better — the mood in the team will change rapidly. If the car remains unpredictable despite the changes, the conversations around Verstappen's future, the team's direction, and the entire 2026 project will intensify. Red Bull cannot afford another Miami without answers.
