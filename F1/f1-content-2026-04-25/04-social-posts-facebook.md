### STORY 3: Ferrari's Major Miami SF-26 Overhaul

**Long-Form Post**
Window: 12:00 PM ET

Ferrari did not use the April break to rest. They used it to rebuild.

In the days since the Japanese Grand Prix, the team transformed a scheduled filming day at the Autodromo Nazionale Monza into a critical validation session for what is being described as the most comprehensive single-event upgrade the SF-26 will receive this season. Some analysts have characterised the Miami package as effectively redesigning around 50 per cent of the car — a headline figure that speaks to just how dramatically Ferrari believes they need to change direction after three races without a race win.

The key components of the upgrade include a revised and lighter floor, a new aerodynamic package, and the full competitive debut of the "Macarena" rear wing — a rotating device designed to reduce drag on straights while maintaining cornering stability, which Ferrari have been developing and testing for several months. Both Charles Leclerc and Lewis Hamilton reported positive sensations during their Monza runs, and the team's engineers were sufficiently encouraged by the data to confirm the package will be raced in Miami.

Ferrari enter Miami sitting 45 points behind Mercedes in the constructors' championship. Team principal Frédéric Vasseur has described Miami as a "reset" moment for the Scuderia. Whether that reset delivers on its promise will say a great deal about where this team's season is heading.

### STORY 4: Miami Sprint Weekend Preview

**Long-Form Post**
Window: 2:00 PM ET

Five weeks is a long time in Formula 1. When the lights went out in Suzuka at the end of March, Kimi Antonelli was the new championship leader — a 19-year-old with two wins from two races and a nine-point advantage over his Mercedes team-mate George Russell. When they light up again in Miami on Saturday May 3, the paddock could look completely different.

The Miami Grand Prix is a Sprint weekend, meaning teams get limited practice before the action begins in earnest. FP1 has been extended to 90 minutes this year to help teams adapt, and it becomes even more valuable given the volume of upgrades arriving in Florida. Red Bull have essentially rebuilt the RB22. Ferrari are debuting what could be their biggest mid-season upgrade package in years. McLaren have new components arriving too. The FIA's regulation changes — raising the super-clipping limit from 250 to 350 kilowatts and adjusting the energy recovery system — also take effect in Miami, adding another variable for every team to manage.

Mercedes have won all three races so far. Antonelli leads with 72 points, Russell sits second with 63. Can anyone interrupt the silver streak? Miami will begin to answer that question.

Reports suggest Williams are not alone in carrying excess weight — Mercedes are said to be marginally over the limit, and Ferrari a couple of kilograms above — but the gap between Williams and their rivals in this area is described as in a different category entirely. Miami represents the first opportunity to see whether the slimming program has made a meaningful difference.
