### STORY 4: FIA Thermal Loophole

#### Long-Form Post — 2:00 PM ET

One of the quietest and most significant stories of the 2026 F1 season is getting less attention than it deserves.

The FIA changed how it measures engine compression ratios mid-season — a decision that was quietly significant in what it acknowledged. The 2026 regulations set a maximum compression ratio of 16:1. The original rules required that measurement to be taken at ambient (cold) temperature only. The allegation from rival engine manufacturers: certain teams designed components that thermally expand when hot, effectively raising the on-track compression ratio beyond the intended limit while still passing cold static tests.

Mercedes and Red Bull were among the teams flagged by rivals.

The FIA's response was to introduce a two-temperature measurement protocol from June 1, 2026: measurements are now taken at both ambient temperature and 130°C. This change amounts to an acknowledgment that the original measurement method was insufficient to enforce the rule as intended.

Here's where it gets complicated: no retroactive penalties were issued for the first part of the season, when the old measurement protocol was still in place. If the loophole existed and certain manufacturers benefited, those championship points are not going to be removed.

Whether that's the right call is something reasonable people can disagree about. But the controversy will resurface anytime the affected teams perform well in the second half of the season.
