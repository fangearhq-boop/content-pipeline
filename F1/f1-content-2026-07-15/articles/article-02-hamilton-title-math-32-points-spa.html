Hamilton Is 32 Points Behind With Spa Ahead — History Says Don't Count Him Out

By Ryan Calloway | July 15, 2026

At 41 years old, the Ferrari driver is chasing his eighth championship — and heading into a circuit where he has been nearly unbeatable over the course of his career.

The numbers are easy to recite. Lewis Hamilton is 41 years old, 32 points behind the championship leader, driving in what could be the final title campaign of his career. Taken in isolation, those facts sound like a closing chapter.

What they do not convey is where he is going this weekend.

Spa-Francorchamps — the circuit that winds through the Ardennes forest in Belgium — has been one of Lewis Hamilton's best tracks over the course of a career that has accumulated more wins than anyone in Formula 1 history. When Hamilton arrives at a circuit where his instincts and the car's characteristics align, the outcome tends to be predictable in a way that makes the opposition uncomfortable. This weekend could be one of those weekends.

The Championship Math

Kimi Antonelli has 179 points. George Russell has 154. Hamilton has 147. The 32-point gap to first place sounds significant. In a season where the top three are separated by that amount with dozens of races remaining, it is not a deficit that requires any special circumstances to overcome — it requires a few good weekends combined with the kind of mechanical fortune that has not always gone Hamilton's way this year.

Antonelli's Silverstone weekend illustrated the brittleness that can exist even at the top of the standings. The young Mercedes driver led the British Grand Prix in commanding fashion before a wheel shield failure compromised his race and dropped him to P11 with a subsequent penalty. He went from leading the championship by a comfortable margin to absorbing a painful zero when he had done nothing wrong. These things happen in Formula 1. They can happen again.

Russell, meanwhile, has been the picture of consistency — fewer dramatic moments in either direction, which has kept him within touching distance of first without ever appearing to be in direct contention for victory on any given Sunday. That is a double-edged position: safe, but not winning.

What Hamilton Has That the Others Don't

The straightforward answer is seven world championships. That number represents a specific kind of knowledge about how to navigate a title fight — when to push, when to manage, when the team needs something from its driver that goes beyond raw speed. Antonelli is exceptional. He has also never been in this position before. Hamilton has been here many times.

Ferrari's Leclerc won at Silverstone. Hamilton finished third. The car is clearly capable of victories. The question is whether Hamilton can produce the kind of consistent, race-winning weekends that make a 32-point gap close to irrelevant by the time the season reaches its climax.

He is capable of it. This weekend at Spa is the place to start.

What's Next

Qualifying at Spa on Saturday will be a telling session — it is where championships begin to reveal themselves. A Hamilton pole position or front-row start would immediately change the tone of the weekend and the conversation around his title bid. A win on Sunday would make the next eight weeks of summer break a significantly different experience for everyone above him in the standings.
