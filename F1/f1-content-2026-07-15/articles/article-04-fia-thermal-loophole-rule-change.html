The FIA Changed F1's Engine Rules Mid-Season — Here's What the Thermal Loophole Story Really Means

By Elena Voss | July 15, 2026

A quiet regulation change to how compression ratios are measured has put a spotlight on one of the most technically complex controversies of the 2026 season — and the questions it raises have not been fully answered.

There is a story in Formula 1 this season that has not received the attention it deserves. The FIA changed how it measures engine compression ratios in the middle of the championship, in response to allegations from rival manufacturers that a loophole had been exploited during the first half of the year.

Understanding why that matters requires a brief explanation of how the 2026 power unit regulations work — and where the gap between the rule as written and the rule as intended turned out to be significant.

The Technical Issue

The 2026 regulations set a maximum compression ratio of 16:1 — a limit on how much the fuel-air mixture inside the engine's cylinders can be compressed before ignition. Higher compression ratios generally produce more power, which is why the regulation exists. The original rules required that measurement to be taken at ambient, or cold, temperature.

The allegation raised by rival engine manufacturers is that certain teams designed engine components that thermally expand when the engine reaches operating temperature, effectively raising the compression ratio above the 16:1 limit once the car is actually running at speed — while still technically passing cold static tests that the regulation required. In plain terms: the engine passes the test at room temperature, then operates differently on the track.

Mercedes and Red Bull were among the manufacturers flagged by rivals as potentially benefiting from this situation.

The FIA's Response

The governing body introduced a two-temperature measurement protocol from June 1, 2026. Engines are now measured at both ambient temperature and at 130°C — a much closer approximation of real operating conditions. The change effectively closes the measurement gap that rivals alleged was being exploited.

By making that change, the FIA implicitly acknowledged that the original single-temperature test was insufficient to enforce the rule as the regulation's authors intended. That is a significant admission, even if it was delivered quietly and without drama.

What the FIA did not do is issue retroactive penalties for the first part of the season, when the old measurement protocol was in place. Championship points earned during that period remain on the books.

What's Next

The controversy will not fully resolve itself with the rule change. If Mercedes or Red Bull continue to perform well in the second half of the season — particularly Mercedes, who lead the constructors' standings — the thermal loophole question will resurface as context for that performance. Whether the advantage was real, how large it was, and whether the FIA should have acted sooner are debates that will run in the background of this championship for some time.

What the mid-season change does guarantee is that the second half of the year is being contested on a more level playing field than the first. In a three-way title fight this close, that detail matters.
