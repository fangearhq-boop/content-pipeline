Ferrari vs McLaren: Miami Is an Upgrade War With Championship Stakes

By Elena Voss | F1 Fanrecap

April 29, 2026

McLaren team principal Andrea Stella did not mince words when describing what his team is bringing to Miami. "A completely new car from aerodynamic standpoint," he said — a description that would be extraordinary in any other context, but which, in the compressed development war of the 2026 Formula 1 season, feels perfectly logical. Ferrari, for their part, are debuting the full SF-26 specification for the first time: the Macarena rear wing, a comprehensively revised floor, and heavily updated energy management software, with roughly 50 percent of the car changed since the Japanese Grand Prix.

Both teams targeted Miami from the moment the 2026 calendar was confirmed. The five-week gap created by the cancellation of Bahrain and Saudi Arabia gave their engineering departments even more time to develop, test, and prepare. Ferrari used a Monza filming day specifically to validate Miami-bound upgrades. The results, by Ferrari's own account, were positive.

Pundit and former F1 driver Jolyon Palmer captured the moment perfectly by calling it an "upgrade-off." And that's exactly what it is — two of the biggest teams in the sport arriving at the same race with entirely new cars, each believing their package is the one that will close the gap to Mercedes.

What Each Team Is Bringing

Ferrari's full SF-26 is centered on the Macarena wing — a distinctive aerodynamic concept that attracted attention from rival teams when it was first photographed during testing. Combined with the revised floor and updated electrical software that governs the car's hybrid power deployment, Ferrari believe they have addressed the fundamental performance gaps that left them second-best to Mercedes through the opening three rounds. Charles Leclerc and Lewis Hamilton will be the first to know whether those gains are real, within minutes of FP1 beginning on Friday.

McLaren's package is broader still. Stella's "completely new car" framing reflects the scale of aerodynamic changes that the Woking team believes are necessary to compete with Mercedes' benchmark. After a catastrophic double DNS in China — both Norris and Piastri failing to start from separate power unit failures — McLaren need a performance statement. Their 56 constructors' points trail Mercedes by 79. Every point now matters enormously.

Mercedes Cannot Stand Still

With a 135-point constructors' lead and back-to-back wins for Kimi Antonelli in China and Japan, it would be easy for Mercedes to arrive at Miami in a comfortable position. But Formula 1 history is littered with dominant teams that allowed rivals to catch them through complacency. Mercedes will not be doing that. They are expected to bring their own developments to Miami, and Kimi Antonelli's nine-point championship lead over George Russell gives the Silver Arrows internal motivation as well.

The interesting question is whether Mercedes has a performance ceiling that Ferrari and McLaren's upgrade packages can reach — or whether they still have development headroom of their own that will keep them ahead regardless of the upgrade contest.

What's Next

Free Practice 1 on Friday is the moment of truth for everyone. Within the 90-minute session — the only traditional practice available in a Sprint weekend — teams will run their upgrade packages for the first time in competitive conditions and see whether the simulator data matches reality. By Friday evening, when Sprint Qualifying begins, the engineers will have made critical decisions that shape the entire weekend.

If Ferrari or McLaren emerges from Miami as the fastest package, the 2026 Constructors' Championship becomes a genuine three-way battle. If Mercedes maintains its advantage, the rest of the field faces another long wait for reinforcements. The upgrade war starts Friday. The results will tell us who won it.
