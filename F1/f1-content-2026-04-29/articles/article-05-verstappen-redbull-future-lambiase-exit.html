After Lambiase's Departure, Verstappen's Red Bull Future Hangs in the Balance

By Elena Voss | F1 Fanrecap

April 29, 2026

There is a specific kind of professional relationship in Formula 1 that goes beyond engineering — where the person on the pit wall and the driver in the car develop an almost intuitive understanding of each other's needs, instincts, and limits. Max Verstappen and Gianpiero Lambiase had that relationship. Built over ten years at two different teams, tested through four world championships and dozens of high-pressure moments, it was one of the defining working partnerships in recent F1 history.

And now it ends — at the conclusion of the 2027 season, when Lambiase leaves Red Bull for McLaren's Chief Racing Officer role. The confirmation came Tuesday, and while it was framed diplomatically by everyone involved, the implications for Verstappen's own Red Bull future are impossible to ignore.

A Partnership That Won Four Titles

Lambiase joined Verstappen's programme when Max was still a teenager at Toro Rosso in 2016. Their relationship matured as Verstappen's career accelerated — through near-misses in 2021, the dramatic Abu Dhabi championship finale, and then three further dominant title campaigns. Whatever the pressure, Lambiase was the voice in Max's ear guiding him through it. Their radio communications — sometimes heated, sometimes humorous, always purposeful — became a signature element of the Red Bull years.

Verstappen's comment on the news — "You would be stupid not to do that" — reveals the character of their relationship. There is no bitterness, no public frustration. Max blessed the move and means it. But that maturity does not prevent observers from reading into what it signals about his own state of mind. Lambiase has already begun planning his post-Red Bull chapter. That timeline — end of 2027 — sits at the same horizon as questions about Verstappen's own contract situation and reported exit clause.

Red Bull's Deteriorating Position

Red Bull is fourth in the Constructors' Championship after three races with 16 points. They have not finished on the podium. Verstappen has publicly described the car as having "fundamentally something wrong" with it — language that leaves no room for diplomatic interpretation. The team that produced the most dominant season in Formula 1 history just three years ago is currently trailing not just Mercedes, Ferrari, and McLaren, but sitting within range of midfield teams.

Team boss Laurent Mekies has been vocal about the intensity of Red Bull's efforts to recover. "The campus is on fire," he said this week — a phrase designed to convey urgency and commitment, and one that probably reflects reality. Red Bull's technical infrastructure remains formidable, and the team has the resources to fight their way back to competitiveness. But fighting their way back while also navigating Verstappen's contract situation, the Lambiase departure, and the loss of institutional knowledge that comes with it, represents an exceptionally complex challenge.

What's Next

Miami is the immediate focus for Verstappen and Red Bull — a Sprint weekend that offers six championship points from the Sprint Race alone, in addition to the Grand Prix itself. Every point matters when you are fourth in the Constructors' standings and trying to rebuild credibility after a difficult start to the season. Red Bull will bring their own upgrades to Florida, and they desperately need to show they can compete with the front runners.

The longer question — whether Verstappen activates his exit clause, and whether losing Lambiase accelerates that thinking — will not be answered in Miami. But it will be the storyline that shadows the entire second half of the 2026 season, growing louder with every race where Red Bull fails to reach the top step. Max has been loyal. Max has been patient. How much longer that patience holds is the central question in Formula 1 right now.
