Antonelli's Title Lead at 25 Points — Belgian GP Is His Biggest Test Yet

By Marcus Cole July 9, 2026

The Gap That Changed at Silverstone

Forty points became 25 in a single Sunday. George Russell crossed the line in second at Silverstone while Kimi Antonelli finished in 16th, and a championship lead that had looked like a comfortable buffer entering the British Grand Prix looked considerably less so by the time the results were final. The standings now read Antonelli 179 points, Russell 154, Hamilton 147. A race that was supposed to be another step toward the title turned into an alarm.

The Belgian Grand Prix runs July 17 through 19, with practice opening Thursday, qualifying Saturday, and the race Sunday. Spa-Francorchamps is the longest circuit on the 2026 calendar at 7.004 kilometers. It is a circuit that has a way of sorting out where teams actually stand, and right now the question of where Antonelli's team actually stands is more open than it has been at any point this season.

What Spa Demands

Spa-Francorchamps is not a circuit that rewards half-measures. The Kemmel Straight and the long run through Raidillon put a premium on power-unit output and aerodynamic efficiency at speed. The Bus Stop chicane demands braking precision and the ability to carry momentum through a technical section that has ended championship hopes for drivers who got it wrong. Spa's elevation changes and its full-circuit nature mean that a car with a weakness in any one area will be exposed somewhere on the lap.

The timing matters here. The FIA has officially confirmed a mid-season rule change that shifts compression ratio enforcement from cold ambient temperatures to hot operating conditions of 130 degrees Celsius. That change closes an exploit that was alleged to have given Mercedes and Red Bull a performance advantage estimated at approximately 0.3 seconds per lap. The full effect of that correction on the competitive order at Spa is not yet known, but it is a significant variable heading into the weekend.

Ferrari's Momentum

Charles Leclerc won at Silverstone. That result was more than a points swing for Ferrari. It confirmed that the team has found performance in the second half of the season and that Leclerc, sitting fourth in the championship with 108 points, is capable of taking race victories when the car is working. Ferrari has traditionally been competitive at Spa, a circuit that rewards the kind of high-downforce, power-sensitive setup that the team has historically executed well.

The combination of Leclerc's form and Ferrari's Spa history makes this a weekend where the team can realistically target a top-two finish. Whether that comes at Antonelli's direct expense, or at the expense of Russell who is currently Antonelli's closest challenger, determines a lot about how the championship picture looks when the cars leave Belgium.

Russell's Trajectory

Russell's charge through the second half of the season has been the defining story of the title fight. The Silverstone result was not an isolated performance. Russell has been consistently quicker than his championship position suggested he should be, and the gap between him and Antonelli reflects Antonelli's earlier-season points accumulation more than it reflects the current pace comparison between the two drivers.

At a circuit like Spa, where track position matters and weather creates uncertainty, a driver in form who does not need to manage a championship lead has a different kind of freedom than a leader who needs to protect points. Russell goes to Belgium with momentum and nothing to lose. Antonelli goes to Belgium needing to arrest a run of form that has seen his lead cut in half.

The Weather Factor

Spa is notorious for conditions that change within a single lap. Rain can be falling at Raidillon while the sun is out at the Bus Stop. Mixed conditions through qualifying and the race have decided Belgian Grands Prix before, and there is no reason to expect July 2026 to be different. Weather forecasts are of limited use three days before qualifying begins, but the circuit's record makes it a variable that every team's strategists will be building contingency plans around from the moment the cars arrive Thursday.

For Antonelli, unpredictable conditions present both risk and opportunity. A wet race at Spa rewards composure and technical judgment as much as outright pace. If conditions mix the order up, the championship leader needs to be the driver who makes the right calls, not the one who is caught out by a strategy decision made half a second too late.

What's Next

The Belgian GP is also significant in the calendar context. It is the final race before the Hungarian Grand Prix (July 24-26), where Max Verstappen's Red Bull exit clause is expected to trigger mathematically. The driver market story that has dominated headlines will likely reach its conclusion between now and the race in Hungary. The paddock heading into the Belgian GP weekend carries more storylines than usual, and the championship situation ensures that all of them intersect.

Antonelli heads to Spa with a 25-point lead that needs to be protected, against a field that has found form at a circuit where nothing about the result is guaranteed in advance. Practice begins Thursday. Everything changes from there.
