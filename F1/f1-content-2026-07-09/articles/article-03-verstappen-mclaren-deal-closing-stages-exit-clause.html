Verstappen to McLaren: 'Closing Stages' as Exit Clause Mathematically Nears

By Ryan Calloway July 9, 2026

Where Things Stand

Max Verstappen's move to McLaren is being described as in the "closing stages" of what is believed to be an initial three-year deal. The characterization comes from multiple sources tracking what has been described as a lengthy negotiation process. Verstappen sits seventh in the 2026 championship with 76 points, a product of a season that has included four retirements. The most recent came at Silverstone with a DRS fault, the same issue that struck at the Austrian Grand Prix.

The mathematical reality of his Red Bull contract is now central to the story. Verstappen holds a contractual exit clause that triggers if he cannot reach the top two in the standings before a specified point. The Hungarian Grand Prix runs July 24-26. With 76 points and the standings where they are, Verstappen cannot reach the top two before that race. The clause triggers.

The Exit Clause and What It Means

Exit clauses of this type are written into contracts precisely because drivers and teams want protection against scenarios that make continuing the arrangement unworkable. Verstappen negotiated his clause in the context of his competitive expectations. A season of four retirements and seventh place is not that context.

The Belgian Grand Prix runs July 19, one race before the Hungarian GP. That leaves one race weekend in which events might theoretically move, though the mathematics are no longer favorable. The clause triggering is not the same as a transfer being confirmed. It creates the legal framework for one to happen. The deal being described as in closing stages suggests the parties have moved well past the framework question and into the terms of an agreement.

The McLaren Angle

Zak Brown, McLaren's CEO, has publicly and repeatedly denied any conversations with Verstappen. That is a standard position for a team president during an active transfer of this magnitude, and it is not an unusual one. Teams routinely maintain that posture through the final phases of deals precisely because confirming discussions changes the dynamic with the player's current employer and with other parties in negotiation.

The deal being described as an initial three years is meaningful. A shorter arrangement might suggest a team taking a calculated risk on a driver at a difficult moment in his career. Three years signals confidence that Verstappen at McLaren is a long-term project, not a bridge arrangement. McLaren, which has built genuine competitiveness over recent seasons, would be pairing that infrastructure with one of the most recognized names in the sport.

The Piastri Question

The most widely reported resolution to the driver shuffling involves Oscar Piastri moving from McLaren to Red Bull in a straight swap, partnering Isack Hadjar. Piastri, who signed with McLaren out of the Renault/Alpine junior program, would be moving to a team in the midst of significant transition. Red Bull without Verstappen and without Adrian Newey in an active design role is a different proposition than the team that dominated the sport in recent years.

Piastri at Red Bull alongside Hadjar would be Red Bull's attempt to rebuild around a new generation of drivers in what amounts to a reset period. The question of who leads the technical development effort and what the car looks like in 2027 and beyond shapes how attractive that reset looks from the outside.

Ralf Schumacher's Assessment

Former Formula 1 driver Ralf Schumacher addressed the situation July 9 with directness. His characterization of Verstappen and Red Bull as "on the brink of divorce" with "no going back" was not hedged. Schumacher, who has followed the sport closely since his own racing career and has commented on personnel and team dynamics for years, was stating what multiple sources have been circling around for weeks.

The language of "no going back" is particularly pointed. It acknowledges that even if a deal were not finalized, the relationship between Verstappen and Red Bull has reached a point where resuming it as it was structured before is not a realistic outcome. The exit clause is a legal mechanism. The brink of divorce is a description of the dynamic that has made it necessary.

What's Next

The Belgian GP runs July 19. The Hungarian GP runs July 24-26. If the exit clause triggers as the mathematics suggest it will, the formal announcement of whatever deal has been reached would follow. The calendar creates its own pressure on the timeline. Watch the period between Belgian GP qualifying and the start of the Hungarian GP weekend for movement. The story that has been building through the first half of the season is close to its conclusion.
