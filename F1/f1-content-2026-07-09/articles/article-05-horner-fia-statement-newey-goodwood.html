FIA President on Horner: 'He Will Get Back' — The Statement That Changes the Conversation

By Elena Voss July 9, 2026

What Ben Sulayem Said

FIA president Mohammed Ben Sulayem addressed Christian Horner's future in Formula 1 on July 8 with a directness that stands apart from the usual language of governing body statements. "He will get back," Ben Sulayem said. He added: "Did he deliver? Do you want to win or not?"

That is not a neutral framing. It is an explicit argument — that Horner's track record at Red Bull during his tenure is the relevant measure of his value to the sport, and that any team motivated by winning should arrive at the same conclusion. The FIA president is not a team principal. He does not hire team principals. But his public endorsement of a figure's return to the paddock carries a kind of institutional legitimacy that matters in a sport where the governing body's relationship with team leadership is a constant variable.

Horner at Silverstone

Horner appeared at the British Grand Prix last weekend as a guest of the FIA and FOM. It was his first paddock appearance since his departure from Red Bull. The nature of that appearance, hosted by the two entities that govern and commercially manage Formula 1, carried its own signal. This was not a discreet social visit. It was a visible return to a space he occupied for decades.

Horner built Red Bull from a midfield team into one of the most successful operations in the sport's history. The championship record across his tenure is not a matter of debate. Whether the circumstances of his departure change the calculus for potential future employers is a different question, but Ben Sulayem's framing points directly at the record and asks teams to evaluate on that basis.

What Horner Has Said About a Return

Horner's public position on a return to Formula 1 has been consistent: he would only come back for a team he believes can win. That condition narrows the realistic options considerably. Not every team on the grid is in a position to credibly pitch a championship-winning trajectory to a team principal of Horner's standard. The teams that can make that case are a shorter list, and those are the conversations that would determine whether Ben Sulayem's prediction is right.

The timing of Ben Sulayem's statement, coming while driver market activity around Red Bull is at its most turbulent, adds another layer of context. Red Bull is in the process of losing Verstappen. Newey is already at Aston Martin. The team is navigating a transition period. A team principal of Horner's caliber returning to the sport does not necessarily mean returning to Red Bull, but the coincidence of the timing is visible.

Newey at Goodwood

Adrian Newey is making a different kind of appearance this week. The Goodwood Festival of Speed runs July 10-13, and Newey — who joined Aston Martin after leaving Red Bull — will drive a Red Bull car described as his "parting gift" project making its public running debut at the event. Newey is 67. The car he will drive is one he designed before his departure, a project he completed as a farewell gesture to the team where he spent decades shaping championship-winning machinery.

Driving a Red Bull he designed at a public automotive event is a symbolic act. It is not a return to Red Bull, and it is not a reversal of his move to Aston Martin. It is a conclusion to one chapter, executed in front of an audience. Goodwood is an appropriate setting — a gathering that celebrates automotive history and the people who made it, where a farewell drive carries exactly the weight it is meant to.

What's Next

Horner's return to Formula 1 is now a question of which team and when, not whether it is possible. Ben Sulayem's statement will have been heard by every team principal and ownership group in the paddock. Horner's own condition — a team that can win — frames what conversations are worth having.

For Newey, Goodwood closes one chapter. His design work at Aston Martin, which Alonso has described as producing the forthcoming Hungarian GP upgrade package, is the next chapter already in progress. The two men whose names defined Red Bull's dominant era are each moving forward. One of them may be heading back into the sport in a formal capacity sooner than most expected.
