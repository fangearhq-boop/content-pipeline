Five Days to Fix Formula 1: The FIA April 9 Summit Explained

By Elena Voss | F1 Fanrecap | April 4, 2026

In five days, Formula 1's most consequential off-track meeting of the 2026 season will determine whether the sport addresses its biggest safety crisis in years — or defers it until something worse happens.

The FIA has convened a technical summit for April 9, bringing together team technical directors, power unit manufacturers, and FIA representatives to address the "super clipping" phenomenon that produced Oliver Bearman's 50G crash at the Japanese Grand Prix. The trigger event — a 50 km/h closing speed differential created when Bearman's car and Franco Colapinto's Alpine were operating in opposing energy management modes — has exposed a structural flaw in the 2026 power unit regulations. Five weeks remain before the Miami Grand Prix begins the racing calendar again. This meeting is supposed to produce the fix.

What Is "Super Clipping" and Why Does It Matter?

The 2026 Formula 1 power units introduced a 50-50 split between combustion and electric power, creating an entirely new energy management paradigm. "Super clipping" describes the dangerous speed differential that occurs when one car is in full harvest mode — recovering energy and effectively experiencing a form of engine braking — while a following or adjacent car is deploying full electric power. The result is a sudden, unpredictable closing speed that neither driver can anticipate and no warning system currently addresses.

Bearman experienced this phenomenon at Suzuka's Spoon Curve, where Colapinto's energy management state created an effective 50 km/h speed difference. The impact registered at 50G — a force severe enough to destroy a car and, under slightly different circumstances, injure or kill a driver. The FIA's acknowledgment that the system created the dangerous closing speed is implicit in the meeting itself being called.

The Five-Point Agenda

The summit's agenda covers five areas: modifications to the super clipping regulations; closing speed warning systems for drivers; qualifying energy management thresholds; potential adjustments to 2027 power unit ratios; and real-time deployment notifications that would alert drivers to the energy state of nearby cars. Each item represents a different layer of the same underlying problem — an energy management system with more potential failure modes than anticipated.

The most immediately actionable item is the closing speed warning system — a relatively straightforward technical addition that could alert drivers before they encounter a super clipping situation. The more structural changes (ratio adjustments, qualification thresholds) require deeper regulatory work and are less likely to reach Miami implementation.

The Unanimous Agreement Problem

Any change implemented before Miami requires unanimous agreement from all ten teams. This is the most significant obstacle the meeting faces. Formula 1 teams are competitive organizations with conflicting interests — any regulatory change that disadvantages a team's power unit configuration will face resistance, regardless of safety rationale. Lewis Hamilton has already expressed frustration with the process: "Drivers have no power with the FIA." Carlos Sainz, representing the Grand Prix Drivers' Association, has pushed for pre-Miami fixes, but the diplomatic reality of unanimous consent remains the primary obstacle.

The most realistic outcome is a partial fix: warning systems and threshold adjustments that don't require fundamental regulatory changes and can be agreed on quickly. The deeper structural modifications are more likely to appear in the 2027 regulations than the 2026 calendar.

What's Next

The April 9 summit takes place the Friday before Miami preparations begin. Watch for official FIA statements in the days following the meeting — any pre-Miami regulatory adjustments will be announced publicly. If the meeting produces meaningful changes, look for them to appear in the Miami sporting and technical regulations. If it doesn't, the 2026 season will continue with the same super clipping risks that produced Japan's crash. The drivers know it. The teams know it. April 9 is where the sport decides what it's going to do about it.
