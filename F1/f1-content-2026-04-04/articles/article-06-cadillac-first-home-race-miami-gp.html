Cadillac F1's First Home Race: Miami Is the Biggest Test Yet for the American Team

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap | April 4, 2026

When Formula 1 descends on the Hard Rock Stadium complex in Miami Gardens this May, one team will be racing at home for the very first time.

Cadillac F1 — the American team backed by General Motors that made its debut in 2026 — arrives at the Miami Grand Prix carrying the full weight of expectation that comes with a home race. Sergio Perez and Valtteri Bottas will race in front of a crowd that represents the fastest-growing F1 fan base in the sport's history. The team has scored zero constructors' points through the opening three rounds. Miami is the chance to begin writing a different story.

The Drivers and the Context

Cadillac's pairing of Perez and Bottas brings extraordinary Formula 1 experience to a young team still learning how to operate at the front of the grid. Between them, the two veterans have accumulated hundreds of race starts and multiple Grand Prix victories — experience that is invaluable when a team is navigating the technical and operational complexity of their debut season.

Perez, who spent several seasons at Red Bull Racing and has raced at the Miami circuit multiple times during his career, carries specific circuit knowledge that the team will lean on heavily. His familiarity with the Hard Rock Stadium layout's demands — the balance between braking stability and traction out of the slow corners, the management of the three DRS zones, the tire strategies that favor the circuit's particular surface — is an asset that only comes from race experience.

Bottas, meanwhile, brings championship-level race craft and technical feedback skills that have served teams well throughout a career that includes race wins in multiple countries. His ability to develop a car in real time and communicate setup changes clearly is exactly what a first-year team needs when they arrive at their home race under maximum external scrutiny.

The Upgrade and the Expectations

Cadillac is bringing a significant aerodynamic upgrade package to Miami — described by those close to the team as the most substantial development step since the car's launch. Perez has described the Miami weekend as "the biggest test for the team," reflecting both the upgrade's significance and the elevated stakes of racing at home.

The team has shown incremental progress through the opening rounds. Perez completed the Japanese Grand Prix on the lead lap for the first time in his Cadillac career — a modest milestone by traditional standards, but meaningful for a team building toward consistent points-scoring. The upgrade at Miami is intended to accelerate that progress curve significantly.

What a Home Race Means

Formula 1's growth in the United States has been one of the sport's defining commercial narratives over the past five years. The addition of Cadillac — with its General Motors backing, American workforce, and Indianapolis base — represents the ultimate expression of that growth. A home Grand Prix is the moment that narrative becomes emotional rather than commercial.

American fans who have followed the team's formation, factory construction, and debut season will fill the Hard Rock Stadium grandstands wearing Cadillac livery colors. The atmosphere will be unlike anything the team has experienced in their three previous races. Whether or not they score points, Miami 2026 is the race that defines Cadillac F1's first chapter.

What's Next

Watch Cadillac's qualifying pace data as the first indicator of whether the Miami upgrade has delivered its intended performance step. Any improvement that moves them significantly closer to the midfield — where points are regularly available — would represent meaningful progress. A points finish at their home race would be one of the most celebrated results of the 2026 season for American F1 fans.
