Red Bull's Miami Reality: Mekies Says No Miracle — But the Three-Stage Plan Is Underway

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap | April 4, 2026

Laurent Mekies is delivering a message Formula 1 fans rarely hear from a team principal: honest expectations.

The Red Bull Racing team principal confirmed this week that while the team is targeting meaningful improvements for the Miami Grand Prix (May 1-3), no "miracle turnaround" should be expected. After three rounds that have produced just 16 constructors' points — tied for sixth with Alpine — and a season that has Max Verstappen ninth in the drivers' championship with 12 points, Red Bull is in territory that would have seemed impossible 18 months ago.

The five-week break before Miami is the team's window. They are not shutting down the factory. The development program is running at full intensity. And a structured three-stage plan is in place to restore the RB22 to something approaching competitive performance.

Understanding the Three-Stage Plan

The plan addresses the RB22's problems in sequence, targeting the most tractable issues first. Stage one, scheduled for Miami, involves aerodynamic floor and fence revisions designed to reduce the understeer that has plagued Verstappen's ability to rotate the car through slow corners. Stage two, also targeting Miami, revises the power unit deployment maps — addressing the MGU-H management issue that Mekies has identified as a significant factor in Verstappen's formation lap problems across all three opening rounds. Stage three, planned for Imola, is a full aerodynamic overhaul.

The MGU-H issue is particularly significant. The removal of the MGU-H from the 2026 power unit regulations placed new demands on turbo management during formation laps, and Red Bull's deployment sequences have been calibrated incorrectly — Verstappen has reported getting the procedure wrong in each of his three races. Revised maps should address the most immediate drivability problem, even if they don't solve the underlying performance deficit.

The Hadjar Factor

Verstappen's situation is made more uncomfortable by the performance of his teammate. Isack Hadjar, a 21-year-old in his first Formula 1 season, has out-qualified the four-time world champion multiple times through the opening rounds. The data point matters not because it reflects poorly on Verstappen's driving — the car is clearly problematic — but because it illustrates how far the RB22 falls short of the performance expectations that produced four consecutive championship seasons.

Verstappen has not suddenly become a worse driver. The same talent that produced 54 race wins and four titles in three years is still present. What has changed is the car around him — a completely new technical platform that Red Bull, for all their engineering excellence, has not yet cracked. When they do, Verstappen's performance relative to the field should recover substantially.

Staying at Red Bull

Amid months of retirement and exit speculation, Verstappen is expected to remain at Red Bull through 2026. Per reports, the exit clause in his contract was specifically tied to the 2026 regulations — with the FIA acknowledging the regulations need modification and scheduling the April 9 summit to address them, the immediate trigger conditions for any exit have softened. The bigger question is what 2027 looks like, and whether Red Bull can rebuild their technical advantage before the next generation of regulations rewrites the competitive order again.

What's Next

Miami is the first real test of Red Bull's recovery. Watch for the RB22's qualifying lap time relative to the Mercedes and Ferrari. If the floor revisions produce measurable improvement in slow-corner rotation, and the deployment map changes eliminate the formation lap problem, the team will have made meaningful progress. A single race does not reverse a three-race deficit. But it can establish the trajectory — and right now, an upward trajectory is all Red Bull is asking for.
