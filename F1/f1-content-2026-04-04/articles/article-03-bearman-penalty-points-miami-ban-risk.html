Bearman Is Two Penalty Points From a Miami Race Ban — And There's No Buffer

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap | April 4, 2026

Oliver Bearman will arrive in Miami with 10 FIA penalty points on his license — two short of the threshold that triggers an automatic one-race ban. More pressingly, his earliest point expiry doesn't come until May 23, after the Miami Grand Prix concludes. That leaves the Haas driver with absolutely no margin for error across the entire Miami weekend: free practice, qualifying, the Sprint session, and the race itself.

The irony of the situation is hard to ignore. Bearman accumulated the bulk of these points from the Japanese Grand Prix crash — an incident the FIA has since acknowledged was caused by a regulatory flaw in the 2026 energy management system, not by driver error. The "super clipping" phenomenon that created a 50 km/h closing speed differential at Suzuka's Spoon Curve was the system working as designed. Bearman was penalized for being in the wrong place when that design produced a dangerous outcome.

The Penalty Points System Explained

The FIA penalty points system was designed to track driver behavior across a 12-month rolling window. Points are assigned for driving infractions — dangerous driving, ignoring marshals' flags, causing avoidable collisions — and accumulate until they reach 12, at which point a one-race ban is automatically triggered. Points expire 12 months after they are issued.

For Bearman, the critical expiry dates create an uncomfortable window. Two points from his Monaco 2025 outing expire on May 23, 2026 — after Miami's race weekend, which concludes May 3. A further four points from the British Grand Prix in 2025 expire on July 5, 2026. Until those two dates pass, Bearman sits at 10 points with only two to spare.

Haas's Approach and Miami Confirmation

Haas has confirmed that Bearman will race in Miami barring any additional incidents. The team is well aware of his points total and has presumably briefed him on the need for clean weekends between now and Miami. One avoidable contact incident, one safety car infraction, one yellow flag violation — any of these, if penalized, could tip him into ban territory.

The practical burden this places on Bearman in Miami is significant. A 21-year-old driver in his debut Formula 1 full season will be navigating not just the competitive pressures of a complex street circuit in heat, but the psychological awareness that any minor judgment error carries consequences far beyond a five-second time penalty. Managing that mental load without overdriving or under-driving requires a level of composure that even experienced drivers find difficult.

The Broader Regulatory Conversation

Bearman's situation points to a structural issue with how the penalty points system interacts with the new 2026 regulations. If the super clipping phenomenon is a regulatory flaw — as the FIA's own April 9 summit acknowledges — then drivers caught in its consequences arguably should not be carrying license points for it. The GPDA and several drivers have raised questions about the fairness of the current penalty allocation, but any change to already-issued points is unlikely to come before Miami.

For now, the points are real, the gap is two, and the Miami weekend is five weeks away. Bearman's job between now and Florida is straightforward: keep the license clean.

What's Next

Watch Bearman's driving across Miami's free practice and qualifying sessions carefully — any FIA stewards' inquiries will be significant in the context of his penalty points total. If he reaches Miami clean, the May 23 expiry removes two points and his situation becomes considerably safer heading into the European summer. The FIA's April 9 meeting could also produce recommendations about how crash-related penalty points from regulatory incidents are handled going forward — though any retroactive change to Bearman's total is unlikely.
