FIA's April Technical Meeting: What Today's Session Means for F1's 2026 Rule Fixes

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap

April 16, 2026

Formula 1's governance rarely moves this quickly — but when three rounds of racing expose genuine safety concerns and a chorus of driver complaints, the process accelerates. Thursday's FIA technical meeting is the second in a rapid-fire series of April sessions designed to finalize regulation changes before the Miami Grand Prix on May 3.

Today's session follows Tuesday's sporting regulations meeting and brings together the technical heads of the teams and FIA technical department. The agenda focuses on the engineering specifics of proposed changes to the 2026 rules: the super clipping power delivery limit, qualifying energy allocation, and refinements to the ADUO (Automatic DRS-like Upgrade Operation) mechanism. The key measure — raising the super clipping threshold from 250 kilowatts to 350 kilowatts — has already emerged from earlier discussions as the most likely to advance to a vote.

The Problem That Triggered It All

The 2026 regulations introduced a dramatically different powertrain architecture, and with it, an unintended consequence: cars recovering energy during qualifying are travelling at significantly slower speeds than fully charged cars on the same straight. At high-speed circuits like Suzuka, the FIA reduced the qualifying energy recovery allocation specifically to manage the speed differential — but critics, including several drivers, have called this a band-aid solution that distorts qualifying strategy and produces what they call "artificial" racing.

The speed differential issue is more than a sporting concern. When cars with different energy states are navigating the same straight, the faster-moving cars may approach slower ones at over 200 kilometers per hour of closing speed. That is the safety case that has driven the FIA's urgency to act before racing resumes.

The Road to a Vote

The process now has a clear timeline. Today's technical meeting provides the final layer of engineering analysis before a high-level session on April 20, when team principals and senior figures from both F1 and the FIA will gather to evaluate the agreed proposals. An electronic vote is expected shortly after April 20, with any ratified changes requiring formal approval from the FIA World Motor Sport Council before taking effect.

Teams have generally welcomed the process, even if the discussions have covered what the FIA described as "difficult topics." The overarching direction — fewer restrictions on energy deployment, more consistent power delivery across the field — aligns with what most drivers have called for since the season began.

What's Next

The April 20 high-level meeting is the decisive moment in this process. If team bosses and FIA officials reach sufficient consensus, the e-vote and ratification process could be completed within days — leaving the regulation changes in place for the Miami Grand Prix on May 3. That race, the first sprint weekend of the 2026 season, would then become the first test of whether the fixes work the way their architects intend.
