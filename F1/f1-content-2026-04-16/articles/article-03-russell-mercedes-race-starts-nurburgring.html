Russell Reveals "Huge Amount" of Behind-the-Scenes Work on Mercedes' Race Start Problem

By Elena Voss | F1 Fanrecap

April 16, 2026

George Russell sat down to speak with Sky Germany this week at the Nürburgring, where Mercedes and McLaren participated in a Pirelli dry-tyre test during the sport's enforced April break. His message was direct and pointed at one specific problem: Mercedes can't hold a race lead off the starting line.

"There is a huge amount going on behind the scenes," Russell said, specifically referencing the team's work on race starts. "There are lots of days on the simulator analysing the first three races and looking ahead to the next couple that are coming up."

The statistic that defines this particular weakness is striking: in the 2026 season so far, Mercedes has secured the front row of the grid in all four qualifying sessions — not once has either Russell or his teammate Kimi Antonelli led after the first lap. The Silver Arrows are the fastest team in Formula 1, and they cannot hold their starting position.

The Ferrari Advantage at Starts

Technical observers have pointed to a structural explanation. Ferrari's 2026 power unit features a smaller turbo design that spins up faster than Mercedes' equivalent, giving their drivers a more predictable and narrower clutch engagement window when launching from the grid. Lewis Hamilton, in particular, has repeatedly gotten off the line ahead of the Mercedes cars — a pattern that was obvious in Australia and repeated throughout the opening flyaway races.

Russell acknowledged last year that he noticed Hamilton's fast Ferrari starts ahead of the 2026 season opener. That concern was well-founded. Ferrari has consistently converted their starting positions into race lead moments that have forced Mercedes to play catch-up and cost them what could have been dominant race wins.

The Nürburgring Tyre Test

The joint Pirelli test this week gave both Mercedes and McLaren an opportunity to gather real-world performance data outside the race weekend format. For Russell, it appears to have been as much a starts development exercise as a tyre evaluation. The simulator work and engineer analysis from the first three rounds are feeding into adjustments that the team hopes to bring to Miami.

The Miami sprint weekend format gives Mercedes an immediate test of whatever they've found. Saturday's sprint begins with a standing start, and Sunday's main race adds another. Two opportunities in one weekend to demonstrate that the fix is real.

What's Next

If Mercedes solves their start weakness, they become very difficult to beat. Their qualifying pace is already unmatched, and Kimi Antonelli's race-day speed in clean air has been exceptional. The combination of pole position and a strong launch from the grid would close off the main avenue opponents have used to disrupt the championship leader's pace. George Russell and the engineering team at Brackley know exactly what's at stake — and they're spending the five-week break making sure Miami looks different from Japan.
