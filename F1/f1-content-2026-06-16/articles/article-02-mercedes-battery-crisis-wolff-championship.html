Mercedes Battery Crisis: Wolff Says Team "Can't Compete for a Championship" With Reliability Issues

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap

June 16, 2026

Two battery failures in three races. Two championship points gifts to their rivals. And now, a candid admission from the top that the situation is not acceptable. Mercedes team principal Toto Wolff emerged from the Barcelona paddock with a stark assessment of his team's power unit crisis: "We can't compete for a championship with this power unit reliability."

It is the kind of statement that arrives when you can no longer soften the problem with careful language. The facts demand frankness. At the 2026 Barcelona-Catalunya Grand Prix, Kimi Antonelli was running second place when his Mercedes shut down on lap 62 of 66 — three laps from the finish. It is the second time in three races that a Mercedes car has failed in exactly this fashion.

A Pattern That Cannot Be Ignored

The first failure came at the Canadian Grand Prix in Montreal, where George Russell's car cut out with a battery failure while he was running competitively. Now Barcelona. Two cars, two races, the same category of failure, in the final stages of both races.

The investigation into the Barcelona failure is complicated by an extraordinary logistical challenge. The battery unit suffered damage so severe that it cannot be safely transported by air — the damaged cells present a safety risk that prohibits air freight. Instead, the unit is being shipped by sea from Spain back to the Mercedes facility in Brackley, England. That means weeks before the engineers can fully understand what happened at a molecular level inside the battery.

A Wider Problem Across Mercedes Customers

What makes this more alarming is that the failures are not isolated to the factory Mercedes team. McLaren, Alpine, and Williams — all of which run Mercedes power units — have experienced battery failures during the 2026 season. This is a systemic issue across the Mercedes power unit ecosystem, not a quirk of one chassis or one car's specific installation.

For Antonelli, the psychological toll is real. He had just passed Russell for second place — a move that would have maintained his championship lead comfortably — when the car stopped. "I feel a bit empty," he said after the race. For a 19-year-old in his first championship battle, that is a significant moment to absorb.

What's Next

The Austrian Grand Prix is June 26-28 at the Red Bull Ring. Mercedes has ten days to find answers, implement fixes, and give both Antonelli and Russell confidence that the power unit will complete a race distance. If they cannot, another DNF in Austria could make this championship genuinely difficult to defend. The fastest car in Formula 1 right now is only valuable if it finishes.
