F1 Championship Math: How Two DNFs Turned Antonelli's Runaway Lead Into a Real Race

By Elena Voss | F1 Fanrecap

June 16, 2026

Here is the simplest way to understand the 2026 Formula 1 championship: Kimi Antonelli is the best driver in the best car, and his team's power unit keeps letting him down at the worst moments. The result is a championship that should be a procession but is instead a nail-biter.

After the Barcelona-Catalunya Grand Prix, Antonelli leads Hamilton by 41 points and Russell by 50. That is a healthy lead — but considerably smaller than it would have been without two power unit failures at critical moments. Russell retired from the Canadian Grand Prix with a battery failure while running competitively. Antonelli retired in Barcelona on lap 62 of 66 with a battery failure while running second. Two retirements, two races, two chunks of points handed to rivals who were beaten on pure pace.

The Points That Weren't

Had Antonelli finished second in Barcelona — the position he was in when his car stopped — he would have collected approximately 18 additional points. Had Russell completed Montreal in a strong finishing position, the team's aggregate points total would be meaningfully higher. Instead, both retirements benefited Ferrari (through Hamilton's win in Barcelona) and the broader grid.

The gap to Hamilton could reasonably be estimated at over 90 points without the two failures. At 41 points, it is still a lead — but it is a lead that Hamilton, Russell, and potentially Lando Norris can see the top of. That changes how Mercedes must race: defensively, conservatively, always with one eye on the power unit warning lights.

The 2026 Antonelli Paradox

At 19 years old, Kimi Antonelli is doing something extraordinary. He is leading a Formula 1 world championship in his first full season, against experienced champions, in the best car on the grid. His race pace, qualifying pace, and racecraft have all exceeded expectations. He has won multiple races. He has managed pressure from veterans far more experienced than himself.

And yet the narrative around his season is shaped by what his team's power unit has cost him. That is the Antonelli paradox of 2026: a driver outperforming his age and his rivals, held back by a technical problem outside his control.

What's Next

Mercedes needs to demonstrate before the Austrian Grand Prix (June 26-28) that they have identified and resolved the battery reliability issue. If Antonelli finishes Austria cleanly and scores big points, the lead stabilizes and the conversation shifts back to his dominance. If another DNF occurs, the championship math becomes genuinely uncomfortable. The fastest car in Formula 1 is only valuable when it sees the checkered flag.
