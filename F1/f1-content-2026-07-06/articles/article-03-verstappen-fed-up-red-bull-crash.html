Verstappen "Fed Up" After Second Rear Wing Crash in a Week: "I Don't Want Anything to Do with It for a While"

By Elena Voss | F1 Fanrecap | July 6, 2026

Max Verstappen has run out of patience.

The four-time world champion crashed out of the British Grand Prix at Silverstone on Lap 48, his Red Bull spinning off the circuit at Stowe corner — the same rear-wing fault that triggered his crash in Austrian Grand Prix qualifying just days earlier. The DRS wing, designed to open on straights for additional top speed, failed to close properly when it was supposed to. For the second time in a week, Verstappen's car became a projectile.

"I was lucky in Austria, I was lucky here," he said afterwards, the words measured but the meaning clear, "but that's why you get really fed up with it."

A Car with Too Many Problems

Verstappen did not stop there. He described Red Bull's 2026 season as plagued by systemic failures — not just the rear wing, but the power unit and the chassis as well. For a team that dominated the sport in a way that bordered on embarrassing from 2022 to 2024, the 2026 RB22 represents a profound step backward.

The rear wing issue is the most immediately dangerous. A wing that opens for speed on a long straight and then fails to create the downforce needed for the corner at the end of it does not just cost a driver time — it can send a car directly into the barriers. It happened in Austria's qualifying. It happened at Stowe in the race. Red Bull has not yet produced a public explanation of what exactly is failing or how it will be fixed.

The Relationship Under Strain

When a Dutch journalist asked Verstappen whether he needed to have a direct conversation with Red Bull management before the Belgian Grand Prix at Spa-Francorchamps, the response was telling: "To be honest, I don't want anything to do with it for a while."

The relationship between Verstappen and Red Bull has been the most successful driver-team partnership in the sport for four consecutive years. He won four world championships with them. He has publicly defended the team through media storms and internal politics on multiple occasions. This level of public frustration — not coded discontent, but explicit exhaustion — is new territory.

Verstappen's title defence is, for all practical purposes, over. He has collected too few points in the first half of the season to be a realistic contender against Antonelli, Russell, and Hamilton. His current focus, it seems, is simply getting through race weekends without injury.

What's Next

Red Bull has two weeks before the Belgian Grand Prix at Spa-Francorchamps — July 17-19 — to diagnose and repair the rear wing fault. Spa's long straights and high-speed corners are among the most demanding in the calendar; arriving at Spa-Francorchamps with a DRS system that cannot be trusted would be a serious risk. If Red Bull cannot resolve the fault, there will be difficult conversations to have — not just about Belgium, but about whether Verstappen's future with the team extends into 2027.
