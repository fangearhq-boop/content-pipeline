The April 20 FIA Vote That Could Change F1 2026 Forever

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap

April 11, 2026

Nine days from now, the people who run Formula 1 will gather to vote on changes that could fundamentally alter how the 2026 cars race. April 20 is the date circled on every team principal's calendar — the binding FIA Commission meeting that will decide whether proposed energy management rule changes are approved in time for the Miami Grand Prix on May 1-3.

The road to April 20 is methodical: a Sporting Regulations meeting on April 15, a technical session on April 16, and then the full commission meeting on April 20. The timeline is tight. But the urgency is real, and it was made visible by something that happened in Japan.

Bearman's Crash and the Energy Problem

At Suzuka's fearsome Spoon Curve, Oliver Bearman's Haas experienced a sudden and violent deceleration as the car ran out of electrical energy at high speed. The resulting crash subjected Bearman to approximately 50G — a force that tested both the car's safety structures and the driver's body to their limits. No serious injuries followed, but the crash became a symbol of what critics had been warning about: the 2026 energy management rules create a dangerous speed differential when cars deplete their battery reserves at the wrong moment.

Drivers have also complained throughout the first three rounds about qualifying sessions compromised by energy conservation tactics — a game of cat and mouse that reduces the spectacle and complicates strategy in ways that feel at odds with the spirit of competition.

What's Proposed

Two principal proposals are on the table. The first would increase the super clip limit — the cap on electrical power deployment — from 250kW to 350kW, allowing more consistent power delivery through corners. The second would reduce the energy recovery quota from 9MJ to 6MJ, simplifying the in-race energy arithmetic and reducing the temptation to lift-and-coast in order to manage battery reserves.

If both proposals are adopted, the effect would be felt primarily in qualifying and in the corners where energy management currently warps lap time differentials. For fans watching at home, the racing should look more natural. For safety marshals, the speed differentials when cars hit energy limits should narrow.

What's Next

The April 20 FIA Commission meeting will be followed by an electronic vote, with results expected within days. If approved, changes would be ratified by the FIA World Motor Sport Council and implemented ahead of Miami May 1-3. The Miami Grand Prix — a sprint weekend — would be the first test of whether the regulatory adjustments produce the intended effect. Whether Formula 1 can navigate this regulatory change this quickly is an open question, but the sport's leadership has signaled it is committed to moving fast. The alternative — leaving the rules unchanged while safety concerns persist — is not a comfortable option.
