Hamilton's Ferrari Renaissance: How Year Two Is Already Different

By Elena Voss | F1 Fanrecap

April 11, 2026

The question when Lewis Hamilton moved to Ferrari for the 2025 season was always about fit — whether the seven-time world champion could adapt quickly enough to a new machine, new engineers, and a new culture to remain competitive at the highest level.

Year one provided an uncomfortable answer. Hamilton scored no podiums across 24 races in 2025 — the first such season of his career. The 2026 season, two months in, is telling a different story.

Through three rounds, Hamilton has finished fourth, third, and sixth. His third-place finish in China — his first podium in a Ferrari — was the visible proof that the partnership had turned a corner. He sits fourth in the championship with 41 points, running eight behind teammate Charles Leclerc and close enough to the top of the Ferrari standings to matter in the internal competition that drives innovation at Maranello.

The "Pact of Steel" Winter

The change did not come from chance. Hamilton made a deliberate, intensive commitment to Ferrari over the winter — what he later described as a "pact of steel." He parked his motorhome inside Ferrari's Fiorano test circuit and spent extended periods in the simulator, working through the car's characteristics with engineers in granular detail. The sessions ran late into the evenings. The goal was not just to learn the 2026 car but to understand Ferrari's methods and culture well enough to influence it from the inside.

The work translated. Ferrari's 2026 car — the SF-26 — is lighter and more nimble than its predecessor, generating less downforce but offering a more responsive feel through the corners. According to the team's performance data, Hamilton's style — precise corner entry, late braking, linear steering inputs — suits the new car's characteristics better than the previous generation. Total Motorsport's analysis of the first three rounds showed Hamilton and Leclerc running nearly equal on car performance metrics.

The Ferrari Rivalry

Ferrari's history of intra-team rivalries is well-documented, and the Hamilton-Leclerc dynamic has its edges. At the Chinese Grand Prix, the two drivers engaged in what was described as a "feisty" on-track battle that ultimately ended with Leclerc ahead — but only after contact and controversy that made both Ferrari and neutrals nervous about what the season might bring. Ferrari will need to manage that tension carefully if both drivers are to extract maximum points from a car that is consistently the third-fastest package on the grid.

Hamilton has handled the dynamic publicly with characteristic equilibrium. He has praised Leclerc's speed, acknowledged the competitive nature of their relationship, and focused his communication on what Ferrari can do together — rather than against each other.

What's Next

The Miami Grand Prix on May 1-3 will be the next opportunity for Hamilton and Ferrari to close ground on Mercedes, whose package is currently the fastest in Formula 1. The ADUO engine balancing rules — which allow underperforming power units targeted upgrade opportunities — are expected to allow Ferrari to narrow some of the gap as the season progresses. If that happens, Hamilton's trajectory in the championship could improve meaningfully. A driver of his caliber with a car capable of winning is a combination that changes any season's calculus.
