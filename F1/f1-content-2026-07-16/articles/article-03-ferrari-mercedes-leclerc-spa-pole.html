Ferrari vs Mercedes: Charles Leclerc Chases Fourth Spa Pole in a Qualifying Battle That Could Define the Belgian GP

By Elena Voss | F1 Fanrecap | July 16, 2026

With Ferrari winning two of the last three races and rain forecast for Saturday qualifying, the battle for Belgian Grand Prix pole position is shaping up as one of the most compelling sessions of the 2026 F1 season.

Charles Leclerc knows Spa-Francorchamps. He has taken pole position at this circuit in 2019, 2023, and 2024 — three times in three attempts across a career that has blossomed into genuine championship-threatening form. With Ferrari arriving at Belgium on the back of two wins in the last three races and a transformed SF-26 that has rediscovered its aerodynamic edge, Leclerc enters the weekend as many analysts' favorite for Saturday pole.

But Mercedes will not simply step aside. Spa is a power circuit. The Kemmel Straight rewards straight-line speed, and the long flat-out sections through Eau Rouge-Raidillon put engine performance at a premium. George Russell and Lewis Hamilton both have exceptional track records here, and Mercedes' power unit advantage on high-speed layouts is a structural strength that Ferrari's aerodynamic improvements cannot fully offset.

Ferrari's Transformation

The SF-26 that competed in the first few rounds of the 2026 season was a different machine from the Ferrari that has won two of the last three races. The team's aerodynamic development program has delivered significant downforce improvements that have made Leclerc particularly dangerous on circuits where commitment through fast corners makes the difference.

Spa is exactly that kind of circuit. The final sweep through Pouhon, the approach to Stavelot, the commitment required through the Raidillon — these are corners that reward a driver willing to push the absolute limit of adhesion. Leclerc, at his best, is that driver. Ferrari's engineers have given him a car capable of exploiting his talent at Spa in a way previous iterations of the SF-26 could not.

The Rain Factor

The forecast for Saturday qualifying shows a 65% probability of rain — significant enough to make wet-weather performance a genuine qualifying variable. A damp track changes the qualifying calculus dramatically. Tire choice, timing of laps, and the ability to read a constantly evolving track surface become as important as pure pace.

Both Leclerc and Russell have demonstrated wet-weather skill throughout their careers. Hamilton, the most experienced driver on the grid, has built his reputation partly on his ability to dominate in challenging conditions. If Saturday is wet, qualifying becomes less about raw machinery and more about driver read — and that opens the door for any of the three to claim pole.

What's Next

Friday's practice sessions will provide the first real data points: who has the pace, who handles the predicted damp conditions best, and which car is truly suited for Spa's unique demands. Qualifying Saturday at 10:00 AM ET delivers the first competitive verdict of the Belgian GP weekend. Leclerc, Russell, Hamilton — and potentially Verstappen — will each be pushing for the front row. This could be one of the great qualifying sessions of 2026.
