Aston Martin's "Horror Show": Zero Points, Honda Vibrations, and No Quick Fix in Sight

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap | April 17, 2026

There is no diplomatic way to characterize Aston Martin's 2026 Formula 1 season. Martin Brundle, the veteran broadcaster and former driver, put it plainly: this is a "horror show." Three races into the new-generation regulations, the Silverstone-based team has scored zero points, struggled to finish races, and is dealing with a technical problem so severe that it is threatening the physical well-being of their drivers.

The Vibration Problem

At the heart of Aston Martin's crisis is a debilitating vibration issue originating from their Honda power unit. The vibration travels through the car's chassis and into the steering wheel, transmitting directly into the driver's hands and fingers with enough intensity that extended stints become physically damaging.

The consequences, as described by the drivers themselves, are deeply concerning. Lance Stroll has indicated that he cannot sustain more than approximately 15 consecutive laps before the risk of permanent nerve damage becomes a serious concern. Fernando Alonso — one of the most experienced racing drivers in history — placed his own limit at around 25 laps. In a sport where races last 50 to 70 laps, this represents an extraordinary constraint on every race strategy Aston Martin can contemplate.

An 80 percent improvement to the vibration issue was trialled during the Japanese Grand Prix before being withdrawn due to reliability concerns. The test represented progress in isolation, but reliability and race fitness are non-negotiable in Formula 1, and a fix that introduces new failure modes cannot be deployed in competition.

The Scale of the Performance Gap

The vibration problem does not exist in isolation. Stroll told Formula1.com that the team is "four-and-a-half seconds" behind the leading teams — a staggering deficit that goes beyond any single issue. "It's a combination of things: engine, balance, grip," has been the internal characterization of the car's weaknesses, and Stroll was blunt in assessing the team's position: "We have a lot of work to do."

Fernando Alonso's 18th-place finish in Japan remains the sole instance of either Aston Martin car completing a full Grand Prix distance in 2026. That statistical reality captures just how dysfunctional the campaign has been. Former world champion Damon Hill said Aston Martin's problems were "so bad" that he initially could not believe them.

The April Break and What Comes Next

During F1's five-week April break, Lance Stroll took a practical step away from the team's problems by competing in a GT racing event. "This year we don't have a very competitive car," he said simply. The escape is understandable, but the car will be waiting when the paddock reconvenes in Miami.

Adrian Newey — the legendary designer who joined Aston Martin ahead of this season as part of a long-term transformation project — is working on the car's future, but meaningful contributions to the current AMR26 remain limited. His impact will be felt in 2027 and beyond. For the remainder of 2026, Aston Martin and Honda are focused on incremental improvements: addressing the vibration, recovering reliability, and finding the baseline performance that would allow their drivers to simply race competitively.

What's Next

Miami marks the return to racing for the whole field, including Aston Martin. With six points available in the Sprint and 25 in the Grand Prix, every opportunity matters — but the team needs to first produce a car that both drivers can push to its limit for a full race distance without physical risk. Until that happens, Miami is another weekend of damage limitation rather than a turning point. The "horror show" label will follow this team until the work being done in Silverstone finally produces results on track.
