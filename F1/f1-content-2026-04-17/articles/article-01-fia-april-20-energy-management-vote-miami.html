F1's Most Important Off-Track Moment: FIA Votes April 20 on Energy Management Tweaks for Miami

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap | April 17, 2026

Formula 1's April break has been anything but quiet on the regulatory front. Monday, April 20, marks the most consequential off-track meeting of the 2026 season so far: a high-level FIA Commission gathering at which team principals, power unit manufacturers, FIA officials, and Formula 1 management will vote on targeted tweaks to the brand-new energy management rules. The outcome will directly shape what racing looks like when the sport returns at the Miami Grand Prix on May 1.

Why the Rules Need Fixing

The 2026 regulations were designed to be the most technically ambitious in decades, introducing a revolutionary 50/50 split between internal combustion and electrical power, along with sophisticated systems governing how energy is deployed and recovered throughout a lap. Three races into the season, the early evidence has revealed unintended consequences that the FIA is now urgently addressing.

The most alarming concern is safety. At the Japanese Grand Prix in Suzuka, Haas driver Oliver Bearman suffered a significant accident caused in part by extreme speed differentials — a direct result of cars running out of electric energy and suddenly slowing dramatically while others approach at full power. When a car at maximum attack encounters a car that has momentarily lost electrical deployment, the closing speeds can be dangerously high. This scenario was not anticipated to manifest so clearly in race conditions, and the FIA's safety officials identified it as requiring immediate attention.

Beyond safety, drivers and teams have objected to qualifying sessions under the new regulations — which theoretically should reward flat-out performance — being effectively converted into energy management exercises where drivers must carefully ration their electrical deployment rather than simply pushing to the limit every lap.

What's On the Table April 20

The proposed fixes fall into three categories. The super clipping limit — which caps the electrical power a car can deploy — is being discussed at a potential increase from 250 kilowatts to 350 kilowatts, which would give drivers more freedom to push harder without as severe a penalty for energy usage. The qualifying energy recovery limit is also under review; the Suzuka round already saw it reduced from the standard figure to 8 megajoules, and further reductions toward 6 megajoules are under active discussion. Refinements to the ADUO (Automatic Drag Reduction Override Unit) system — another new 2026 feature — are also on the agenda.

Monday's meeting is the third in a sequence: preparatory discussions on April 15 (sporting regulations) and April 16 (technical heads) preceded it. The FIA confirmed "constructive dialogue" from those earlier sessions and issued a formal statement acknowledging a "commitment to making tweaks to some aspects of regulations." An e-vote is expected within days of Monday's gathering, with any agreed changes requiring ratification by the FIA World Motor Sport Council before implementation.

What's Next

If the April 20 vote produces consensus and the e-vote proceeds quickly, changes to the energy management framework could be in place for the Miami Grand Prix Sprint weekend — the very next event on the calendar. Miami is scheduled from May 1 to 3, giving the FIA and teams roughly two weeks to implement, test, and publish any new directives. The changes, while potentially modest in headline terms, could meaningfully shift how teams approach qualifying and the opening laps of each race. The 2026 season has been technically brilliant and occasionally thrilling, but the sport's governing body is right to act now — before a safety incident of greater severity forces a less measured response.
