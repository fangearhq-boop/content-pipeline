Verstappen's Nürburgring Weekend: Four-Time F1 Champion Chases GT3 Redemption in 24-Hour Qualifiers

By Elena Voss | F1 Fanrecap | April 17, 2026

Formula 1's April break means different things to different people in the paddock. For most drivers, it is rest, recovery, and physical preparation for the demanding Sprint weekend in Miami. For Max Verstappen, it is an opportunity to race. This weekend — Saturday April 18 and Sunday April 19 — the four-time world champion is competing in the ADAC 24 Hours of Nürburgring Qualifiers at the famous Nordschleife, driving a Mercedes-AMG GT3 alongside co-drivers Dani Juncadella and Jules Gounon.

The Context: Redemption After a Disqualification

Verstappen's relationship with the Nürburgring endurance scene this year carries a specific subplot. Earlier in the NLS season, he and his team delivered a dominant performance in the NLS2 race — winning by a margin of one full minute — before being disqualified from the results. The reason was a tyre infringement: his car had used seven tyres over the course of the event, one more than the regulations permit. The performance itself was untouchable. The result was erased by a technical detail.

This weekend's qualifiers represent Verstappen's return to the circuit where that frustration remains fresh. The Qualifiers are official preparation races for the full 24 Hours of Nürburgring, scheduled for May 14-17 — meaning this is not casual participation but structured and purposeful preparation for one of motorsport's most demanding events. The format runs a four-hour race on Saturday afternoon and a second race on Sunday.

Why the Nürburgring Matters to Verstappen

At 28 years old and with four consecutive F1 world titles behind him, Verstappen's ambitions extend beyond Formula 1's paddock. He has spoken publicly about the Nürburgring's 24-hour race as an event he genuinely wants to win — not as a sideshow to his F1 career, but as a legitimate goal in its own right. "It brings a big smile," he said of returning to the Nordschleife, a 25.378-kilometer circuit that has tested drivers and cars for nearly a century.

The GT3 environment provides a useful competitive contrast to the control and precision of Formula 1. In a GT3 field, Verstappen is one driver among dozens navigating traffic, managing tire wear over longer stints, and adapting to the unique rhythms of endurance racing. There are no DRS zones, no energy harvesting systems, no engineers directing his pace through a predictive model. There is the car, the circuit, and the stopwatch — which is, in many respects, where Verstappen is at his most comfortable.

The F1 Picture Behind the Nürburgring Visit

Verstappen's 2026 Formula 1 season has not matched the standard that defined his championship years. Red Bull's new-generation car carries approximately a one-second deficit to the leading Mercedes package — roughly 0.8 seconds from the chassis and 0.2 seconds from the Ford power unit. His results through three races — a sixth, a DNF, and an eighth — reflect a package that is working hard to find competitive footing under radically changed regulations.

The April break offers no magic solutions for Red Bull's engineers. But for Verstappen personally, the Nürburgring weekend provides something valuable: the reminder that he can still go out on any given day, in any category, and compete at the very highest level.

What's Next

After this weekend's qualifiers, Verstappen returns focus to Formula 1 and the Miami Grand Prix Sprint weekend from May 1-3. Red Bull is expected to have updated components, and Verstappen will need to close the gap to Mercedes if he is to mount any serious championship challenge. The 24 Hours of Nürburgring follows in May, offering a separate and equally compelling storyline. For now, though, the Nordschleife calls — and Max Verstappen has a score to settle.
