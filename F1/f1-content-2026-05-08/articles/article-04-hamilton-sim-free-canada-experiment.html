Hamilton Goes Sim-Free for Canada — The Seven-Time Champion's Bold Ferrari Experiment

By Ryan Calloway | May 8, 2026 | F1 Fanrecap

Lewis Hamilton is skipping the Ferrari simulator for the Canadian Grand Prix, and his reasoning is grounded in data that his own 2026 season has produced.

His best result of the year is a third-place finish at the Chinese Grand Prix — a race weekend where he arrived without any simulator preparation, relying on back-to-back scheduling and race instinct rather than factory preparation. In Miami, Hamilton reversed that approach, putting in significant simulator hours before the weekend. The result was a sixth-place finish despite Ferrari bringing the largest upgrade package of any team.

The pattern is clear enough that Hamilton has drawn a conclusion: "The car feels different when you get to the track," he said after Miami. For Canada, the simulator stays at Maranello.

A Departure From a Lifetime of Practice

Hamilton's willingness to abandon the simulator is a striking departure from a career built on meticulous preparation. For more than two decades at McLaren and Mercedes, the simulator was central to how Hamilton developed his understanding of a car's characteristics before arriving at a circuit. Mercedes' simulation tools were among the best in the sport, and Hamilton used them to build the technical understanding that made him so effective at optimizing set-up throughout a race weekend.

At Ferrari, something is different. The correlation between what the simulator suggests and what the car does at the track has not been consistent. The worst part of that correlation gap is that it seems to direct Hamilton toward set-up choices that make the car feel settled in the sim and unbalanced at the circuit. China was the proof of concept for the opposite approach: arrive without preconceived notions from factory prep, respond to what the car actually does, and find performance in real time.

The Stakes for Canada

Canada's sprint format makes this an unusually high-stakes test of the hypothesis. Drivers get limited practice time before competitive sessions in a sprint weekend, which means the baseline understanding of the car must either come from simulator work or from extremely efficient use of the short practice window available. Going in without simulator prep under those conditions is a significant decision — but Hamilton appears convinced that arriving with a clean mindset outweighs the risk of limited preparation.

He is currently 51 points into the season, joint fourth with Norris, and Ferrari needs him to start delivering consistent podium results if they are going to stay competitive in the constructors' championship. The gap to Mercedes in constructors is 121 points — a chasm that requires both Ferrari drivers producing results together. If the sim-free approach at Canada delivers another P3 or better, it validates a preparation methodology that could reshape how Hamilton approaches every race for the rest of the season.

What's Next

The Canadian Grand Prix (May 22-24) at Circuit Gilles Villeneuve is the first sprint format weekend of 2026. It offers Hamilton an immediate competitive test of his preparation theory and a chance to recapture the momentum he showed in China before the Miami disappointment.

Whether the experiment works or not, the broader message from Hamilton's decision is significant: he believes Ferrari's simulation tools are currently steering him in the wrong direction, and he is willing to go without them to prove it. For a team that has invested enormously in simulation infrastructure, that is a challenge as much as it is a strategy. Canada will provide an answer.
