Ferrari's Miami Upgrade Package Flops — 11 Changes, 0.07s Gained, Leclerc Demands Answers

By Ryan Calloway | May 8, 2026 | F1 Fanrecap

Ferrari arrived in Miami with the biggest upgrade package on the Formula 1 grid — 11 new components fitted to the SF-26 for both Lewis Hamilton and Charles Leclerc. The scale of the package suggested a team that had identified its problems and found solutions. The results suggested otherwise.

Hamilton finished sixth. Leclerc eighth, with a 20-second time penalty for repeated track limit violations on top of it. Ferrari's race pace deficit to Mercedes improved by just 0.07 seconds per lap, dropping from 0.53 seconds before the weekend to 0.46 seconds after it. For the first time in the 2026 season, Ferrari did not have a single car inside the top three in a grand prix or sprint.

Charles Leclerc's post-race message was direct: "I would like to understand."

When the Car Goes Backwards

What made Miami particularly troubling for Ferrari was the contrast between Saturday's performance and Sunday's. Team principal Fred Vasseur acknowledged that Ferrari's pace was strong in clean air during the early laps and that Leclerc had been "fighting at the front" before the situation deteriorated. "Once we dropped into traffic it became more difficult," Vasseur admitted, "and consistency was the main issue."

The tyre management problem — specifically an inability to keep the hard Pirelli compound in the right operating window in Miami's high temperatures — was something neither driver could overcome once the race simulation put them in close quarters with competitors. Leclerc found himself slipping away from the lead battle on the hard tyre in a way that was impossible to arrest, and by the time Hamilton's race strategy ran out of answers, the day was beyond salvaging.

Engine Gap and Electrical Problems

Complicating the aerodynamic shortfall is a reported 30-horsepower deficit between Ferrari's engine and the Mercedes power unit. That gap — enough to affect not just outright pace but also the effectiveness of every aerodynamic element — means Ferrari cannot simply upgrade its way out of trouble by bolt-on components alone. The power unit sits at the core of what makes the 2026 regulation cycle work, and a 30HP disadvantage is significant enough to blunt the impact of any aerodynamic gains.

A reported "deployment bug" in Ferrari's energy recovery system added further complexity to Miami, disrupting the energy delivery both drivers were seeking at key moments on the circuit. This kind of software and electrical issue is not solved at the factory between races in the space of two weeks — it requires systematic investigation and careful recalibration before Canada.

Vasseur Stays Measured, But the Questions Are Loud

Fred Vasseur is a team principal who chooses his words with precision, and his post-Miami assessment was calm rather than alarmed. But the absence of alarm does not mean the absence of a problem. Leclerc's call for an internal investigation reflects a driver who has been patient with Ferrari's development trajectory and is now asking the team to give him a clearer explanation for why the most significant upgrade of the season produced results this far below expectation.

Ferrari is third in the constructors' championship at 59 points — 121 behind Mercedes on 180. With McLaren closing on 94 points and continuing to upgrade, the gap to second place is growing more complicated, not less.

What's Next

The Canadian Grand Prix (May 22-24) gives Ferrari a two-week window to identify what went wrong in Miami, address the tyre management issue, and give Hamilton and Leclerc a car capable of fighting for the podium. Canada's Circuit Gilles Villeneuve offers a different challenge — lower temperatures, different tyre demands, and a circuit profile where Ferrari's power deficit may be less exposed than in the high-temperature, high-load conditions of Miami.

But the engine deficit, the deployment bug, and the gap to Mercedes are not going away between now and Montreal. Leclerc wants to understand. The question is whether Ferrari can give him answers worth hearing.
