Bearman on Race Ban Watch: 10 Penalty Points, Two Away From Missing Miami

By Ryan Calloway April 3, 2026

The Math That Haunts Haas

Ollie Bearman walked away from one of the most frightening accidents of the 2026 Formula 1 season — a 50G impact at Suzuka's Spoon Curve — with a knee contusion. He then walked into a penalty points situation that is arguably more dangerous to his season than the crash itself.

Bearman now carries 10 penalty points on his FIA Superlicense. The automatic race ban threshold is 12 points accumulated over a rolling 12-month window. With the Miami Grand Prix on May 1-3 as the next race on the calendar, Bearman is exactly two penalty points away from missing what would be his biggest career moment: an American debut at one of the most commercially significant events on the Formula 1 calendar.

The Suzuka Crash Explained

The context around Bearman's current penalty points total is critical — and the argument that he is being punished for a systemic regulatory failure rather than personal negligence is a strong one. At Spoon Curve during the Japanese GP, Bearman's Haas was in full deployment mode — accelerating — as Franco Colapinto's Alpine ahead was in energy harvesting mode, effectively decelerating to recharge. The closing speed differential between the two cars was approximately 50 kph. There were no warning lights visible to Bearman indicating that the car ahead was harvesting.

Bearman himself stated plainly after the incident: "We've warned the FIA what can happen, and this has been a really unfortunate result of a massive delta speed that we've never seen before in Formula 1 until these new regulations." The FIA scheduled review meetings in response. Bearman's team Alpine also issued a formal open letter defending driver Colapinto, who was on the receiving end of fan backlash that the team characterized as "hateful messages."

What This Means for Haas

Haas has confirmed that Bearman will race in Miami barring any further incidents or penalties. But the math creates an uncomfortable reality for the team. Should Bearman receive any additional penalty points between now and Miami — from any incident, in any session — the accumulation could trigger the automatic ban. A race ban at Miami would cost the team its highest-profile event of the year and remove one of the few bright spots of the 2026 Haas season. Bearman sits 7th in the drivers' championship with 17 points, making him the team's entire performance argument.

What's Next

The FIA's April 9 meeting with all team directors will address the energy harvesting regulations that created the Suzuka incident. Any changes approved there — particularly warning systems for harvesting phases — would reduce the risk of future incidents like the one that put Bearman in this position. For Bearman personally, the next several weeks require careful, clean driving. Two points is a thin margin between racing at Miami and watching from the garage.
