FIA April 9 Meeting: Six Days to Change Formula 1's Broken Regulations

By Elena Voss April 3, 2026

The Meeting That Could Reshape Miami

In six days, all 10 Formula 1 team directors will sit down with the FIA for a technical working group meeting that has become the most consequential administrative event of the 2026 season. The agenda: what to do about a set of energy management regulations that produced a 50G crash at Suzuka, have generated multiple dangerous closing-speed incidents, and have drawn unified condemnation from the driver community.

Lewis Hamilton — who described his Japanese Grand Prix weekend as "pretty terrible" and stated bluntly that "drivers have no power with the FIA" — has become the most prominent voice in the driver coalition pushing for change. Carlos Sainz, serving as director of the Grand Prix Drivers' Association, is pushing for specific reforms to be implemented before Miami on May 1-3.

What the Drivers Want

The April 9 meeting agenda, as outlined by GPDA representatives, covers four primary areas. The first is qualifying "super-clipping" — zones where the 200kW power differential between a car in full deployment and a car harvesting creates closing speeds that were previously unimaginable at circuits like Suzuka. The second is minimum warning thresholds: the demand that some visible or audible signal alert following drivers when a car ahead is harvesting. The third is battery management warning systems, similar to the yellow warning light systems that flag other sudden changes in car behavior. The fourth is longer-term: a review of the electric-to-thermal power ratio for 2027 regulations.

The drivers' position is unified. Hamilton's public statements, Sainz's formal GPDA warnings, and Bearman's post-crash remarks all point to a coherent view: the 2026 regulations created predictable dangers that the drivers identified before the season started, and the FIA must act.

The Political Reality

The obstacle to change is political rather than technical. Any regulation modification in Formula 1 requires unanimous agreement from all 10 constructors. That structure gives every team a veto — and some teams, particularly those whose power unit advantages are partly derived from the current harvesting dynamics, may have limited incentive to vote for changes that equalize performance.

Hamilton acknowledged this reality when he noted that drivers have "no power with the FIA." The stewards can impose penalties. The FIA can propose agenda items. But teams hold the votes, and the unanimity requirement means one holdout can block any change. The April 9 meeting will test whether the safety arguments — backed by a 50G crash, multiple near-misses, and driver testimonials — are sufficient to achieve unanimous agreement.

What's Next

If the April 9 meeting produces agreement on even the minimum measures — warning light systems for harvesting phases — the safety profile of Miami will improve. If it does not, drivers will race under the same conditions that produced the Suzuka crash. The FIA has publicly stated that the meeting agenda is open and that all options are on the table. Whether that translates into action before May 1 is the question that every driver, team, and fan will be watching closely over the next six days.
