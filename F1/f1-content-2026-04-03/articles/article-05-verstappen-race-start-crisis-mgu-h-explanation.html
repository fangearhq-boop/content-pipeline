Verstappen's Race Start Crisis Explained: The MGU-H Problem Costing Him Points

By Marcus Cole April 3, 2026

A Mystery With a Technical Answer

Something is wrong with Max Verstappen's 2026 Formula 1 season. Three races, 12 points, and a retirement in Japan have left the four-time world champion 9th in the standings — a position that would have seemed almost satirical before the year began. His Red Bull is slow. But there is a specific, repeatable problem that has cost him positions in every race, and it has been identified: race starts.

Verstappen has had poor getaways from the grid in Australia, China, and Japan — losing positions in all three races before the first corner. This pattern has a root cause that analysts at Motorsport.com have now pinned down, and it lies in one of the most significant technical changes of the 2026 regulation overhaul.

The MGU-H and Why It Matters

Under the pre-2026 regulations, Formula 1 power units included a Motor Generator Unit — Heat (MGU-H), a component that harvested energy from turbo exhaust gases and used it to maintain turbo spool. The MGU-H continuously balanced turbo pressure, smoothing energy transitions and giving drivers a consistent power band from which to manage starts and acceleration.

The 2026 regulations eliminated the MGU-H entirely. In its place, drivers must now manually manage turbo spool through a specific formation lap procedure — revving the engine to designated thresholds while managing the battery's energy state. The process is precise, time-sensitive, and newly demanding in ways that did not exist under the previous rule set. Execute it correctly and the car arrives on the grid with properly spooled turbo and adequate energy reserves for the start. Execute it incorrectly and the initial getaway is compromised before the lights even go out.

Three Races, Three Problems

Verstappen has not solved this procedure in any of the three 2026 races. In Australia, his start dropped him multiple positions. In China, he failed to score a point across both the sprint and main race. In Japan, he retired from the race. The pattern of a poor foundation — the start and first lap — compounding into difficult race positions has been consistent.

What makes this particularly notable is who is getting it right where Verstappen is not. Rookie Isack Hadjar, driving for Racing Bulls in his first Formula 1 season, has out-qualified Verstappen in multiple sessions this year. Kimi Antonelli — 19 years old — is leading the championship with 72 points. Younger drivers with less experience are adapting faster to the new technique demands than one of the sport's most decorated practitioners.

What's Next

Red Bull's development plan for the five-week break specifically addresses the deployment maps that govern formation lap management. If the software changes are sufficient, Verstappen could arrive at Miami with a solved start procedure for the first time in 2026. A driver of his caliber adapting to a new technical demand is not an if — it is a when. The question for Red Bull and for Verstappen is whether the answers arrive before his contract exit clause becomes an active conversation.
