Red Bull's Three-Stage Fix Plan: No Factory Shutdown as Team Races to Save Verstappen's Season

By Ryan Calloway April 3, 2026

A Sprint Through the Break

Other Formula 1 teams will use the five-week gap between Japan and Miami for rest and recalibration. Red Bull is not other teams right now — and so Red Bull will not be resting.

Team principal Laurent Mekies has confirmed that the Milton Keynes factory will operate with no shutdown during the break, running a full development sprint to address the RB22's persistent performance deficits. The team has outlined a three-stage improvement plan across the Miami and Imola Grands Prix, with each stage targeting a specific layer of the car's problems.

The Three Stages

Stage one targets Miami: updated floor fences and edges, specifically designed to reduce the understeer that has plagued Verstappen's ability to get the RB22 rotating through corners. Helmut Marko confirmed the floor changes address the understeer characteristic, which has cost Verstappen lap time and made the car difficult to drive at its limit.

Stage two, also for Miami, focuses on revised power unit deployment maps — the instructions that govern how energy is distributed between the thermal and electric components of the hybrid system throughout a lap. The formation lap management problem that has cost Verstappen race starts is a deployment map issue as much as a driver technique issue, and Red Bull is addressing it at the software level.

Stage three, targeted at Imola, is described as a more comprehensive aerodynamic overhaul — a larger-scale package that will address the RB22's fundamental balance issues rather than applying targeted fixes to specific symptoms.

The Power Unit Caveat

Within the break plan, one detail stands out as a signal of how deep Red Bull's current problems run. While rivals — including Mercedes — are planning to install fresh power unit hardware for Miami, Red Bull has decided to revert to an older Honda power unit. The decision is effectively an admission that the team's newest PU hardware is not yet reliable enough for race conditions.

Running older hardware at a circuit as demanding as Miami's street circuit means Red Bull will enter Round 4 at a power disadvantage relative to what fresh units would provide. The floor and deployment updates are designed to compensate. Whether they can fully offset the power deficit remains one of the 2026 season's central open questions.

What's Next

Mekies has been clear about the stakes: "By the time we give him a fast car, he will be a much happier Max." Verstappen, whose contract with Red Bull contains an exit clause tied to the 2026 performance regulations, has been publicly frustrated with a car that has left him 9th in the championship after three races. The five-week break is the team's most critical development window of the season. Miami's result will tell the paddock whether Red Bull found its answers — or whether Verstappen's patience with the RB22 project is running out.
