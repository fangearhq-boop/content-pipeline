Alpine Issues Open Letter Defending Colapinto: 'Hateful Messages Must Stop'

By Marcus Cole April 3, 2026

A Team Defends Its Driver

The 50G crash at Suzuka's Spoon Curve was one of the defining incidents of the 2026 Formula 1 season. Now its aftermath is generating a different kind of conversation — one about how fans respond to drivers when accidents happen, and who is actually responsible when the regulations themselves are the cause.

Alpine Formula 1 has issued a formal open letter to the sport's fanbase, describing "hateful messages" directed at driver Franco Colapinto following the Japanese Grand Prix crash and calling for an end to the abuse. The letter is unambiguous in its defense: Colapinto did nothing wrong. What happened at Spoon Curve was not a mistake. It was an inevitable consequence of racing under regulations that multiple drivers, engineers, and teams warned about before the season even started.

What Actually Happened at Spoon Curve

The physics of the crash require context to understand why Alpine is so firm in its defense. Colapinto's Alpine was in an energy harvesting phase — a state in which the car aggressively decelerates to recharge the battery — as Bearman's Haas approached at full deployment power. The resulting speed differential between the two cars was approximately 50 kph. No warning system existed to alert Bearman that the car ahead was in a dramatically slower mode.

Under the 2026 Formula 1 power unit regulations, cars can shift between harvesting and deployment in a fraction of a second. The removal of the MGU-H — which historically smoothed energy transitions — means the power swings are more abrupt and severe than anything the sport has seen before. The combination of the abrupt deceleration and the absence of any visual warning created the conditions for the Suzuka crash. Alpine's case is not that Colapinto was unlucky. It is that the regulation set made this outcome statistically inevitable.

The Broader Significance

Alpine's decision to issue a public letter, rather than simply moving on, signals how seriously the team views both the safety issue and the fan conduct question. The letter positions Alpine as a responsible actor in a debate that the FIA is being forced to address through its April 9 meeting with all team directors.

For Colapinto, a 22-year-old Argentine driver in only his second F1 season, the public defense from his team matters. Racing drivers absorb enormous pressure from within their profession. Fan abuse on top of that pressure is an entirely different category of stress — and Alpine's willingness to call it out explicitly is a signal that the team values its driver's wellbeing over the noise.

What's Next

The FIA's April 9 meeting will determine whether the regulations that caused the Suzuka crash are changed before Miami. If warning systems and harvesting zone restrictions are approved, the risk of similar incidents drops significantly. For Colapinto and Alpine, the priority heading into the five-week break is preparation for Miami — where Colapinto will aim to show that his pace in the new Alpine is enough to earn him points and, in doing so, make the crash a footnote rather than the headline.
