FIA Meets Today as Formula 1's Most Critical Regulatory Week Builds to April 20 Vote

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap | April 15, 2026

Today's sporting regulations session is the second of three pre-vote meetings that will determine whether F1's 2026 energy management rules change before Miami.

The Timeline That Matters

The FIA is holding its Sporting Regulations meeting today — the second formal step in a three-stage consultation process before Sunday's landmark April 20 vote. That vote will determine which 2026 Formula 1 regulatory amendments, if any, are implemented before the Miami Grand Prix on May 3.

Yesterday brought the first technical session, which the FIA described in an official statement as "constructive dialogue." Today's meeting focuses on the sporting side — specifically, Section B of the F1 sporting regulations, which must be adjusted to align with any technical changes that emerge from the process. Thursday brings a follow-up technical session to finalize outstanding details. Then on Sunday, a high-level meeting gathers FIA officials, team principals, and Formula 1 management to vote on the preferred package of changes.

Any agreed amendments are then subject to ratification by the FIA World Motor Sport Council via e-vote, expected within days of the April 20 meeting. The timeline is extraordinarily tight for regulatory change at the highest level of motorsport.

The Energy Management Problem at the Heart of It

The core technical issue driving all of this is energy management — specifically, the difficulty drivers and teams have experienced managing the electrical output of the 2026 power units within the constraints of the current regulations.

The new 2026 PUs can deploy three times the electrical energy of their predecessors. That sounds like an advantage — and in straightline acceleration it is. But the problem emerges when drivers need to recover that energy under braking. At most circuits, cars are struggling to harvest enough energy to sustain full deployment throughout the lap, creating extreme speed differentials between cars at different stages of their energy recovery cycles.

In qualifying, the current energy allocation stands at nine megajoules per lap. When Suzuka was approaching, the FIA made an emergency adjustment, reducing the Suzuka qualifying allocation to eight megajoules — a last-minute change made specifically to reduce the chaos of cars with wildly different speeds on track at the same time.

Discussions at this week's meetings have centered on potentially reducing the allocation further — some proposals have suggested as low as six megajoules per lap, which would represent a 33 percent cut from the current baseline. A reduction of that magnitude would fundamentally change how teams approach qualifying strategy.

The Safety Case — and What Bearman's Crash Changed

The energy management debate might have remained a technical inconvenience if not for Oliver Bearman's crash at the Japanese Grand Prix, which brought the safety dimension of speed differentials into sharp focus. A car at full electrical deployment and a car in energy-saving mode can be separated by significant velocity on the same straight — creating a genuinely dangerous situation for drivers joining the circuit from the pit lane or catching slower-moving cars.

That crash gave the calls for change an urgency and moral weight that was harder to dismiss as mere competitive complaint. It is widely understood within the paddock that the Bearman incident strengthened the hand of those advocating for the strongest possible energy management reforms.

What's Next

Thursday's follow-up technical session will conclude the pre-vote consultation period. Then on Sunday, April 20, the binding vote takes place. If a package of changes is agreed and ratified in time, drivers and teams arriving in Miami for the first sprint format weekend of the 2026 season will do so under amended regulations. The changes could affect everything from qualifying strategy to race energy deployment windows. Five days from now, the picture will be clearer.
