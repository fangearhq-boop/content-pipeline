Red Bull's "Second Season Launch": Inside the Miami Overhaul That Must Rescue Verstappen's 2026

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap | April 15, 2026

Team principal Laurent Mekies has labeled Miami their "second season launch" — language that captures both the scale of Red Bull's upgrade package and the scale of their problem.

The Worst Start in Years

Max Verstappen came into the 2026 Formula 1 season as the four-time defending champion who had come within two points of a fifth consecutive title in 2025. Three races into the new season, he has a sixth place, a retirement, and an eighth place. He has not stood on a podium. He sits ninth in the drivers' championship with 12 points. His rookie team-mate Isack Hadjar has, in certain qualifying sessions, outperformed him.

For a driver of Verstappen's ability, the only plausible explanation is the car — and the technical diagnosis from within Red Bull has been stark. The RB22 suffers from a fundamental concept error: the design team prioritized a low-drag philosophy that came at the direct expense of aerodynamic downforce. In a 2026 regulatory environment where high-downforce is crucial for cornering performance, that philosophical miscalculation has left the car approximately one second per lap behind Mercedes.

The breakdown is damaging on two fronts: roughly 0.8 seconds is attributable to chassis aerodynamics and roughly 0.2 seconds to the Ford power unit, which is in its first year of partnership with Red Bull's engineering operation. Neither problem has a quick fix — but Red Bull is attempting a rapid solution anyway.

What Miami Brings

Laurent Mekies, Red Bull's team principal, has described the Miami Grand Prix package as a "second season launch" — language that signals an upgrade cycle closer in scope to the original car launch than to standard mid-season development. The team is bringing what multiple reports have described as an extensive set of aerodynamic changes intended to address the fundamental downforce deficit.

Red Bull also expects to qualify for the ADUO — the Additional Development Upgrade Opportunity that the FIA introduced as part of the 2026 power unit regulations framework. The ADUO provides extra development tokens to power unit manufacturers who are officially recognized as operating at a significant disadvantage, offering a regulated pathway to accelerate improvement. If Red Bull and Ford are granted ADUO eligibility, it would represent an important additional resource for closing the 0.2-second PU gap.

The Scale of the Task

One second per lap is not a gap that a single upgrade cycle normally closes. Racing engineers tend to work in the tenths — hunting for improvements measured in hundredths and adding them across aerodynamic surfaces, power unit mapping, and mechanical setup until tenths become fractions of a second, and fractions become competitive margins. A full second suggests not a development lag but a conceptual starting point that requires a different car.

Mekies knows this. The "second season launch" framing is honest about the scale of the reset, not a guarantee that it will deliver competitive results from lap one in Miami. The realistic best case is that the package closes the gap meaningfully — not eliminates it overnight — and gives Verstappen a car capable of regularly fighting for positions in the top five while the longer-term development programme continues.

What's Next

Miami, May 3. That is the first test of the "second season launch." Red Bull will know within a handful of Friday practice sessions whether the upgrade has moved the needle as intended, or whether the season remains an exercise in damage limitation. The whole paddock will be watching — not just because of Verstappen's star power, but because a competitive Red Bull changes the dynamic of every race for every driver in front of or near them. If the Milton Keynes outfit gets it right in Miami, 2026 gets a lot more interesting.
