Hamilton Targets First Ferrari Win at Monaco — and He's Probably Better Without the Simulator

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap | May 31, 2026

Lewis Hamilton made a confession after the Canadian Grand Prix that landed differently than your standard post-race debrief: he had skipped Ferrari's simulator before the race. And it had worked. His P2 finish in Montreal was his best result as a Ferrari driver. He had out-qualified Charles Leclerc, produced a clean race, and overtook Max Verstappen on track.

When the headline "Lewis Hamilton says he's probably better without Ferrari simulator" ran across every F1 outlet, the reaction was immediate. Sky Sports described it as "quite damning." Hamilton, typically guarded about internal team matters, had essentially conceded that the tool his employer uses to develop and refine their car had been holding him back.

Now he's heading to Monaco. And he wants to win.

A Circuit Built for His Strengths

Monaco is the one circuit in Formula 1 where raw power unit output matters least. The Circuit de Monaco runs just 3.337 kilometres through the streets of the principality, with virtually no meaningful straight and a constant demand for low-speed precision. Top speeds at Monaco are roughly half what they are at circuits like Monza or Spa. The winner isn't decided by horsepower — it's decided by balance, rhythm, and the confidence to commit to kerbs and barriers at speeds that would be terrifying on any road.

These are conditions that suit Hamilton's instincts perfectly. He has won at Monaco previously — he knows the corners, the rhythm, the feel of a quick lap around streets where a mistake means instant retirement. And with the FIA's announcement this week that Straight Mode will be banned at Monaco, the straight-line speed differential that has sometimes put Ferrari at a disadvantage disappears entirely.

The Ferrari That Woke Up

Hamilton's no-simulator approach has coincided with what appears to be genuine momentum for Ferrari in 2026. The SF-26 has shown consistent excellence in slow-speed sections, and its aerodynamic package is widely regarded as one of the strongest in the field when conditions favor mechanical grip over outright power. McLaren's Lando Norris and team boss Andrea Stella both highlighted Ferrari as the team likely to set the pace in qualifying at Monaco — a striking admission from a team that was running away with races earlier in the season.

The betting markets have Hamilton at 4/1 to win, behind only hometown favorite Leclerc at 21/10. For a driver who hadn't managed a single podium in the entirety of his 2025 season with Ferrari, those odds reflect a remarkable swing in fortunes.

What's Next

Hamilton will take to the Circuit de Monaco for Free Practice on Friday, June 5, with qualifying on Saturday and the race on Sunday, June 7 at 2:00 PM ET on Apple TV+. The question isn't whether Hamilton can win at Monaco — it's whether, for the first time in his Ferrari career, the stars have aligned to let him. The circuit favors his style. The car is competitive. The team's biggest rivals have even endorsed Ferrari as the pace-setter. And a driver who has been liberated by abandoning preparation orthodoxy might be exactly the version of Hamilton that Monaco rewards. June 7 cannot come soon enough.
