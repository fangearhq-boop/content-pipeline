FIA Bans Active Aero at Monaco: What the Straight Mode Decision Means for the Race

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap | May 31, 2026

Formula 1 made an unprecedented regulatory call this week: active aerodynamics — specifically the "Straight Mode" system that replaced DRS in the 2026 regulations — will not be available to drivers at the Monaco Grand Prix. Cars will race with fixed wings through the narrow streets of Monte Carlo, in a decision that reshuffles the competitive order less than a week before the race weekend begins.

The governing body cited two specific reasons for the ban, and both come down to the fundamental nature of the Monaco circuit itself.

Why the FIA Pulled the Plug

The first and primary concern is safety. The 2026 regulations introduced Straight Mode as a replacement for the old DRS system. Unlike DRS, which was activated at specific detection points relative to the car ahead, Straight Mode can be deployed whenever a driver chooses — opening the front and rear wings to reduce drag by approximately 20% and adding up to 20 km/h of top speed on straights.

The FIA's guiding principle for Straight Mode usage is clear: it should only be activated in zones where the car is not at the limit of tyre grip. At Monaco, that's almost never. The Circuit de Monaco is defined by its relentless proximity to the barriers, the constant direction changes, and the demand for cars to be at — or near — the tyre grip threshold at almost every point on the lap.

The second reason is the "three second rule." Each Straight Mode zone must last at least three seconds to give drivers adequate time to react to the changes in wing position and car aerodynamic balance. Monaco's so-called "straights" — the pit straight between Sainte Devote and the first corner, and the section between Anthony Noghes and Sainte Devote — are simply too short to satisfy this requirement.

What Remains Available

Not all active aerodynamic tools are off the table. Overtake Mode — which provides extra electrical power when a driver is within one second of the car ahead — remains available. This preserves some element of the activation benefit from a racing perspective, even if the wholesale drag reduction is gone. Additionally, the FIA confirmed that engine map adjustments will be used to manage cars' top speeds on the circuit's few available acceleration zones.

Who Benefits Most

The removal of Straight Mode is a significant swing of the competitive pendulum toward Ferrari. All season, the SF-26's power unit has been a relative weakness compared to the Mercedes and McLaren packages — teams that have leveraged Straight Mode on high-speed circuits to generate straightforward advantages. Remove that equalization tool, and the battle becomes one of aerodynamic balance, slow-corner grip, and driver skill.

Those are Ferrari's domains. Charles Leclerc, the 21/10 favorite and defending Monaco winner, arrives at his home race with a car that excels in exactly the conditions Monaco creates. Lewis Hamilton, at 4/1 second favorite, enters Monaco with momentum from his P2 in Canada — and the new motivation of knowing that the playing field has been leveled.

What's Next

The 2026 Monaco Grand Prix weekend begins on Friday, June 5 with Free Practice 1 and 2. Qualifying takes place Saturday, June 6 at 3:00 PM local time. The race runs Sunday, June 7, with a 2:00 PM ET start on Apple TV+. The active aero ban adds a new layer of intrigue to a circuit that already rewards the best drivers in the world. With fixed wings and the form of Ferrari and Hamilton pointing in the same direction, the Monaco GP is shaping up to be one of the most technically compelling races of the 2026 season.
