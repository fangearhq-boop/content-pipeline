The Silent Contender: Leclerc Is Third in the Championship and Nobody's Talking About It

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap | May 6, 2026

While the Formula 1 world focuses on Antonelli's historic run and Red Bull's crisis, Charles Leclerc has quietly assembled a points tally that keeps him in realistic title contention heading into the Canadian Grand Prix.

Four races into the 2026 Formula 1 season, the championship narrative is dominated by two stories: Kimi Antonelli's record-breaking demolition of everything in his path, and Max Verstappen's struggles with a Red Bull that is fundamentally compromised by excess weight.

Sandwiched between those two dramatic arcs, virtually unnoticed, is Charles Leclerc. He sits third in the Drivers' Championship with 59 points. He is 41 behind Antonelli. He is 20 behind George Russell. He is 8 ahead of Lando Norris.

And with 20 races remaining, no one should be writing him off.

The Points Are Coming

Leclerc's opening four rounds have been competitive without being spectacular. He finished P3 in the Miami Sprint, collecting eight points. In the main race, a 20-second penalty for track limit violations at Miami dropped him to P8 from what should have been a higher-finishing position — a painful result that masked genuine pace.

The key for Leclerc is that he has scored in every race. He has not had a reliability retirement, not suffered a catastrophic qualifying error, and not found himself in the kind of headline-grabbing incident that might explain away a weaker points position. He is simply doing his job — methodically, efficiently, and quietly — in a Ferrari that is performing well enough to keep him in the championship picture.

Ferrari sits second in the Constructors' Championship with 110 points, 70 behind Mercedes' 180. The gap is significant but not insurmountable if the Maranello outfit can begin converting their pole position and race pace opportunities into wins.

Ferrari's Power Unit Advantage

One factor that gives Leclerc a potentially underappreciated edge is Ferrari's power unit. The SF-26 runs on Ferrari's own engine — not a customer supply — giving the team full control over power unit development, energy deployment, and strategic adaptation. Lewis Hamilton has publicly noted that Ferrari is surrendering three to four tenths in straight-line speed at some circuits, suggesting room for improvement. But Ferrari's unit is also one of the stronger packages at circuits with long straights and high power demand.

Circuit Gilles Villeneuve — the next race on the calendar — is exactly that type of track. Long straights, heavy braking zones, relatively low aerodynamic downforce requirement. Ferrari's power delivery profile and Leclerc's exceptional racecraft in wheel-to-wheel situations make him a genuine podium threat and potentially a race winner in Montreal.

The Math Still Works

The 2026 season's title picture can change quickly. A maximum sprint race weekend delivers up to 34 points. A race victory at a standard weekend is worth 25. If Antonelli hits a reliability issue or a strategic error — both of which are possible when a team is stretching its machinery to deliver unprecedented results — the gap closes fast.

Leclerc finished the first four rounds of 2024 in a similar points position relative to the championship leaders. He knows how to play a long game. His patience is one of his most underrated qualities as a competitor.

The 2026 season has 24 rounds. Four are done. The story is nowhere near written.

What's Next

Leclerc heads to Montreal for the Canadian Grand Prix on May 22-24, a sprint weekend at Circuit Gilles Villeneuve. If Ferrari can solve the drag issue Hamilton identified and deliver the kind of qualifying performance the power unit should theoretically produce, Leclerc could emerge from Canada with a significantly improved championship position. After Canada, Monaco (June 5-7) is the next event — a circuit where Leclerc has historically been one of the standout performers on the entire grid.
