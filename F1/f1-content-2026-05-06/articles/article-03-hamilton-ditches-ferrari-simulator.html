Hamilton Won't Touch Ferrari's Simulator Before Canada: "It's Sending Me in the Wrong Direction"

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap | May 6, 2026

Lewis Hamilton is taking an unconventional approach to preparation before the Canadian Grand Prix — skipping simulator work entirely after noticing a pattern his data team may have missed.

Lewis Hamilton is done with the simulator — at least for the next three weeks.

The seven-time world champion revealed that he will completely bypass Ferrari's simulator in the build-up to the Canadian Grand Prix, making a bold decision based on a pattern he has noticed in his own data this season. His reasoning is straightforward: the Ferrari simulator is, in his words, "sending me in the wrong direction."

"I prepare on the simulator for the track, get the car set-up to a certain place, and then come to the track and find that set-up doesn't work," Hamilton explained. "So I'm going to do something different for Canada."

The Evidence Behind the Decision

Hamilton's best result of the 2026 season so far was P3 at the Chinese Grand Prix — a result achieved after he had zero simulator preparation time because the race was a back-to-back event immediately following the Australian Grand Prix. There was simply no time to use the simulator between the two events, and Hamilton had to rely purely on his experience and feel for the car.

Contrast that with Miami. Hamilton spent significant time in the simulator preparing for the race. The result was one of his most disappointing weekends of the season — finishing P6 while struggling with a car that didn't behave the way the simulator had predicted. The correlation between sim and reality, as Hamilton sees it, was poor.

This is not merely a personal perception. Simulator correlation — the degree to which a simulator accurately replicates real-world track and car behavior — is one of the most critical and technically demanding challenges in Formula 1 engineering. When correlation is poor, simulator preparation can actively steer a driver toward incorrect setups, compounding the problem rather than solving it.

Ferrari's Broader Technical Challenge

Hamilton's simulator concerns exist within a wider context of technical challenges at Ferrari. He has publicly stated that the SF-26 is surrendering three to four tenths of a second per lap in straight-line speed compared to the fastest cars on the grid — a significant drag problem that the aerodynamic team must address.

Ferrari sits second in the Constructors' Championship with 110 points, 70 behind Mercedes' 180. The team is competitive, and Charles Leclerc's consistency has kept Ferrari in realistic championship contention. But Hamilton, currently P5 in the Drivers' standings with 51 points tied with Norris, needs more from his car — and from his preparation — to challenge for victories.

Going back to factory meetings in Maranello without simulator work is an approach that prioritizes engineering communication and physical track feel over data-driven simulator optimization. For a driver of Hamilton's caliber — who relies more on sensory feedback than many of his peers — it may well prove to be exactly the right call.

A Precedent for the Bold Move

Hamilton has a long history of trusting his instincts over conventional methodology. Some of his greatest wins at Mercedes came from car setups that conflicted with what the engineers initially recommended. The decision to abandon the simulator echoes that same pattern — prioritizing lived experience and sensory data over tools that aren't matching the on-track reality.

Circuit Gilles Villeneuve in Montreal is a circuit where feel matters enormously. The braking zones, the wall proximity, and the unpredictable safety car deployments all require an instinctive driving approach. A driver going into that weekend with clearer mental preparation, rather than confused by contradictory simulator data, might find it a very effective strategy.

What's Next

Hamilton will attend factory meetings in Maranello during the three-week break before Canada, maintaining team contact and technical discussions — just without the simulator component. The Canadian Grand Prix weekend begins May 22. Whether the no-sim approach translates to better performance will be one of the key individual storylines to watch when the cars take to the grid in Montreal.
