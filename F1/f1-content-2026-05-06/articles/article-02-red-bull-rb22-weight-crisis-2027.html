Red Bull's Weight Crisis: RB22 Approximately 30kg Over Target and the Fix Might Not Come Until 2027

By Elena Voss | F1 Fanrecap | May 6, 2026

Reports confirm the RB22 is carrying significant excess weight — costing Max Verstappen nearly a second per lap in pure performance — and the cost cap makes a 2026 solution nearly impossible.

When Red Bull admitted to "significant shortcomings" with the RB22 after the Chinese Grand Prix, most assumed it was the kind of diplomatic understatement that Formula 1 team principals are known for. The reality, it turns out, is considerably worse.

Reports have confirmed that the RB22 is running approximately 30 kilograms over its minimum weight target. With the driver weight included, the car is estimated to be carrying more than 798 kilograms on track — compared to the 768-kilogram target set under the 2026 regulations. In a sport where a single kilogram can cost a team roughly three hundredths of a second per lap, that weight penalty translates to approximately 0.9 seconds per lap at most circuits before any aerodynamic, power unit, or setup deficit is even considered.

It is not a small problem. It is, as Verstappen himself has reportedly described it, a "terrible" one.

Where the Weight Comes From

The 2026 regulations represent the most significant overhaul in Formula 1 since 2022. The removal of the MGU-H from the power unit, combined with new active aerodynamic requirements, new battery specifications, and updated structural regulations, meant that every team was building essentially a new car from the ground up.

For Red Bull, the transition has been particularly painful. The chassis and suspension design, the cooling system layout, the battery packaging, and structural reinforcements have all reportedly contributed incremental weight that, combined, has created a deficit that exceeds what their initial design targets anticipated.

Red Bull targeted the weight issue as the "central" priority for their Miami Grand Prix upgrade package, designing replacement components that look identical to existing parts but weigh less. The result was insufficient. The car remains significantly overweight, and the team publicly acknowledged after Miami that the issues seen at previous rounds were still present.

The Cost Cap Trap

The reason Red Bull cannot simply throw engineering resources at the weight problem is Formula 1's financial regulations — the cost cap that limits how much teams can spend per season. Red Bull have not allocated enough of their cost cap budget to simultaneously fix the fundamental weight issue on the RB22 and maintain adequate development investment in their 2027 car.

This creates a genuine strategic dilemma. Spending heavily on fixing the 2026 car risks under-developing the 2027 machine, potentially resulting in two consecutive seasons outside the title picture. Not spending enough on the 2026 fix means Verstappen continues to operate at a roughly one-second structural disadvantage through the season's remainder.

The expectation within the paddock is that the real fix arrives only when Red Bull introduces a new chassis — likely at the start of the 2027 season. In-season adjustments may shave some kilograms, but the kind of weight reduction needed to genuinely close the performance gap requires new structural components that cannot be retroactively incorporated into the existing RB22 architecture.

Verstappen's Reality

Max Verstappen is currently seventh in the 2026 Drivers' Championship with 26 points — a position that would have been unthinkable twelve months ago, when he was the reigning four-time world champion. He has expressed dissatisfaction with the regulations and described the current car experience as one he does not "enjoy."

His contract includes performance-related clauses, though reports from January 2026 suggested those clauses were modified before the season began. What remains clear is that one of the most talented drivers in the history of the sport is being held back by machinery rather than ability — and the timeline for a fix is measured in seasons, not races.

What's Next

Red Bull heads to the Canadian Grand Prix on May 22-24 with limited expectations of a dramatic improvement. The team continues to develop the car on an incremental basis, but without a structural weight solution in place, Canada will look similar to the four races that preceded it. The real question for Red Bull is whether they can avoid the weight deficit compounding with other technical challenges — rear instability and energy management issues that have also been identified with the RB22 — and deliver at least some qualifying and race performances that suggest a path forward.
