### STORY 5: Works vs. Customer Gap

**Long-Form Post:**
In 2025, McLaren won the Formula 1 World Constructors' Championship with Lando Norris claiming the drivers' title.

In 2026, they are 154 points behind Mercedes. With the same engine.

The 2026 technical regulations introduced the most radical power unit changes in a decade. The hybrid system's electric motor — the MGU-K — now produces 350 kilowatts, nearly three times the output of the previous specification. Fuel flow has been reduced, synthetic fuel is mandatory, and the sheer complexity of managing energy deployment through a single lap has increased by an order of magnitude.

That complexity is where works teams thrive. Mercedes, Ferrari, Red Bull, Aston Martin, and Audi design, build, and optimize their own power units — including the proprietary software that controls energy deployment. Their engineers know every parameter. Customer teams, including McLaren, receive the hardware but not the full software depth.

McLaren has suffered four reliability failures through nine rounds — a direct consequence of running energy systems they didn't fully design. They've brought significant upgrade packages: 7 new parts at Miami, further updates since. Their engineers acknowledge the gap is software-driven and that closing it requires more than hardware.

The FIA is aware of the structural advantage. Ferrari, Honda, and Audi have filed complaints about specific loopholes they allege benefit Mercedes and Red Bull. The regulator has not issued a ruling.

The bottom line: the new era of Formula 1 has created a two-tier championship within the championship. Works teams lead both titles. Customer teams are playing catch-up — and the defending champions are 154 points back.
