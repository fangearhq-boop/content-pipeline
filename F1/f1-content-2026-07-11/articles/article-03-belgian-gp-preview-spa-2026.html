Belgian GP Preview: Rain, Spa, and a Championship That Can Still Go Either Way

By Elena Voss | July 11, 2026

F1 Race Preview

Formula 1 returns to its most iconic venue at its most consequential moment of the season. The Belgian Grand Prix — Round 10 of 22 at Spa-Francorchamps — takes place July 17-19, and the forecast looks precisely the way that circuit's legends demand.

It's going to rain. Possibly all weekend.

The Circuit

Spa-Francorchamps spans 7.004 kilometers through the Ardennes — the longest circuit on the Formula 1 calendar. Its 44-lap race distance demands sustained energy management, strategic tire deployment, and the kind of mental resilience that separates champions from contenders.

Eau Rouge and Raidillon — the blind, uphill complex exiting the opening chicane — define the character of the track. Flat in qualifying trim, terrifying in wet conditions, the sequence has produced some of the sport's greatest moments and worst accidents. Pouhon, the high-speed left-hander in the second sector, loads the car at maximum downforce. Blanchimont, taken flat in dry conditions, becomes a judgment call in the rain.

The circuit has been modified in recent years — safety upgrades following significant incidents — but its core character remains: Spa rewards the brave and punishes the careless.

The Weather Forecast

Forecasts one week out carry significant uncertainty, but the current picture calls for rain across all three days of the Belgian GP weekend. The European heatwave that has dominated the continent through early July is expected to break just as the F1 paddock arrives in Stavelot.

Wet conditions at Spa are a specific phenomenon. The circuit's geography — carved through hills and valleys — means rainfall can be intense in one sector and dry in another simultaneously. Strategy calls become guesses. Safety Car deployments become unpredictable. The energy management advantage that has made Mercedes dominant in 2026 could be partially neutralized by conditions that force every team to make decisions without perfect data.

For the championship chasers, this is the opportunity the calendar provides.

Championship Stakes

Kimi Antonelli leads with 179 points — 25 ahead of George Russell and roughly 78 ahead of Charles Leclerc in the drivers' standings (exact Hamilton/Leclerc figures vary by source). 341 points remain across 13 rounds. The title is not decided.

Russell needs a win in Belgium and Hungary to seriously threaten Antonelli's lead. Leclerc, coming off his British GP victory with Ferrari momentum building, will view Spa as a venue suited to his particular skill set — precision at high speed, comfort in variable conditions, the ability to manage a race over its full duration.

Verstappen, whose exit clause is essentially guaranteed to trigger after these two remaining pre-summer rounds, has historically been one of Spa's most capable performers. A strong result in Belgium is not irrelevant to his negotiating position for wherever he goes next.

What to Watch

The weekend schedule: FP1 and FP2 on Friday July 17; FP3 and qualifying on Saturday July 18 (qualifying at 15:00 CEST); race Sunday July 19 at 15:00 CEST (9:00am ET).

Key storylines to follow through the weekend: Whether rain conditions arrive on schedule; how Mercedes' energy management software performs in variable conditions; whether Ferrari can replicate their Silverstone momentum; and what Verstappen's performance signals about his final two pre-summer appearances at Red Bull.

Belgium is followed immediately by the Hungarian Grand Prix on July 24-26 — the last race before the summer break. The two rounds together will do more to define the 2026 championship narrative than any other pairing remaining in the calendar.

What's Next

Gates open at Spa on Thursday. Qualifying on Saturday determines grid positions. Sunday determines championship mathematics.

Rain or shine — though rain seems more likely — Spa-Francorchamps will deliver. It always does.
