McLaren Won in 2025. Now They're 154 Points Behind — Here's Why the 2026 Regulations Changed Everything

By Marcus Cole | July 11, 2026

F1 Technical Analysis

Twelve months ago, McLaren were champions. Lando Norris held the drivers' title. The team had just ended its longest championship drought. The 2026 season was supposed to build on that foundation.

Through nine rounds, McLaren are 154 points behind Mercedes in the constructors' standings. They're running the same engine. The difference isn't the hardware — it's everything that surrounds it.

What Changed in 2026

The 2026 Formula 1 technical regulations introduced the most significant power unit overhaul in the sport's modern era. The hybrid system's electric motor — the MGU-K — now produces 350 kilowatts, nearly three times the output of the previous specification at 120kW. Fuel flow has been reduced to approximately 75 kilograms per hour, down from 100. Every team must use fully sustainable, non-fossil synthetic fuel.

These changes don't sound like they should produce a 154-point gap between a works team and its customer. The reason they do comes down to a single word: software.

Managing 350kW of electric output alongside a reduced combustion engine across 44 to 78 laps of a Grand Prix — while responding to tire conditions, fuel loads, safety cars, and competitor strategies — requires energy deployment algorithms of extraordinary complexity. Works teams design, develop, and optimize those algorithms from the ground up, with direct access to every parameter their power units can measure. Customer teams receive the hardware. They do not receive the full software depth.

The Customer Disadvantage in Practice

McLaren have experienced the consequences directly. Through nine rounds of the 2026 season, they have suffered four reliability failures — DNFs or DNS events — directly related to power unit management. Each failure represents a zero-point round in both championships: the worst possible outcome in a points race where consistency defines the title.

The reliability issue compounds the performance gap. When McLaren does finish races, they're operating their Mercedes power unit at a level below what the Mercedes works team achieves with the same hardware. The difference in qualifying pace, race pace, and tire preservation all trace back to software-driven energy management decisions that McLaren cannot fully replicate without access to Mercedes' core development tools.

Ferrari, by contrast, is a works team. Their 11-part upgrade package at Miami and further development at Silverstone have brought the Maranello team to within approximately 78 points of Mercedes in the constructors' standings — a gap that has closed from over 100 points at the season's midpoint. Red Bull, also a works team with their Ford power unit partnership, retains the same fundamental advantage even while struggling to match their own four-year dominance from 2021-2024.

The Complaint on the Table

Ferrari, Honda, and Audi have formally raised objections to what they describe as a technical loophole benefiting Mercedes and Red Bull. The allegation involves compression ratios: the 2026 regulations reduced the maximum from 18.0 to 16.0, but the measurement occurs with a cold engine. At operating temperature, the allegation is that engines run higher effective compression ratios — worth up to 0.3 seconds per lap according to the complainants.

The FIA has not issued a ruling. The controversy remains unresolved. If the allegation is upheld, the competitive picture of 2026 becomes even more complicated retroactively.

McLaren's Response

The team has been public about their situation without being paralyzed by it. They acknowledged a timeline to "close the gap" and have brought significant upgrade packages — 7 new parts at Miami, further development at subsequent rounds. Their engineers understand the root cause. The question is whether they can close the software gap before the season ends.

The defending champions were not counted out at mid-season. They have the resources, the technical staff, and the competitive drive to respond. But the structural disadvantage created by the 2026 regulations doesn't resolve through hardware alone — and hardware is the only avenue available to a customer team.

What's Next

Belgium and Hungary represent the final two rounds before the summer break. Both circuits reward consistent energy deployment — Spa's long straights and Hungaroring's low-speed corners present different challenges, but both expose the software gap that has defined the season.

McLaren's best hope is wet conditions at Spa. In the rain, energy management calculations become secondary to car control and strategic judgment. On a dry track, the works-versus-customer differential doesn't disappear. The forecast suggests Belgium might offer McLaren their best opportunity for a result that reflects their actual pace rather than their technical disadvantage.

They won last year. They haven't gone anywhere. But closing 154 points before the calendar runs out requires something more than they currently have.
