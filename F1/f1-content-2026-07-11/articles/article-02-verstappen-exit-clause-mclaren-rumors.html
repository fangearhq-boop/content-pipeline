Verstappen's Exit Clause Is Coming — McLaren Denies Everything, But the Horner Calls Keep Happening

By Marcus Cole | July 11, 2026

F1 Driver Market

Max Verstappen is seventh in the 2026 Formula 1 World Drivers' Championship with 76 points. He is 103 points behind Kimi Antonelli. Two rounds remain before the summer break — Belgium and Hungary — with a combined maximum of approximately 50 points on offer including Sprint weekends. The mathematics of reaching a top-two championship finish are no longer possible.

His Red Bull contract runs through the end of 2028. It also contains performance-related exit clauses on Verstappen's side. One of them is about to trigger.

How the Clause Works

The specific terms of Verstappen's exit clause have not been publicly disclosed in their entirety, but credible reporting since the Canadian Grand Prix has established the framework: the clause activates if Verstappen cannot achieve a top-two finish in the drivers' championship. Given his current position and the points available before the summer, that threshold will mathematically become impossible to reach before August.

The clause doesn't automatically transfer Verstappen anywhere. It gives him the right to exit his Red Bull contract — not an obligation to do so. The next step involves his choosing whether to exercise that right, and if so, where to go.

This is where the rumors intensify.

McLaren Says No

Reports from multiple outlets claimed Verstappen was in "closing stages" of a deal with McLaren that would involve Oscar Piastri moving to Red Bull in a swap arrangement. McLaren CEO Zak Brown categorically denied any such talks. Piastri's camp indicated nothing had changed. RacingNews365, citing sources close to the Australian, reported he is set to stay at McLaren for the foreseeable future.

McLaren's denial is firm enough that it either ends the McLaren conversation entirely — or confirms it so completely that denying it becomes the team's only available public position. In F1 driver market negotiations, categorical denials from teams whose driver is under active contract are standard procedure regardless of what's actually happening in private meetings.

Piastri, for his part, signed a deal through 2028 that reportedly contains exit clause options of his own. His situation mirrors Verstappen's in that flexibility exists on both sides.

The Horner Factor

Verstappen has confirmed he speaks to former Red Bull team principal Christian Horner "almost every week." Horner was fired by Red Bull following the 2025 British Grand Prix after a highly public internal controversy and has not returned to the paddock until Silverstone last weekend — his first race attendance since his departure.

Horner has indicated he would return to F1 "only on his terms." The interpretation of what that phrase means has driven significant speculation: a return to Red Bull, a new team, or some involvement in whatever move Verstappen ultimately makes.

The weekly Verstappen-Horner calls are the most concrete indicator that the situation is more than passive. Two of the most successful driver-principal partnerships in the sport's recent history don't maintain weekly contact simply as a social habit.

Verstappen's Own Words

Verstappen has been clearer about what he doesn't want than where he's going. At the Canadian Grand Prix, he described the 2026 regulations as "not mentally doable" — specifically citing the near 50/50 combustion-to-electric power split that requires constant "lifting and coasting" to manage battery deployment. He called it "anti-driving." Sources close to him characterized the retirement threat as genuine rather than political.

The FIA reached an agreement in principle to shift to a 60/40 combustion-favored split for 2027. Ferrari, Audi, and Cadillac are officially objecting to that change, which could block the regulatory relief Verstappen is seeking. If the fix he wants doesn't materialize, the motivation to remain in F1 at all is genuinely in question.

What's Next

Belgium and Hungary will complete the pre-summer chapter of the 2026 season. When the summer break begins, Verstappen's exit clause window will almost certainly be open. His decision — exercise it or not — will reshape the 2027 driver market for every team on the grid.

McLaren's denial is on record. Piastri's commitment is stated. Verstappen's conversations with Horner are ongoing. None of those three things are contradictory — yet. Watch the summer break for when one of them changes.
