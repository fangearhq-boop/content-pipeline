Verstappen's Exit Clause Window Opens After This Weekend — Aston Martin Now Leads the Race

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap | July 23, 2026

When the Hungarian Grand Prix ends on Sunday and Formula 1 enters its three-week summer break, another countdown will begin simultaneously. Max Verstappen's exit clause at Red Bull Racing — linked to the performance of Red Bull Powertrains-Ford's 2026 power unit — becomes actionable between the summer break and an October deadline.

In other words: the summer break is also the Max Verstappen decision season.

McLaren Is Off the Table

The most talked-about destination for months was McLaren. That door is now firmly closed. Oscar Piastri confirmed he will remain at McLaren for the 2027 season. CEO Zak Brown stated publicly that there were "no conversations" with Verstappen. The rumoured seat-swap — Verstappen to McLaren, Piastri to Red Bull — was officially denied by both McLaren principals and appears to be dead.

Verstappen himself addressed the topic at Spa, offering a series of dismissals before moving on. For a driver who typically doesn't offer more words than necessary on sensitive subjects, the message was clear.

Why Aston Martin Is Now the Leading Candidate

With McLaren closed, the picture narrows. The most credible destination currently is Aston Martin, and the reason can be summarized in two words: Adrian Newey.

Newey, the legendary aerodynamicist and technical director who built the cars behind Verstappen's first four World Championships at Red Bull, left the team this year and joined Aston Martin. His first full design project at Aston Martin will be the 2027 car. For a driver like Verstappen — who has consistently performed at his absolute ceiling in machinery built around his feedback, and who credits much of his success to the quality of the cars beneath him — the opportunity to have Newey build a new-generation car specifically around his preferences is a genuine draw.

The financial backdrop at Aston Martin is also significant. Lawrence Stroll has committed substantial resources to making Aston Martin a championship contender. The team has signed elite engineering talent across the board. The timeline is credible.

The Alonso Variable

There is, however, a complication. Fernando Alonso's contract with Aston Martin expires at the end of the 2026 season. His future has not been resolved. Aston Martin has not publicly indicated whether it will renew Alonso — a 45-year-old two-time world champion who remains competitive — or open a seat for a different driver.

If Verstappen's move to Aston Martin is contingent on a seat opening, the Alonso negotiation is part of the calculus. Verstappen is not going to wait indefinitely — the October deadline on his clause means decisions need to be made before the end of the year.

Red Bull's Internal Landscape

The situation at Red Bull also adds context. Both Gianpiero Lambiase — Verstappen's race engineer and a crucial part of his championship-winning operation — and Helmut Marko, the long-serving senior advisor who has been one of the most influential figures in Red Bull's F1 programme, are departing or have been replaced. These are not small personnel changes. They represent a meaningful shift in the environment that helped make Verstappen the driver he is.

Raymond Vermeulen, Verstappen's manager, has offered supportive language about Red Bull without closing the door on the clause. It's the kind of carefully worded statement that says nothing while reserving everything.

What's Next

The summer break is where these conversations typically move from rumour to reality in Formula 1. Three weeks of quiet is rarely quiet at all — it's when phones ring, meetings happen, and terms get discussed. Verstappen's 2027 decision is the biggest remaining story in the driver market, and the answer is likely coming before the season resumes.
