The Entire F1 Grid Is Confronting the FIA Stewards This Weekend — Here's Why

By Elena Voss | F1 Fanrecap | July 23, 2026

Something unusual is happening at the Hungarian Grand Prix, and it has nothing to do with lap times or tyre strategies. The entire Formula 1 driver grid has formally requested — and been granted — a meeting with FIA stewards during the race weekend to discuss driving standards and stewarding consistency.

This is not a common occurrence. F1 drivers are, by design and temperament, competitive individuals who typically address officiation through official team protest channels. When every driver on the grid speaks with one voice, it reflects something deeper.

What Happened at Spa

The immediate flashpoint was the Belgian Grand Prix at Spa-Francorchamps on July 19. In that race, two separate stewarding decisions drew scrutiny — and their contrast is what pushed the drivers toward collective action.

Lewis Hamilton received a 5-second time penalty for an unsafe pit release. During Hamilton's pit stop, a mechanic was in the path of the departing car. The stewards ruled it a dangerous release and issued the time penalty. Ferrari, as the constructor, was also separately fined. In a race decided by seconds, these penalties directly affected Hamilton's final result.

In the same race, Charles Leclerc was investigated for contact with Oscar Piastri. The stewards cleared Leclerc — no further action. Sky Sports commentator David Croft called out the apparent inconsistency publicly during post-race coverage. Ferrari itself challenged the stewards' approach in formal channels. And across the paddock, drivers were having the conversation privately that they eventually agreed to have publicly.

What Consistency Actually Means in F1

Stewarding in Formula 1 is inherently complex. No two incidents are identical — speed, angles, intent, and circuit characteristics all factor into decisions. The FIA has, over the years, moved toward a rotating panel of stewards that includes a former driver, a structure designed to bring racing perspective to the process.

But complexity is not the same as arbitrariness, and drivers are asking for the latter to be addressed. The question they're bringing to Budapest is essentially: what is the consistent standard, and how is it applied? If contact in one corner results in no action while an unsafe release in the pit lane results in a time penalty, what principle connects those outcomes?

It's a fair question. And the fact that all twenty drivers on the grid agreed it was worth asking together suggests they believe the current state of answering it is unsatisfactory.

What Comes Out of Budapest

Meetings of this kind typically produce one of two outcomes: a meaningful dialogue that results in FIA communication about standards and applications (which then either manifests in changed race week decisions or doesn't), or a broadly worded statement about ongoing collaboration that changes nothing visible in practice.

The 2026 season's new technical regulations have also introduced new types of incidents — the active aerodynamics system, new power unit management requirements, and lighter cars have all changed the physics of overtaking and contact. The stewards are applying principles to new circumstances, and drivers feel the application has been uneven.

What's Next

The meeting is scheduled for this weekend in Budapest. F1 Fanrecap will report what comes out of it. Whether it produces meaningful change in how the second half of the 2026 season is officiated remains to be seen — but the fact that the drivers organized a unified front to demand it is itself the news.
