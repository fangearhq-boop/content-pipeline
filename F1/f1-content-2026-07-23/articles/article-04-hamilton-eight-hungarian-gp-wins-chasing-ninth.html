Eight Wins and Counting: Can Hamilton Make It Nine in Hungary?

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap | July 23, 2026

No driver in Formula 1 history has won the Hungarian Grand Prix more times than Lewis Hamilton. His eight victories at the Hungaroring — accumulated across his years at McLaren and then Mercedes — represent a dominance at a single venue that is essentially without comparison in the modern era of the sport.

This weekend, Hamilton arrives at the same circuit wearing different colours. He is in his second season at Ferrari. He is 45 points behind Kimi Antonelli in the championship. And he needs something significant to happen in Budapest.

The Record

Hamilton's eight Hungarian GP victories place him alone at the top of the all-time list for this event. No other driver has won here more than five times. The Hungaroring — with its narrow, twisty layout and premium on high-downforce setup — has historically been a track that rewards precise technical driving, the methodical building of lap times across a stint, and the ability to manage tyre temperatures in tricky conditions.

Those are things Hamilton has done as well as anyone in the history of the sport. The circuit suits his style, and his history there is not a coincidence.

Ferrari at the Hungaroring

The other reason Hamilton enters the weekend as a favourite: the SF-26 is well-suited to this circuit. Ferrari's 2026 car has shown consistent strength in medium-speed, high-downforce environments. The Hungaroring is defined by those characteristics. Both Hamilton and Charles Leclerc have been identified as pre-event favourites based on the circuit data and Ferrari's technical assessment.

A Ferrari victory at Hungary would also be Ferrari's first Hungarian GP win since — and the team will be keenly aware of that additional motivation. For a team with Ferrari's history, specific circuit records have their own weight.

The Championship Calculation

Hamilton needs a strong result in Budapest for straightforward reasons: Antonelli's 45-point lead is substantial, and the summer break that follows this race freezes the standings for three weeks. A win or podium closes the gap. A race outside the top five — especially if Antonelli finishes well — begins to push the title out of mathematical conversation.

The Hungarian GP is one of the best opportunities Hamilton will have in the second half of the 2026 season to make a statement. He knows the track. His car suits the conditions. The historical record suggests it is his venue.

What's Next

Practice begins Friday, qualifying Saturday, and the race Sunday at 8:00 AM ET. A ninth Hamilton victory at the Hungaroring would be both a record extension and one of the most meaningful results of his Ferrari tenure. Whether the record extends or remains at eight, the race will be one of the weekend's most-watched storylines.
