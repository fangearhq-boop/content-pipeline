Verstappen's Exit Clause Is Getting Real: 7th in Standings, Weeks to Turn It Around

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap | June 5, 2026

When Max Verstappen negotiated his new Red Bull contract, he built in an escape hatch. The clause allows him to terminate the agreement unilaterally if he is not within the top two of the drivers' championship by the end of July 2026. He insisted on it because of his "longstanding apprehension" about the new 2026 regulations — the shift to a 50-50 internal combustion and electric power split that redesigned Formula 1 from the ground up.

The exit clause once seemed hypothetical. As the Monaco Grand Prix weekend begins, Verstappen sits seventh in the championship standings.

It no longer seems hypothetical.

How Red Bull Got Here

The 2026 regulations that Verstappen feared have played out largely as he suspected. Red Bull's RB22 has lacked the pace of Mercedes and Ferrari through the first five rounds of the season. The team that dominated Formula 1 for the better part of four years — winning championships in 2021, 2022, 2023, and again in 2024 — has not been able to replicate that dominance under the new technical rules.

Verstappen finished outside the top two in four of the first five races. His podium drought ended at the Canadian Grand Prix, where he crossed the line third — behind race winner Kimi Antonelli and Lewis Hamilton — for his first top-three finish of the year. It was a moment of relief for both Verstappen and the team, a signal that the car is improving.

But "improving" and "competitive enough to challenge for the championship" remain very different things. Verstappen is seventh in the standings. The leader, Antonelli, has 131 points. The third-place driver, Hamilton, sits well ahead of Verstappen's position.

The Math

To avoid triggering the exit clause — or to make the clause's expiration irrelevant because the car is genuinely fast — Verstappen needs a dramatic improvement over the coming weeks. The calendar between now and the end of July includes Monaco this weekend, then several more rounds before the deadline closes.

Monaco is not Red Bull's best circuit in a normal year. It rewards precise car-circuit fit, mechanical grip, and driver feel in tight spaces. Verstappen is one of the best drivers on the planet in those conditions, but if the car does not give him confidence through the barriers, there is little he can do to manufacture results that the machinery cannot produce.

Verstappen has reportedly expressed concerns that Monaco's streets may further expose Red Bull's weaknesses relative to Ferrari and Mercedes. That is not a driver who expects a transformative weekend.

What's Next

Verstappen will compete at Monaco this weekend — qualifying Saturday, race Sunday June 7 at 9:00 AM ET on Apple TV+. The exit clause deadline, by most reports, falls at the end of July. Red Bull has several upgrades scheduled that the team hopes will close the gap to the front. Whether those upgrades arrive in time, and whether they are significant enough, remains the central question of what has become one of the most compelling storylines in the 2026 F1 season.

The clause was designed to protect Verstappen if the new rules went badly. The new rules have, so far, gone exactly as he feared. What he decides to do about that will be one of the biggest stories in Formula 1 regardless of how his Monaco weekend unfolds.
