Mercedes Keeps the Leash Off: Antonelli and Russell Free to Race at Monaco

By Elena Voss | F1 Fanrecap | June 5, 2026

After the Canadian Grand Prix produced some of the most tense racing action between teammates that Formula 1 has seen in years, Mercedes sat down with Kimi Antonelli and George Russell this week and gave them their answer: race freely.

The message from the team, following a thorough review of all the on-track incidents in Montreal, was that neither driver would have their racing constrained — as long as they maintained mutual respect and avoided situations that could put both cars out of contention. No team orders. No artificial separation. No protected status for the championship leader.

"You can race each other freely, as long as there's respect, as long as you don't put yourself in situations that could damage one of you or both of you," Antonelli described the team's position. "The team doesn't want to put in any rules."

The Numbers Behind the Decision

The decision is not a risk-free one for Mercedes. Antonelli leads the championship with 131 points — 43 ahead of his teammate Russell in second — and he has won four consecutive grands prix. He is the most dominant driver in Formula 1 right now.

Russell has not won since before Canada, where he was forced to retire from the race following an incident. He is 43 points back and watching a gap that has grown through each of the last few rounds. Monaco, however, is a circuit analysts have identified as one that suits Russell's style — technical, precise, patient — more than most.

If Russell wins in Monaco and Antonelli does not score maximum points, the gap narrows. If both Mercedes drivers push hard at a circuit where barriers are centimeters from the racing line and there is no margin for error, the team risks both cars failing to score. That is the calculation Mercedes has decided to make.

Antonelli's Composure

What has been most striking about Kimi Antonelli this season is not just the results — four wins at 19 years old, leading the championship by 43 points — but the manner in which he carries it. Asked before Monaco whether the championship pressure feels different as the leader versus the hunter, he was almost puzzled by the question.

"Honestly, I don't know. To be frank, I don't really feel this pressure."

He has addressed comparisons to the Hamilton-Rosberg rivalry inside Mercedes that eventually tore the team apart. He understands the history. He is not oblivious to the risks. But he is also a 19-year-old driving the fastest car on the grid in a year when almost everything has gone right for him. The scale of what he is doing has not yet settled into the weight it might someday become.

Russell, for his part, has been candid about the situation. "Right now it's his to lose," he said of Antonelli. That is both accurate and a form of pressure — the implication being that Antonelli can only be beaten if he makes mistakes, and Russell intends to be there when that happens.

What's Next

Monaco is the venue where internal Mercedes storylines have historically come to a head. The narrow barriers and impossible-to-overtake circuit mean that qualifying position dictates everything. If both drivers are in Q3 and fight for the same space on a flying lap, or if they emerge from the pits into traffic at the same time during the race, the team's laissez-faire racing rules will be tested in real time.

Qualifying is Saturday. The race is Sunday at 9:00 AM ET on Apple TV+. Antonelli is the defending champion of the 2026 season so far, but Monaco has its own set of rules — and Russell has been waiting for an opportunity to close the gap.
