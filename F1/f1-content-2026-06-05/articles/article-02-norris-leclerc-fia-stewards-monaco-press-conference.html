Norris and Leclerc Summoned to FIA Stewards Over Monaco Press Conference Breach

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap | June 5, 2026

Lando Norris and Charles Leclerc arrived in Monaco for the 2026 Grand Prix and were promptly handed stewards' summonses — not for anything that happened on the circuit, but for being late to Thursday's mandatory FIA press conference.

Both drivers were cited for an alleged breach of Article B10.1.1a of the FIA Sporting Regulations, which governs attendance at official FIA media obligations during race weekends. A McLaren team representative was also required to attend the hearing, scheduled for 10:10 local time Friday morning — before the first wheel had turned in Free Practice 1.

The incident: the press conference, scheduled to begin at 14:30 local time on Thursday, was delayed by a couple of minutes after Norris and Leclerc did not arrive on time. It is an inelegant start to a weekend that both drivers had been anticipating as a potential breakthrough — particularly Leclerc, whose home circuit sits just outside his front door.

The Likely Outcome

Summonses for late press conference attendance are not unusual in Formula 1. Drivers have been through this process before, and the standard outcome is a financial penalty — a fine that ranges from a few thousand euros to a more significant sum depending on the specific circumstances and any prior violations.

There is no indication from any source that this incident will result in a grid penalty or any action that affects either driver's ability to compete this weekend. FIA enforcement of media obligations, while taken seriously as a matter of contract and regulation, does not typically carry sporting consequences unless the breach is repeated and egregious.

Still, having to appear in front of stewards before practice even starts adds an administrative layer that neither driver would have chosen on a high-pressure weekend. For Leclerc in particular — who needs Friday to gather as much setup data as possible on a circuit where qualifying position is decisive — the timing was suboptimal.

Monaco's Capacity for Absurd Pre-Weekend Drama

Monaco has an almost supernatural ability to generate incident before the serious racing begins. The same FP1 session that followed Norris and Leclerc's stewards' summons featured a red-flag collision between Leclerc and Lance Stroll at the Loewes Hairpin. Stroll was found wholly at fault, received a grid drop, and Leclerc went on to post the fastest time despite a broken front wing.

The Monaco Grand Prix does not wait for things to settle down before introducing complications. That is partly why the race has retained its status as the crown jewel of the F1 calendar for more than 70 years. Every lap, every session, every day in Monte Carlo carries the possibility of something extraordinary happening — and sometimes the extraordinary things happen before the cars leave the pits.

What's Next

Norris and Leclerc will complete their stewards' obligations and move forward with their respective race weekends. Norris sits fifth in the championship, 73 points behind leader Kimi Antonelli, and has identified Monaco as a circuit where McLaren can challenge for a strong result. Leclerc, as mentioned, is the 2024 Monaco winner and the early favorite to take pole position this weekend.

The fine, if one is issued, will be an afterthought by the time Sunday's race begins. Monaco's actual drama — the one that involves 78 laps through the most demanding street circuit on the calendar — begins Saturday in qualifying. That is the session both drivers were summoned for being distracted from. It is the only result that will matter when the week is over.
