Monaco 2026 Strategy Explained: FIA Scraps Mandatory Two-Stop Rule

By Elena Voss | May 27, 2026 | F1 Fanrecap

The Monaco Grand Prix is nine days away. Before it arrives, there is one strategic reality every Formula 1 fan needs to understand: the mandatory minimum two-stop rule that defined the 2025 Monaco GP has been scrapped by the FIA for 2026.

Monaco returns to free strategy. Teams choose how many times they pit. There is no minimum obligation. The Circuit de Monaco, which runs June 5-7, will be governed by the same sporting regulations that apply to every other race on the calendar.

For viewers who watched the 2025 Monaco GP wondering why cars were lapping significantly below their potential pace at key moments in the race, the answer is about to change.

Why the 2025 Rule Failed

The mandatory two-stop concept was introduced for the 2025 Monaco Grand Prix with a legitimate goal: create more action on a circuit where overtaking is nearly impossible and processional racing has become the race format's biggest criticism. If teams had to pit twice, the theory went, the additional pit windows would shuffle the order and create racing moments that the circuit itself cannot generate.

The problem was a loophole that teams exploited almost immediately. When two cars on the same team are running in close proximity, the team can instruct the car behind to deliberately run slower — backing up the field — to create artificial traffic that freezes competitors on track. The lead car then pits at its ideal time, re-enters traffic-free, and maintains position. The second car pits during the artificially created chaos.

Racing Bulls and Williams were among the teams seen exploiting this tactic in 2025, running four seconds per lap below their potential pace to manipulate pit windows. The result was racing that felt even more contrived than the one-stop processional races the rule was designed to replace.

The FIA removed the clauses relating to Monaco's mandatory tyre usage from the 2026 sporting regulations, and the rule is gone.

What Free Strategy Means for 2026

With no minimum pit stop obligation, Monaco in 2026 becomes a pure strategic puzzle. Teams will model tire degradation across the entire 78-lap race distance and choose whether a one-stop or two-stop approach maximizes their finishing position.

The 2026 Pirelli compound selection for Monaco will be central to this calculation. Soft tires offer the best single-lap performance but degrade faster, usually forcing an earlier stop. Medium tires last longer but surrender lap time. Hard compounds at Monaco are rarely used in race trim because they never reach optimal operating temperature on the cool, low-speed streets of Monte Carlo.

In practical terms: the team that gets its pit timing right in clear air will often win Monaco. Track position is so valuable — passing is so rare — that the margin for error on strategy timing is almost zero. Free strategy amplifies that pressure, because there is no backup obligation forcing a second stop. Every decision is entirely self-determined.

How It Affects the Championship Contenders

For Kimi Antonelli and his championship lead, Monaco free strategy is relatively low-risk. Antonelli is 43 points clear of George Russell and could theoretically take a conservative single-stop approach that prioritizes finishing over winning. A P3 or P4 in Monaco extends the lead without exposing the car to unnecessary risk on the narrow streets.

For Charles Leclerc — who won Monaco in 2024 and whose Ferrari SF-26 is expected to be competitive at the low-speed circuit — free strategy creates an opportunity. If Ferrari identifies a faster one-stop window that Mercedes cannot match on tire life, a strategic undercut could produce the race result that closes the championship gap without requiring an on-track overtake that Monaco cannot deliver.

For George Russell, whose stated goal after Canada is to "win every single race," free strategy removes the constraints that could limit an aggressive approach. He can run long on the first set of tires if the degradation allows, take advantage of a late safety car, or simply build a gap early and manage it to the line.

Monaco 2026 will be a different race from 2025. Whether it is a better one depends on who has the fastest car, the cleanest strategy call — and who avoids the barriers.
