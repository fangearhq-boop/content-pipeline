FIA's 2026 Rules Summit Begins: Technical Meetings Underway as April 20 Vote Looms

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap

April 9, 2026

The process that could reshape the 2026 Formula 1 season — or keep it exactly as it is — began on Thursday, April 9, with the FIA convening the first of its formal technical meetings to evaluate potential rule changes. Technical representatives from all teams, the FIA, and FOM were present. Team principals were not. No decisions were made.

That last point is important context. Today's session was discussion only — a structured technical forum for presenting proposals, stress-testing assumptions, and building consensus before the meeting that actually matters: April 20, when team principals will gather for a session where binding decisions could be made, subject to World Motor Sport Council approval. The goal remains getting changes in place before the Miami Grand Prix on May 1-3.

What's Being Discussed

The core proposals center on the energy management architecture that has defined — and frustrated — the 2026 cars. The FIA is evaluating raising the "super clipping" threshold from the current 250 kW to 350 kW. Super clipping is the cut-off at which a car's power delivery is artificially limited; raising the ceiling would mean less severe power differential between cars in different energy states.

A companion proposal would reduce the maximum energy recovery allowed per lap from 9 megajoules to 6 MJ. Together, these changes are intended to reduce the dramatic speed differentials that occur when one car is in full boost mode while another is in energy-conservation mode — the exact scenario that created Oliver Bearman's 50G crash at Spoon Curve during the Japanese Grand Prix.

Additional proposals include changes to qualifying format and the designated energy-saving zones around circuits.

The Safety Case

Bearman's crash has become the central exhibit in the argument for change. The Haas driver was deploying energy through Spoon Curve when he encountered the Alpine of Franco Colapinto, which was in lift-and-coast mode — the speed differential between the two cars in that moment was approximately 50 kilometers per hour. Bearman hit Colapinto at closing speed and survived a 50G impact. That he escaped with a bruised knee is, in the assessment of many in the paddock, fortunate.

The crash crystallized a danger that drivers had been warning about throughout pre-season and the opening races. Max Verstappen's calls for the regulations to be fundamentally overhauled — however loudly delivered — are rooted in exactly this risk: cars behaving unpredictably relative to each other depending on their energy state is an inherently dangerous condition at Formula 1 speeds.

What's Next

A second technical meeting follows Thursday's session, refining proposals and aligning technical positions before the team principals enter the room on April 20. Any rule changes agreed on that date would need World Motor Sport Council approval to take effect — an additional procedural layer that means the Miami Grand Prix serves as the practical deadline for implementation.

Whether the changes that emerge from this process are the substantial overhaul that Verstappen and others have demanded, or the more "marginal" adjustments that Verstappen himself expected, will shape not just the racing product in Florida — but potentially the future of F1's most prominent driver.
