Verstappen's Exit Clause: Four Weeks to the Hungarian GP Deadline

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap

June 29, 2026

Four weeks from today, one of the most consequential races in recent Formula 1 history will take place — not because of what happens on track, but because of what happens to Max Verstappen's contract afterward. The Hungarian Grand Prix on July 26 is the trigger date for a performance clause that could upend the sport's competitive order for 2027 and beyond.

Verstappen's Red Bull contract is understood to contain a clause tied to the constructors' championship standings at the conclusion of the Hungarian GP: if Red Bull is not within the top two constructors at that point, Verstappen gains the right to exit his deal early. Red Bull currently sits approximately 98 points behind championship leader Mercedes in constructors — a gap that has proved impossible to close at this stage of the season.

The Numbers Behind the Deadline

To be clear about what this clause actually requires: it is not a performance threshold on Verstappen's personal results. It is a team benchmark. Red Bull must be No. 1 or No. 2 in constructors when the Hungarian GP concludes on July 26. With nine rounds remaining (counting Hungary), Ferrari sits at No. 2 with 204 points — ahead of Red Bull at approximately 115.

The gap between Red Bull and Ferrari in constructors is now large enough that even a run of perfect results from this point would be an enormous challenge. The clause, practically speaking, is widely considered already triggered in all but official terms — the Hungarian GP is simply the date it becomes contractually formal.

Verstappen's P2 finish in the Austrian GP was among the most impressive drives of his recent career — recovering from near the back of the grid to finish on the podium. It keeps him mathematically alive in the drivers' championship, but at 98 points behind Antonelli, the title picture is extremely difficult.

The Buyout and the Market

Behind the scenes, negotiations are reportedly already in motion. Red Bull is understood to be offering an 8-figure buyout — a nine-figure contract settlement — to facilitate Verstappen's early departure and allow all parties to plan the 2027 season with clarity rather than ambiguity.

McLaren has been the name most frequently connected to Verstappen's future. Reports of secret talks between McLaren and Verstappen's representatives — with a Piastri swap scenario reportedly among the scenarios discussed — have been circulating for weeks. Any deal involving McLaren would require moving Oscar Piastri, who has been a cornerstone of McLaren's long-term planning. That makes such a swap significant rather than routine.

Mercedes has been definitively ruled out. Team principal Toto Wolff publicly confirmed that the 2027 driver lineup will be George Russell and Kimi Antonelli — the same pairing that is currently first and second in the drivers' championship. There is no seat available at the dominant team.

What This Means for the Hungarian GP

The Hungaroring on July 26 now carries weight that transcends the race itself. However the weekend unfolds on track, what happens in the aftermath — whether the clause activates, whether Red Bull accepts it, whether a buyout is agreed — will shape the 2027 grid in ways that the race result almost certainly will not.

Verstappen has not made any public statement about his intentions or preferences beyond what can be inferred from reports. That silence, in the context of a career that has featured considerable public engagement on contract and competitive matters, speaks to the sensitivity of the situation.

What's Next

The British Grand Prix at Silverstone (July 3-5) comes before Hungary, and Verstappen will race knowing the clock is running. Watch for any statement — from Red Bull, from McLaren, from Verstappen's management — in the weeks between Silverstone and Budapest. The official deadline is July 26, but the negotiating window is already open.

The F1 landscape going into 2027 will look dramatically different from what anyone imagined at the start of this season. The only question is how different — and for whom.
