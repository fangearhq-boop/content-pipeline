Cadillac's Austrian GP Disaster: Both Cars on Fire, Zero Points in Eight Races

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap

June 29, 2026

There are bad race weekends, and then there are the kind of race weekends that define a team's early struggles in the most dramatic and visible way possible. The Austrian Grand Prix was the latter for Cadillac.

The Mario Andretti Cadillac team — racing the MAC-26, powered by a Ferrari customer engine — entered the Red Bull Ring with their most significant upgrade package of the 2026 season: new sidepods and a revised floor designed to unlock aerodynamic performance they had been unable to access through the first seven rounds. What happened instead is now a piece of Formula 1 debut season history.

Valtteri Bottas retired on lap 2. Sergio Perez retired on laps 4-5. Both cars caught fire due to brake failures. Both retirements came before the race had properly begun. The upgrade package that was supposed to represent a turning point never ran a meaningful lap at speed.

Zero Points in Eight Races

Those two retirements left Cadillac's 2026 championship points total at the same number it has held since the season opener: zero.

In eight race weekends, the MAC-26 has not scored a single point. For context, the current points structure awards points to the top 10 finishers — meaning Cadillac has not managed a top-10 finish that the scorer has counted in their debut season. The only time they came close was Monaco, where a P10 finish offered the promise of their first point — only for a 10-second time penalty to strip it away officially, leaving them with nothing to show from their only near-result of the campaign.

"We cannot have these sort of issues," said Perez after climbing out of his car at the Red Bull Ring. The statement was measured and accurate. A team running a Ferrari customer engine — one of the most competitive power units in the paddock — should not be producing brake fires that retire both cars in the opening minutes of a race, particularly on a weekend when they brought their biggest development package.

The Anatomy of a Double DNF

Brake fires in Formula 1 typically result from overheating caused by inadequate cooling, over-aggressive brake bias settings, or brake component failures during the specific demands of a circuit. The Red Bull Ring features a challenging series of heavy braking zones that place particular demands on brake systems — but the same circuit has been raced by every other team without comparable failure rates.

The MAC-26's upgrade package — the new sidepods and revised floor — was the team's primary focus entering Austria. Whether the aerodynamic changes affected brake cooling architecture, or whether the failures were independent of the upgrade, is a question the team's engineering group (led by technical principal Graeme Lowdon and engineering consultant Pat Symonds) will need to answer before Silverstone.

The upgrade itself remains uncharacterized by performance. After two retirements in five laps, there is simply no data from which to judge whether the new aerodynamic package would have improved pace. The team entered Austria with hope and left without information.

The Bigger Picture

Cadillac entered Formula 1 as America's first new constructor in decades — a moment of genuine historic significance for the sport's expansion into the United States market. The team carries enormous weight in terms of commercial and political significance beyond their racing results. The Andretti brand carries tremendous prestige in American motorsport history.

But Formula 1's paddock is unsparing. Eight races and zero points is a record that speaks for itself, regardless of debut season status or the complexity of building an F1 car from scratch. The Monaco penalty added a particularly painful footnote — a P10 finish that could have been the team's first milestone moment, erased by a time penalty.

What's Next

The British Grand Prix at Silverstone is next (July 3-5), and Cadillac will need to diagnose the brake failure root cause before arriving at a circuit that places different demands on the braking system than Austria. Silverstone's high-speed corners generate different types of aerodynamic and thermal loads than the heavy-braking zones at the Red Bull Ring.

The upgrade package — if the brake issue is resolved — could theoretically still show progress at Silverstone. But Cadillac needs races, not more retirements. Eight rounds in, the margin for "debut season growing pains" is narrowing. The team must find points, and soon, or the 2026 season will become a historical footnote of a very different kind.
