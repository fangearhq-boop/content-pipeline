FIA Unlocks Mid-Season Power Unit Development After Canada GP: What ADUO Means for Honda and Audi

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap | May 17, 2026

Formula 1's 2026 power unit regulations were always going to produce a shakedown period. After four races, it's clear which manufacturers are at the front and which are struggling to keep pace. The FIA has now opened a pathway for the latter group to close the gap.

The governing body has confirmed a mid-season tweak to the 2026 power unit rules, introducing the ADUO — Accelerated Development and Update Option — for manufacturers facing significant performance deficits. The option activates after the fifth race of the 2026 season: the Canadian Grand Prix, running May 22-24 at Circuit Gilles-Villeneuve.

Honda (supplying Aston Martin) and Audi (in their debut F1 season with the former Sauber team) are expected to be the primary beneficiaries.

The 2026 Regulations and the Problem

The 2026 F1 regulations represent the most significant rule change the sport has seen in years. At the heart of the changes is a radical shift in the power unit: the internal combustion engine has had its output reduced while the electric motor's contribution has tripled. The result is roughly a 50/50 split between petrol and electric power — with the new MGU-K delivering approximately 350kW compared to around 120kW in previous seasons.

The MGU-H, the complex heat recovery system that was previously part of the hybrid package, has been abolished entirely. This simplification was intended to reduce costs and encourage new manufacturers to enter. Audi, arriving in 2026, is the most visible result of that policy.

But simplification doesn't mean easy. The new power unit demands a fundamentally different approach to energy management, deployment strategy, and mechanical reliability. Mercedes arrived with answers. Others arrived with questions.

Honda and Audi's Difficult Start

Honda returned to F1 for 2026 as a fully-fledged manufacturer, entering into an exclusive works team agreement with Aston Martin. The Japanese manufacturer had previously supplied Red Bull before ending that relationship ahead of the 2026 regulation change and Red Bull's switch to a new Ford-partnered power unit.

Audi's debut season has been widely described as difficult. Their Miami Grand Prix in particular was reported as "disastrous" by multiple outlets. Beginning with Audi's first-ever F1 campaign in the most complex regulatory environment the sport has seen in a generation, some struggles were expected — but the gap to the front has been larger than the team hoped.

ADUO allows these manufacturers to develop and deploy power unit updates at a faster rate than standard regulations would normally permit, specifically to address performance deficits rather than gain an advantage over already-competitive rivals. The FIA also confirmed a qualifying format tweak as part of the same package of changes.

What's Next

The Canadian Grand Prix (May 22-24) is the final race before ADUO takes effect. If Honda and Audi can introduce meaningful power unit upgrades in the races that follow, the competitive picture could look significantly different by the time F1's European swing gets underway in June. Mercedes' dominance at the front of the field is real — but the regulations are still young, and now the tools exist to accelerate the catch-up. Watch closely in the second half of 2026.
