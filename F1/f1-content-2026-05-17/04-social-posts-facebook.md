The 2026 Canadian Grand Prix (May 22-24) at Circuit Gilles-Villeneuve will be the first-ever sprint race weekend held at this legendary track. The circuit moved to May for the first time since 1982, as part of F1's calendar reshuffle that concentrates the European season from June onward.

### STORY 2: Antonelli Historic Start

#### Long-Form Post — 10:00 AM ET

The record books are being rewritten in real time by a teenager.

Kimi Antonelli, at 19 years old, is the first driver in Formula 1 history to win his first three Grand Prix starts from pole position. Not the first young driver. Not the first Mercedes driver. The first driver — ever, in the sport's entire history.

The wins came at the Chinese Grand Prix, Japanese Grand Prix, and Miami Grand Prix. Each time, Antonelli took pole and converted it to victory. At Miami, he overcame wheel-to-wheel battles with Charles Leclerc and Lando Norris to take the flag.

He also became the youngest championship leader in F1 history — just 19 years, 7 months, and 4 days old when he first claimed the points lead.

His current tally of 100 points after four races represents the strongest points-scoring start to an F1 season in ten years. He leads teammate George Russell by 20 points, with Ferrari's Leclerc (59 pts), Hamilton (51 pts), and McLaren's Norris (51 pts) further back.

The question for Montreal: can anyone slow him down?

### STORY 3: Verstappen on Lambiase Departure

#### Long-Form Post — 12:00 PM ET

Max Verstappen has broken his silence on one of the biggest stories in Formula 1 this season.

Race engineer Gianpiero Lambiase — Verstappen's partner for four world championships — will leave Red Bull at the end of 2027 and join McLaren. This week, Verstappen revealed what he told Lambiase when he found out.

"He told me about the offer he received," Verstappen explained. "I told him, 'You'd be crazy not to take it.' We've achieved so much together and now he has a chance to move forward."

It's a remarkably mature and gracious response from a driver who has been publicly open about the fact that Lambiase's departure could affect his own decision-making. Verstappen previously said he would consider leaving Red Bull when Lambiase goes.

Red Bull is maintaining that nothing has changed. Their Ford engine partner has stressed that their "F1 commitment is not based on one person." Team Principal Laurent Mekies says Lambiase will fulfill his existing contract through 2027 before taking his new role.

But Verstappen still has an exit clause at the Hungarian Grand Prix (July 24-26). And he's 7th in the championship with 26 points, well behind the leaders, as Red Bull continues to struggle with the 2026 regulations.

The Lambiase saga isn't over yet.

### STORY 4: FIA Mid-Season Power Unit Rule Change

#### Long-Form Post — 2:00 PM ET

Formula 1's 2026 regulation overhaul was always going to produce winners and losers in the early races. Now the FIA is giving the losers a lifeline.

The governing body has confirmed a mid-season tweak to the 2026 power unit rules, introducing ADUO — the Accelerated Development and Update Option — for manufacturers who are struggling with the new regulations. The option becomes available after the fifth race of the season: the Canadian Grand Prix, taking place May 22-24.

Honda, powering Aston Martin, and Audi, in their debut season after taking over Sauber, are expected to benefit most. Both have faced significant early-season difficulties adapting to F1's new 50/50 electric-ICE power split, which features a greatly strengthened MGU-K electric motor (350kW, up from 120kW in previous seasons) and the removal of the MGU-H heat recovery system.

The FIA also confirmed a tweak to the qualifying format alongside the power unit changes.

Mercedes has dominated with the new regulations — Antonelli leads the championship at 100 points — but this change could reshape the second half of 2026 considerably. Audi in particular has had a difficult start to their F1 debut, and the chance to accelerate development mid-season could produce significant improvement by autumn.

If Ferrari is going to make a serious championship run, Montreal might be the place to start.

Lewis Hamilton has won six times at Circuit Gilles-Villeneuve. Six. No driver knows this track better. The heavy-braking chicanes, the hairpin, the Wall of Champions — Hamilton has navigated all of it more often than almost anyone who has ever driven in Formula 1.

He arrives for the 2026 Canadian Grand Prix fourth in the championship with 51 points — 49 behind Kimi Antonelli. Teammate Charles Leclerc is third with 59 points, 41 behind the leader.

Ferrari needs a strong result before the calendar shifts fully to Europe. Mercedes has been dominant through four races, but Circuit Gilles-Villeneuve has unique characteristics. The low-downforce, stop-start layout rewards brake management and efficient energy recovery — areas where Ferrari has historically been competitive.

Both Hamilton and Leclerc know the math. A win for either Ferrari driver, combined with an Antonelli non-score, would reopen the championship entirely. That's not a fantasy — it's exactly the kind of volatile weekend that Canada has historically produced.

Hamilton has done it here before. Can he do it as a Ferrari driver?
