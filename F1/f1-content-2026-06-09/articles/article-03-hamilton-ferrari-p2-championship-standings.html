Hamilton Is No. 2 in the World at Ferrari — And the Legacy Project Is Working

By Elena Voss | June 9, 2026 | F1 Fanrecap

When Lewis Hamilton joined Ferrari ahead of the 2025 Formula 1 season, the move prompted as much skepticism as excitement. He was 40. He had driven for Mercedes his entire F1 career. He was leaving the team with which he had won six of his seven world championships. Was it the right call at the right time?

Six races into 2026, the answer is taking shape. Hamilton sits second in the Formula 1 championship with 90 points. He leapfrogged his former Mercedes teammate George Russell at Monaco, where a clean P2 finish reinforced what has been a consistent theme of Hamilton's Ferrari tenure: he is still performing at the very highest level.

What Monaco Showed

Monaco is a race that punishes mistakes and rewards precision. It is one of the few circuits where experience and composure can compensate for a car disadvantage. Hamilton took everything Monaco asked for and delivered it — clean qualifying, disciplined race management, and a podium finish in second place that kept Ferrari firmly in the championship conversation.

His Monaco result — P2 behind Kimi Antonelli — was widely described as one of Ferrari's strongest recent finishes at the street circuit in Monte Carlo. Where Ferrari has historically struggled in Monaco's confined, low-speed environment, Hamilton found pace when it mattered and delivered points with efficiency.

The Championship Context

At 90 points, Hamilton trails championship leader Antonelli by 66. The gap is real and would require significant Antonelli difficulties to meaningfully close. But second place in the championship at this stage of the season is not a consolation prize — it is the foundation for what could become a genuine late-season push if circumstances shift.

Consider the comparison to Russell: Hamilton's former teammate is now 68 points behind Antonelli at third in the standings, two points below Hamilton, after a series of results that have fallen apart through a combination of reliability and racing incidents. Hamilton, by contrast, has been the model of consistency — taking the points available, avoiding unnecessary risks, and banking the kind of results that make him dangerous in the second half of the season.

The Ferrari Picture

Ferrari's 2026 season is, by most assessments, exceeding expectations. They trail Mercedes in the constructors' championship but they are clearly the second-fastest team, with Hamilton providing the consistent scoring needed to build a constructors' challenge. Charles Leclerc, despite a Monaco DNF after crashing at the Anthony Noghes corner during a red flag restart, remains a race-winning talent on his day.

The narrative of Hamilton's time at Ferrari was always going to be written in titles, not podiums. One podium doesn't answer the championship question. But the track record through six rounds — second in the championship, consistent pace, a teammate who is pushing from a strong foundation — suggests the Ferrari move is developing into exactly what Hamilton hoped it would be.

What's Next

Hamilton will take his championship position to Barcelona for the Spanish Grand Prix this weekend. It is a circuit that should suit a fast, well-balanced car, and Ferrari's recent form at Spanish circuits has been strong.

The title gap to Antonelli is significant but not insurmountable over 16 remaining races. The legacy project continues. And at 41 years old, Lewis Hamilton is proving that the ambition and the ability are still entirely intact.
