Red Bull's Miami Moment of Truth: 'No Miracle Turnaround' as Three-Stage Fix Begins

By Elena Voss April 6, 2026

The Honest Assessment

Red Bull team principal Laurent Mekies did not soften it. "There will be no miracle turnaround before Miami." That kind of candor — an F1 team principal publicly acknowledging that their car will remain significantly uncompetitive at the next race — is unusual in a sport that runs almost entirely on carefully managed optimism. The honesty reflects the scale of the problem and the difficulty of solving it on a Formula 1 race weekend timeline.

Max Verstappen sits ninth in the 2026 drivers' championship with 12 points after three races. He has not finished in the top five in any Grand Prix this season. His rookie teammate Isidore Hadjar has outqualified him multiple times. The RB22, reportedly carrying approximately 10 kilograms of excess weight relative to the minimum, has struggled with aerodynamic balance issues that don't suit Verstappen's high-entry-speed driving style — and with a power unit configuration that creates formation-lap management problems unique to Red Bull among the top teams.

The Three-Stage Recovery Plan

Mekies laid out the structured response: a three-stage fix plan targeting sequential improvement across the coming rounds. Stage 1, arriving at Miami: revised floor geometry and fence configurations designed to address the aerodynamic balance deficit, combined with updated power unit maps that improve deployment consistency in the braking zones — the core of the formation-lap and race-start issues that have haunted Verstappen's grid starts. Stage 2, targeted for Imola: a full aerodynamic overhaul, the most comprehensive single-race update Red Bull has brought in years. Stage 3 represents further refinements based on what Stage 1 and 2 data reveals.

The sequential approach is deliberate. Red Bull's aerodynamic group is working on fundamentally different downforce-generation solutions for the 2026 car, and rushing incomplete updates to Miami would risk introducing new problems on top of existing ones. The floor and PU map changes at Stage 1 are relatively low-risk, high-confidence updates that should yield incremental improvement without negative side effects. The bigger bets come at Imola and beyond.

Verstappen's Unprecedented Position

For a driver who won back-to-back-to-back world championships from 2021 through 2023 and a fourth title in 2024, ninth place in the standings after three races represents a reckoning with circumstances entirely beyond his control. Verstappen hasn't been slow — his lap times on comparable fuel loads and tire states have been reasonable. The car simply isn't fast enough to compensate in the areas that translate to race results.

The exit clause narrative — widely reported but officially unconfirmed — has added an additional layer to what should be a purely technical recovery story. Mekies has confirmed that Verstappen is fully committed through 2026 and has not triggered any exit mechanism. That may be true. It's also worth noting that a driver of Verstappen's caliber watching the championship slip entirely from realistic contention in three races would test the patience of any contract commitment. Red Bull need Stage 1 to show something encouraging at Miami — not just for the constructors' championship, but for the internal dynamics that sustain a team's belief in its own recovery.

What's Next

Red Bull arrives at Miami with Stage 1 of their three-stage fix plan: revised floor, updated fences, and new power unit deployment maps. The Miami Grand Prix begins May 1, with the race on May 3. Whether the Stage 1 update moves Verstappen from ninth toward the points-paying positions — and whether it closes enough of the gap to Mercedes and Ferrari to suggest Stage 2 is on the right trajectory — will determine the narrative of Red Bull's 2026 season. Mekies has set expectations low. The floors will do the talking.
