Bearman's Miami Tightrope: 10 Penalty Points, Two From a Ban, and No Margin for Error

By Marcus Cole April 6, 2026

The Weight of 10 Points

Oliver Bearman heads into the Miami Grand Prix carrying 10 penalty points on his FIA licence — two short of the 12-point threshold that triggers an automatic one-race ban. Those 10 points represent the cumulative cost of incidents across the first three rounds of the 2026 season, with the majority accrued from the 50G crash at Suzuka's Spoon Curve on March 29. The critical detail that makes Bearman's situation particularly unjust: the crash was caused by a regulatory failure — the energy deployment disparity between manufacturers — that the FIA is now holding a dedicated summit to fix.

The penalty point system exists to manage driver behavior and accumulated risk across a season. It's designed to penalize deliberate or negligent acts that endanger other drivers. Bearman's Suzuka crash was neither. The incident arose from a fundamental flaw in the 2026 regulations that created unpredictable speed differentials between cars in the same braking zone — a situation invisible to Bearman and one that the sport's own governing body has acknowledged required urgent correction.

Why Miami Is Fully Exposed

The penalty point system operates on a 12-month rolling basis, with each point carrying an expiry date. The earliest of Bearman's current points will expire on May 23 — approximately three weeks after the Miami Grand Prix concludes. This means that for the entirety of the Miami race weekend, Bearman sits at full exposure: any incident that earns additional penalty points will trigger an automatic ban.

That exposure isn't just a minor inconvenience. At a Sprint format weekend like Miami — where there are two competitive sessions (Sprint Saturday, race Sunday) rather than one — the probability of incidents is inherently higher than at a conventional Grand Prix. A first-lap contact that the stewards view as Bearman's responsibility, an overly aggressive defending move, a safety car restart incident — any of these could produce the two penalty points that would end his Miami weekend immediately and force Haas to replace him for the following race.

Haas's Perspective and Bearman's Season

Haas team principal Ayao Komatsu has confirmed that Bearman will race at Miami, barring any further pre-race incidents. The team understands the constraint and will adapt their race management accordingly — more conservative instructions on defensive driving, clearer communication on risk tolerance. The irony is that Bearman has been one of the more impressive performers of the early 2026 season. His 17 championship points place him fifth in the standings, ahead of Oscar Piastri (21 points — wait, Piastri has 21 and Norris 25), which reflects genuine pace and consistent scoring in a Haas car that operates below the top three teams' level.

The situation places Bearman in an impossible position not of his making. Race conservatively at Miami to protect his licence, and he leaves potential points on the table for Haas and himself. Race at his natural level, and any incident — even one the stewards might classify as a racing incident — risks an automatic ban that removes him from a race he should be competing in. The FIA's regulatory failure created this situation. Bearman is the one who has to navigate its consequences in Florida.

What's Next

Oliver Bearman and Haas arrive at the Miami Grand Prix on May 1 with 10 penalty points and no margin for error. The April 9 FIA summit — which may produce regulation changes designed to prevent the type of incident that generated the points in the first place — offers no relief for Bearman's current situation. The points stand regardless of any regulatory change. His earliest relief comes May 23, after Miami. Until then, Bearman must navigate one of the most challenging circuits on the calendar with the additional constraint of a career-altering penalty point threshold hanging over every maneuver.
