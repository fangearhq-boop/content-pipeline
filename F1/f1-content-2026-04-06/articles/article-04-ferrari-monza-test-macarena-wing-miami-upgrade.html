Ferrari's Monza Test in 16 Days: The Macarena Wing, ADUO Power Unit, and Miami's Three-Point Plan

By Ryan Calloway April 6, 2026

Sixteen Days to Monza, 25 Days to Miami

On April 22 — sixteen days from today — Ferrari will conduct a private test at Monza. Hamilton and Leclerc will drive the SF-26 with a specification closer to what they plan to race at Miami on May 1, including the 'Macarena' rear wing, revised floor geometry, and a power unit software overhaul targeting the super clipping behavior that has limited Ferrari's top-speed performance relative to Mercedes. The test is non-public, low-key, and deeply consequential. What Ferrari learn at Monza will determine whether their Miami package is genuinely competitive or theoretically improved.

The 'Macarena' wing — named, somewhat improbably, after the 1990s dance hit — represents a new aerodynamic philosophy for the SF-26's rear end. Ferrari's aerodynamic team developed the concept during the five-week break, targeting improved airflow management in the diffuser exit area. The wing's distinctive shape creates a different drag-to-downforce relationship than the previous rear wing, theoretically improving efficiency on Miami's combination of medium-speed corners and brief straights.

The ADUO Power Unit: Ferrari's Biggest Prize

The most significant component of Ferrari's Miami package isn't the wing. It's the ADUO power unit application, which has received FIA approval. Ferrari's internal estimates suggest the ADUO unit closes approximately 20 brake horsepower against Mercedes — not enough to eliminate the deficit entirely, but enough to make the SF-26 genuinely competitive in the high-speed sectors where the power gap currently costs the most lap time. For context: the Mercedes power unit advantage has been estimated at 0.5 seconds per lap in some qualifying configurations. Twenty brake horsepower doesn't fully close that gap, but it narrows it from a competitive chasm to a competitive challenge.

Ferrari's power unit director confirmed the FIA approval in the days following Japan, with the ADUO application targeted for introduction at Miami. Hamilton and Leclerc will experience the power unit for the first time under race conditions at Monza, giving the team critical data on reliability and performance before committing it to a Grand Prix weekend.

Hamilton's New Partnership

Lewis Hamilton will also be working with a new race engineer at Miami — Cedric Michel-Grosjean takes over the radio relationship from the previous pairing. In Formula 1, the race engineer-driver relationship is one of the most intimate professional partnerships in sport. The engineer is the voice in the driver's ear during every stint, every pit stop call, every strategy discussion. Hamilton has worked with various engineers across his career, and the change at Miami suggests Ferrari is optimizing every variable as they approach the circuits they expect to be strongest on.

Ferrari's competitiveness has improved significantly since the opening round. Leclerc's 49 points (P3 in championship) and Hamilton's 41 (P4) represent genuine performance in the context of Mercedes' dominance. If the Monza test confirms the Macarena wing and ADUO package work as designed, Ferrari could arrive at Miami as a genuine threat to Mercedes' race win streak.

What's Next

Ferrari's Monza test takes place April 22 — with results expected to remain private until Miami paddock day. The Miami Grand Prix begins May 1, with the Sprint on Saturday May 2 and the race on Sunday May 3. If the three-point package — Macarena wing, ADUO power unit, revised floor — delivers as Ferrari hopes, the constructors' championship gap of 45 points could look very different by the end of May. The 2026 season isn't over. Ferrari is making sure of that.
