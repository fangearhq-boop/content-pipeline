FIA April 9 Summit: Six Regulation Fixes, Three Days, and a Unanimous Agreement Needed

By Ryan Calloway April 6, 2026

The Most Important Meeting of the 2026 Season

Three days from now, the most consequential gathering in the 2026 Formula 1 season will take place. The FIA's April 9 regulation summit brings together team technical chiefs, power unit representatives, and the sport's governing and commercial leadership to address the energy deployment crisis that has threatened safety across the first three rounds of the season. Six potential fixes are on the table. One condition applies: unanimous agreement from all ten teams is required before any change can be implemented.

That condition is the meeting's central tension. The proposed changes — primarily raising the super clipping power threshold from 250kW to 350kW and reducing the maximum energy harvesting per lap from 9MJ to 7MJ — would benefit teams with lower-output power units while limiting the competitive advantage of higher-output manufacturers. Getting ten teams, each with distinct commercial interests, to agree on measures that directly affect the competitive order is a challenge the sport has never approached easily.

The Safety Case That Made This Summit Unavoidable

The catalyst for the April 9 meeting was Oliver Bearman's 50G crash at Suzuka's Spoon Curve on March 29. Bearman's Haas, decelerating after a high-deployment phase, was struck from behind by Franco Colapinto's Alpine at a closing speed of 45km/h. The incident was traced directly to the energy deployment disparity between manufacturers — different cars harvesting and deploying energy at different rates in the same braking zones, creating unpredictable speed differentials invisible to following drivers.

In subsequent analysis, 93% of fans who responded to an FIA poll stated that regulatory changes were necessary. The FIA and Formula 1 management acknowledged the systemic nature of the issue — unusual candor from a body that typically resists attributing incidents to regulatory failures. Bearman, who emerged unhurt from the 50G impact, received ten penalty points for the incident — points he now carries into Miami with zero margin for error.

What the Six Fixes Involve

The six-point agenda addresses both the immediate safety concern and the medium-term competitive implications of the energy deployment rules. The two most-discussed measures are the super clipping threshold increase (250kW to 350kW) and the per-lap energy cap reduction (9MJ to 7MJ). Super clipping is the FIA's mechanism that limits instantaneous power output during energy recovery; raising the threshold allows more manufacturers to operate at higher output levels, narrowing the gap between the field. Reducing the energy cap limits total available energy per lap, compressing the deployment differences between manufacturers.

The remaining three measures address ancillary issues: aerodynamic zone restrictions that contribute to speed differentials, real-time deployment notification systems for following drivers, and qualifying threshold adjustments. Any or all of these could be adopted independently, provided teams reach agreement — which is why the summit's agenda was structured to allow partial agreement on specific measures rather than requiring all-or-nothing unanimity.

The Voices For and Against

Carlos Sainz has been the summit's most vocal advocate among drivers. His position is straightforward: the Suzuka crash was preventable, the regulatory gap is documented, and the sport owes it to drivers to fix what it created. Lewis Hamilton has expressed public skepticism — not about the need for change, but about the feasibility of implementing meaningful changes in the time available before Miami and the practical implications of retroactive regulation changes mid-season. The divide reflects a genuine tension between urgency and due diligence that will be at the center of April 9's discussions.

What's Next

The FIA April 9 summit takes place in three days. If unanimous agreement is reached on any of the six proposed fixes, the FIA has indicated implementation before the Miami Grand Prix (May 1-3) is the target. If agreement is not reached, the current regulations remain in effect for Miami, and the safety situation that triggered this summit continues into the next race. All ten teams have confirmed attendance. The outcome of April 9 will define the trajectory of the 2026 season's regulatory environment.
