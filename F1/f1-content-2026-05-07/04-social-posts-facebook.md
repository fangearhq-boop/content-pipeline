### STORY 2: Red Bull Miami Upgrade

**Long-Form Post (12:00 PM ET)**
There are finally signs of life at Red Bull.

Heading into the Miami Grand Prix, the RB22 was reportedly carrying approximately 30 kilograms of excess weight — well over the 768kg minimum set by F1's 2026 regulations. That translated to an estimated 0.9 seconds per lap of pure performance deficit. It was a crisis.

Then came the Miami upgrades. Red Bull brought a significant package of components designed to look identical to the outgoing parts but weigh substantially less. The result? Sources report the excess weight was roughly halved — bringing the deficit down to an estimated 15kg range.

Red Bull staff, by multiple accounts, have been "smiling" again inside the paddock since Miami.

But here's the honest truth: halved is not solved. At roughly 0.03 seconds per kilogram of excess weight, the gap is still measurable and meaningful. A full resolution isn't expected until summer — possibly a new chassis.

For Max Verstappen, currently seventh in the championship with 26 points and 74 behind Antonelli, summer can't come soon enough.

### STORY 4: Hamilton Sim-Free Canada

**Long-Form Post (2:00 PM ET)**
Lewis Hamilton has made a radical decision ahead of the Canadian Grand Prix: he won't be using the Ferrari simulator.

This isn't a casual choice in modern Formula 1, where teams rely heavily on simulation data to plan setups, practice starts, and refine strategy. For Hamilton to explicitly say "I won't do it" before a race weekend is a significant signal.

His reasoning is data-driven. Hamilton says the correlation between Ferrari's simulator and what he experiences on the real track is poor — the car behaves differently than the sim suggests it will. The clearest example? His best 2026 race result was P3 in China — which came in a back-to-back weekend with no simulator prep available. Then at Miami, he went in heavily prepared via the simulator. His words: "We go on it and then get to the track and the car feels different."

The implication is that the sim prep has actually been working against him — building muscle memory and setups that don't translate.

Hamilton is still attending factory meetings and staying involved in technical discussions. He's just stepping away from the sim specifically.

If this approach works in Canada, it asks a deeper question: what does it mean for Ferrari's overall simulator program?
