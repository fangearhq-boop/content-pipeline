Three Wins, Three Poles, History at 19: Why Kimi Antonelli Is Already Rewriting F1's Record Books

By Ryan Calloway | May 7, 2026

F1 · Championship Analysis

In the history of Formula 1, no driver had ever converted each of their first three career pole positions into race victories. Not Ayrton Senna, whose 65 career poles became the benchmark for a generation. Not Michael Schumacher, who dominated the sport for more than a decade. Not Lewis Hamilton, who owns more poles than anyone who has ever driven an F1 car.

Then came Kimi Antonelli. The 19-year-old Italian won from pole in China, Japan, and Miami — three consecutive races, three consecutive pole-to-win conversions — and in doing so, wrote a line into the record books that no one before him ever could.

A Start That Matches the Legends

Before achieving what Senna and Schumacher never did, Antonelli first matched them. He took pole position in each of his first three F1 race starts — a feat shared only by those two all-time greats. That alone would be remarkable for a 19-year-old in his debut season. What came next put him in entirely uncharted territory.

His three wins have come in different conditions and against different pressures. The Miami race, his most recent, was the sternest test: Lando Norris — the reigning world champion, a driver with the experience of fighting for and winning a world title — mounted a genuine late challenge. Antonelli held firm. That composure under direct pressure from a seasoned champion is perhaps the most telling detail of his season so far.

The Youngest Championship Leader in F1 History

At the Japanese Grand Prix earlier this season, Antonelli became the youngest driver ever to lead the Formula 1 Drivers' Championship. He was 19 years, 7 months, and 4 days old. The record had stood for decades; he erased it with the casual efficiency of someone who does not yet fully comprehend what he is doing.

After four races of the 2026 season, Antonelli leads with 100 points. His Mercedes teammate George Russell is second at 80. The gap is 20 points — meaningful but not yet decisive in a 24-race season. Antonelli himself has acknowledged this, saying the "road is still long" after his Miami victory. That self-awareness, combined with the results, is what makes this season so extraordinary to watch.

What's Next

The next test for Antonelli arrives at Circuit Gilles Villeneuve in Montreal on May 22-24 — a sprint weekend that will offer up to 34 additional points in a compressed 72-hour window. McLaren's upgraded package from Miami is well-suited to the low-drag Montreal circuit, meaning Norris and Oscar Piastri will arrive with genuine ambitions to challenge. Antonelli has been tested and he has passed. The question for Canada, and for the rest of 2026, is not whether the record books will be altered again — it's by how much.
