74 Points Back and the Exit Clause Question: Where Does Verstappen's 2026 Season Go from Here?

By Marcus Cole | May 7, 2026

F1 · Championship Analysis

Through four races of the 2026 Formula 1 season, Max Verstappen sits seventh in the Drivers' Championship with 26 points. The leader, Kimi Antonelli, has 100. The gap is 74 points — and while a 24-race season offers considerable room for recovery in theory, the reality of the RB22's technical challenges makes a championship comeback difficult to envision in the immediate future.

This is the same Max Verstappen who won four consecutive world championships between 2021 and 2024. The same driver who, in dominant years, made the sport feel like a formality. The 2026 season has been a different experience entirely — and the reason lies not with Verstappen himself, but with the car beneath him.

The Weight Problem and What's Been Done About It

The RB22 arrived at the start of 2026 carrying what has been reported as approximately 30 kilograms of excess weight over Formula 1's 768kg minimum. At roughly 0.03 seconds per kilogram, that translated to an estimated 0.9-second per lap performance penalty — a gap that no amount of driving genius can fully overcome in a close field.

Red Bull brought a significant upgrade package to the Miami Grand Prix specifically designed to address this. The new components look visually identical to the outgoing parts but are substantially lighter. According to sources, the upgrades halved the excess weight deficit — bringing the gap to approximately 15 kilograms. Red Bull staff were reportedly "smiling" in the paddock for the first time in weeks.

But halved still means a meaningful disadvantage. A full resolution is not expected until summer, when Red Bull may introduce a more fundamental chassis revision. The cost cap — Formula 1's annual spending limit — creates a genuine constraint on how quickly the team can develop both a 2026 fix and a 2027 car simultaneously.

The Exit Clause and What It Means

Verstappen's contract with Red Bull has been widely reported to contain a performance-based exit clause — an option to leave if his championship standing falls too far outside the top two. The specific terms have not been officially disclosed. What is clear is that Verstappen is currently seventh in the standings, far outside any threshold that would normally be considered competitive for a driver of his stature.

Whether the exit clause is formally triggered is less important than the broader dynamic it creates. Verstappen has leverage. Red Bull has a development timeline. The two need to align before the summer break, or the conversations around Verstappen's future at the team will intensify substantially.

What's Next

The Canadian GP (May 22-24) is the next opportunity for Verstappen to score meaningful points. Montreal has historically been a circuit that suits Red Bull's braking-zone strengths — if the team can extract more performance from the updated package there, it would be the first genuine signal that the RB22 is becoming a competitive car. The summer upgrade remains the bigger moment. For now, Verstappen is in an uncomfortable position that no one — least of all the man himself — expected when this season began.
