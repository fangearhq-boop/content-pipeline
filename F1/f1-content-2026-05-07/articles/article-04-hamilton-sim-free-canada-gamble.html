Hamilton's Simulator Gamble: Skipping Ferrari Prep for Canada in Hopes of Replicating China

By Ryan Calloway | May 7, 2026

F1 · Driver Strategy

Lewis Hamilton has made a decision that breaks one of modern Formula 1's most fundamental preparation habits: he will not use the Ferrari simulator before the Canadian Grand Prix. In a sport that relies heavily on simulation data for setup development, tire modeling, and strategic planning, Hamilton's explicit rejection of that process is a meaningful departure from the norm.

His reasoning is direct. The correlation between what Ferrari's simulator tells him and what he experiences on the actual race track is poor. "Ultimately it's always a matter of correlation," Hamilton has said. "We go on it and then get to the track and the car feels different." He describes the sim as "sending me in the wrong direction" — not just unhelpful, but actively counterproductive.

China Was the Proof of Concept

Hamilton's best result in the 2026 season so far was a third-place finish at the Chinese Grand Prix — the third race of the year, which followed immediately after the second (the Japanese GP) in a back-to-back weekend. In a back-to-back, there is no time for simulator preparation between races. Hamilton had to go to China relying on his accumulated knowledge of the circuit, team debrief data from Japan, and his own instincts.

He drove to the podium. It was his strongest showing of the season.

Miami told a different story. Hamilton came in with extensive simulator preparation and finished outside the podium, struggling with the same correlation issues he had been flagging throughout the year. The contrast was stark enough that he has drawn a clear line: the sim is the variable that changed between his best result and his average ones.

A Deeper Ferrari Problem

Hamilton's decision to skip the simulator is a personal approach, but it points to a structural issue at Ferrari. Simulator correlation — the accuracy with which a team's virtual model represents the real car on the real track — is a fundamental part of modern F1 engineering. When a driver as experienced and analytically sophisticated as Hamilton says the correlation is wrong, it is not a minor complaint. It is an engineering finding that the team needs to address.

Ferrari is understood to be working on improving the correlation between their simulation environment and real-world track behavior. The process is complex and cannot be resolved overnight. Until it is, Hamilton is essentially operating with one of F1's major preparation tools removed from his toolkit — by his own choice, but out of necessity.

What's Next

The Canadian GP on May 22-24 will be the immediate test of Hamilton's theory. Circuit Gilles Villeneuve is a circuit he has won on before — his record at Montreal is one of the strongest in F1 history. If the sim-free approach yields a result comparable to China, it validates the decision and applies pressure on Ferrari to address their correlation problem systematically. Hamilton sits joint fourth in the championship on 51 points. Montreal is exactly the kind of opportunity that could nudge him higher — if the gamble pays off.
