Red Bull's Miami Upgrade Halved Their Weight Problem — But the War Isn't Won

By Marcus Cole | May 7, 2026

F1 · Technical Analysis

Red Bull Racing have made progress. Real, meaningful progress. The upgrade package they deployed at the Miami Grand Prix has reportedly halved the RB22's excess weight deficit, bringing the car from approximately 30 kilograms over the 768kg minimum weight threshold to an estimated 15 kilograms. In the paddock, sources describe Red Bull staff as "smiling" again — a stark contrast to the grim mood that had followed the team through the opening rounds of the 2026 season.

That progress deserves recognition. But it also needs context. Halved is not solved. At roughly 0.03 seconds per kilogram of excess weight, the remaining gap still translates into meaningful lap time — in the order of 0.45 seconds if the estimates are accurate. In a sport where fractions of tenths separate the best teams, that is still a significant performance disadvantage.

How the Miami Upgrades Worked

The approach Red Bull took at Miami was methodical rather than dramatic. Rather than redesigning major structural components, the team developed new parts that look visually identical to the existing items but are manufactured with lighter materials or revised construction methods to reduce mass. The goal was weight reduction without the risk of changing the car's aerodynamic characteristics in ways that might introduce new problems.

The strategy worked to an extent. Red Bull used the break between the Japanese Grand Prix and Miami to design and manufacture these components. The ambition was to address the weight disadvantage that had been estimated to cost Max Verstappen and Isack Hadjar approximately 0.9 seconds per lap at the circuit where the problem was worst. Getting that number down to 0.45 seconds is progress. Getting to zero is the goal.

The Summer Target and the Cost Cap Trap

A complete resolution of the weight crisis is not expected before summer. Reports suggest Red Bull may need a new chassis to fully address the issue — a more fundamental intervention than revised components. The complication is Formula 1's cost cap: developing a new 2026 chassis while simultaneously investing in the 2027 car design is extraordinarily expensive, and the cost cap creates genuine resource constraints that cannot simply be solved by spending more money.

For Verstappen, currently seventh in the championship with 26 points — 74 behind Kimi Antonelli — the summer upgrade is his most realistic opportunity to enter proper championship contention. The Canadian GP (May 22-24) will be the next important data point: can Red Bull build on Miami's progress, or does the remaining weight deficit persist?

What's Next

Red Bull's path forward is clear in direction if not certain in timeline: continue reducing the RB22's excess weight, possibly through a summer chassis revision, while protecting their position in the Constructors' Championship. Verstappen needs results soon. His options after summer — if Red Bull cannot produce a competitive car — include an exit clause that has been widely reported as part of his contract. For now, the smiles in the Red Bull garage after Miami represent genuine hope. Whether that hope becomes competitive performance by Canada will be the next major test of whether the team's recovery is real.
