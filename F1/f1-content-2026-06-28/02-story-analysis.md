## Tier Rationale

### STORY 1: Austrian GP Race Day (Tier 1)
Assigned Tier 1 because this is the anchor event of the day — the actual race is happening today, and this story serves as the tent-pole preview driving all other coverage. Russell's pole position is a major narrative hook (he hasn't had a run like this all season), the Verstappen disaster subplot adds drama, and the championship implications of every position gained or lost make this the story readers open first thing in the morning. Race-day preview content generates consistently high traffic for event-following audiences. Any race weekend where the championship leader, a reigning seven-time world champion, and a crashing former champion are all in the same grid is Tier 1 by definition.

### STORY 2: Verstappen Exit Clock (Tier 1)
Assigned Tier 1 because the Verstappen situation is one of the biggest ongoing narratives in F1 for 2026. A qualifying crash at his home track, at a circuit he has dominated for years, combined with a confirmed exit clause deadline six weeks away creates the kind of "season-defining moment" energy that drives clicks and shares regardless of whether the reader planned to follow Austria coverage. This story stands alone — it would be Tier 1 even without the race. The shareholder split angle and retirement hint add further dimension that elevates it above a standard qualifying incident.

### STORY 3: Antonelli vs Hamilton Championship Math (Tier 2)
Assigned Tier 2 because while the championship battle is compelling, the math-focused angle is a complementary analytical piece rather than a breaking news item. The 41-point gap and 2.7 pts/race calculation are important context for race day but don't carry the immediacy of the race itself or the Verstappen saga. Tier 2 is appropriate — solid evergreen-adjacent content that informs the serious fan without competing for the top scheduling slots.

### STORY 4: Cadillac Zero Points Upgrade (Tier 2)
Assigned Tier 2 because Cadillac's story, while genuinely newsworthy (zero points is a historic low), is a subplot rather than a lead narrative. The Austria upgrade is speculative in outcome — we don't know yet whether it works. The FP incidents (electrical failures, cockpit fire) add colour and urgency but don't constitute a breaking crisis. This is the kind of "watch this space" story that builds audience investment in a secondary team storyline.

### STORY 5: F1 Silly Season 2027 Seats (Tier 2)
Assigned Tier 2 because seat speculation is evergreen background noise elevated by a specific, verifiable hook: 15 of 22 drivers unconfirmed for 2027, with a real deadline (July 26 Hungarian GP) creating structure. This is an afternoon/evening story for fans who have digested the race and want to think about the bigger picture. The Verstappen domino angle ties it back to the day's dominant narrative, which is good connective tissue.

## Platform Strategy Notes

**X (Twitter):** Prioritise drama and speed. Tier 1 stories get 2 posts each to build momentum before and after the race start. Tier 2 stories get 1 post each to maintain presence without oversaturating the timeline. All posts should work as standalone reads — assume most readers haven't seen the previous post.

**Facebook:** Longer-form, more conversational. The F1 Facebook audience trends slightly older and more loyal — they reward depth and storytelling over raw newsworthiness. Lead with emotion and context, not just the headline fact.

**Articles:** These are the SEO backbone and the click destination from social. Every article is a 400–800 word standalone piece of journalism. Since the race hasn't happened yet, all articles are previews. Avoid hedging language that makes the piece feel tentative — be authoritative about what we know and clear about what's still to come.
