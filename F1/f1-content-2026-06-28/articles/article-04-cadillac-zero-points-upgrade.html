Cadillac's Austrian GP Upgrade: Zero Points, Final Gamble

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap

June 28, 2026

Seven races into the 2026 Formula 1 season, Cadillac has scored zero championship points. Not a handful. Not a tally the statisticians round toward something flattering. Zero. It is the worst start for any constructor in the modern era of the sport, and it is the context that hangs over everything Cadillac does at the Red Bull Ring in Austria this weekend.

The team arrived in Spielberg carrying their largest upgrade package of the season — a set of changes designed specifically to address the problem that has defined their 2026 campaign: the tire degradation cliff that consistently appears around lap 15 of every race stint and costs them positions, pace, and ultimately any chance of scoring.

Then practice happened.

A Practice Week to Forget

Sergio Perez suffered two separate electrical failures in FP1, both significant enough to bring out red flags that disrupted the session for the entire field. The reliability issues on Perez's side of the garage meant that the very upgrade package Cadillac had brought to Austria as their most important development push of the year went almost entirely untested in the opening practice session.

Valtteri Bottas experienced something more dramatic in FP2. His car caught fire — a cockpit fire significant enough to require marshal intervention and the deployment of fire suppression equipment. The fire was extinguished. Then it reignited. Bottas stepped out of the car. The team spent the remainder of FP2 assessing damage that was never supposed to happen at what should have been a productive, data-heavy practice day.

Across two practice sessions, Cadillac gathered almost none of the upgrade comparison data they needed. The package that was supposed to give the team its clearest picture yet of their 2026 competitive position arrived at the track and was effectively unproven heading into race day.

The Lap-15 Problem

The tire degradation cliff is not a vague complaint or a convenient explanation. It has been a consistent, predictable, and damaging phenomenon across every race this season. While other teams manage their tire temperatures and maintain competitive pace through long stints, Cadillac's car hits a performance wall around lap 15 that the drivers cannot manage around and the engineers cannot strategy their way out of.

The consequences compound quickly. By the time the car drops into its degradation phase, it has already surrendered track position to the cars around it. An early pit stop to escape the cliff either undercuts into traffic or leaves the car on fresher tires in clear air but without the pace to make that advantage meaningful. The result, race after race, has been the same: no points, no indication that the problem is being solved, no path to the scoreboard through the current aerodynamic and mechanical configuration.

The Austria upgrade targets what engineers believe are the root causes — the aerodynamic load balance and downforce distribution that create the thermal stress the tires experience. Whether that diagnosis is correct will only become clear in Sunday's race, the first real opportunity to test the upgrade in a competitive environment after practice provided so little usable data.

Perez and Bottas: Different Struggles

Inside the garage, the season has not been equal misery for both drivers. Perez, who brings experience from his years at the front of the grid, has outperformed Bottas on raw pace in the sessions where reliability has allowed him to actually demonstrate that pace. The electrical failures in Austria fell heavily on his side of the team and cost him the clearest opportunity yet to run the upgrade package through a full practice programme.

Bottas has been with the Cadillac organisation through its transition from Sauber, and his experience with the team's development culture gives him a different kind of institutional knowledge. But the cockpit fire in FP2 was the most serious single incident either driver has experienced this season, and it was not the statement of reliability that a team needing to turn a corner in Austria required.

Gene Haas, the team's founder, has been clear about where Cadillac stands: the commitment to solving the tire degradation problem is real, and the Austria upgrade represents that commitment in tangible engineering form. The team is not running out of belief. It is running out of races in which belief alone is enough.

What Failure Would Mean

If Sunday's race passes and Cadillac again fails to score — if the lap-15 cliff remains, if the reliability issues that plagued practice continue into race conditions, if neither Perez nor Bottas can fight their way into the points — the questions about the team's 2026 season become harder to deflect. Zero points through eight races is an unambiguous data point. The upgrade either works or it does not. There is no interpretation available that turns continued zero-point finishes into a story about future potential.

The Austrian Grand Prix is not officially a make-or-break event for Cadillac. Sixteen races remain. There is time to recover. But in a sport where momentum, confidence, and constructor standing all feed each other, time without points is not neutral ground. It is ground that gets harder to recover with every passing race.

What's Next

The Austrian Grand Prix starts at 9:00 AM ET today. For Cadillac, Sunday is the first meaningful test of their biggest upgrade of the season under real competitive conditions. If Perez or Bottas can break into the points for the first time in 2026, the team will have both the result and the data to build on.
