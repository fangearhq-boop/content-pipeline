Carlos Sainz Hit With F1's Most Unusual Penalty: Dropped From P12 to P17 After British GP Software Error

By Marcus Cole | July 8, 2026

F1 Race Result

Carlos Sainz crossed the finish line of the 2026 British Grand Prix classified in 12th place. By the time the post-race investigations concluded, he had been placed 17th — one lap behind the leader. The penalty applied was, by the assessment of multiple outlets including motorsport.com and formula1.com, unlike anything previously seen in Formula 1 history.

And at the center of the controversy was an admission from the FIA itself: a software error had generated a false message during the race's final laps, contributing to the confusion that led to the penalty.

What Happened on the Track

On Lap 48 of the British Grand Prix, Max Verstappen crashed at Stowe Corner — a high-speed right-hander near the end of the lap. A safety car was deployed for the final laps of the race. Lapped cars were given the instruction to overtake the safety car and rejoin the field at the back, as is standard procedure. Sainz was not among the cars listed in that instruction.

The complication arose from Silverstone's pit lane layout, which has a unique configuration near the entry and exit points. As Sainz navigated this section under the safety car, his car temporarily "unlapped" itself relative to the safety car — crossing the pit lane entry in a way that placed him ahead of where he should have been positioned according to the FIA's lapping instruction. He was not authorized to do so.

Williams, monitoring the situation from the pit wall, failed to identify that Sainz had committed an infringement. The team argued afterward that the pit lane edge case was genuinely difficult to detect in real time, particularly at a circuit with Silverstone's specific configuration.

The FIA's Software Error

Compounding the chaos: on the penultimate lap, the FIA's timing and display systems briefly showed the message "Safety Car in this lap" — the standard indicator that the safety car would come in and the race would restart. The message was wrong. It was, the FIA acknowledged in a post-race statement, the product of a software error that generated an erroneous output on display screens and timing systems.

The race did not restart. It finished under the full safety car — the first time an F1 race has concluded under safety car conditions since the 2022 Italian Grand Prix at Monza. Drivers and teams were left briefly uncertain about whether to prepare for a restart that never came.

The FIA's admission of the software error drew immediate comparisons in coverage to the 2021 Abu Dhabi Grand Prix finale — another safety car controversy that altered a race result and generated lasting controversy in the sport. The comparison is imprecise in its details, but the underlying theme — technological or procedural failure affecting race outcomes — resonates.

The Unprecedented Nature of the Penalty

The "one-lap penalty" applied to Sainz — adding a lap to his classified result, dropping him from P12 to one lap down in P17 — does not appear to have a precedent in modern Formula 1. Motorsport.com and formula1.com both used the word "unprecedented" in their coverage. Williams did not immediately indicate whether they would appeal the ruling.

For Sainz and Williams, the sporting consequence was tangible: the points that a P12 finish would have earned (nothing, as points are paid only to the top 10) did not change, but the classified result affects championship statistics and the broader record of the season.

What's Next

The FIA's software systems and the Silverstone pit lane procedure are expected to face scrutiny heading into the Belgian Grand Prix at Spa-Francorchamps on July 17-19. Whether Williams files a formal appeal of the Sainz penalty, and whether the FIA announces any procedural changes in response to the software error, will be among the technical storylines to watch before the next race weekend begins.
