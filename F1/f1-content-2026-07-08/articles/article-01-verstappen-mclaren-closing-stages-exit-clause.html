Verstappen and McLaren in "Closing Stages" of Deal as Exit Clause Becomes Mathematically Inevitable

By Elena Voss | July 8, 2026

F1 Driver Market

The transfer saga that has dominated Formula 1 this season reached a new level of intensity on Tuesday, with SportBible reporting that Max Verstappen and McLaren are in the "closing stages" of a deal — and that a three-year contract is believed to be "imminent." The reported arrangement includes Oscar Piastri moving to Red Bull Racing as part of a driver swap.

McLaren CEO Zak Brown moved quickly to deny the reports, stating that "a move for Verstappen will not happen any time soon." But even as the he-said/they-said debate continued on social media and in the paddock, one thing became impossible to dispute: the mathematics of the 2026 Formula 1 championship have made Verstappen's exit clause activation a certainty.

The Math That Cannot Be Argued

Following his crash at Stowe Corner on Lap 48 of the British Grand Prix, Verstappen sits seventh in the World Drivers' Championship with 76 points. George Russell is second with 154 points — a gap of 78 points. Before the summer break, two races remain: the Belgian Grand Prix at Spa-Francorchamps (July 17-19) and the Hungarian Grand Prix at the Hungaroring (July 26). The maximum points available across those two events is 50.

Put simply: even if Verstappen wins both races and scores maximum points, and even if Russell scores zero, Verstappen would still be third in the standings heading into the break. The exit clause in his Red Bull contract, which activates if he is not in the top two of the championship at the start of the summer break, will fire. There is no mathematical path that prevents it.

The State of the Red Bull Relationship

According to Dutch publication De Limburger, cited by GPFans, the relationship between Verstappen and Red Bull has reached "boiling point." Red Bull team principal Laurent Mekies acknowledged the situation is "very unpleasant."

Red Bull attempted to prevent the clause from ever becoming relevant earlier this season, holding talks with Verstappen in Austria and reportedly offering an eight-figure buyout. Verstappen declined. Red Bull then attempted to argue that the clause could not be enforced — F1 insiders quoted earlier in the season suggested the clause was "impossible to block." The Austrian Grand Prix result, followed by the British GP crash, removed any remaining ambiguity.

One detail stood out: Verstappen confirmed to reporters that he speaks to fired former Red Bull boss Christian Horner — who was notably present at Silverstone, his first race since being dismissed following the 2025 British Grand Prix — "almost every week." The closeness of that relationship, at a moment when Verstappen is on the verge of departing the team Horner built, carries its own narrative weight.

What McLaren Is Saying — and What the Reports Are Saying

The official McLaren position, delivered through Zak Brown, is a flat denial. Brown's statement that a Verstappen deal will "not happen any time soon" is a categorical negative — not a "we're not commenting" or "we don't discuss driver contracts." It is a direct refutation of the SportBible report.

Whether Brown's denial reflects genuine absence of a deal, or serves as a standard pre-announcement denial, will be answered by events. The F1 transfer window does not have a formal structure like other sports. Deals are announced when all parties are ready. If the clause activates at the Hungarian Grand Prix on July 26, Verstappen would technically be free to negotiate with other teams — and to announce a move — from that point forward.

Piastri's situation adds an additional layer of complexity. The McLaren driver is contracted through 2028. For a move to Red Bull to happen, McLaren would have to agree to release him early — which would require compensation, a trade (Verstappen), or both.

What's Next

The Belgian Grand Prix at Spa-Francorchamps (July 17-19) and the Hungarian Grand Prix (July 26) are the final scheduled chapters before the summer break. After Hungary, the exit clause conversation moves from speculation to formal procedure. Whether an announcement comes in August, September, or October — and who the announcement names — remains the most consequential unknown in Formula 1.
