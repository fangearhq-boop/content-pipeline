The Ticking Clock: Verstappen's Exit Clause and What Happens Next

By Elena Voss | F1 Fanrecap | June 20, 2026

Max Verstappen currently sits seventh in the 2026 FIA Formula One World Championship with 55 points, 101 behind leader Kimi Antonelli. He has not won a race this season. And buried inside his Red Bull contract is a clause that could, if the math stays where it is, give him the right to walk away from the team this autumn.

The details of the exit clause, as revealed by multiple reports: if Verstappen is not in the top two of the Drivers' Championship by the end of July, he can terminate his contract with Red Bull unilaterally. No compensation owed. The activation window runs from August through October. His deal otherwise runs to the end of 2028.

The math right now makes the clause relevant. Getting from seventh to second in the championship by July's end would require an extraordinary collapse from the drivers ahead of him and a complete turnaround in Red Bull's car performance. The clause, inserted because Verstappen had "longstanding apprehension" about the 2026 regulations, may have been written precisely because everyone involved understood this scenario was possible.

Red Bull's Divided Response

Reports published as recently as June 19 indicate that Red Bull's senior leadership is split on how to handle the situation. One faction believes the team should proactively buy out the exit clause — essentially pay Verstappen to stay even beyond what his contract requires, removing the uncertainty before it metastasizes. Another faction believes Verstappen won't exercise the clause regardless of the championship math, because his loyalty to Red Bull runs deep enough to override the contractual right.

The tension in the boardroom is real. Red Bull built their dynasty with Verstappen at the center — four consecutive world championships from 2021 to 2024. The prospect of losing him to a competitor, potentially to Mercedes or Aston Martin, is not one the organization treats casually.

Who's Watching

Mercedes and Aston Martin are the names most frequently attached to any Verstappen-available scenario. Mercedes, having placed their 2026 championship hopes with Antonelli, would have an obvious motivation to lock up the four-time champion as insurance for future seasons. Aston Martin has been positioning themselves as a front-running team for the latter half of this decade and would covet Verstappen's star power.

Whether either team has the financial appetite to sign Verstappen — who commands some of the highest earnings in the sport — is a separate question. But the conversations are happening in the background.

The Austrian GP as Context

The Red Bull Ring in Spielberg is Red Bull's home race. Their factory is headquartered in nearby Milton Keynes, but their name and identity are bound up with this Austrian circuit. Verstappen has historically performed well here. A strong result next weekend — a podium, or better — wouldn't change the championship math that underpins the exit clause. But it might change the conversation.

A Verstappen win at the Red Bull Ring, on home soil, in the team's own backyard, would be a reminder of what the partnership can produce when the car is right. Sometimes those reminders matter more than the numbers.

What's Next

The July cutoff is six weeks away. Between now and then, there are two race weekends — Austria and the British GP at Silverstone. If Verstappen wins both and others falter, the championship picture changes. If the results continue as they have been, the exit clause remains live through the summer.

Red Bull needs to decide whether to address the clause proactively or trust that a relationship built over a decade will hold. The clock is ticking.
