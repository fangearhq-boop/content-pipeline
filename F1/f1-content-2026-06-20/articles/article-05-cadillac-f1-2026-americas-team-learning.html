America's F1 Team Is Learning — And Cadillac Is Making Progress

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap | June 20, 2026

For the first time in decades, an American constructor is on the Formula 1 grid. Cadillac F1 joined the sport in 2026 as the 11th team, bringing two experienced drivers — Sergio Pérez and Valtteri Bottas — and a plan to learn their way into competitiveness. Months into the season, the picture is complicated: no points scored, but genuine progress visible.

Cadillac's debut season philosophy was deliberate. CEO Dan Towriss was explicit about the choice to pair experience with experience: "their experience, their leadership and their technical acumen really are what we need" at this formative stage. Pérez has six Grand Prix wins and 39 podiums to his name. Bottas has 10 wins and 67 career podiums. Combined, they have more than 500 Formula 1 starts.

The idea was that experienced drivers provide reliable feedback and development direction rather than making mistakes a rookie would make. In theory, the fastest path from zero to competitive.

The Reality So Far

Through the first several races of 2026, Cadillac has not scored a championship point. Neither car has qualified beyond Q1. The gap to the midfield teams has been real and consistent. After three races, team officials described their position honestly: they are learning.

The unexpected subplot has been the gap between the two drivers. Pérez has significantly outperformed Bottas in race situations — a gap that caught even the team's technical leadership off guard. Cadillac's own personnel have publicly described the gap as something they "can't explain," given that both drivers arrived with broadly similar Formula 1 credentials. Pérez has extracted a level of race pace from the Cadillac that has allowed him to mix it with the lower midfield. Bottas has been slower.

The Miami Upgrade and What Changed

Cadillac brought an upgrade package to the Miami race that centered on a new front wing and a reprofiled floor. The results were meaningful: after the upgrades, the team was able to race Aston Martin on pace in race conditions. That's not a win, and it's not points, but it's a reference point. Aston Martin has been an established midfield competitor for years.

Closing the gap to an established midfield team within the first season of operation is the kind of incremental progress that builds a racing team. Cadillac's trajectory — still without points, but benchmarking against recognizable competitors — suggests the foundation is sound even if the results aren't showing yet.

The Bigger Picture

Cadillac's arrival is significant beyond the results. General Motors' investment in Formula 1 — plans include a specific F1 engine factory in Charlotte, North Carolina — represents the most serious American commitment to the sport in modern history. The team was never expected to win in Year 1. The goal was to establish infrastructure, processes, and competitive DNA that would compound over time.

The 2026 regulations, which are entirely new, actually benefit a debut team slightly: everyone is learning simultaneously. The teams that were dominant in previous regulations don't automatically carry that dominance into a new technical era. Cadillac is starting from the same technical baseline as everyone else, just with less experience in executing at the highest level.

What's Next

Austria's Red Bull Ring is next, and then the British Grand Prix at Silverstone. Cadillac will be watching the data from every session, looking for the next opportunity to upgrade and the next reference point to benchmark against. Points remain the goal — getting one car into Q2 would be a milestone. Racing a midfield team for a points position in the race would be the next.

America's F1 team is learning. The classroom just happens to be the fastest racing series on the planet.
