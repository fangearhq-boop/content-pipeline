Ferrari's Engine Upgrade Arrives in Austria — If Red Bull Can't Stop It

By Ryan Calloway June 24, 2026

The Upgrade Ferrari Has Been Building Toward

Ferrari is planning to introduce a new power unit at the 2026 Austrian Grand Prix, targeting a meaningful performance gain for both Lewis Hamilton and Charles Leclerc. This is the Scuderia's first significant internal combustion upgrade of the 2026 season, and the timing is deliberate. The Red Bull Ring in Spielberg is a power-sensitive circuit — its 4.326-kilometer layout includes long acceleration zones where raw engine output determines lap time more than aerodynamic efficiency. Ferrari chose Austria for a reason.

The upgrade is expected to provide a quantifiable improvement in both horsepower and deployment characteristics, which would give Hamilton more tools to attack at circuits where Ferrari's chassis has occasionally been found wanting in 2026. After his Barcelona victory cut the championship gap to 41 points, any additional edge matters considerably more than it would have three weeks ago.

The ADUO Appeal — What Red Bull Is Doing

Red Bull Racing filed an appeal against the FIA's ruling on ADUO — Active Drag and Undercut Override — a new technical system introduced under the 2026 aerodynamic regulations. The FIA approved Ferrari's interpretation of how ADUO functions in conjunction with their power unit systems, and Red Bull is challenging that approval.

If Red Bull's appeal is successful before the Austrian GP weekend, Ferrari may be unable to run the upgrade in the configuration they planned. This isn't Red Bull trying to win on-track through strategy — it's a technical-legal maneuver designed to shape how the regulations are interpreted for the rest of the season. The Austrian GP serves as the battleground because of the timing, but the implications run deeper. Whoever wins this regulatory dispute gains an advantage at every remaining circuit in 2026.

Hamilton's Position: "Nothing Is Impossible"

Hamilton's response to questions about the appeal and the title race has been consistent since Barcelona. "Nothing is impossible," he said, which from most drivers sounds like diplomatic hedging. From a 41-year-old who just won his first race for Ferrari and cut a 66-point gap to 41 in one afternoon, it reads differently.

Former world champion Jacques Villeneuve added another layer this week, arguing that Ferrari needs to formally designate Hamilton as their No. 1 driver and structure strategy around his championship challenge rather than splitting resources equally with Leclerc. That call puts Ferrari management in an awkward position, but the math supports it: Hamilton needs consistent wins and Antonelli needs more trouble. Having two Ferrari drivers competing with each other in the top five helps neither of them.

What's Next

Ferrari's appeal situation should clarify before Free Practice 1 on Friday, June 26. If the upgrade clears, watch for performance data in FP2 that compares Hamilton and Leclerc's sector times to pre-upgrade pace. If the appeal blocks it, expect Ferrari to announce an alternative configuration and a revised timeline. Either way, the power unit story will be the defining technical subplot of the Austrian GP weekend.
