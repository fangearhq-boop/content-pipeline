Two DNFs From the Lead: Is Mercedes Reliability Becoming Antonelli's Biggest Threat?

By Ryan Calloway June 24, 2026

The Pattern Takes Shape

In Canada, George Russell was leading when his Mercedes stopped. In Barcelona, Andrea Kimi Antonelli was leading when his Mercedes stopped. Both retirements were mechanical. Both happened from the front of the field. Two separate drivers, two separate circuits, the same outcome. That is either bad luck or a pattern, and at Round 7 of a 24-race season, the difference matters enormously for the championship.

Antonelli's Barcelona DNF came with three laps remaining. He had controlled the race from the front and was on course for his sixth win of the season, which would have made this title defense one of the most dominant in modern F1 history. Instead, the car stopped, Hamilton took the lead and the victory, and the gap that once stood at 66 points narrowed to 41 in one afternoon.

What These Failures Suggest

Mercedes has not publicly detailed the root cause of either retirement. What is known: Antonelli's Barcelona failure was described as power-related; Russell's Canada DNF involved a different system. Two different failure modes in two different cars at two different circuits could mean these are genuinely isolated events. But teams do not typically disclose failure details unless forced to, which makes independent analysis difficult.

The more concerning reading is that the W16's systems are showing stress at the front of races. When cars run at race pace without DRS-intensive overtaking or defensive driving, they operate in consistent aerodynamic conditions that place different thermal demands on power unit components. If the W16 has a weakness in its long-run front-of-race power management, that would explain two retirements from leads that look otherwise unexplained.

The Championship Arithmetic of Another DNF

Antonelli's current lead is 41 points. A win in Austria for Hamilton combined with an Antonelli DNF would cut that to roughly 16 points — a gap that becomes genuinely precarious with 14 races remaining. A standard Antonelli second place combined with a Hamilton win would reduce the gap to 16 as well. Any of these scenarios would change how the season is discussed going into the summer break.

What Antonelli has going for him is that 19-year-olds in their first season rarely have the kind of statistical advantage he's built, and his talent is not in question. Five wins in seven races is a record pace. But the championship calculus is now different after Barcelona: Hamilton has the momentum, Ferrari has the upgrade, and Mercedes needs to prove they can get their car to the checkered flag from the front.

What's Next

Austria is the immediate answer to the reliability question. Free Practice begins Friday. If Antonelli leads in Austria and finishes, the narrative around two DNFs fades quickly. If he encounters trouble again from the front, the conversation at the summer break — with seven races remaining before the hiatus — will center on whether his championship lead is as safe as the points table suggests. Race start Sunday, June 28 at 9:00 AM ET.
