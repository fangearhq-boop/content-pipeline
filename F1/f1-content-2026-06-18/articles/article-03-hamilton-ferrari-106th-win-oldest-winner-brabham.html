Dream the Impossible: Hamilton's 106th Win Makes Him the Oldest GP Winner Since Jack Brabham in 1970

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap | June 18, 2026

When Lewis Hamilton's voice broke on the team radio in Barcelona, the moment crystallized something that had been building since the day he announced he was leaving Mercedes for Ferrari.

"You've helped me achieve this dream and I can't thank you enough. Thanks for everyone pushing so hard back at home. I'm so proud of you."

He was 41 years old. He had just won his 106th Formula 1 Grand Prix. He had become the oldest race winner in the sport since Jack Brabham in 1970 — a 56-year record. And he had done it in red.

The Context Behind the Win

Hamilton's early months at Ferrari in 2026 did not suggest this was coming. He admitted at one point to feeling "absolutely useless" as he struggled to understand a car with fundamentally different handling characteristics to the Mercedes he had driven for over a decade. The media narratives were not kind. Doubts about whether the Ferrari move was a mistake began circulating openly.

The turnaround was gradual, then sudden. Ferrari made targeted technical developments to better suit Hamilton's driving style. Hamilton adapted his approach to work with the Ferrari's preferred entry characteristics. The podiums started coming. Then Barcelona.

Ferrari gambled on a three-stop strategy when a Virtual Safety Car period emerged in the race's middle phase. The call worked perfectly — Hamilton received a free pit stop, emerged with fresh tires and clear air, and systematically built his lead to nearly 20 seconds over George Russell by the flag. The strategy was the technical key, but Hamilton's ability to execute it — to manage tire temperatures across a complex stint structure while managing traffic — was the driver's contribution.

The Historical Weight

Jack Brabham won the 1970 South African Grand Prix at the age of 43. Before Barcelona, he was the oldest Formula 1 race winner in the modern era. Hamilton, at 41, doesn't surpass that record — but he joins a very short list of drivers who have won a Grand Prix after the age of 40.

The 106th victory also extends Hamilton's own record as the all-time Grand Prix wins leader. Michael Schumacher's 91 wins stood as the benchmark for years before Hamilton surpassed it. At 106, the record is approaching an abstract number — a dimension of achievement rather than a competition.

What gives the Barcelona win its specific weight is the Ferrari context. Hamilton left Mercedes specifically for the opportunity to win at Maranello — to be part of the Scuderia's next chapter the way Schumacher was part of its previous golden era. The first win, at 41, with the emotional radio message and "DREAM THE IMPOSSIBLE" social post, closes a loop that began when the transfer was announced.

The Title Picture

Hamilton is 41 points behind Antonelli in the championship with 15 races remaining. The combination of his Barcelona win and Antonelli's retirement produced the largest championship swing of the season in a single afternoon.

The question that follows every Hamilton development in 2026 is the same: is this a title season? The gap says maybe. The momentum says yes. The reliability concerns around Mercedes suggest the answer may not be determined by the driving quality of either Hamilton or Antonelli, but by which cars finish.

What's Next

The Austrian Grand Prix is 10 days away. Hamilton goes to the Red Bull Ring without the circuit advantage Antonelli has at the Mercedes-friendly layout, but with pace, momentum, and a clear shot at the title if Mercedes' reliability issues persist.

He posted two words after Barcelona. They are worth remembering as the championship resumes: Dream the impossible.
