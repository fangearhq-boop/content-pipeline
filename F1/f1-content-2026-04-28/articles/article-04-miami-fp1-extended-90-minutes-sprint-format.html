Why Miami's 90-Minute FP1 Matters More Than Any Practice Session This Season

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap

April 28, 2026

For the majority of the Formula 1 season, Free Practice One is little more than an administrative box to tick — a useful warm-up, but rarely decisive. The Miami Grand Prix's FP1 session, beginning at noon local time (1PM ET) this Friday May 1, is a different proposition entirely. With the FIA confirming it will be extended to 90 minutes rather than the standard 60, and with three converging factors making it uniquely consequential, teams and engineers will be treating it with the urgency of a qualifying session.

The first factor is the calendar gap. Formula 1 last competed on March 29 at the Japanese Grand Prix. Between then and Miami, a five-week break — created by the cancellation of the Bahrain and Saudi Arabian Grands Prix — means every driver is returning to a Formula 1 cockpit after the longest mid-season absence in recent memory. Muscle memory, car feel, and tyre understanding all need to be re-established quickly.

The second factor is regulatory. Miami is the first race where the FIA's revised 2026 energy management rules take effect — adjustments to the super-clip ceiling and maximum recharge per lap that are intended to change how the cars behave through each corner and straight. No team has yet driven their car under the new parameters. Everything the engineers believe they know about setup, balance, and energy deployment strategy needs to be verified in real conditions.

The Sprint Format Pressure

The third factor — and the most operationally urgent — is the Sprint weekend format. Unlike standard grand prix weekends where teams have FP1, FP2, and FP3 across two days to gather data before qualifying, Sprint weekends compress the schedule dramatically. After FP1 on Friday, the programme moves directly to Sprint Qualifying on Friday afternoon. There is no additional practice before the race on Sunday.

That means 90 minutes is the entirety of the practice data teams will collect before committing to a race setup. Every run plan, every tyre comparison, every wing angle experiment, and every energy management calibration must happen in this one window. Teams that maximise their FP1 programme will have a measurable advantage over those who lose time to technical issues, setup dead-ends, or inefficient run plans.

The FIA acknowledged all three factors explicitly when announcing the extended session length. For teams debuting major upgrade packages — Ferrari's overhauled SF-26, McLaren's aerodynamic overhaul, Red Bull's Macarena wing — those extra 30 minutes could be the difference between understanding the new components immediately or spending the whole Sprint weekend chasing a baseline.

What's Next

FP1 at Miami International Autodrome begins at noon local time on Friday May 1. Sprint Qualifying follows in the afternoon. The Sprint itself runs Saturday. The grand prix is Sunday at 4PM. For anyone trying to predict what happens this weekend, the single most important data point will be the FP1 timing sheet — and whether Ferrari and McLaren's upgrades show up immediately on the stopwatch.
