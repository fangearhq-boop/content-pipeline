Miami GP Faces Thunderstorm Threat on Race Day Sunday

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap

April 28, 2026

Formula 1 returns to Florida this weekend carrying one of the year's most anticipated upgrade battles — and the threat of a thunderstorm crashing the party. The Miami Grand Prix is scheduled for Sunday, May 3 at 4PM local time, but weather forecasts are raising genuine concerns about race-day disruption. Current projections show 100% cloud cover at the start of the grand prix, a 37% chance of rain, and the potential for afternoon thunderstorms — the kind of weather that activates Miami International Autodrome's lightning safety plan.

That safety plan carries teeth. Under its provisions, if lightning or thunderstorms are forecast to strike within a defined proximity of the circuit, event organisers are obligated to consider postponing or cancelling on-track activity. For a race as logistically complex as a Formula 1 grand prix, that means the worst-case scenario is not just a red-flagged race but a potential abandonment entirely.

The pre-race days look far more benign. Friday and Saturday are expected to deliver dry, hot conditions with temperatures around 30-32°C — ideal for the kind of focused practice and Sprint running the teams need. Saturday's Sprint race is expected to proceed without weather complications. It is specifically Sunday afternoon's window that carries the uncertainty, driven by the afternoon convective heating cycle that Florida is notorious for in spring and early summer.

What Rain Would Mean for the Race

A wet Miami Grand Prix would immediately scramble the narrative around this weekend's most anticipated storyline: the major upgrade packages arriving from Ferrari, McLaren, and Red Bull. Teams have spent the entire five-week break developing their most significant aerodynamic overhauls of the season. Ferrari has overhauled approximately half the SF-26 specification. McLaren's Andrea Stella described the package as "a completely new car from an aerodynamic standpoint." Those upgrades are designed for dry, high-speed running — rain changes everything.

Wet conditions tend to level the playing field and reward driver skill and team adaptability over raw downforce numbers. The result is that the team with the most effective wet-weather setup and the quickest strategic decision-making often wins — not necessarily the one with the fastest car in the dry. If thunderstorms hit race day, the upgrade battle teams have been building toward for five weeks could be rendered irrelevant in a matter of laps.

What's Next

The weather forecast will continue to evolve through the week, and the picture will sharpen significantly by Friday. If the thunderstorm risk intensifies, teams will need contingency plans for strategy and tire selection — and the FIA may need to lean on Miami's event protocols more than any race this season. For now, F1 fans in Florida and watching globally should keep a close eye on the skies. The championship crossroads moment Miami was supposed to represent may come with an unexpected meteorological dimension.
