Brundle: Hamilton Still Capable of Victory as Ferrari Brings Miami Upgrade

By Elena Voss | F1 Fanrecap

April 28, 2026

Three races into the most scrutinised career move in Formula 1 history, Lewis Hamilton's season at Ferrari has been a study in promise and frustration in roughly equal measure. Now, as the sport reconvenes in Miami with the biggest upgrade packages of the year in tow, former F1 driver and Sky Sports analyst Martin Brundle has offered one of the most compelling assessments yet of where the seven-time champion stands heading into what may be the season's defining weekend.

"If there's a sniff of a victory, I think Lewis is still very much capable of taking that," Brundle said ahead of the Miami Grand Prix. It is the kind of endorsement that carries weight precisely because it comes without blind optimism — Brundle acknowledged that Hamilton's 2026 campaign has been uneven, but identified the reasons why Miami represents a genuine turning point.

Central to his analysis is the nature of the 2026 cars themselves. Unlike the brutal, stiff ground-effect machinery of recent seasons that many drivers found physically punishing and technically challenging to manage, the 2026 regulations have produced cars that Hamilton, by Brundle's assessment, "clearly enjoys much more." The active aerodynamics and revised handling characteristics suit the Briton's technical feel for a car — a nuance that matters enormously when the machine is developed around a driver's feedback rather than fighting it.

A Season of Two Halves So Far

Hamilton's first three races for Ferrari have told a nuanced story. In China, he delivered the result Ferrari fans had been waiting for — a podium finish at the Shanghai International Circuit that showed genuine pace and composure. It was the first podium of his Ferrari tenure and a proof of concept that the partnership could work at the front of the grid. Suzuka, however, brought a return to the difficulty that has characterised Red Bull's dominance era — a sixth-place finish that reflected both Hamilton's efforts to extract performance from an SF-26 not yet at its best and Mercedes' continued superiority under the new regulations.

The gap between those two results is also the gap Ferrari intends to close with the Miami upgrade package. Team principal Fred Vasseur declared before the season break that "a new championship will start" in Miami, framing the SF-26's comprehensive overhaul as a genuine reset of the competitive order. With approximately half the car changed since Japan — including a new Macarena-style front wing, revised floor, and updated sidepod aerodynamics — Hamilton arrives with the most competitive package of his Ferrari career so far.

What's Next

The extended 90-minute FP1 session on Friday May 1 will be the first indication of whether Ferrari's promises translate to lap time. If the SF-26 shows genuine pace improvement from the very first session, the story of Hamilton's first Miami weekend in red takes on an entirely different shape. Brundle's "sniff of a victory" could become something considerably more tangible before the weekend is out. Hamilton is currently fourth in the drivers' standings with 41 points — a gap to close, but not an insurmountable one if the upgrade delivers.
