Verstappen: FIA Rule Tweaks Not Enough — "Fundamentally Something Is Wrong"

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap

April 28, 2026

The FIA attempted to address driver frustrations with the 2026 Formula 1 regulations by announcing a series of tweaks ahead of the Miami Grand Prix — increasing the super-clip ceiling from 250kW to 350kW and reducing the maximum energy recharge per lap from 8MJ to 7MJ. The intent was clear: reduce the energy management burden on drivers and allow the cars to feel more natural to race. But Max Verstappen, the four-time world champion whose vocal criticism has been at the center of the regulation dispute, made clear that the changes do not go far enough.

"The problem is simply that you can tweak these regulations a bit, but fundamentally something is wrong," Verstappen said. He went further than any other prominent driver, calling for a wholesale reconsideration of the power unit concept and specifically advocating for a return to V10 or V8 engines — formats last used in Formula 1 in 2005 and 2013 respectively. The current power unit, with its complex hybrid architecture and mandatory energy management, is what Verstappen has labeled "anti-driving."

The contrast with the sport's official position could not be sharper. F1 president Stefano Domenicali, who has been engaged in private discussions with Verstappen, has defended the 2026 regulations as a framework for "pure racing." The hybrid formula is central to Formula 1's sustainability commitments and the agreements with manufacturer partners who joined the sport based on the current technical direction. Reversing it would require a renegotiation of deals spanning years.

The On-Track Reality

Verstappen's frustrations are not purely philosophical. Red Bull Racing, the dominant force in the sport from 2021 through 2025, have failed to translate their engineering excellence into competitiveness under the new formula. Verstappen sits ninth in the drivers' standings with just 12 points from three races — a stark contrast to the relentless winning that defined his recent career. Red Bull are sixth in the constructors' standings with 16 points, well behind even teams that were considered secondary in the previous era.

The four-time champion's contract runs through 2028, but it is widely understood to contain performance-related exit clauses that could allow him to leave the sport or join a rival team if Red Bull's competitive trajectory does not improve by a specified mid-season threshold. Whether those clauses are triggered will depend partly on what Red Bull's own Miami upgrade package delivers on track — the team has brought its own significant aerodynamic developments to Florida.

What's Next

Miami is a critical data point for Red Bull as much as it is for Ferrari and McLaren. If the Macarena wing and accompanying floor changes deliver genuine lap time, the conversation around Verstappen's future will ease somewhat. If Red Bull remains off the pace of the leading Mercedes, the pressure on the team and the championship will intensify heading into the European summer races, where the exit clause timelines are understood to become relevant. Verstappen has made his position clear. What the car tells him in Miami may be more important than any regulation adjustment the FIA can offer.
