Cadillac's Austrian Disaster: Two Brake Fires, Eight Races, Zero Points — What Happens Now?

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap | June 30, 2026

Eight races. Zero points. Two cars on fire in the opening five laps.

Cadillac F1's debut season in Formula 1 has reached a new low — and that's saying something for a team that has been in damage control mode since the opening round of the 2026 season.

What Happened in Austria

The Austrian Grand Prix was supposed to be Cadillac's breakthrough. The team arrived at the Red Bull Ring with the most significant upgrade package they had brought to any circuit in their debut season — targeting the lap-15 tire cliff that had been their identified Achilles heel, with new floor geometry and revised rear suspension among the changes.

It didn't make it past lap five.

Valtteri Bottas suffered a brake fire on lap 2 and was forced to retire. Sergio Perez lasted slightly longer — his car came to a halt on laps 4-5 with the same issue. Both cars parked. Both drivers climbed out. Cadillac left Austria without a single championship point from an upgrade that was supposed to turn their season around.

The Bigger Problem

Cadillac is the only team on the 2026 grid without a championship point. Williams, Haas, and Audi — all teams that entered the season with significant challenges under the new technical regulations — have managed to score. Cadillac has not.

The team's performance issues go beyond Austrian bad luck. The MAC-26 has shown a fundamental rear instability problem through the first eight rounds, manifesting in the tire cliff that arrives around lap 15. The upgrade brought to Austria was designed to address exactly that — but the brake failure prevented any data from being gathered in race conditions.

The drivers are Valtteri Bottas — who arrived at Cadillac after years at Mercedes and Alfa Romeo — and Sergio Perez, who has previous race wins with Red Bull and experience as a championship contender. Neither is lacking ability. The car is the problem.

The Road to Silverstone

Before Cadillac can address their performance deficit, they need to diagnose and fix the brake failure that struck both cars in Austria. Getting both cars to the checkered flag at Silverstone would represent genuine progress from where they stood last Sunday.

The team's engineering group faces a difficult task: repair confidence in the car's mechanical reliability while simultaneously chasing lap time. Both are necessary. Neither is simple. And the clock is ticking — by the summer break, falling further behind the rest of the midfield would make their first season an embarrassment rather than a learning exercise.

What's Next

Cadillac needs a clean race at Silverstone, followed by a points finish as soon as possible. Their first point would change the narrative dramatically — from disaster story to underdog battling back. But until that first point arrives, the zero counter keeps climbing, and the questions about the program's direction grow louder. British GP starts July 3. For Cadillac, getting both cars to lap 26 would be a victory in itself.
