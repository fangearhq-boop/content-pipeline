Antonelli Joins Ascari: First Italian Back-to-Back F1 Winner in 73 Years

By Elena Voss April 7, 2026

A Record 73 Years in the Making

In 1953, Alberto Ascari won back-to-back Formula 1 races. He was Ferrari's champion, Italy's hero, and one of the foundational figures of grand prix racing in the sport's earliest years. For 73 years, no Italian driver had matched his achievement of winning consecutive F1 races. Then Andrea Kimi Antonelli arrived in 2026.

Antonelli's victories in China and Japan — back-to-back wins in the season's second and third rounds — made him the first Italian to accomplish what Ascari did more than seven decades ago. The historical weight of that is not lost on anyone who follows the sport. Ascari remains one of the most celebrated names in Italian motorsport history. Antonelli is now in his company.

The Youngest Champion Leader in F1 History

The Ascari comparison is one part of what makes Antonelli's start to 2026 so remarkable. The other is his standing at the top of the championship. At 19 years and 216 days old when he took the championship lead after Japan, Antonelli became the youngest drivers' championship leader in Formula 1 history — breaking the record previously held by Lewis Hamilton, who led the standings during his debut season in 2007.

Antonelli holds 72 points after three races, nine ahead of Mercedes teammate George Russell (63). Ferrari's Charles Leclerc sits third with 49, Hamilton fourth with 41. Mercedes as a constructor leads the field with 135 points — 45 ahead of Ferrari. The picture is overwhelmingly favorable for Antonelli entering the five-week break before Miami.

The Contrast With Ascari

The comparison with Ascari tells us something interesting about the two men. Ascari was aggressive, brilliant, and operated in an era where the margin between speed and catastrophe was razor thin. His back-to-back wins in 1953 were part of a dominant championship campaign in the early sport. He was Italy's driver in the fullest sense — racing for Ferrari when Ferrari represented the heart of Italian identity in motor racing.

Antonelli is a different kind of champion-in-waiting. His driving style in 2026 has been defined not by aggression but by composure. He does not make the mistakes that young drivers typically make. He doesn't need to win from the front if winning from second place is the smarter option. That intelligence, combined with the performance package Mercedes has delivered, has produced a combination that has made him almost unassailable in the opening phase of the season.

What's Next

Antonelli will have five weeks to process his championship lead before the Miami Grand Prix on May 1-3. The break gives his rivals time to develop upgrades — Ferrari has its ADUO power unit cleared for the Canadian GP and a Monza test on April 22, while McLaren is bringing a 10-change package to Miami. Antonelli's championship lead is healthy, but the upgrade cycle at Miami will test whether Mercedes can maintain the pace advantage that has defined the first three rounds. Whatever happens, the historical marker has already been set — and Alberto Ascari's record no longer stands alone.
