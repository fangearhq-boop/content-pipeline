Two Days to Fix F1: Inside the April 9 Summit That Could Save the 2026 Season

By Marcus Cole April 7, 2026

What Thursday's Meeting Needs to Accomplish

On Thursday, April 9, every Formula 1 team technical director, power unit manufacturer representative, FIA president Peter Bayer, and Formula 1 leadership will sit down in what may be the most consequential regulatory meeting in the sport's modern era. Their goal: agree on changes to the 2026 technical regulations in time to implement them before the Miami Grand Prix on May 3. Their obstacle: unanimous agreement from all ten teams is required. Their urgency: the alternative is another race that looks like Japan — dangerous speed differentials, chaotic qualifying, and a sport that cannot explain itself to its own fans.

The six proposed fixes address the root problems identified in the first three grands prix. They are not tweaks. They are substantive changes to the energy deployment framework that defines the character of 2026 F1 racing.

The Six Fixes

The first and most discussed proposal is raising the super clipping limit from 250 kilowatts to 350 kilowatts. Super clipping is the mechanism by which the electrical motor is limited in its power output when the battery reaches certain charge levels — a safety measure that creates dramatic deceleration when activated. The current 250kW threshold is generating the dangerous speed differentials between cars in boost mode and those conserving energy.

The second major proposal reduces the maximum energy recovery permitted per lap from 9 megajoules to as low as 6MJ. Reducing recovery means cars spend less time in the dramatic energy-saving modes that create the disparity problem. The third key change would remove the fixed aerodynamic deployment zones entirely — the grid squares that dictate where on the circuit cars can activate their aerodynamic systems — replacing them with a more flexible framework.

Three additional measures complete the six-point plan. The combination, if adopted, would fundamentally alter how the 2026 power units operate in racing conditions.

The Challenge: All Ten Must Say Yes

F1's regulatory structure requires unanimous consent from the teams' technical representatives for mid-season rule changes of this magnitude. That means Ferrari, Mercedes, Red Bull, McLaren, Aston Martin, Alpine, Haas, Williams, Kick Sauber, and VCARB Racing Bulls must all agree. Each has slightly different interests. Teams with stronger power units may resist changes that level the playing field. Teams suffering the most may push for changes that overcompensate.

The safety argument is the strongest lever available to the FIA. Oliver Bearman's 50G crash at Suzuka — caused by a 50km/h speed differential between his car in boost mode and Franco Colapinto's energy-saving Alpine — was the most visceral demonstration of the problem. In an FIA fan poll, 93% of respondents said rule changes were needed. The political will to act exists. Whether it can align ten competing teams is another matter entirely.

What's Next

The summit takes place Thursday. Whatever emerges — full agreement, partial agreement, or no agreement — will shape the trajectory of the 2026 season. If fixes are implemented for Miami, drivers and fans will be watching to see whether they achieve their intended effect during practice on May 1. If no agreement is reached, the FIA faces a harder conversation about whether the regulations can be adjusted unilaterally or whether the sport must navigate the full season with the current framework.
