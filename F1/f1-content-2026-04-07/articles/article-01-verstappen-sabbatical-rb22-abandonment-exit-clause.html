Verstappen Retirement Speculation Grows as Red Bull Reportedly Debates Scrapping the RB22

By Ryan Calloway April 7, 2026

The Public Language Has Changed

For months, the F1 world has watched Max Verstappen navigate the 2026 season with visible frustration. The four-time world champion sits ninth in the drivers' championship with 12 points in three races — a period in which his VCARB junior team's rookie Isack Hadjar has out-qualified him multiple times. Red Bull's RB22 is not working. Verstappen is not thriving. And now the language around his situation has shifted from "challenge" to something more serious.

Verstappen has called the 2026 formula "terrible" and "fundamentally flawed" in public. Reports have emerged of people inside Red Bull discussing whether to abandon the RB22 entirely — a car described internally as having lost the team's confidence — and start fresh with a new concept. And with those reports has come renewed discussion about Verstappen's contract and future, including the possibility of a sabbatical.

The Contract Situation

Reports have confirmed that Verstappen's Red Bull contract reportedly included a clause tied to his championship position — specifically, that if he were not on pace to finish at least second in the drivers' standings by the end of July, he would have the right to terminate the deal. Red Bull's Helmut Marko has suggested the clause was modified ahead of the season, but the fact that the clause's details are now public reporting is itself significant. It signals how seriously the situation is being taken by those close to it.

At 28 years old, Verstappen is at a career intersection that very few drivers of his caliber ever reach — dominant enough to win on talent alone, but trapped in a car and a regulatory environment that his talent cannot overcome. The frustration is real, the exit options may be real, and the coming weeks will determine whether any of them are exercised.

The Road to April 9 and Beyond

Verstappen is reportedly engaged with the FIA in discussions ahead of the April 9 summit, pushing for the rule changes that might make the 2026 formula workable. If the summit produces meaningful fixes — raising the super clipping limit, reducing energy recovery per lap, removing the fixed aero zones — the racing environment improves and perhaps Verstappen's calculus changes with it.

Red Bull's three-stage recovery plan for the RB22 includes a Stage 1 upgrade package at Miami, a more comprehensive aerodynamic overhaul at Imola, and further development beyond. If those stages deliver and the regulatory environment stabilizes, the team believes Verstappen can compete again. But the window is tight. The credibility of both the recovery plan and the rule-change process will be tested in the next 30 days.

What's Next

The FIA summit on April 9 is two days away. Its outcome will tell us much about whether the 2026 formula is salvageable in the near term. Verstappen will race a GT3 at the Nurburgring qualifiers on April 18-19 during F1's break — an indication that his love of racing remains undimmed even as his F1 situation deteriorates. The Miami Grand Prix on May 1-3 will be the next real data point for Red Bull's recovery. The question is whether Verstappen will still be motivated to fight when it arrives.
