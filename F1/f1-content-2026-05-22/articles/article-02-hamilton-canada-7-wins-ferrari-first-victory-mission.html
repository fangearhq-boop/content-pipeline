Hamilton Returns to His Greatest Circuit With Ferrari Still Seeking Its First Win From Him

By Elena Voss | F1 Fanrecap | May 22, 2026

Lewis Hamilton's Formula 1 career began at Circuit Gilles Villeneuve. His very first Grand Prix victory — from a McLaren, in his rookie season, in 2007 — came in Montreal, where a teenage prodigy served notice to everyone watching that something unprecedented had arrived.

In the 19 years since, he has won here six more times. Seven total. Seven wins at a single circuit — a record he shares only with Michael Schumacher. Every one of those victories came in silver: Mercedes or McLaren livery, team colors that have defined his career's greatest chapters.

He arrives in Montreal in 2026 wearing Ferrari red. And Ferrari has never won with him.

The Weight of the Winless Run

Hamilton joined Ferrari for the 2025 season with the expectation — from the team, from fans, and from himself — that a driver of his quality and experience would unlock a championship charge. Two seasons in, the Ferrari chapter remains without a victory. The SF-26 has shown genuine pace in Charles Leclerc's hands, but Hamilton has yet to string together the kind of Saturday-to-Sunday performance that turns his talent into a result.

He has 105 Grand Prix wins in total. None at Ferrari. The gap between what has happened and what most people assumed would happen is the defining tension of his current career chapter.

Canada offers a different kind of reset. Jenson Button, who raced alongside Hamilton at McLaren and understands his preparation style better than most, has publicly backed the 41-year-old for a strong performance here. Hamilton has famously experimented with his pre-race simulator preparation — abandoning it entirely for his third-place finish in China — and is still calibrating the approach that best prepares him for a race weekend in the current car.

Why Canada Is Different

Every driver has a circuit where the connection is something beyond technical analysis. Montreal is that circuit for Hamilton. The walls are close, the straights are long, and the hairpin demands the kind of rhythmic braking precision that plays to his core strengths. He has won here in wet conditions and dry, from the front and from further back on the grid.

The historical resonance adds another dimension. Circuit Gilles Villeneuve is named for the father of Michael Schumacher's closest competitor in the sport's greatest rivalry — and it is here that Hamilton and Schumacher are tied forever in the record books at seven wins each. The moment Hamilton wins here in a Ferrari, he stands alone.

Ferrari has brought upgraded technical packages to Montreal. Hamilton's own input into the car's development has reportedly grown in recent weeks. The conditions at this circuit — demanding, physical, requiring commitment through the Wall of Champions section — suit his driving style in a way that smoother, more technical circuits sometimes don't.
