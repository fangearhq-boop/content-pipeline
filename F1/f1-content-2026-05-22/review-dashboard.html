### Long-Form Post There is no other driver in Formula 1 history who has won the Canadian Grand Prix more often than Lewis Hamilton. With seven victories at Circuit Gilles Villeneuve — shared only with Michael Schumacher — Hamilton has made Montreal his personal domain over the course of a 19-year career. All seven of those wins came wearing silver. McLaren first, Mercedes later. He has never won wearing red. Hamilton arrives in Montreal for the 2026 Canadian Grand Prix as the most decorated driver in this race's history — and still chasing his first Ferrari victory. Two full seasons (2025 and part of 2026) have passed without a win for the Scuderia. The Ferrari SF-26 has shown pace in the hands of Charles Leclerc, but Hamilton, now 41, has yet to find his peak form in the Italian car. Canada feels different, and not just because of the numbers. This is the circuit where Hamilton's Formula 1 career began — his maiden F1 victory came right here in 2007, when an 18-year-old McLaren prodigy stunned everyone and announced himself as something entirely new. Canada is the place where Hamilton becomes Hamilton. Jenson Button, who knows Hamilton as well as anyone from their years as McLaren teammates, has publicly backed him to deliver a strong Canadian GP performance. Ferrari has brought upgrades to Montreal. The conditions at this circuit — high-speed straights, sharp hairpin, variable weather — suit aggressive, precise driving. That is Hamilton's signature. The record books still list him tied with Schumacher at seven wins each. Montreal 2026 offers the chance for eight — and the first that matters in a different way than all the others. ### Image Post Caption 7 Canadian GP wins. All at McLaren or Mercedes. ### Long-Form Post Mercedes had a choice to make. They could bring updates gradually across the season, adding them as they became ready, reducing risk and managing the development curve. Or they could wait — hold back the bulk of their engineering work until a key moment when concentration would have maximum effect. They waited. Canada is the moment. The Mercedes W17 arrives in Montreal with the team's most significant aerodynamic upgrade of the 2026 season — a revised floor designed to close what engineers have privately called a "black hole" in the car's stability at high-speed corner entries. The update is projected to be worth approximately 0.3 seconds per lap. To put that in context: in modern Formula 1, 0.3 seconds per lap is enormous. The gap between the top two in qualifying is often measured in hundredths. McLaren, Red Bull, and Ferrari have also brought technical packages to Canada — this is shaping up as the biggest development race of the season. But Mercedes' confidence in their upgrade timing is notable. Toto Wolff was unusually direct about the importance of the package, saying simply that it "has to work." The sprint format means the upgrade will face a rapid competitive test tonight in Sprint Qualifying. By the time Sunday's race ends, there will be a very clear picture of whether Canada reshuffled the technical order — or whether Mercedes simply pulled further ahead. ### Image Post Caption 0.3 seconds per lap. ### Long-Form Post Max Verstappen's 2026 Formula 1 season has not gone to plan. He is seventh in the standings with 26 points. No wins. No podiums. Four races in, and the four-time world champion is fighting for relevance in a year that was supposed to announce the new F1 regulation era. Then he left the F1 paddock during the Miami break and drove the Nurburgring 24 Hours for Mercedes-AMG — and that went wrong too. He led the endurance race by more than 30 seconds when a driveshaft failure stranded the car with hours left in the race. It was the exact kind of mechanical cruelty that his 2026 F1 campaign has delivered in different ways. He lands in Montreal needing things to start going right again. Red Bull's Miami performance offered encouragement — team principal Laurent Mekies described it as "an important step forward," and the team believes a revised technical direction is finally producing results. A new upgrade package has arrived in Canada. Verstappen's technical partnership with his new race engineer is developing, and the team's understanding of the 2026 regulations has sharpened significantly since the opening races. Circuit Gilles Villeneuve is not historically Red Bull's strongest venue — their cars have often struggled with the unique demands of the long straights and the punishing walls. But Verstappen's ability to extract pace from cars that are not at full strength is one of his defining qualities. He is not out of this championship mathematically.
