Norris Returns to Miami: Can McLaren Recapture the Magic of 2024?

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap | April 26, 2026

Every driver has a circuit that changed everything. For Lando Norris, it is Miami. On May 5, 2024, he drove away from the field on a sun-baked Florida afternoon to score the first Formula 1 victory of his career — ending a 110-race wait in a moment so emotionally charged that it prompted tears in the McLaren garage and a performance celebration that the sport had not seen in years. Miami was where Norris became a winner.

He returns in 2026 as something different: a title contender on a team that needs a good weekend more than it needs symbolism. McLaren, the 2025 constructors' champion, sits third in the standings with 56 points — 79 behind Mercedes. Norris is fifth in the drivers' championship with 25 points, a gap that makes Miami's Sprint weekend particularly valuable for accumulating points across multiple sessions.

A Different Kind of Pressure

When Norris won in Miami two years ago, McLaren was emerging — a team building confidence with every strong result, not yet burdened by the expectation that comes from being the reigning champion. Now the expectation is very much present. McLaren won the 2025 constructors' title. The 2026 technical regulations reset the competitive hierarchy and left the Papaya team playing catch-up from the opening race in Australia.

The team has upgrades planned for Miami, part of a development push that will continue into Montreal. The Miami package is not the sweeping overhaul that Red Bull has prepared, but it represents meaningful progress on the MCL63's aerodynamic performance. How much that translates to lap time on a circuit with long straights and technical sections that reward both mechanical grip and aerodynamic efficiency will be the key question of the weekend.

Miami's Circuit Characteristics

The Miami International Autodrome is a challenging circuit for car setup because it demands balance between straight-line speed — where DRS deployment and minimal drag matter — and downforce in the technical middle section where hard braking and corner exit traction determine lap time. The Macarena wing concept that several teams are debuting could prove valuable here, where the ability to reduce drag on command offers a competitive advantage without sacrificing cornering performance.

McLaren's success at Miami in 2024 was partly attributable to a car that carried speed through the turns efficiently. If the 2026 upgrade moves the MCL63 in that direction, Norris could find himself competitive in qualifying — and qualifying matters at Miami, where overtaking is possible but front-running is far preferable.

What's Next

For McLaren, Miami is a measuring stick. A strong performance — points in the Sprint, a qualifying result near the front, a podium in the Grand Prix — would suggest the upgrade direction is correct and the gap to Mercedes is closable. A difficult weekend would prompt harder questions about whether 2026 becomes a year of transition rather than title contention. Norris arriving at the circuit where his career transformed adds a personal layer to a weekend where McLaren's championship ambitions need answers quickly.
