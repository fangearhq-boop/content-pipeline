Red Bull's "Second Season Launch": What Verstappen's RB22 Overhaul Must Deliver at Miami

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap | April 26, 2026

Laurent Mekies is not known for understatement, but when the Red Bull team principal described the Miami Grand Prix as a "second season launch," he was being precise rather than dramatic. The package Max Verstappen tested at a Silverstone filming day on April 25 is not a collection of tweaks. It is a near-complete aerodynamic rethink of the RB22 — and it has to work, because the alternative is a second half of 2026 that becomes very difficult for everyone at Red Bull Racing to navigate.

The updated RB22 features a redesigned floor with improved stability in cornering, completely reworked sidepods at a more aggressive angle, a revised front wing, two small winglets adjacent to the Halo, and — most significantly — Red Bull's own version of the Macarena rear wing. The concept, which Ferrari debuted earlier in the season, involves a rear wing capable of significant rotation to reduce drag on long straights. Early technical estimates suggest the full package could add five to ten kilometers per hour at top speed.

Verstappen tested every element at Silverstone in conditions that provided useful data without the pressure of a race weekend format. The filming day was used to verify that the components behave as the simulation predicted — a critical step before committing to the package in race trim at Miami.

The Stakes Are Enormous

Three races into 2026, Red Bull is sixth in the constructors' championship with 16 points and zero race victories. Verstappen is ninth in the drivers' standings with 12 points. At Suzuka, he failed to reach Q3 for the first time since his 2015 rookie season — a benchmark that illustrated how far the team has fallen from the dominance that produced four consecutive world championships.

The RB22 has been described by Isack Hadjar, Verstappen's teammate, as having a chassis that lurches between understeer and oversteer — technically difficult to place at the limit, which is fatal in qualifying trim. The car was built around a philosophical bet on high drag levels that has not delivered the downforce payoff the team expected when the new technical regulations were introduced.

Miami is the reset. Mekies has been careful to avoid overselling the package — he noted publicly that the upgrade is not a "miracle" — but the directional confidence from the Silverstone test was clear. The team believes the RB22 can be made to work with this aerodynamic approach.

What's Next

If the upgrade delivers in Miami, Verstappen could be fighting for points in the top six and the mood inside Red Bull Racing will shift. If it doesn't, the questions about Verstappen's exit clause — reportedly set to activate if he is not in the top two of the championship by summer — become unavoidable. Miami is not just a race weekend for Red Bull. It is a referendum on whether the team can save its 2026 season before the situation becomes irreversible.
