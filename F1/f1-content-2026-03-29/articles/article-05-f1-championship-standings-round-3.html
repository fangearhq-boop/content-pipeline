F1 Championship Standings After Round 3: Antonelli 72, Mercedes Dominant, Verstappen Drowning

March 29, 2026 | F1 Fanrecap

Three races into the 2026 Formula 1 season, the championship picture is emerging with unusual clarity. Mercedes lead, by a distance. Ferrari are second, also by a distance. And Red Bull — the team that won four consecutive constructors' championships — are fourth, with 16 points, scrambling for relevance.

Here is the full picture after the Japanese Grand Prix at Suzuka.

Drivers' Championship

Kimi Antonelli leads the championship with 72 points — a total built on two victories from pole (China, Japan) and a second-place finish in Bahrain. George Russell is second with 63 points, nine behind his teammate. The intra-Mercedes battle is the closest competitive relationship at the top of the standings, and it will define the second half of the season if no other team finds the pace to challenge.

Charles Leclerc is third with 34 points, Lewis Hamilton fourth with 33. Ferrari's two drivers are separated by a single point after three rounds — a tight battle that has defined the SF-26's impressive but not dominant early season. Both are 38 points behind Russell and 39 behind Antonelli: within striking distance mathematically, but facing a Mercedes team that currently has a different pace level entirely.

The midfield standings tell the season's strangest story. Ollie Bearman sits fifth with 17 points before his DNF at Suzuka — his crash triggered the safety car that Antonelli navigated so effectively. Lando Norris is sixth with 15. Pierre Gasly is seventh with 9. And Max Verstappen, the four-time world champion, is eighth with 8 points.

Verstappen trailing Bearman and Norris in the standings is the single most dissonant fact in Formula 1 right now. He drove for the team that built the dominant car of the previous four seasons. He is considered by many to be among the two or three greatest drivers in the sport's history. And he has 8 points.

Constructors' Championship

Mercedes lead with 135 points — a total that reflects both Antonelli's victories and Russell's consistent point-scoring. Ferrari are second at 90 points, a 45-point deficit. The gap is significant but not yet insurmountable: over 19 remaining races, an average 2.4-point swing per race would be enough to close it. That requires Ferrari to find a consistent edge they have not yet demonstrated.

McLaren sit third with 56 points — a remarkable position given they scored zero from the first two rounds. Piastri's P2 at Suzuka, added to Norris's earlier results, has given McLaren genuine standing in the championship. They are 79 points behind Mercedes and 34 behind Ferrari, but the trajectory is positive.

Red Bull are fourth with just 16 points. The scale of their collapse from dominant force to fourth-placed team is the defining narrative of the 2026 regulation reset. The new technical rules, including lighter minimum weight requirements and revised aerodynamic concepts, appear to have exposed fundamental weaknesses in Red Bull's design philosophy. Verstappen's "undriveable" comment after Suzuka qualifying suggests the problems go beyond simple development gaps.

What the Break Changes

The five-week gap between Japan and the Miami Grand Prix on May 3 is a development window that every team will use differently. Mercedes, comfortable in both championships, will focus on extending their advantage. Ferrari will search for qualifying pace — Leclerc and Hamilton have been consistently close to the top on Sundays but have not been able to challenge for poles. Red Bull's priority is structural: the RB22's reported 10-kilogram weight excess needs to be addressed before the car can be properly developed.

McLaren's task is reliability. Their three-race start produced two DNS and one podium — a variance that is unsustainable across a full championship challenge.

The 2026 season has found its shape early. Mercedes are the class of the field. Ferrari are the bridge. McLaren are the wildcard. And Red Bull are the cautionary tale of a regulation reset that punished complacency and rewarded investment in the new era. Miami will tell us whether any of that is about to change.
