Piastri Skips Two Races, Then Finishes P2 at Suzuka: McLaren's Comeback Driver

March 29, 2026 | F1 Fanrecap

Oscar Piastri had not started a single Formula 1 race in 2026 before Sunday. In the sport's most unforgiving championship format — where every race and every point is cumulative — he had been effectively absent from his own season. And then, on his first race start of the year, he finished second at Suzuka.

The result is one of the more quietly extraordinary of the 2026 season. Piastri qualified P3 and drove a composed, controlled race to finish behind only Antonelli — whose commanding pole-to-flag win was in a class of its own. Everything behind the Mercedes was fair game, and Piastri claimed the best of it.

How McLaren Got Here

The context makes the result more remarkable. At the Australian Grand Prix, Piastri was involved in a crash on the formation lap — before the race had even started. He did not take the grid. In China two weeks later, both Piastri and Lando Norris failed to start due to separate electrical failures, leaving McLaren with a historic double DNS and zero championship points from the first two rounds of the season.

Heading into Suzuka, McLaren had scored no points from their entire 2026 campaign. The team that had been among the championship contenders in 2024 and 2025 was sitting at the bottom of the constructors' standings with nothing to show for two race weekends.

Piastri's P2 at Suzuka, combined with points scored by Norris in earlier rounds, moves McLaren to 56 points and third in the constructors' championship. The jump from zero to third in a single race is a statistical artefact of a strange season start — but the underlying message is clear: McLaren's problems were reliability, not pace.

The Drive Itself

Piastri's performance at Suzuka was not spectacular in the way that Antonelli's was. There were no wheel-to-wheel battles for the lead, no decisive overtakes. What Piastri delivered was something different: a measured, error-free drive from a driver who had every reason to feel uncertain about his form and his car.

Starting P3, he held his position through the opening phase of the race, managed his tyres effectively during the mid-race safety car period triggered by Ollie Bearman's crash, and maintained the gap to Leclerc in P3 through to the flag. He crossed the line 7.2 seconds behind Antonelli — further evidence of the Mercedes' pace advantage — but comfortably ahead of the Ferrari.

For a driver making his first race start of the season, it was the kind of performance that answers questions rather than raises them.

The McLaren Internal Picture

Norris, Piastri's teammate, has made all three race starts this season and accumulated 15 points. Piastri, making one start, has 18. The arithmetic is not lost on anyone: Piastri's single-race return surpassed Norris's three-race tally from the year's opening rounds.

The question of who is McLaren's true No. 1 driver is an ongoing conversation in the paddock. Piastri's P2 at Suzuka does not settle it, but it does establish that when he is given a functioning car and a clean race start, he is capable of matching or exceeding expectations at any circuit.

McLaren's Path Forward

The five-week break before Miami gives McLaren time to understand what went wrong in Australia and China, and to ensure the reliability issues that cost them two complete race weekends do not recur. The pace is clearly there — qualifying P3 in Piastri's case suggests the MCL39 is capable of competing with Ferrari and potentially challenging Mercedes in the right conditions.

With 79 points to close on Mercedes heading into 19 remaining races, McLaren's championship challenge is a long shot. But as a team that arrived at Suzuka with zero points and left with a podium, the trajectory is exactly right. Piastri, in particular, has given the team and its fans something to build on.
