Verstappen Makes Nürburgring 24 Hours Debut in Mercedes-AMG GT3

By Elena Voss | F1 Fanrecap | May 16, 2026

Max Verstappen is spending his Formula 1 off-weekend doing something that most racing drivers would find quite daunting: competing at the Nürburgring 24 Hours.

The four-time world champion made his debut at the legendary German endurance race on Saturday, driving a Mercedes-AMG GT3 alongside his co-drivers. The team qualified fourth on the grid for the race, which started at 2 PM local time (2 PM BST / 9 AM ET) and will run for exactly 24 hours around the famed Nordschleife circuit through Saturday night and into Sunday.

It's a remarkable contrast — the same man who has spent years navigating F1's 20-plus race calendar is now taking on one of motorsport's most grueling single events, on one of the world's most dangerous racetracks, during what could be the most consequential off-season of his career.

The Green Hell Challenge

The Nordschleife is not a track to be taken lightly. At nearly 26 kilometers in length with over 100 corners, dramatic elevation changes, and a reputation for rapidly changing weather conditions, it demands a completely different skill set from what Verstappen deploys in Formula 1.

Verstappen himself acknowledged the difficulty, calling qualifying "super difficult." That admission from a driver of his calibre is telling — this is not a track that simply bows to talent or reputation. It demands respect, patience, and a willingness to learn.

The GT3 class machinery is also vastly different from an F1 car. More weight, less downforce, different brake characteristics, and a fundamentally different driving philosophy. Endurance racing prioritizes consistency and team coordination over 24 hours rather than the white-knuckle one-lap excellence of Formula 1 qualifying.

Why Now?

Verstappen's presence at the Nürburgring 24 Hours comes at an interesting time in his career. His Red Bull team continues to struggle with the 2026 regulations — he sits seventh in the drivers' championship with just 26 points, over 70 behind leader Kimi Antonelli. The partnership that defined his four title-winning seasons, with race engineer Gianpiero Lambiase, is coming to an end as Lambiase prepares to leave for McLaren.

Against that backdrop, Verstappen's decision to race in an endurance event on his F1 off-weekend speaks to his genuine love of motorsport in all its forms. This is a man who races because he loves racing — not just because he's paid to.

What's Next

The Nürburgring 24 Hours concludes Sunday, May 17, with Verstappen's team aiming to convert their fourth-place qualifying position into a strong race result. In F1, attention then turns to the Canadian Grand Prix (May 22-24) at Circuit Gilles-Villeneuve — a race where Verstappen will need to find both form and pace to stay relevant in the championship battle. For now, though, it's 24 hours around the Nordschleife — the Green Hell — and Verstappen has never been one to take the easy path.
