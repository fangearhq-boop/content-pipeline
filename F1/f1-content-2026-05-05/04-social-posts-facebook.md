When teammate Isack Hadjar occasionally gets a better start, Verstappen suggests it comes down to "luck" — which does not suggest confidence in a technical fix.

The gap between the dominant Verstappen of recent years and where he stands today is jarring — and for Red Bull, the challenge of closing it grows more serious with each passing race.

He finished third in the Chinese Grand Prix — ending a 16-month podium drought — and was sixth at Miami, showing consistent race craft in a Ferrari that is beginning to unlock its potential.

The key, says Ferrari team principal Fred Vasseur, is integration. "It's always much easier the second season," Vasseur explained. "He was in the simulator in the middle of 2025, when we started the project. He's part of the project from the beginning."

Hamilton acknowledges that Mercedes currently has a significant performance advantage — he estimates roughly half a second per lap in race conditions — but he is not treating that as a permanent state of affairs. And legendary champion Nigel Mansell, watching from the outside, has described Hamilton as looking "reinvigorated."

At 41 years old, with eight world titles as his ambition, Lewis Hamilton has not come to Ferrari to play a supporting role. The 2026 season is still long. His best chance at a race win could come at the Canadian Grand Prix in three weeks.

### STORY 5: 2026 Regulation Report Card

**Long-Form Post**
Every Formula 1 regulation reset creates winners and losers. The 2026 rule changes — the biggest since 2022 — have been no exception. After four races, a clear hierarchy is emerging.

Mercedes deserve an A+ for how they've approached this new era. They built a dominant car and gave it to two drivers capable of maximizing it. Four race wins from four starts, a commanding constructors' championship lead. They clearly understood what the new technical regulations demanded — active aerodynamics, optimized energy deployment without the MGU-H — better than anyone.

Ferrari are the second-best story of the season so far. The Maranello team is using their own power unit, which appears competitive in the new regulatory environment. Charles Leclerc is third in the drivers' standings. Lewis Hamilton is scoring points regularly. The car is not as fast as Mercedes, but it is consistent and reliable.

McLaren started the season behind but are fighting back hard. The 2024 constructors' champions recognize they didn't do enough on their power unit integration before the season and have been trying to make up ground through chassis development. The Miami upgrade showed they can close gaps quickly.

Then there is Red Bull. The team that won four consecutive constructors' titles is currently struggling to reach the podium regularly. The RB22 has rear instability issues, graining tire problems and energy harvesting deficiencies. The regulation change has exposed gaps in their technical approach that simply did not exist in recent seasons.

History says Red Bull will eventually solve their problems — they are too good a team to remain in this position. But for now, 2026 belongs to Mercedes. And to a 19-year-old Italian who has taken to it all like he was born for this moment.
