128 Points, No Peer: Antonelli's F1 Championship Lead Is Already Historic at Just 19

By Elena Voss | F1 Fanrecap | May 5, 2026

Four races, four poles, three wins — Kimi Antonelli holds a 48-point championship lead and has already made history as the first driver to win his opening three grands prix from pole position.

The question heading into the 2026 Formula 1 season was whether Kimi Antonelli was truly ready. After four races, the 19-year-old has provided an answer so emphatic that it has silenced most of the doubters.

Antonelli leads the drivers' championship with 128 points. George Russell, his Mercedes teammate and the pre-season favorite in many quarters, sits 48 points behind on 80. Charles Leclerc is third with 59 points. Reigning champion Lando Norris and Ferrari's Lewis Hamilton are tied on 51.

The raw numbers tell part of the story. The historical context tells the rest.

Making History With Every Race

Antonelli has won the Australian, Chinese, Japanese and Miami Grands Prix — every race in which he qualified from pole position in the first three rounds, and then again in Miami. That makes him the first driver in Formula 1 history to win his first three grands prix from pole position. The achievement underscores not just raw speed but the ability to convert advantage into victories — a skill that separates great champions from great qualifiers.

He is also the youngest championship leader in the sport's history, having carried that distinction since his first victory in Melbourne. That he has extended the lead to 48 points in just four rounds makes the milestone even more meaningful.

At Miami, Antonelli did something that may become a signature of his approach. He recovered from a poor start — a recurring issue he and Mercedes have been working to resolve — resisted late pressure from Norris over the final laps, and crossed the line first. It was not easy. But it was clinical.

Mercedes Dominance Behind the Numbers

Antonelli's lead reflects not just his own performance but the broader superiority of the Mercedes W16 under the 2026 regulations. The new technical rules — which eliminated the MGU-H and introduced fully active aerodynamics — appear to have been solved more completely by Mercedes than any other team.

Russell has regularly finished in the top four alongside his teammate, meaning Mercedes is accumulating points efficiently at both ends of their driver lineup. The constructors' championship is also firmly in Mercedes' grasp after four rounds.

Antonelli has been the sharper edge of that double-ended sword. His qualifying pace has been exceptional across all four circuits, and his race management — for a driver still in his debut season of Formula 1 — has drawn comparisons to champions who took years to develop those skills.

Can Anyone Close the Gap?

A 48-point advantage after just four of a planned twenty-four rounds is real but not impenetrable. The mathematics of Formula 1 allow for big swings when mechanical failures or accidents intervene, and with twenty races remaining, there is plenty of track left.

McLaren's Miami performance offered the clearest signal yet that the championship picture could tighten. Norris took second in the race with a significantly upgraded car, and the team has openly discussed its ambition to replicate the kind of mid-season momentum shift that produced their constructors' title in 2024.

Ferrari are also consistent threats, and Hamilton's improving form adds another dimension to a title fight that was in danger of becoming a procession. But closing a 48-point gap requires the leader to falter as much as the chasers to surge.

What's Next

The 2026 F1 season resumes with the Canadian Grand Prix at Circuit Gilles Villeneuve in Montreal, running May 22-24. Canada is a sprint weekend, offering additional points. The circuit's combination of slow corners, chicanes and heavy braking zones provides a different challenge than the high-speed passages of Miami. Whether the characteristics suit Antonelli and Mercedes as well as those recent tracks remains to be seen — but based on what he has shown in four races, the burden of proof has shifted entirely onto those trying to catch him.
