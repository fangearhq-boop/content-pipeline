Verstappen's Red Bull Nightmare: Bad Starts, a Struggling Car, and Questions That Won't Go Away

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap | May 5, 2026

Four races into the 2026 Formula 1 season, Max Verstappen and Red Bull are confronting the most serious technical crisis of their dominant era — and the questions about their future together are growing louder.

A year ago, few would have predicted that the biggest storyline of the 2026 Formula 1 season would be the decline of the sport's most dominant team. Yet after four rounds of the new regulatory era, Red Bull Racing and Max Verstappen are deep in unfamiliar and uncomfortable territory.

The RB22, Red Bull's challenger under the new 2026 regulations, has significant technical problems. The team has publicly acknowledged "significant shortcomings" with the car. Verstappen, four times the world champion, has been candid about the fact that he does not enjoy racing these new-generation vehicles. And behind the scenes, a reported contract exit clause looms as an uncomfortable backdrop to every difficult weekend.

A Car Built on the Wrong Foundation

The 2026 Formula 1 regulations represent the biggest technical reset since the ground-effect era began in 2022. The removal of the MGU-H — the small but powerful heat-energy recovery device that teams spent years optimizing — fundamentally changed how power units are built and how energy is deployed on track.

Red Bull's engineering approach, which had been so comprehensively superior under the previous regulatory framework, appears to have misread key aspects of the new era. The RB22 struggles most in high-speed corners, where it harvests energy at lower speeds than competitors. At the rear of the car, instability creates handling problems that television cameras have captured in Verstappen's onboard footage — multiple steering corrections on single flying laps, the kind of constant adjustment that burns energy and driver focus simultaneously.

Tire degradation has compounded the problem. The car appears to grain its tires more than competitors, which in long race runs translates directly into lost lap time and constrained strategy options.

The Start Problem That Defines Every Race

Even when Red Bull's outright pace is competitive enough to qualify well, the races have been compromised at the very first moment. Under the 2026 regulations, launch procedure is dramatically more complex than in previous seasons. Without the MGU-H, drivers must rev their engines much higher for extended periods before launching, while also managing precisely how much electrical energy they harvested on the formation lap to fuel their initial acceleration off the line.

Verstappen has failed to execute this procedure satisfactorily in every race so far. At Miami, he spun at Turn 1 and received a five-second penalty for a pit exit breach, dropping from a potentially strong position. When his teammate Isack Hadjar occasionally manages a better start, Verstappen has attributed it to "luck" — a response that suggests either frustration with a technical issue that resists solution, or a difficulty accepting that a younger teammate is finding answers he has not.

The Exit Clause Question

Reports that emerged in 2025 established that Verstappen's Red Bull contract contains a performance-linked exit clause — the specific terms allow him to leave if he falls outside the top two positions in the championship at a defined point in the season. After four rounds, he is currently fifth in the standings, well clear of the top two required to keep that clause dormant.

Red Bull has always been Verstappen's team in a meaningful sense, and the four-time champion has given little public indication that he is actively exploring other options. But the situation is no longer entirely theoretical. A team that has provided him with championship-winning machinery in each of the past four seasons is now struggling to provide machinery that wins at all. The sport's best driver and its current worst-performing top team is a situation that cannot persist indefinitely without generating pressure on both sides.

What's Next

Red Bull will have three weeks before the Canadian Grand Prix to work on the RB22's fundamental issues. Circuit Gilles Villeneuve's layout — with its heavy braking zones, slow chicanes and tight final sector — is different from the high-speed character of Miami and may theoretically offer marginally better conditions for Red Bull. But the team's problems run deeper than circuit-specific characteristics, and until the underlying rear stability and energy deployment issues are addressed, Verstappen's prospects of mounting a genuine championship challenge remain constrained. How Red Bull responds over the coming months will say much about whether this is a temporary setback or a more fundamental shift in the sport's competitive order.
