Not Giving Up: Norris and McLaren Have a Title Recovery Plan — and a 2024 Blueprint to Follow

By Elena Voss | F1 Fanrecap | May 5, 2026

Lando Norris won the Miami Sprint and finished second in the race with a heavily upgraded car. The reigning world champion says McLaren is not surrendering the 2026 title fight — and their recent history says that claim deserves to be taken seriously.

Seventy-seven points. That is the gap between Lando Norris and championship leader Kimi Antonelli after four rounds of the 2026 Formula 1 season. By some measures, it looks like a gap that renders the title fight theoretical. By Norris's measure — and by the example of 2024 — it looks like something to work toward.

"We're not giving up," Norris said after the Miami Grand Prix, where he finished second behind Antonelli following a weekend in which McLaren looked more competitive than at any point in 2026. "We've learned from previous difficult starts."

That is not empty bravado. McLaren have done exactly this before.

The Miami Turnaround

McLaren's performance at the Miami Grand Prix weekend was a significant departure from what had been a difficult opening to the season. Norris won the Sprint race — McLaren's first race win in any session of the 2026 campaign — and then finished second in the Grand Prix itself, the closest any non-Mercedes driver had come to Antonelli this year.

The reason was an upgrade package that team principal Andrea Stella described as "completely new from an aerodynamic standpoint." New floor, new bodywork, new rear wing, new front and rear brake ducts. It is the kind of wholesale revision that teams spend months developing and deploy at a moment when they believe it will have maximum impact.

The fact that it delivered immediately — a sprint win and a race podium — suggests McLaren's engineering team has correctly diagnosed their deficit and found a meaningful response.

The Honest Assessment

McLaren have also been candid about where they went wrong in the season's opening phase. The team acknowledge that they did not spend enough time optimizing their relationship with the Mercedes power unit for the new 2026 regulations. "Tiny margins make significant differences," has been the internal acknowledgment.

The challenge under 2026 rules is that energy deployment — specifically how efficiently teams manage the electrical systems without the MGU-H — is central to both qualifying performance and race pace. McLaren, by their own admission, arrived in Melbourne without that relationship fully understood. They have been working to close that gap throughout the opening rounds.

What they have clearly got right is the chassis. The MCL40's aerodynamic package, as substantially revised for Miami, appears capable of competing with Mercedes when performing as intended. That gives Norris and teammate Oscar Piastri something to build on.

Why 2024 Matters

McLaren's reference to their 2024 season is more than motivational storytelling. Two seasons ago, they genuinely trailed the championship standings significantly after the early rounds, brought a transformative car upgrade, won multiple races through the second half of the season and took the Constructors' Championship. It happened. The team that did it is largely intact. The lessons are institutional.

They have also been explicit about their commitment to this season's title fight: McLaren will not switch development resources to their 2027 car prematurely. The 2026 championship is still the priority. That focus is a statement of intent that Norris and Piastri can race behind.

What's Next

The Canadian Grand Prix on May 22-24 at Circuit Gilles Villeneuve is a circuit that traditionally rewards mechanical grip, strong braking stability and the ability to put power down cleanly out of slow corners. All of those characteristics align reasonably well with what McLaren has historically been good at. Norris will arrive in Montreal having won a race this season, with an upgraded car that is trending in the right direction, and with 77 points to overcome. The trajectory has changed. Whether it has changed enough — and quickly enough — will be the defining question of the months ahead.
