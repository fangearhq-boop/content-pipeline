2026 F1 Regulations Report Card: Four Races In, the Winners and Losers Are Already Clear

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap | May 5, 2026

The biggest Formula 1 rule overhaul since 2022 was always going to produce winners and losers. After four grands prix, the grades are in — and some of them are shocking.

Every major Formula 1 regulation change creates a new competitive order. The 2022 ground-effect rules elevated Red Bull and Ferrari, producing Max Verstappen's four-year dynasty. The 2026 rules — which eliminated the MGU-H, introduced fully active aerodynamics and overhauled power unit architecture — were the most significant technical reset since that generation began.

Four races in, the grades are becoming clear. Some teams understood the brief. Others did not.

Mercedes: A+

No team has responded to the 2026 regulations more completely than Mercedes. Four races, four wins. Kimi Antonelli — a 19-year-old in his first Formula 1 season — is the championship leader by 48 points. George Russell is second. The Constructors' Championship is firmly in Mercedes' control.

Mercedes' advantage appears to stem from their approach to energy deployment. Without the MGU-H — which teams spent years using to recover heat energy from exhaust gases — the key differentiator in 2026 is how efficiently teams harvest and deploy electrical energy through other systems. Mercedes appears to have solved this more completely than any competitor, giving both Antonelli and Russell a power and aerodynamic efficiency advantage that manifests lap after lap.

The Silver Arrows could not have asked for a more complete vindication of their 2026 technical program.

Ferrari: B+

Ferrari enter their second year with Lewis Hamilton with something that matters in this new era: their own competitive power unit. The Ferrari power unit appears well-suited to the 2026 regulations, and Charles Leclerc sits third in the drivers' championship with 59 points. Hamilton is fifth on 51 points and showing strong consistency.

Ferrari are second in the Constructors' Championship. They are not winning races yet — Mercedes holds a significant performance advantage that Hamilton himself estimates at roughly half a second per lap — but the trajectory is positive. The team is scoring regularly, developing in the right direction, and has two drivers who are capable of winning when the gap closes.

McLaren: B

The defending constructors' champions had a difficult start to the new regulations before their comprehensive Miami upgrade began to change the picture. McLaren acknowledge they under-invested in understanding their Mercedes power unit integration for the 2026 season, arriving in Melbourne with gaps they are still working to close.

The chassis, however, has proven to be a strong platform. The Miami upgrade — described by team principal Andrea Stella as "completely new from an aerodynamic standpoint" — delivered a sprint win and a race podium immediately. Lando Norris took second in the Grand Prix. Oscar Piastri has been consistently in the points. McLaren know how to develop a car through a season, and their 2024 precedent demonstrates they can close a large points gap when the engineering momentum shifts in their favor.

The grade is a B rather than higher because the opening four rounds have been harder than they needed to be. But the trajectory after Miami is encouraging.

Red Bull: F

There is no kind way to describe Red Bull's 2026 regulation response. The team that dominated Formula 1 for four consecutive seasons — winning every drivers' and constructors' championship from 2022 through 2025 — is currently in the midfield.

The RB22 has multiple compounding problems. The rear of the car is unstable at high speed. Tire degradation has been excessive. The team struggles to harvest electrical energy efficiently at the speeds where it matters most. And the race launch procedure — made more complex by the removal of the MGU-H — has produced poor starts across all four rounds for Max Verstappen.

Red Bull have publicly acknowledged "significant shortcomings" with their challenger. Verstappen has said he does not enjoy racing the new generation of cars. The four-time world champion is fifth in the championship standings. The team that won 21 of 24 races in 2023 is currently without a podium in 2026.

History says Red Bull will not accept this position permanently. They are too capable and too well-resourced to remain here. But the gap to Mercedes is substantial, and fundamental engineering problems of this kind — rear geometry, energy management, aerodynamic philosophy — take time to resolve at the level required to compete with a team that clearly got it right from the start.

What's Next

The development race for the 2026 season will intensify over the coming months. Every team at the back of this report card is working aggressively to close the deficit. Canadian Grand Prix arrives on May 22-24, providing the next data point in this evolving technical picture. Mercedes's challengers will need to find their answers quickly — because at 19 years old, with four wins from four starts, Kimi Antonelli is not standing still and waiting.
