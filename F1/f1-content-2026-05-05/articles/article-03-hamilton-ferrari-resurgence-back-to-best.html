"Back to My Best": Hamilton's Second Season at Ferrari Is Already Showing What the First Could Not

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap | May 5, 2026

Lewis Hamilton finished P6 at Miami, claimed a podium in China, and says he is "both mentally and physically" at his best. Ferrari believe his integration is now complete — and the results are beginning to reflect that.

When Lewis Hamilton left Mercedes after twelve seasons to join Ferrari, the transition was widely expected to be difficult. His first year in Maranello in 2025 confirmed that expectation. The car, the team culture, the working relationships — all of it required time to build.

This year, that time has paid off.

"I'm back to my best — both mentally and physically," Hamilton said following the 2026 Miami Grand Prix, where he finished sixth. It is a bold claim from a driver who spent most of 2025 finding his footing. But the evidence from the opening four rounds of 2026 supports it.

A Winter That Changed Everything

Hamilton credits an exceptional off-season for his improved form. "Training this winter has been the heaviest and most intense that I've ever had," he said. He worked with a personal trainer from Christmas Day onward, combining physical preparation with significantly more simulator time at Maranello.

That simulator work is where the second season effect becomes clearest. Ferrari team principal Fred Vasseur explained: "It's always much easier the second season, because you are part of the project from the beginning. He was in the simulator in the middle of 2025, when we started the project."

For Hamilton, this meant arriving at the 2026 season with a deeper understanding of the car he would drive, the engineers he would work with, and the direction Ferrari intended to take the chassis. The difference between learning a car mid-season and having shaped its development is significant at any level of motorsport. At Formula 1's elite level, it is often the difference between promising and competitive.

The Results Are Reflecting the Investment

Hamilton's Chinese Grand Prix podium — a third-place finish — ended a 16-month drought without a rostrum result. He had not stood on a Formula 1 podium since before his move to Ferrari, and the return came on a circuit that historically rewards technical precision alongside outright pace.

In Miami, a sixth-place finish kept points flowing. Ferrari are second in the Constructors' Championship, with Hamilton and Charles Leclerc (third in the drivers' standings with 59 points) both contributing regularly to that total.

Eight-time world champion Nigel Mansell observed from the outside that Hamilton appears "reinvigorated" in 2026. It is an external validation of what Hamilton himself has been articulating, and what Ferrari can see in the data.

The Honest Gap Assessment

Hamilton has not allowed this improved form to cloud his assessment of where Ferrari actually stands relative to the competition. He estimates that Mercedes holds roughly half a second per lap advantage over Ferrari in race conditions — a substantial gap at this level of the sport, where tenths and hundredths decide championships.

That acknowledgment is strategically honest. It signals to his engineers that he understands the challenge and is not satisfied with merely claiming podiums while Mercedes wins races. The history of Formula 1 is full of moments when gaps that seemed fixed proved bridgeable. McLaren closed a significant deficit to Mercedes through the 2024 season. Ferrari themselves have done it before.

Whether 2026 provides that kind of swing remains to be seen. But a confident, prepared, physically peak Hamilton — with Ferrari now building the 2026 car around his feedback from the start — makes that possibility considerably more plausible than it would have been twelve months ago.

What's Next

The Canadian Grand Prix at Circuit Gilles Villeneuve on May 22-24 represents one of the better opportunities on the calendar for Hamilton and Ferrari. The circuit's heavy braking zones and slower corners suit a driving style that rewards precision over pure aerodynamic downforce — historically a circuit where Ferrari-powered cars have been competitive. If Hamilton is truly "back to his best," Canada may be where that claim is tested most forcefully.
