Four races into the 2026 season — the biggest regulation reset since 2022 — Red Bull finds itself in unfamiliar territory. The RB22 has significant technical problems. The rear of the car is unstable, making it difficult to manage in high-speed corners. Tire degradation has been heavy. Energy harvesting — critical under the new regulations — lags behind the leading teams. And the race starts. That has been a persistent problem since the first race in Melbourne. Under 2026 rules, the removal of the MGU-H makes the launch procedure dramatically more complex, requiring drivers to rev their engines much higher for longer and carefully manage energy on the formation lap. Verstappen has struggled with this every single round. Red Bull have publicly admitted to "significant shortcomings" with their 2026 challenger. Verstappen has made no secret of the fact that he doesn't enjoy racing these new regulations cars. There is also reported to be a contractual exit clause in Verstappen's deal, allowing him to leave if he falls outside the top two in the championship at a specific point in the season. He is currently fifth. NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post When Lewis Hamilton left Mercedes for Ferrari after the 2024 season, many in the paddock expected a difficult transition. They were right — his first year in Maranello was challenging. This year is different. "I'm back to my best — both mentally and physically," Hamilton said recently. And the results are beginning to back that up. He finished third in the Chinese Grand Prix — ending a 16-month podium drought — and was sixth at Miami, showing consistent race craft in a Ferrari that is beginning to unlock its potential. The key, says Ferrari team principal Fred Vasseur, is integration. "It's always much easier the second season," Vasseur explained. "He was in the simulator in the middle of 2025, when we started the project. He's part of the project from the beginning." Hamilton also credits the most intense winter training of his career. He and a new personal trainer worked together from Christmas Day, combining simulator sessions with physical preparation at a level he says exceeded anything he had done before. The results show Ferrari as second in the Constructors' Championship, with Hamilton and Leclerc both scoring regularly. Hamilton acknowledges that Mercedes currently has a significant performance advantage — he estimates roughly half a second per lap in race conditions — but he is not treating that as a permanent state of affairs. And legendary champion Nigel Mansell, watching from the outside, has described Hamilton as looking "reinvigorated." At 41 years old, with eight world titles as his ambition, Lewis Hamilton has not come to Ferrari to play a supporting role. The 2026 season is still long. His best chance at a race win could come at the Canadian Grand Prix in three weeks. ### Image Post Caption Hamilton: "I'm back to my best." P3 in China. P6 in Miami. Ferrari P2 in Constructors. 2026 Regulation Report Card — Mercedes Aces It, Red Bull Fails, Ferrari and McLaren Chase
NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post Every Formula 1 regulation reset creates winners and losers. The 2026 rule changes — the biggest since 2022 — have been no exception. After four races, a clear hierarchy is emerging. Mercedes deserve an A+ for how they've approached this new era. They built a dominant car and gave it to two drivers capable of maximizing it. Four race wins from four starts, a commanding constructors' championship lead. They clearly understood what the new technical regulations demanded — active aerodynamics, optimized energy deployment without the MGU-H — better than anyone. Ferrari are the second-best story of the season so far. The Maranello team is using their own power unit, which appears competitive in the new regulatory environment. Charles Leclerc is third in the drivers' standings. Lewis Hamilton is scoring points regularly. The car is not as fast as Mercedes, but it is consistent and reliable. McLaren started the season behind but are fighting back hard. The 2024 constructors' champions recognize they didn't do enough on their power unit integration before the season and have been trying to make up ground through chassis development. The Miami upgrade showed they can close gaps quickly. Then there is Red Bull. The team that won four consecutive constructors' titles is currently struggling to reach the podium regularly. The RB22 has rear instability issues, graining tire problems and energy harvesting deficiencies. The regulation change has exposed gaps in their technical approach that simply did not exist in recent seasons. History says Red Bull will eventually solve their problems — they are too good a team to remain in this position. But for now, 2026 belongs to Mercedes. And to a 19-year-old Italian who has taken to it all like he was born for this moment. ### Image Post Caption Four races in: Mercedes leads everything. Ferrari in contention. McLaren fighting back.
