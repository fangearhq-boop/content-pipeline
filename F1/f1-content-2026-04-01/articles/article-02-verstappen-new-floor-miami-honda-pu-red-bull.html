Verstappen Gets New Floor for Miami — But Red Bull's Older Honda PU Complicates the Picture

By F1 Fanrecap Staff April 1, 2026

The Floor Update

Red Bull Racing will arrive in Miami with a significant aerodynamic update for Max Verstappen. The RB22's modified floor fences and floor edges — specifically the tunnel inlets and contoured flanges along the outside of the floor — represent the second of three planned update stages for a car that has badly underdelivered through the first three rounds. Per Helmut Marko, the new design addresses the RB22's most consistent weakness: understeer in slow and medium-speed corners.

Team principal Laurent Mekies confirmed that Miami is specifically identified in Red Bull's internal development roadmap: Jeddah brought bodywork tweaks, Miami brings the floor changes, and further updates are scheduled for Imola. Mekies has repeatedly stated his confidence that Verstappen will be "much happier" once the updates are in place — a statement that contains both genuine technical optimism and the unmistakable subtext of a team managing a frustrated driver.

The Power Unit Problem

The complication is mechanical. Red Bull has reverted to an older Honda power unit for Miami at Honda's request, after running a fresh unit in Jeddah. The reason: engine development is frozen under 2026 regulations, but fresh units still provide a marginal advantage over units that have accumulated usage cycles — as degradation sets in across heat cycles, software resets, and accumulated wear. Mercedes has installed a new power unit for Miami. Red Bull's decision to run a used unit is not laziness — it is Honda's assessment of where in the allocation cycle their units perform best.

The net effect is a zero-sum equation: Red Bull gains performance from an improved floor but may give some of that back through a used power unit relative to what Mercedes is running. In a sport decided by tenths of seconds, that gap could be decisive at a circuit where straight-line speed competes with the kind of technical corners the new floor is specifically designed to address.

The Context of Ninth Place

Verstappen enters Miami ninth in the drivers' championship with 12 points — a position that would have been unthinkable a year ago. Three rounds into the 2026 season, the four-time world champion has not finished in the top five. His frustrations have been public and pointed, directed primarily at the 2026 regulations rather than solely at his team. Red Bull's best response is not words. It is delivering a car at Miami that lets him race at the front.

What's Next

The Miami Grand Prix (May 1-3) is a Sprint weekend, meaning Verstappen will have limited practice time to understand how the new floor behaves before qualifying for both the Sprint and the main Grand Prix. Red Bull's update cadence — floor at Miami, further package at Imola — means this is not a one-race fix. If the floor data is positive, it feeds into Imola preparations. If it disappoints, Red Bull faces a longer rebuild. For Verstappen, who has hinted that his F1 future is not guaranteed beyond 2026, Miami is far more than a race weekend. It is the first tangible test of whether Red Bull can give him what he needs to compete.
