FIA Sets April 9 Meeting to Fix F1's 2026 Regulation Crisis Before Miami GP

By F1 Fanrecap Staff April 1, 2026

A Deadline Has Been Set

Formula 1 has a concrete deadline: April 9. That is when a FIA technical working group will convene to evaluate proposals from team technical directors for changes to the sport's embattled 2026 energy harvesting regulations. The five-week break created by the cancellation of the Bahrain and Saudi Arabian rounds — both axed due to safety concerns related to the Iran conflict — has given the sport an unexpected window to address a problem that nearly turned catastrophic at Suzuka.

The meeting date was confirmed by Racing Bull's Peter Bayer, a former senior FIA official. With Miami set for May 1 through 3, the April 9 session gives teams and the FIA three weeks to evaluate any approved changes and incorporate them into the regulations before Round 4 begins.

The GPDA's Warning

GPDA director Carlos Sainz did not soften his language in the aftermath of the Japanese Grand Prix. "We, as GPDA, have warned the FIA that these accidents are going to happen a lot with this set of regulations, and we need to change something soon," Sainz stated. His warning came directly after Oliver Bearman's 50G crash at Suzuka's Spoon Curve, where the speed differential between a harvesting car and a deploying car reached 45 kilometres per hour at approximately 190 mph.

The crash was the inevitable consequence of an energy management system that forces cars to dramatically slow down during harvesting phases — creating unpredictable closing speeds when neighbouring cars are simultaneously deploying electrical power. Alpine's warning indicators were not illuminated when Bearman encountered Colapinto's slowing car, and the crash happened before either driver had time to react.

What Changes Are Being Considered

Three proposals are expected to feature prominently at the April 9 meeting. The first and most broadly supported is removing energy management requirements from qualifying entirely, restoring the flat-out single-lap attacks that have historically defined the sport's most electric sessions. A Chinese Grand Prix meeting between team bosses and the FIA produced "broad agreement" that qualifying should be the top priority. Championship leader Kimi Antonelli acknowledged the problem: "The FIA is already looking into how to improve for Miami, both in qualifying and race."

Race-day proposals include adjustments to harvest deployment thresholds to reduce speed differentials between cars in different energy phases, and restrictions on super-clipping zones where the 200kW power differential is most dangerous. Any changes require unanimous agreement from all ten teams — an unusually high bar that means compromise, not transformation, is the realistic outcome from April 9.

What's Next

April 9 is the beginning of a process, not the end of one. Even if unanimous team agreement is reached, the logistics of implementing regulation changes for Miami require rapid approval through FIA governance channels. The most likely immediate outcome is a qualifying modification that removes mandatory energy management, with race-day changes phased in later. For the 2026 season — already stripped to 22 rounds from 24 — the April break either becomes the moment Formula 1 corrected course, or the starting point of a longer crisis. Max Verstappen's future in the sport may depend on which outcome arrives first.
