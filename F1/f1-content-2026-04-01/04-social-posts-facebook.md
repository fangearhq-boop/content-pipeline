### STORY 6: The 2026 Regulation Problem — Explained

#### Long-Form Post — 2:00 PM ET

```
If you have watched F1 in 2026 and wondered why drivers sometimes seem to deliberately slow down, why overtakes appear random, and why a catastrophic crash happened at Suzuka — this is the explanation.

The 2026 power units operate on a 50/50 split between combustion and electric power. To keep the battery charged, cars must periodically enter a "harvesting" phase — where the electric motors reverse their function and absorb energy from braking and coasting. During this phase, the car significantly slows down. The problem: if the car behind it is in "deployment" mode (using the battery for acceleration), the speed differential is enormous.

At Suzuka, that differential reached 45 km/h. Oliver Bearman, at full deployment, encountered Franco Colapinto's harvesting car on the racing line at Spoon Curve, hitting at 190mph and sustaining a 50G impact. GPDA director Carlos Sainz, who chairs the drivers' safety group, described it as predictable: "We have warned the FIA that these accidents are going to happen a lot with this set of regulations."

The FIA has three options on the table: remove energy management from qualifying so it returns to flat-out attacks, adjust harvest thresholds to reduce speed differentials in races, and restrict super-clipping zones where the 200kW deployment differential is most dangerous. None of these fixes can be implemented unilaterally — all ten teams must agree. The April 9 meeting begins that process. What happens next will define the rest of the 2026 season.
```
