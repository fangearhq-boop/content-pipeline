Six Days, Six Fixes: The FIA Vote That Could Transform F1 2026 Before Miami

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap | April 14, 2026

The FIA's April 20 binding vote on 2026 energy regulation tweaks could change the competitive order before the season's next race — and six specific proposals are now known.

The Energy Management Problem

Formula 1's new 2026 technical regulations were designed to create spectacular, efficient racing. In practice, they've produced something more complicated: cars that run out of electrical power at critical moments on track, creating large speed differentials on straights and unpredictable overtaking scenarios.

The most striking example came in China qualifying, when Charles Leclerc briefly lifted off the throttle mid-lap, accidentally fell outside an energy deployment threshold, and burned too much battery power in the wrong part of the circuit. The car effectively confused itself out of a fast lap. No driver should lose a qualifying effort to a software trigger buried in a regulation document, and the FIA has acknowledged as much.

Six days from Tuesday, the FIA Commission holds a binding vote on a package of fixes. The April 15 session covers sporting regulations, April 16 addresses technical specifics, and April 20 is the formal vote. An e-vote is expected to follow within days, with World Motor Sport Council ratification completing the process. The implementation deadline: Miami GP, May 1-3.

The Six Proposals

The first and most straightforward proposal is to increase the super clip harvesting limit from its current 250 kilowatts to 350 kilowatts — matching the ceiling available under lift-and-coast. By equalizing the two methods, super clipping becomes a more attractive strategy option for drivers and teams, reducing the frustrating lift-and-coast sections that look artificial and slow racing down.

A second proposal would open up "straight mode" usage more freely. This aerodynamic mode reduces drag in high-speed sections, extends the battery's effective range, and allows teams to pitch their wing levels with more strategic freedom. The effect on racing could be significant: cars would carry more speed into braking zones, theoretically improving overtaking opportunities.

More ambitious in scope is the possibility of increasing the internal combustion engine's contribution via a fuel flow limit increase. This would ease the battery headache by shifting more performance back toward the combustion side. The complication: the 2026 power units were designed around existing fuel flow limits, and meaningful changes here carry reliability risks. Most technical observers consider a fuel flow fix a 2027 conversation at the earliest.

Qualifying energy allocation adjustments represent the most counterintuitive proposal. The principle is that giving drivers less available energy in qualifying actually forces them to be more deliberate and concentrated — removing the artificial "lap management" element. The remaining proposals center on ADUO framework refinements and general regulation simplification to reduce the frequency of the "car confusing itself" scenario.

What Changes Most Depend On

The vote requires consensus, and not all teams are equally enthusiastic about every proposal. Teams that have already cracked the energy management puzzle to some degree — primarily Mercedes — have less incentive to support changes that narrow the performance gap. Teams struggling, like Red Bull, Ferrari in certain conditions, and others, want the fixes immediately.

The April 20 meeting includes team representatives, the FIA, Formula 1 management, and 2026 power unit manufacturer representatives. That last group matters: any changes to energy allocation or ICE contribution directly affects the competitive balance between PU suppliers. The conversation will be complex.

If the vote passes and the regulations are ratified in time for Miami, race teams will have approximately 10 days to simulate and understand what the new rules mean for their cars and strategies before the first practice session. That's a very short runway for something that could materially change competitive order.

What's Next

The April 15 sporting and April 16 technical sessions serve as the final prep before the binding vote on April 20. The e-vote results are expected within a few days of the meeting, followed by World Motor Sport Council ratification. If the timeline holds, the FIA could announce formal regulation changes by April 23-25 — leaving teams about one week of genuine preparation before Miami practice begins May 1. The outcome of this vote may be the single most consequential decision of the entire 2026 F1 season.
