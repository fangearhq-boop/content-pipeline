Verstappen Exit Clause: 24 Days Out, McLaren Talks Denied, Red Bull Can't Block It

By Elena Voss | July 2, 2026 | F1 Fanrecap

The McLaren rumors have been denied. The exit clause is very much real. And the countdown to the Hungarian Grand Prix on July 26 — the event that triggers Max Verstappen's ability to walk away from Red Bull Racing — has reached 24 days.

Reports emerged in late June suggesting "secret preliminary talks" had taken place between Verstappen's management and McLaren CEO Zak Brown. Manager Raymond Vermeulen issued a flat denial when contacted by German outlet Bild: "There's no truth in that. There have been no negotiations." Analysis from The-Race and Autosport has since examined whether the McLaren speculation represents genuine intelligence or a calculated pressure tactic designed to push Red Bull toward a compromise position on Verstappen's future.

The answer may not matter as much as the underlying mechanics. The exit clause exists regardless of who Verstappen talks to or doesn't talk to.

How the Clause Works

Verstappen's contract with Red Bull runs through 2028. Embedded within it is a performance exit clause with a specific trigger condition: if Verstappen is not within the top two of the championship standings at the summer break reference point — the Hungarian Grand Prix on July 26 — the clause becomes activatable. He then has until October to invoke it.

The current reality makes activation near-certain. Verstappen sits seventh in the championship, approximately 113 points behind leader Kimi Antonelli. With just 25 points available at each standard race weekend (plus 8 for Sprints), closing a 113-point deficit in 24 days across one Sprint weekend is mathematically impossible. The condition will not be met. The clause will activate.

F1 insiders told GPFans that the clause is now "impossible for Red Bull to block." Red Bull has reportedly made an eight-figure buyout offer — a financial bid to persuade Verstappen to remain committed despite the performance trigger. Verstappen's manager Vermeulen has previously stated his client "wasn't born to compete in the midfield."

What Options Remain?

Mercedes is effectively ruled out. George Russell has a confirmed multi-year deal beyond 2026, and the team reportedly has no contractual room to accommodate Verstappen. McLaren publicly deny talks. Ferrari has not been mentioned as a realistic option.

That leaves a narrowing list. Aston Martin, Red Bull's junior team Racing Bulls, or a surprise move to a midfield constructor would represent significant step-downs from a driver who has won four world championships and is under 30. Staying at Red Bull under a renegotiated deal remains possible if the car's performance improves dramatically — or if Verstappen's team decides to use the clause activation purely as leverage in new contract talks rather than as an actual departure notice.

Verstappen himself gave a telling glimpse of his mentality ahead of Silverstone. Asked about the British GP circuit in the 2026 era, he described his simulator experience: "I did a few laps on the simulator, I just started laughing. You barely have battery around the lap." It was a technical observation, but it speaks to a driver who is already thinking about everything the new regulations are doing wrong — and whether his current team has the answers.

The Cascade Effect

Verstappen's decision, when it comes, will be the most consequential individual move in the F1 driver market since his own arrival at Red Bull in 2016. Wherever he goes, the cascade effect ripples through the entire grid. Teams will need to find space for him. Other drivers will be displaced. 15 of 22 seats for 2027 are currently unconfirmed, partly because the market is waiting on what Verstappen does next.

The clock says 24 days. The clause says activatable. The destination remains the most compelling open question in Formula 1.

What's Next

The Hungarian Grand Prix on July 26 is the reference point. After that date, Verstappen has through October to formally invoke the clause. An announcement could come as early as late July or as late as the October deadline — but most analysts expect clarity before the summer break ends. The F1 world is watching.
