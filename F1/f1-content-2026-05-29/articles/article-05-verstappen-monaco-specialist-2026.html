Verstappen's Monaco Magic: First 2026 Podium Opens the Door in the Principality

By Elena Voss | F1 Fanrecap | May 29, 2026

It took five races, but Max Verstappen finally stood on a Formula 1 podium in 2026. His third-place finish in Montreal — described by Red Bull Racing team principal Laurent Mekies as "truly historic" for the RBPT power unit program — broke a streak that had been grinding on the four-time world champion since the season opened under the new regulations.

Now comes Monaco — and Verstappen has a complicated but compelling relationship with this circuit. He won there in 2021, in one of the most controversial and strategically-driven Monaco Grand Prix weekends in memory. He won there again in 2023, with the kind of dominant display that reminded the world why he is considered one of the greatest drivers of his generation. In a season where the Red Bull cannot simply overpower the field, Monaco may be where Verstappen's specific skill set gives the team something the raw data does not.

Why Monaco Is Different for Verstappen

The Circuit de Monaco is not won on power. It is not won by having the fastest car in a straight line or the most sophisticated energy deployment system. It is won by millimeter-perfect car placement, lap after lap, at speeds that are simultaneously slow by modern F1 standards and breathtakingly precise in their demands.

Verstappen's car-control skills — the ability to place a Formula 1 car exactly where he intends to, consistently, under pressure — are among the most celebrated in the sport. These skills do not diminish when the car is not the fastest on merit. In fact, they become more visible, because the gap between what the car theoretically should deliver and what Verstappen actually delivers can be significant at Monaco.

Red Bull's 2026 machine has been uncompetitive on pure pace metrics. Canada's podium was partly a function of circumstances — Russell's retirement, the competitive fluidity of a sprint weekend — as much as raw speed. But Monaco operates on different terms, and those different terms could suit Verstappen in a way that most other circuits on the calendar do not.

The Exit Clause Context

Verstappen's contract with Red Bull includes a performance-linked clause allowing him to depart if his championship position falls below a certain threshold at the summer break. With the Canada podium giving him momentum, the exit clause question has become slightly less acute — but it has not disappeared.

A Monaco win would make the conversation irrelevant for the foreseeable future. Strong points from the principality would change the narrative from "is Red Bull competitive?" to "Verstappen is still finding ways." Both outcomes are meaningful, and both depend on what happens June 7.

What's Next

The Monaco Grand Prix weekend opens Friday, June 5 with practice at 12:30 PM ET and 4:00 PM ET. Qualifying is Saturday, June 6 at 3:00 PM ET. The race starts Sunday, June 7 at 2:00 PM ET. All sessions air on Apple TV.

Ferrari and Charles Leclerc are the slight favorites. Kimi Antonelli's championship run will be tested by a circuit that has historically been unkind to the more powerful packages. Lewis Hamilton — fresh from his happiest Ferrari day in Canada — will be motivated by the one race he has never won.

And then there is Verstappen, arriving from Montreal with a podium in his pocket, two Monaco wins in his career, and the kind of circuit ahead that has rewarded his specific style of genius before. Whether 2026 Monaco becomes the third chapter of that story is the most intriguing subplot heading into next weekend.
