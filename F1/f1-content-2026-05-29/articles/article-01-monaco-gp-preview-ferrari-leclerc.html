Monaco GP Preview: Ferrari Favored as Leclerc Eyes Another Pole at Home

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap | May 29, 2026

After the drama of Montreal — Kimi Antonelli's fourth straight win, George Russell's heartbreaking retirement, Lewis Hamilton's emotional podium — Formula 1 now turns to its most iconic venue. The Formula 1 Louis Vuitton Grand Prix de Monaco takes place June 5-7 at the Circuit de Monaco, and the story heading into the principality is unmistakably one about Ferrari's opportunity.

The SF-26 has shown exceptional cornering speed in slow, technical conditions throughout the 2026 season, and Monaco is precisely that kind of circuit. Its 19 corners, narrow barriers, and complete absence of meaningful straight-line sections reward downforce, precision, and qualifying pace over engine power. Charles Leclerc — who grew up in Monaco, knows every meter of this track, and has taken multiple pole positions here — enters the weekend as one of the favorites to lead the field into the first corner on Sunday, June 7.

The Circuit and the Conditions

Circuit de Monaco presents unique challenges that exist nowhere else in Formula 1. Overtaking is extraordinarily difficult — positioning on the grid is often decisive, which makes Saturday's qualifying session at 3:00 PM ET on Apple TV one of the most consequential hours in motorsport. A single mistake on the tight streets of Monte Carlo ends the weekend on impact with the barriers.

Weather will play an early role. Friday's practice sessions — Practice 1 at 12:30 PM ET and Practice 2 at 4:00 PM ET — are expected to see light rain (high of 19°C), which could shuffle the setup picture heading into Saturday's dry conditions. Practice 3 runs Saturday morning at 11:30 AM ET, followed directly by qualifying at 3:00 PM. The race on Sunday begins at 2:00 PM ET with sunny skies and 27°C forecast — ideal racing conditions for a track where tire management often determines the result.

Why Ferrari Are Smiling

Kimi Antonelli has won the first four races of 2026 from four consecutive poles — an unprecedented run in modern Formula 1. He leads the championship by 43 points over Russell. But Monaco may represent the toughest test of his young season, for reasons that have nothing to do with racing instinct.

Neither Antonelli nor his Mercedes teammate Russell has ever won in Monaco. Mercedes, while dominant in pure pace this season, has historically found the principality's demands less complementary than their usual strengths. Ferrari, by contrast, has one of the most highly-motivated drivers in the field who is effectively racing at home.

Leclerc is third in the championship standings with 75 points, and his teammate Hamilton is 4th with 72. A one-two finish in Monaco — entirely plausible given how well the SF-26 should perform — would dramatically close the gap to Antonelli and make the championship picture far more complex than it currently appears.

What's Next

Practice begins Friday, June 5. Qualifying — where this Monaco weekend will likely be decided — is Saturday, June 6 at 3:00 PM ET on Apple TV. The race follows Sunday, June 7 at 2:00 PM ET.

If Antonelli takes pole and leads from the front, his fifth consecutive victory would make one of the most remarkable starts to an F1 season in history. If Ferrari's strengths manifest the way the data suggests they should, Leclerc and Hamilton could hand Mercedes their first defeat of 2026 at the sport's most prestigious address.

Monaco never does what you expect. But the starting point is clear: Ferrari arrive in Monte Carlo as the team to beat, and Leclerc arrives as the driver who knows these streets better than anyone else on the grid.
