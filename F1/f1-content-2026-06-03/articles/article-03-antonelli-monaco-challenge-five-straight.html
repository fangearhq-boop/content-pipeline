Kimi Antonelli Arrives at Monaco as Champion-in-Waiting — Can He Hold On When He's Not the Favorite?

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap | June 3, 2026

There is a particular kind of pressure that Kimi Antonelli has not faced yet in 2026.

For five races, he has arrived at each circuit as the man to beat — the fastest qualifier, the points leader, the driver his competitors are trying to stop. His Mercedes W17 has been the class of the field. His four victories from five races represent the strongest points-scoring start to an F1 season in a decade.

Monaco is different. Ferrari is the technical favorite. The circuit removes Mercedes' power unit advantage entirely. And Antonelli himself told Sky Sports F1 that Ferrari "will be the team to beat" this weekend.

At 19 years old, in his first championship fight, the 43-point leader must now learn how to protect a lead when he cannot simply go fastest.

How Remarkable This Run Has Been

Context matters when assessing what Antonelli has accomplished. Four wins from five races. Youngest championship leader in Formula 1 history. Converting his first four pole positions into four consecutive victories — a sequence Formula 1 had not seen before.

When he took pole in Shanghai, he became the youngest driver to secure Grand Prix pole position in the sport's history. When he won in Miami, he completed a triple of consecutive wins that drew comparisons to the sport's most dominant championship-opening runs. When he won in Canada — despite a dramatic intra-team battle with George Russell and contact on multiple occasions — he showed that his racecraft under pressure matches his raw pace.

His teammate Russell described the championship as "Antonelli's to lose." That is both a compliment and a warning: the expectation has shifted from can he win to can he avoid losing.

Why Monaco Is the Test

Monaco, more than any other circuit, rewards the driver who has the best car through slow corners. The active aero ban strips away Mercedes' electrical deployment advantage. Ferrari's SF-26 has shown consistently stronger traction and mechanical grip through the kind of low-speed sections that define Monte Carlo's layout.

Antonelli is well aware of this. He did not arrive in Monaco promising victory — he acknowledged the challenge openly. That kind of self-awareness and honesty is itself a sign of maturity that rivals rarely display, let alone 19-year-olds in their first title fight.

The question for Antonelli at Monaco is not whether he can win. It is whether he can finish in the top four, bank the points, and let the lead absorb whatever Ferrari may deliver at the front.

What's Next

If Ferrari wins Monaco and Antonelli finishes third or fourth, the lead shrinks but remains substantial. If Antonelli manages to find a way to the podium's top step — converting Monaco at a circuit where the car is theoretically against him — he becomes a genuine legend-in-the-making for this generation of F1 fans.

Practice 1 on Friday morning gives the first real read on the competitive order under 2026 Monaco conditions. Qualifying on Saturday will be the real test. On the streets of Monte Carlo, there is nowhere to hide — and no second chances if you brush the barriers.
