Monaco GP 2026 Weekend Guide: Schedule, Favorites, and Why This One Is Different

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap | June 3, 2026

The Monaco Grand Prix starts this Friday, and for 2026, it arrives with a twist that reshapes the entire weekend.

The Circuit de Monaco is Formula 1's most iconic venue — a 3.337-kilometer street circuit that winds through the Principality's harbor, tunnels, and hairpins with zero room for error. It rewards mechanical grip, low-speed traction, and qualifying precision above all else. This year, for the first time under the 2026 regulations, the active aero ban means every team competes without the variable wing systems that have defined the season so far — removing Mercedes' power-unit advantage and opening the door for Ferrari and McLaren to compete on genuinely equal terms.

The Full Schedule (All Times ET)

Friday, June 5: Free Practice 1 at 7:30 AM ET. Free Practice 2 at 11:00 AM ET. The opening day gives teams their first real data on how the SF-26, W17, and MCL39 handle Monaco streets under 2026 regulations.

Saturday, June 6: Free Practice 3 at 6:30 AM ET. Qualifying at 9:00 AM ET. Monaco qualifying is unlike any other session on the calendar — a single mistake on these barriers-lined streets can end your weekend before it begins. Pole position wins 27 of the last 30 Monaco Grands Prix.

Sunday, June 7: Race at 9:00 AM ET on Apple TV+ (US). The Monaco Grand Prix runs approximately 78 laps of the 3.337-kilometer circuit, totaling just over 260 kilometers.

The Championship Picture

Kimi Antonelli leads the Drivers' Championship with 131 points after winning four of the first five races. George Russell sits second with 88 points — 43 adrift after his Canadian Grand Prix retirement on Lap 30. Charles Leclerc is third with 75 points, Lewis Hamilton fourth with 72.

For the first time in 2026, Antonelli is not the favorite. Ferrari's SF-26 excels in slow-speed corners, and the FIA's active aero ban removes Mercedes' straight-line speed advantage entirely. Leclerc is the 2/1 bookmakers' favorite; Antonelli and Hamilton are both listed at 4/1.

What Makes This Monaco Different

The 2026 regulations introduced a 50/50 power split between the internal combustion engine and a dramatically more powerful electric motor. In most races, the "Rev1 Mode" — or Straight Mode — allows drivers to deploy extra electrical power when within a second of the car ahead on certain straights. No section of the Monaco circuit is long enough to meet the three-second activation threshold, so the system goes unused entirely this weekend.

That matters because Mercedes has built its W17 around exploiting that electrical deployment advantage. Strip it away, and the car's mechanical weaknesses in slow corners are exposed. Ferrari, which has consistently posted the strongest performance in low-speed sections all season, suddenly has a clear edge.

Drivers predicted this year's Monaco might also produce more overtaking. Antonelli and Leclerc both noted that the lighter, smaller 2026 cars could allow more position changes than recent Monaco editions — though the circuit's narrow layout still makes passing supremely difficult.

What's Next

Practice 1 on Friday morning will give the first real indication of where each team stands on Monaco setup. Ferrari's FP1 pace will be closely watched; if Leclerc and Hamilton are consistently quicker than Antonelli and Russell, expect the odds to shift further toward the Scuderia before qualifying Saturday morning.

After Monaco, the championship heads to Barcelona for the Spanish Grand Prix on June 12-14, and then Austria on June 26-28. But Monaco is the moment the title picture could change permanently — or stay exactly as it is, with Antonelli extending his lead heading into summer.
