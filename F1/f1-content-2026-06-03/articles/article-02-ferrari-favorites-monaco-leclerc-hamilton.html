Ferrari's Best Chance of 2026: Why Leclerc and Hamilton Can Finally Win at Monaco

By Elena Voss | F1 Fanrecap | June 3, 2026

Five races in. Five Mercedes wins. Zero Ferrari victories.

Monaco is where that changes.

Charles Leclerc arrives at his home circuit as the 2/1 bookmakers' favorite — not out of sentiment, but because of cold, hard technical reality. The Ferrari SF-26 has shown the strongest performance in slow-speed corners of any car in the 2026 field. Monaco is essentially 90% slow corners. The FIA's active aero ban removes the Mercedes power unit advantage that has fueled five consecutive victories. The circuit that has resisted Ferrari for years may finally be the circuit that opens the door.

Why the Active Aero Ban Changes Everything

The 2026 power units deliver over 1,000 horsepower, with approximately half coming from a dramatically enhanced electric motor. The Straight Mode — also called Rev1 — allows drivers to deploy extra electrical power on certain straights when within one second of the car ahead. In most races this season, that system has been a weapon for Mercedes.

At Monaco, it is irrelevant. No section of the street circuit is long enough to meet the three-second activation threshold required to trigger the system. Every team competes on equal electrical footing — which means the deciding factor shifts to mechanical grip, traction, and chassis balance through slow corners.

That is precisely where Ferrari is strongest in 2026. Multiple technical observers, including McLaren team principal Andrea Stella, have tipped Ferrari as the pole position favorite at Monaco. Stella does not make that assessment lightly — he is endorsing a rival.

Leclerc: The Monaco Native

Charles Leclerc grew up in Fontvieille, Monaco. The Circuit de Monaco is his home track in the most literal sense — he can see it from his apartment. He has three Monaco pole positions to his name, more than any other active driver. He won the 2024 Monaco Grand Prix.

And for years, none of that translated into wins. Mechanical failures. Safety car timing. Team strategy. For much of his early career, Monaco was where Leclerc suffered instead of celebrated.

That changed in 2024. Now, defending champion at his home race, he carries the same combination of burning motivation and technical advantage that produced that victory. At 2/1, the market is essentially saying he is the driver most likely to win. He tends to agree.

Hamilton: Chasing His First Ferrari Win

Lewis Hamilton made his Ferrari debut at the start of 2026 and has been building toward something special ever since. His second-place finish at the Canadian Grand Prix — fighting from the middle of the field, overtaking Max Verstappen in the closing laps — was the best result of his time in Maranello. He called it "the happiest day at Ferrari so far."

Monaco is where Hamilton believes Ferrari's opportunity becomes real. "Power is not king there," he said in the days before the weekend. "I think our car could be really strong." Hamilton has won three Monaco Grands Prix — in 2008, 2016, and 2019. He knows every centimeter of this circuit from memory.

The one thing missing from his Ferrari chapter so far is a win. If Monaco delivers it, the narrative of the season changes entirely.

What's Next

Ferrari's FP1 pace on Friday will answer the key question: is the SF-26 advantage at Monaco real or theoretical? If Leclerc and Hamilton set the fastest times in opening practice, expect that 2/1 market favorite position to shorten further before qualifying Saturday.

The race on Sunday at 9:00 AM ET on Apple TV+ could deliver what the tifosi and Hamilton fans have been waiting for — Ferrari's first win of 2026 in front of one of sport's most dramatic backdrops. After five races, the door is finally open. The only question is which Ferrari driver walks through it first.
