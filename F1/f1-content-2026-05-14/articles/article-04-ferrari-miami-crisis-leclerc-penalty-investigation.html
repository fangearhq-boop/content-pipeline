Ferrari's Miami Meltdown: Leclerc's 20-Second Penalty, Hamilton's Damage, and a Constructors Lead Nearly Halved

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap | May 14, 2026

Ferrari left Miami ten days ago with one word forming in the paddock: crisis.

Charles Leclerc — who had fought his way into a podium position through the chaos of the closing laps — lost control at Turn 3 on the final tour. He spun. He continued. He cut the circuit multiple times in the damaged car's fight to reach the chequered flag. FIA stewards reviewed the footage after the race and handed him a 20-second time penalty under Article B1.8.6 of the FIA F1 Regulations, for repeatedly leaving the circuit.

From a potential third or fourth-place finish, Leclerc was classified eighth.

Leclerc Takes the Blame — and Asks Questions

Leclerc did not deflect. "The last-lap mistake is all on me," he said after the race, acknowledging the spin and the penalties that followed were self-inflicted. But in the same breath, he demanded an internal Ferrari investigation into what happened to the car during the race — the sudden performance drop that left him scrambling in positions he should not have been defending.

That combination of accountability and scrutiny reflects a team under pressure. Leclerc is Ferrari's title hope in the drivers' championship — he sits third with 59 points — but the gap to Antonelli (100) and Russell (80) is already significant enough to force long-term thinking about what the next ten races need to deliver.

Hamilton Adds to the Pain

Lewis Hamilton's Miami was not the result Ferrari needed either. A lap-1 collision with Franco Colapinto caused floor and sidepod damage to the car, limiting Hamilton's ability to extract full performance from what should have been a competitive machine. He finished sixth — not catastrophic in isolation, but painful context for a team watching its constructors' lead compress race by race.

Hamilton had famously decided against using the simulator in the weeks between Miami and Canada, betting on developing his feel for the 2026 car through track time rather than virtual preparation. It remains to be seen how that approach translates at Gilles-Villeneuve.

The Shrinking Constructors Cushion

After the Japanese Grand Prix, Ferrari held a 44-point constructors' lead over Mercedes. After Miami, that lead has been cut to 16 points. McLaren sits behind both and is closing. The direction of travel is not favourable for Maranello, and the Canadian Grand Prix — where Mercedes is bringing a significant upgrade package — poses another risk to the remaining advantage.

Ferrari will need both drivers to deliver clean, damage-free weekends in Montreal if they are to stabilize rather than continue losing ground. The constructors' championship remains Ferrari's most realistic 2026 title hope. But that, too, is becoming less certain by the race.

What's Next

The Canadian Grand Prix runs May 22-24 at Circuit Gilles-Villeneuve. It is a sprint weekend — maximum points pressure, minimum room for errors. Ferrari needs Leclerc and Hamilton to both score strongly. Mercedes is bringing upgrades. McLaren is developing. The 16-point cushion that separates Ferrari from second in the constructors' standings is not safe enough to absorb another Miami-scale weekend. The Scuderia knows it.
