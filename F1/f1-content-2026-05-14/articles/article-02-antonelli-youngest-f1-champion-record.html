Three Starts. Three Wins. Three Poles. Kimi Antonelli Is Rewriting F1 History at 19.

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap | May 14, 2026

In the 75-year history of Formula 1, no driver has ever won their first three grand prix starts from pole position. Michael Schumacher didn't. Ayrton Senna didn't. Lewis Hamilton didn't. Neither did Sebastian Vettel, Max Verstappen, or any other champion who now occupies a permanent space in the sport's mythology.

Kimi Antonelli has. He is 19 years old.

What the Numbers Say

After four race weekends in 2026, Antonelli leads the Formula 1 World Championship with 100 points. His teammate George Russell is second on 80. Charles Leclerc is third at 59. The gap between Antonelli and the field is not just about raw points — it is about dominance. He has converted every pole to a win. He has led championships laps. He has handled pressure from experienced rivals in the closing stages of grands prix and not flinched.

For a driver making his first full F1 season, this is unprecedented. Teams spend years developing drivers through lower categories, junior programs, and reserve roles precisely because the jump to Formula 1 is supposed to be enormous. For Antonelli, the learning curve appears to run in the wrong direction — toward improvement, not adaptation.

The Youngest Champion Conversation

Sebastian Vettel became the youngest Formula 1 World Champion in history in 2010, winning at 23 years and 134 days old. That record has stood for 15 years. Antonelli is 19. If the championship were to end today — which it will not — he would already be well inside the record's boundary.

Sixteen rounds remain in the 2026 season, including the Canadian Grand Prix in eight days. Antonelli's 20-point lead over Russell is solid but not insurmountable. Championships have been overturned from larger deficits by more experienced drivers. But the manner in which Antonelli is producing — the consistency, the pole positions, the race management — suggests this is not a hot start waiting to cool down.

The Team Dynamic

What makes Antonelli's run particularly striking is that his teammate is George Russell — an experienced, fast, title-calibre driver who spent years developing within the Mercedes junior program and arrived in 2026 as many people's favourite for the championship. Russell is not a weak point in the comparison. The scale of Antonelli's advantage over him is part of what makes the story compelling.

Mercedes, to their credit, appear to be managing the internal dynamic with care. Toto Wolff continues to publicly support both drivers while privately allowing the results to speak. What those results are saying, right now, is that their 19-year-old might be the best driver in the world.

What's Next

The Canadian Grand Prix runs May 22-24 at Circuit Gilles-Villeneuve in Montreal. It is a sprint weekend — the first sprint race ever at this circuit — adding extra points and extra pressure. Antonelli will enter as championship leader and as the driver all the others are measuring themselves against. Sebastian Vettel's record as the youngest F1 World Champion sat untouched for 15 years. At the rate Kimi Antonelli is going, it may not survive the 2026 season.
