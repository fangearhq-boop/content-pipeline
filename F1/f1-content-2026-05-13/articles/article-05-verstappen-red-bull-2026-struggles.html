From Four-Time Champion to P7: The Brutal Reality of Verstappen's 2026 Season

By Marcus Cole

May 13, 2026

In a paddock full of compelling storylines in 2026, none is more jarring than this: Max Verstappen, four-time Formula One World Champion, sits seventh in the standings with 26 points after four races. Leader Kimi Antonelli has 100.

The 2026 season was always going to be a test. Major regulation changes reset the grid and gave every team an equal opportunity to build the best car. Red Bull, the team that dominated the sport with Verstappen from 2021 through much of 2025, simply has not gotten to grips with the new era.

The Numbers Are Damning

Verstappen's point total of 26 through four races represents less than a quarter of Antonelli's. The gap of 74 points is almost two full race wins worth of advantage that Antonelli holds. To put it in context: Mercedes is treating their championship battle as one between Antonelli and Russell, their two drivers — the rest of the grid appears to be competing for third.

Red Bull's issues stem from the fundamental aerodynamic and powertrain changes that define the 2026 regulations. The new framework rewards different design philosophies than the one that made the RB19 and RB20 invincible. Where Red Bull once extracted legendary downforce efficiency from their diffuser packaging, the new rules have changed what the most effective design looks like — and Red Bull's car has not adapted as well as Mercedes or McLaren.

Verstappen Has Done What He Can

To his credit, Verstappen has not capitalized on any team errors. He has finished races, scored what points were available, and pushed the car to its limits. The talent that won four championships is unchanged — but talent requires a competitive machine, and in 2026, he does not yet have one.

The question now is whether Red Bull can find a development breakthrough that re-introduces them to the competitive picture in time to salvage the season. Canada's Circuit Gilles-Villeneuve features a different character to the circuits where Mercedes has most excelled. A course that can punish handling weaknesses through slow chicanes could expose different car strengths — or the same weaknesses, depending on what Red Bull brings to Montreal.

What's Next

The Canadian Grand Prix on May 22-24 is Verstappen's next opportunity to find an answer. Montreal has always been a circuit that produces surprises, and a strong qualifying result from Verstappen would at least suggest Red Bull is finding its footing. But with Antonelli seemingly operating in a different league right now, the conversation in Canada may focus less on who can beat him and more on who can get closest. For Verstappen, that would be a start.
