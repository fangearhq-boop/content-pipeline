Youngest Ever, 3-for-3: Why Kimi Antonelli's F1 Start Is Already Historic

By Marcus Cole

May 13, 2026

When Kimi Antonelli was announced as Lewis Hamilton's replacement at Mercedes, the reaction was mixed. The 19-year-old Italian was talented, clearly, but stepping directly into the most iconic seat in Formula 1? In the first year of a major regulation change? It seemed, to many, like a risk.

Through four rounds of the 2026 Formula One World Championship, the only thing Antonelli has done is make every skeptic look wrong. He leads the drivers' championship with 100 points — the youngest driver in F1 history to ever hold the championship lead — after winning three of four grands prix, each from pole position.

A Record No One Had Ever Set Before

The numbers tell a story the results alone don't fully capture. Antonelli's hat trick of victories — in China, Japan, and Miami — came from his first three pole positions. No driver in the history of Formula One had ever won their first three grands prix from pole. Not Senna. Not Schumacher. Not Hamilton. Not Verstappen.

At Suzuka, after his second consecutive win, he became the youngest driver in the sport's history to lead the championship standings. He was 19 years and several months old when that milestone fell. After Miami, his championship lead stretched to 20 points over teammate George Russell, with Charles Leclerc third on 59 points and Lando Norris fourth on 51.

Context: The New Era

What makes Antonelli's dominance even more striking is the context. The 2026 season features dramatically revised regulations that have reshuffled the competitive order entirely. Mercedes, apparently, have nailed the new era — but Antonelli's adaptation to the new machinery is a story unto itself. He arrived in Formula 1 without so much as a full testing season under his belt at this level, yet his racecraft, tire management, and composure under pressure have been indistinguishable from a veteran.

Mercedes has given him exceptional equipment, but there is no machine in Formula One that drives itself to three consecutive pole-to-victory conversions. Antonelli is doing things that are genuinely difficult at the highest level of the sport.

What's Next

Antonelli heads to the Canadian Grand Prix on May 22-24 at Circuit Gilles-Villeneuve in Montreal, where he will try to extend his winning run to four races. He has said the title race is "still early days" and that he knows "the road is still long." Given what he has accomplished in four races, those are either the words of extraordinary humility or a driver who simply knows he has more to give. Possibly both.
