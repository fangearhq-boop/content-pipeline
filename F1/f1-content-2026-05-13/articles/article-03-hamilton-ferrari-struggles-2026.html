The Ferrari Reality Check: Hamilton Trails Leclerc and Vows a New Approach for Canada

By Elena Voss

May 13, 2026

When Lewis Hamilton joined Ferrari, the narrative wrote itself: the greatest driver of his generation, chasing one last championship with the most storied name in Formula One. Four races into the 2026 season, the narrative has become a little more complicated.

Hamilton sits fifth in the championship with 51 points, eight behind his own teammate Charles Leclerc and 49 behind leader Kimi Antonelli. After the Miami Grand Prix — where Ferrari brought new upgrades and still couldn't crack the front row of pace — Hamilton was candid in his assessment of where things stand.

"I'm going to have a different approach in the next race because the way we're preparing at the moment is not helping," he told media after the race.

The Leclerc Comparison

Leclerc, for his part, has been strong by Ferrari's standards. The Monégasque driver is third in the championship with 59 points, scoring in every race and giving Ferrari their most consistent scoring platform. But his Miami weekend ended with a 20-second post-race time penalty after cutting a corner following a spin, which dropped him two positions from his classified result.

Even with the penalty, Leclerc is outscoring Hamilton. The gap between them — while not enormous — is meaningful for a driver who arrived at Ferrari with championship ambitions and a seven-time world title pedigree. Leclerc has studied Hamilton's methods closely and spoken admiringly of them, but on track in 2026, it has been the younger driver setting the benchmark.

Ferrari's Upgrade Problem

The deeper issue may be the car. Ferrari brought what were described as meaningful upgrades to Miami and came away with limited return on that investment. When upgrades don't work as expected, it creates a compounding problem: more development resources spent, less data clarity on what actually works, and drivers who are asked to find pace that isn't quite there yet.

Hamilton's comment about preparation hints at something beyond just car setup. Whether it relates to simulator work, how the team feeds data to him, or some other aspect of the Mercedes-to-Ferrari transition that is still incomplete, the message is clear: something needs to change before Canada.

What's Next

Hamilton and the Ferrari team have ten days before the Canadian Grand Prix on May 22-24 at Circuit Gilles-Villeneuve. It is a circuit that has produced some of the sport's most dramatic races, and one where driver feel and bold strategy can compensate for a pace deficit. If Hamilton means what he says about a different approach, Canada will be the first test of whatever that looks like. Ferrari, and their fans worldwide, will be watching closely.
