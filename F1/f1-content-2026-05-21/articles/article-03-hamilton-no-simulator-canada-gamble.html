Hamilton Ditches Ferrari Simulator for Canada: 'It Took Me in the Wrong Direction'

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap | May 21, 2026

In the age of Formula 1, where computational analysis, simulator hours, and data correlation determine race preparation as much as any driver instinct, Lewis Hamilton has made a decision that runs directly against the current.

For the Canadian Grand Prix, Hamilton will not use Ferrari's simulator. Not a reduced program. Not a single outlap to validate a set-up change. Nothing. The decision came after his Miami preparation — extensive simulator work, careful correlation, the standard process — produced set-ups that simply did not work once he arrived at the track. "It took me in the wrong direction," Hamilton said.

The Evidence Behind the Decision

This is not impulsive. The data Hamilton used to arrive at his decision is his own 2026 race results. At the Chinese Grand Prix — the one race in the first four rounds where he did not use the simulator as part of his preparation — he finished third. His best performance of the year by a significant margin.

At the other races, where simulator work was part of the routine, Hamilton finished further back. The relationship between simulator preparation and track performance has been negative for him this season, not because the simulator is wrong in principle but because Ferrari's simulator currently doesn't translate accurately to what the car does on track. The correlation between virtual and real is broken.

That is a significant admission, not just for Hamilton personally but for Ferrari as a team. Simulator correlation is one of the fundamental engineering challenges in modern Formula 1. When a driver of Hamilton's caliber identifies that the process is actively hurting him rather than helping, it points to a technical problem that will take more than one race weekend to solve.

What the No-Simulator Approach Means for Canada

Hamilton will still be at the factory for meetings, still involved in engineering discussions, still present in the intellectual process of preparing the car. What he is removing is the physical practice work on the simulator — the laps that he says are misleading his instincts rather than sharpening them.

The Circuit Gilles Villeneuve is one of the few circuits in the modern calendar where feel, braking sensitivity, and car balance judgment from the driver carry enormous weight. The circuit's combination of long straights, tight chicanes, and the iconic Wall of Champions section demands a driver who trusts what he is experiencing. If Hamilton arrives in Montreal with instinct rather than simulator-corrupted muscle memory, the track characteristics could play in his favor.

His best championship result — China — came in exactly those circumstances. He is betting it can happen again.

The Bigger Ferrari Picture

Ferrari heads into Canada as a team that has shown genuine pace in parts of races without consistently converting it to podiums. Hamilton's simulator decision, and the reasoning behind it, frames an uncomfortable truth: the tools they are using to develop and prepare the car may be working against themselves.

Charles Leclerc has been closer to the pace in recent rounds, suggesting the car itself is not the problem. Whether it is the simulator's correlation, the set-up philosophy, or something else will determine whether Canada is a breakthrough weekend for the Scuderia or another installment of what-might-have-been.

What's Next

Hamilton's no-simulator gamble begins on Friday when he gets in the car for Sprint Qualifying. If he finds pace immediately — if Montreal feels right in a way Miami didn't — it will validate the instinct he is trusting. If not, the simulator question will be just one of many Ferrari has to answer.

At 41 years old, in his first Ferrari season, Lewis Hamilton is still learning new ways to be fast. The fact that he's willing to challenge the standard process rather than simply accept it is exactly the quality that made him seven-time champion. Sprint Qualifying: Friday May 22, 9:30 PM ET, Circuit Gilles Villeneuve, Montreal.
