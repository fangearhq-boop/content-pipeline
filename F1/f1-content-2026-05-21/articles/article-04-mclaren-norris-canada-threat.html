McLaren's Canada Threat Is Real: How Norris's Miami Sprint Win Changes the 2026 Picture

By Elena Voss | F1 Fanrecap | May 21, 2026

When Lando Norris crossed the finish line in Miami's Sprint race ahead of Oscar Piastri, he became the first driver in 2026 to win a Formula 1 event in a non-Mercedes car. That fact landed quietly at the time — it was a Sprint race, not a Grand Prix, and the full race went to Kimi Antonelli anyway. But the performance that produced that Sprint win was anything but quiet.

McLaren brought a significant upgrade package to Miami. The car went from competitive-but-not-quite to genuinely threatening across a single race weekend. Norris won Sprint qualifying by pipping Antonelli to pole. He then controlled the Sprint from lights to flag, building a four-second gap over Piastri and describing the upgrades afterward as genuinely transformative. "It feels like everyone's saying the same thing," he said, "but ours have really helped this weekend."

Now McLaren arrives in Montreal with more new parts on top of Miami's developments. The trajectory points in one direction.

Why Canada Could Be Different

The Circuit Gilles Villeneuve has historically been a mixed venue for McLaren. In 2025, Norris and Piastri spent the latter part of the race fighting each other for fourth place until contact between them ended both their days with meaningful points on the table. The team left Montreal with a complicated relationship to the circuit and a lesson about internal race management.

But 2026 is a different car, a different aerodynamic regulation set, and a different pace trajectory. The 2026 machines have changed how circuits rank in terms of team competitiveness, and McLaren's recent development pace — the Miami Sprint being the clearest proof point — suggests the Woking team has unlocked something real in their car concept.

The sprint format also plays to McLaren's strengths. Sprint weekends reward pace and tire management over long-run strategy, and Norris is one of the best in the field at extracting maximum performance across a short burst of laps. If he qualifies well for the Sprint on Friday evening, his ability to execute a 19-lap race with the precision he showed in Miami will put him in position to challenge wherever it matters.

What a Wet Race Would Mean

Rain is forecast for Sunday's main race in Montreal. For McLaren, this is potentially excellent news. Wet conditions neutralize some of the performance delta that a dominant car creates in dry conditions — they demand feel from the driver rather than from the car's aerodynamic efficiency alone. Norris in wet conditions is one of the most compelling combinations in the sport. His sensitivity through low-grip corners and ability to find the limit on a slippery surface are well-documented.

A wet Canadian Grand Prix where McLaren brings its best upgraded car of the season could be precisely the scenario that ends Mercedes' winning streak. That is not a certainty — Mercedes are still fast, Antonelli is still exceptional, and nothing about a sprint format removes risk from the equation. But the ingredients for a different Sunday afternoon outcome are present in a way they haven't been earlier in 2026.

What's Next

Watch Norris's Sprint Qualifying lap on Friday. That will set the tone for McLaren's entire weekend. If he's within a tenth of Mercedes' pace or better, Canada becomes a three-team fight for the first time this season. If he's further back, the upgrades may not have translated to this circuit's specific demands.

Either way, McLaren's momentum is the most significant development in the midfield of the 2026 championship since the season began.
