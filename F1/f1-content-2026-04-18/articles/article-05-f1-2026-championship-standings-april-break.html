2026 F1 Championship at the April Break: Antonelli's Historic Lead, Ferrari's 45-Point Chase, and Red Bull's Recovery Plan

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap | April 18, 2026

Three races into the season, two cancellations, and a five-week break. Here's exactly where every title battle stands before Miami changes everything.

The 2026 Formula 1 season has produced three race weekends, two Grand Prix cancellations — both the Bahrain and Saudi Arabia events called off due to the ongoing Middle East conflict — and one of the most compelling championship leader stories in the sport's modern era. After a five-week break, the paddock reassembles in Miami on May 1-3 with the competitive order established but far from settled.

Here is where every battle stands at the midpoint of the April break.

Drivers' Championship

Kimi Antonelli leads the 2026 World Drivers' Championship with 72 points. At 19 years and 216 days old, he is the youngest championship leader in Formula 1 history. He is also Italy's first championship leader since the legendary Alberto Ascari in 1953 — a historical weight that Antonelli has carried with remarkable composure for someone so young.

Antonelli's teammate George Russell sits second with 63 points, nine back. The Mercedes pair has been the dominant force through the season's early rounds, combining to score 135 of the Silver Arrows' total constructor points — a figure that tells its own story about the W16's superiority in the current regulatory environment.

Charles Leclerc is third with 49 points and Lewis Hamilton fourth with 41 — both Ferrari drivers in touching distance of the podium positions but unable to mount a genuine championship challenge against the Mercedes pace that has defined the opening rounds.

Further back, Lando Norris (25 points) and Oscar Piastri (21 points) represent McLaren's solid if unspectacular season start. Red Bull's total of 16 points across both drivers speaks to the challenge Max Verstappen and his teammate have faced with a car that is estimated to be approximately 0.8 seconds off the chassis pace of the leaders, compounded by a further 0.2 seconds gap in Ford power unit performance.

Constructors' Championship

Mercedes leads the constructors' championship with 135 points. Ferrari sit second at 90 — a 45-point deficit that the Maranello team is working urgently to close with a major upgrade package planned for Miami. McLaren's 56 points puts them third, ahead of a Red Bull team managing just 16 constructor points after an extremely difficult start to the new regulations.

The constructors' gap between Mercedes and Ferrari is the most consequential figure in these standings. Ferrari believe their SF-26 has the underlying potential to challenge the W16 — the revised Macarena rear wing, being tested at Monza on April 22, is their most visible expression of that belief. A strong Miami would close the gap meaningfully; another race where the Ferrari drivers finish behind both Mercedes entries would see it widen further.

Red Bull's Miami Hope

For Red Bull and their Ford power unit partner, the Miami Grand Prix represents a potential turning point. The team has been anticipating eligibility for ADUO (Automatic DRS / Undercut Override) system updates that could recover a significant portion of their current performance deficit. If Monday's FIA vote on energy management changes also produces favorable outcomes, Red Bull's competitiveness could shift substantially from what was seen in the first three rounds.

Verstappen himself has been characteristically pragmatic about the situation — channeling his energies into the Nürburgring this weekend rather than dwelling on a car gap he cannot address from the cockpit.

What's Next

Miami on May 1-3 is the next opportunity for anyone to change these standings. Ferrari's Monza test on April 22 provides a preview of what the Scuderia intends to bring. The FIA vote on April 20 could alter the competitive framework before the race begins. And Kimi Antonelli — already carrying historical significance at 19 years old — arrives as the championship leader, with nothing about his position guaranteed once qualifying begins in Florida.

Three races is a small sample in an 18-round season. The five-week April break has provided teams with exactly the development time they needed. Miami will tell us whether the championship is a two-team fight or whether Ferrari and Red Bull are ready to complicate matters.
