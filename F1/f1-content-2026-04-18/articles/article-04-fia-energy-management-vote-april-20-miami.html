The Vote That Could Reshape F1: FIA's April 20 Energy Management Decision Explained

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap | April 18, 2026

On Monday, the FIA holds its highest-level meeting on 2026's most controversial regulations. Here's what's on the table, why it matters, and what changes at Miami if the vote passes.

The 2026 Formula 1 season's most important regulatory debate reaches its conclusion on Monday, April 20. The FIA will convene a high-level meeting attended by team principals, F1 management, and power unit manufacturers to vote on a series of proposed changes to the regulations governing energy management — the set of rules that has produced some of the season's most controversial on-track behavior.

This is the culmination of a process that began with technical meetings on April 15 and 16, where proposals were refined and preliminary consensus was sought among the technical community. Monday's meeting represents the decision point — after which an electronic vote will follow, with results expected within days.

What's on the Table

Three main proposals are being considered:

Super Clipping Adjustment: The most straightforward change on the table. Currently, drivers who use "super clipping" — a technique where they partially lift the throttle while maintaining ERS harvesting, essentially a halfway point between full throttle and full lift-and-coast — are limited to 250 kW of deployment power. Conventional lift-and-coast allows 350 kW. The proposal is to equalize super clipping to 350 kW, removing the current penalty for choosing the technique and reducing teams' incentive to force drivers into more extreme (and sometimes dangerous) throttle management patterns.

Qualifying Energy Target Reduction: The FIA trialed reducing the qualifying energy recovery limit from the standard ~8-9 MJ to 6 MJ at the Japanese Grand Prix in Suzuka. The smaller target narrows the performance gap between teams with efficient energy management systems and those still developing their approach, leading to more even qualifying sessions. The proposal is to formalize this reduction for the remainder of the season.

ADUO Refinements: Adjustments to the Automatic DRS / Undercut Override system, which several teams have found ways to exploit for competitive advantage beyond its intended purpose. These changes close specific loopholes without fundamentally altering how ADUO functions for the teams operating within its intended parameters.

Why It Matters

The urgency for change was strengthened by a crash at the Japanese Grand Prix involving Oliver Bearman, where an incident at high speed was connected to the unpredictable throttle behavior that energy management tactics can create. The FIA's statement after those technical meetings described "constructive dialogue" with teams — a diplomatic signal that consensus was achievable.

If Monday's vote produces agreement, an e-vote of the relevant stakeholders follows within days. Ratification by the FIA World Motor Sport Council converts the agreed changes into official regulation amendments. The target is to have all changes in effect by the Miami Grand Prix on May 1.

What's Next

The competitive implications of these changes extend well beyond Miami. Teams that built their 2026 cars around specific energy management strategies — particularly in qualifying — may find their competitive advantage reduced or eliminated. Teams that struggled under the current regulations may benefit disproportionately. Red Bull, for instance, is expected to become eligible for ADUO updates at Miami that could significantly close their performance deficit to the front-runners.

Monday's meeting happens on a quiet weekend when most of the paddock's attention is on Verstappen's Nürburgring adventure. But the decisions made in that room could define the second half of the 2026 championship season. Watch for the e-vote results in the days that follow.
