Verstappen Returns to the Green Hell: Nürburgring 24h Qualifiers Begin Amid Red Flags and Grid Penalty Threat

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap | April 18, 2026

After his NLS2 disqualification over a tyre rule breach, Verstappen is back on the Nordschleife this weekend for a four-hour night race — and already facing trouble before the start.

Max Verstappen is spending his Formula 1 break the way most people don't: strapped into a GT3 car at one of motorsport's most punishing circuits. The four-time F1 world champion is racing at the Nürburgring this weekend as part of the ADAC Nürburgring 24 Hours Qualifiers, piloting the #3 Red Bull-liveried Mercedes-AMG GT3 Evo alongside co-driver Lucas Auer for Verstappen Racing.

The two four-hour races this weekend — NLS4 on Saturday evening and NLS5 on Sunday — serve as official qualifying events for the 24 Hours of Nürburgring, scheduled for May 16-17. For drivers and teams competing in the endurance classic, these rounds provide critical data on car setup, tyre strategy, and crew performance ahead of the main event.

For Verstappen, they also provide an opportunity to right a wrong. His team was disqualified from the NLS2 race earlier this season after officials discovered they had used seven tyre sets during the race — one more than the maximum six permitted under Nürburgring Langstrecken-Serie regulations. BMW inherited that victory, leaving Verstappen with nothing to show for a competitive performance.

Qualifying Drama Before the Race Begins

Saturday's qualifying session for NLS4 was anything but straightforward. A crash involving an Aston Martin Vantage AMR GT3 Evo forced a red flag, disrupting the grid-setting session. The driver was unhurt but guardrail repairs were required before competitive running could resume.

When the dust settled, Verstappen had not secured pole position — an Arrow-entered Porsche 911 GT3 R and another Mercedes-AMG GT3 from driver Stolz were faster in the session. Adding further complication, an incident involving co-driver Auer is under investigation by officials, with a potential grid penalty still possible before the race begins.

The field for this weekend's qualifiers is immense: 132 cars have entered, with 37 competing in the GT3-specific SP9 class where Verstappen races. The sheer volume of machinery on the full 25.378-kilometer Nordschleife circuit means traffic management and situational awareness are as important as outright pace.

Why Verstappen Does This

The four-time champion has been asked many times why he chooses to race in endurance events during his F1 breaks — particularly at a circuit with the Nürburgring's reputation for unpredictability and danger. His answer is consistently simple. "I'm not afraid. I like it," he told media ahead of this weekend.

The appeal is real: the Nordschleife offers a racing challenge entirely different from the clinical precision of modern Formula 1. No safety cars reset field intervals the way electronics do. No DRS zones provide artificial overtaking opportunities. The circuit demands genuine skill, adaptability, and car control across nearly 75 corners in a single lap.

NLS4 tonight begins at 17:30 CEST, runs for four hours, and extends into the darkness — part of what makes Nordschleife endurance racing a singular motorsport experience.

What's Next

Sunday brings Top Qualifying to determine grid positions for the NLS5 race, which begins at 12:30 CEST. Both results feed into the overall qualification picture for the 24 Hours of Nürburgring in May. Verstappen has expressed his commitment to that main event, making this weekend's races particularly important for his team's preparation and their standing among the 132-car field.

Back in the F1 paddock, the world waits for Miami and the resumption of a 2026 championship fight that has Kimi Antonelli leading at just 19 years old. But this weekend, Verstappen's only concerns are the Nordschleife's weather, the tyre allocation rules, and getting to the finish line cleanly — something that has proven harder than expected in 2026.
