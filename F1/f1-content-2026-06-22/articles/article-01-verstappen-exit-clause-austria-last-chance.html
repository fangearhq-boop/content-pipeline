Verstappen Exit Clause Confirmed — Austria Is the Last Chance Saloon

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap | June 22, 2026

Max Verstappen's manager has publicly confirmed what the paddock has whispered about for weeks: the four-time world champion's Red Bull contract contains an exit clause that could allow him to leave the team as early as August.

Raymond Vermeulen's confirmation, reported by GPFans, removes any remaining ambiguity about Verstappen's contractual situation. The clause activates if Verstappen is not in the top two of the drivers' championship at the summer break. The window to exercise it — should he choose to — runs from August to October.

The math is brutal: Verstappen is currently seventh in the championship. The Austrian Grand Prix this weekend is the first of just four races remaining before the summer shutdown.

The Numbers Are Working Against Red Bull

Through seven races of the 2026 season, Verstappen sits seventh with zero race victories — the longest winless stretch of his Formula 1 career at this point in a season. The new ground-effect regulations and ADUO engine rules have fundamentally changed the competitive hierarchy, and Red Bull, despite two significant upgrade packages in Miami and Austria, have not yet returned to race-winning form.

Team principal Laurent Mekies has been consistent in his messaging: the car is improving but is not yet a victory contender. That honesty is admirable, but it creates a stark picture for Verstappen, who thrives on championship competition and race wins above everything else.

Even a race victory in Austria this weekend — his home circuit, where he's won four times — would not mathematically transform his championship standing enough to guarantee a top-two position by the summer break. The gap to Kimi Antonelli (first) and Lewis Hamilton (second) is simply too large.

Red Bull's Internal Rift

Rather than waiting for Verstappen to activate the clause, Red Bull is reportedly attempting to buy it out — removing the exit option entirely in exchange for financial compensation or other considerations. But the organization is not aligned on this approach.

Thai stakeholder Chalerm Yoovidhya reportedly favors completing a buyout deal. However, Mark Mateschitz and Oliver Mintzlaff — the other key figures in Red Bull's ownership structure — are not in favor. One F1 insider, quoted by GPFans, has described it as "impossible" for Red Bull to block the clause from being activated.

That internal disagreement may prove decisive. If the buyout effort collapses before the summer break, Verstappen enters August with a fully live exit option in hand.

Where Would He Go?

The destination question is equally compelling. Mercedes — operating the most dominant car on the 2026 grid — has emerged as the frontrunner in speculation. The Silver Arrows would not be simply acquiring a top driver; they would be adding a four-time world champion to a car that already leads the constructors' championship with 262 points. McLaren has also been strongly linked. Ferrari is considered less likely.

The irony is significant: Verstappen could potentially end up racing for the very teams he's been chasing all season long. Whether that prospect motivates him or frustrates him heading into Austria, only he can say.

What's Next

Austria is not just a race for Red Bull this weekend — it's an audition. The home crowd will be behind Verstappen, the upgrade package will give the RB22 its best chance yet at the front, and the psychological stakes could not be higher. A podium or win changes the conversation. Another midfield result may make the exit clause decision inevitable.

By the time the Austrian GP weekend concludes Sunday June 28, the trajectory of one of F1's greatest careers will be considerably clearer.
