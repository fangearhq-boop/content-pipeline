Cadillac's F1 Debut: Four Rounds In, Zero Points, and a Genuine Development Battle Ahead

By Elena Voss | F1 Fanrecap | May 11, 2026

When Cadillac entered Formula 1 as the grid's 11th team for the 2026 season — the sport's first genuinely new entrant since Haas in 2016 — the expectations were calibrated carefully. No one expected immediate victories. The goal was survival, consistency, and development. Four rounds in, the team is hitting the survival target. The performance is lagging behind the ambition.

After four Grands Prix and one Sprint race, Cadillac has no championship points. Their best individual result is a 13th-place finish by Valtteri Bottas, accumulated earlier in the season. In Miami, both cars completed the race — Sergio Perez 16th, Bottas 18th — marking their third consecutive double Grand Prix finish. Reliability has been a genuine positive. Speed has not arrived yet.

Two Veterans, One Steep Mountain

Cadillac's driver lineup represents an unusual combination of experience and expectation management. Valtteri Bottas, 10 Formula 1 race wins across his career, arrived with the ability to extract information from a car systematically and communicate its limits. Sergio Perez, six race victories with Red Bull, brings aggressive racecraft and championship-pressure experience.

Neither man is accustomed to the back of the grid. Perez, in particular, was competing for race wins as recently as 2024. The adjustment has required a recalibration of what success looks like. In 2026, a points finish — the top ten — represents a meaningful achievement. Perez has stated publicly that targeting points by the summer break is the goal, a target that would require the team to jump several cars in race-day performance.

Their power unit — a customer Ferrari package — provides the same specification engine as the Scuderia and Haas, giving Cadillac a reliable technical foundation. The chassis development work, and the understanding of the 2026 aerodynamic regulations, is where the team must close the gap.

The Development Race

Cadillac has been explicit that they are "in a massive hurry to find performance" — a phrase that acknowledges the competitive nature of development in modern Formula 1. Teams do not stand still, and the gap between Cadillac and even the next-slowest cars is not static. Aston Martin, occupying the final position in the constructors' standings, is developing aggressively. The development war between Cadillac and Aston Martin in the back half of the 2026 season will determine which team ends the year with the least ground to make up in 2027.

In Miami, Cadillac deployed a new update package. Perez finished three laps down but reached the checkered flag — information gathered, lessons logged. The team's race-day reliability across four rounds has been one of the more impressive aspects of their debut. Building a team capable of developing a competitive car while also executing race weekends cleanly is not a trivial engineering challenge for a first-year operation.

What's Next

The Canadian Grand Prix on May 22-24 represents Cadillac's next opportunity to score their first championship points. Circuit Gilles-Villeneuve's layout — stop-start, heavy braking, lower-downforce emphasis — may suit a car that is building mechanical strength rather than peak aerodynamic performance. Perez's experience at circuits requiring physical car management gives Cadillac a small but real advantage in circuit-specific setup.

The target is clear, the timeline is specific, and the people are in place. Formula 1 debut seasons are measured in growth, not glory. By that standard, four rounds in, Cadillac is where a new team in the sport's most technically demanding environment should be: finding out what they have, and making it better.
