It's the Chassis, Not the Engine: How Red Bull Is Failing Verstappen in 2026

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap | May 11, 2026

When Red Bull Racing entered 2026 with their new Ford-powered engine — the product of years of investment, recruitment, and ambition through Red Bull Powertrains — the assumption was that the power unit would be the variable to watch. Teams building their own engines for the first time in modern F1 carry enormous uncertainty. The engine could make or break the year.

Four rounds in, the verdict is clear: the engine is not the problem. The RB22 chassis is. Industry analysts and team insiders have identified the car's underlying platform as the team's "primary weakness," one that has left Max Verstappen — a four-time world champion — marooned in seventh place with 26 points, 74 behind championship leader Kimi Antonelli.

The man who built the dynasty that won four consecutive constructors' championships is gone. Christian Horner, who served as Red Bull's team principal for over two decades, was sacked following the 2025 British Grand Prix. New boss Oliver Mintzlaff inherited a car that is struggling to adapt to the 2026 regulations in a way that the team had not anticipated.

Verstappen's Frustration Made Public

Verstappen has rarely been effusive in his criticism of technical situations in the past. In 2026, the frustration has broken through. He has called the new regulations "terrible" and "fundamentally flawed," and suggested that anyone who enjoys watching Formula 1 under the new formula "doesn't understand what racing is about."

Sources have told ESPN that Verstappen is currently leaning toward a sabbatical rather than full retirement — a signal that the driver has not given up on a return to competitive racing, but needs distance from the 2026 experience. The emphasis on "sabbatical" suggests the door to his return is open, under the right circumstances.

Those right circumstances have a specific definition, and a deadline. Verstappen's contract with Red Bull contains a performance exit clause: if he is not in the top two of the Drivers' Championship at the summer break — when the season pauses after the Hungarian Grand Prix on July 24-26 — he can trigger a departure process and inform Red Bull of his wish to leave within three months.

The mathematics are almost impossibly stacked against remaining. To climb from seventh (26 points) to second (currently Russell at 80 points) would require Antonelli, Russell, Leclerc, Norris, Hamilton, and Piastri to all collapse simultaneously. Red Bull did halve their aero weight deficit at Miami — progress, but far short of what is needed.

What Comes Next If the Clause Triggers

The ripple effects of Verstappen activating his exit clause would be felt across the entire paddock. According to industry sources cited by multiple F1 outlets, a potential Mercedes-Verstappen contract would start at $80-90 million annually — the largest in the sport's history by a significant margin. Toto Wolff has publicly pursued Verstappen in each of the past two seasons.

But Mercedes already has Antonelli — a driver who is currently winning every race he enters. And George Russell, who is second in the championship. The logistics of a Verstappen-Mercedes deal involve questions that don't yet have public answers.

For Red Bull, the priority now is salvage: stop the bleeding, find performance in the RB22, and give Verstappen any reason to remain invested. Oliver Mintzlaff has credited Horner for building the engine project's foundation. The chassis is the next hill to climb.

What's Next

The Canadian Grand Prix arrives on May 22-24 as the next opportunity for Red Bull to demonstrate improvement. The Circuit Gilles-Villeneuve layout — stop-start, heavy braking, low-downforce characteristics — has not historically favored Red Bull in recent seasons. Verstappen needs points, and needs them at a circuit that may not suit his current package.

The summer break exit clause window is now the defining narrative of Red Bull's 2026 season. The decision Verstappen makes in the weeks after the Hungarian Grand Prix will have consequences far beyond one team or one contract. It will reshape Formula 1.
