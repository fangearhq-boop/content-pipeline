Miami GP Sprint Weekend: Your Complete Watching Guide for May 1-3

By Ryan Calloway | April 27, 2026

The longest gap between Formula 1 rounds in modern memory is over. Five weeks after Kimi Antonelli took the chequered flag in Japan, the 2026 world championship resumes at the Miami International Autodrome this weekend — and the sport returning to action couldn't look more dramatic.

Every major team has used the extended break to overhaul their car. The championship leader is a 19-year-old in only his fourth race. The four-time world champion is ninth and hinting at retirement. And new rules are in effect for the first time.

Here's everything you need to know.

The Schedule

Miami is a Sprint weekend, which means the format differs from a standard race event. Friday, May 1, features an extended Practice session (FP1) running 90 minutes — 30 minutes longer than normal — to give teams additional time to calibrate their new upgrade packages on track. The FIA approved the extension specifically because of the 5-week gap and the magnitude of development changes teams are bringing.

Saturday, May 2, brings Sprint Qualifying and the Sprint Race itself. Sunday, May 3, is the main event: Qualifying in the afternoon followed by the Grand Prix at 4 PM ET.

The Championship Picture

Kimi Antonelli enters Miami as the championship leader on 72 points, 9 ahead of Mercedes teammate George Russell (63). The Italian teenager has been one of the stories of the season — winning in China and Japan, becoming the youngest driver in F1 history to lead the world championship. He broke Lewis Hamilton's record, set at the 2007 Spanish Grand Prix.

Hamilton himself is fourth in the standings on 41 points for Ferrari, behind Leclerc's 49. McLaren's Norris is fifth on 25 and Piastri sixth on 21. Max Verstappen, the reigning four-time champion, is ninth with just 12 points as Red Bull continues to struggle with the RB22.

The Rule Changes

Miami marks the first race under updated FIA energy management regulations. The super-clip deployment cap increases from 250kW to 350kW, allowing more aggressive power deployment at certain points of the lap. The recharge allowance decreases from 8MJ to 7MJ per lap, changing how teams must manage their hybrid systems through each circuit lap. These changes were introduced in response to concerns from teams and drivers about the original parameters.

What's Next

The big question heading into Miami is simple: can anyone challenge Mercedes? Ferrari, McLaren and Red Bull all believe their upgrades can close the gap — and all three have invested heavily in making that belief a reality. Friday's FP1 session will provide the first competitive data. By Sunday evening, we'll know whether Vasseur's "new championship" prediction was prescient — or whether Mercedes is simply that good. Either answer makes for compelling television.
