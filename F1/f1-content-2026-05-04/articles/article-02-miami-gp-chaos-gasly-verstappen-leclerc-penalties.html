Gasly Upside Down, Verstappen Spinning, Leclerc Penalized: Miami Was F1 at Its Most Chaotic

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap | May 4, 2026

MIAMI — Beyond Kimi Antonelli's historic victory, the 2026 Miami Grand Prix will be remembered for the kind of chaos that only Formula 1 can produce.

A car was flipped upside down. The race leader spun out on the first lap. Multiple drivers received penalties both during and after the race. And the whole thing started three hours earlier than planned because thunderstorms were threatening the Florida sky. Sunday at Miami International Autodrome was, even by the sport's standards, a lot.

The most alarming moment came when Pierre Gasly's Alpine was launched into the air and flipped inverted after contact with Liam Lawson's Racing Bulls machine at Turn 17. Gasly had moved to the outside in an attempt to overtake when Lawson suffered a sudden gearbox failure, rendering him unable to brake for the corner. The impact sent the French driver's car skyward and tumbling into the barrier. Both Gasly and Lawson emerged uninjured. The stewards investigated the incident but did not penalize Lawson — his mechanical failure was deemed beyond his control.

The Turn 1 Chaos and Its Consequences

The mayhem began before the end of the first lap. Max Verstappen, starting second on the grid, and Charles Leclerc, starting third, made contact heading into Turn 1 on the opening lap. Verstappen locked up and spun, dropping to the back of the field and pitting under the Safety Car that was deployed shortly after. His early stop cost him significant ground, and the race never fully recovered for the Red Bull driver.

After the race, Verstappen was handed a five-second penalty by the stewards for crossing the white line separating the pit lane exit from the track during his Safety Car stop. Despite the penalty, he retained fifth place — because Leclerc's fate was worse. The Ferrari driver was issued a 20-second post-race penalty for repeatedly leaving the track at various points during the race without justifiable reason, dropping him from sixth to eighth in the final standings.

The weather also played a role. Race organizers moved the start from the original 3 p.m. local time to 6 p.m. due to a significant thunderstorm threat over the Miami area. The sky stayed clear for the race itself — but the threat of storms had added an extra layer of uncertainty to an already tense weekend.

Amid the Chaos, One Driver Stayed Calm

While the field around him descended into pandemonium, Kimi Antonelli managed the race with the composure of a driver twice his age. The 19-year-old Mercedes driver, starting from his third consecutive pole position, navigated the Safety Car restart cleanly and built a gap to Norris that he controlled through the closing laps. Three wins from three poles — and a championship lead of 20 points. In Miami, that achievement is all the more impressive for what was happening around him.

What's Next

Formula 1 now enters a three-week break before the Canadian Grand Prix at Circuit Gilles Villeneuve on May 22-24. The FIA will review several incidents from the Miami weekend in the coming days. For Gasly, the immediate focus will be on recovery from what was a frightening accident — though he was fortunate to escape without injury from a crash that, at speed, could have been far more serious. The Wall of Champions in Montreal awaits. In F1, drama is never far away.
