Verstappen DNF After Leading: Nürburgring 24 Hours Ends in Heartbreak

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap | May 19, 2026

Max Verstappen made his Nürburgring 24 Hours debut this weekend with one goal: to win the most grueling race on the endurance calendar. For the better part of the event, everything was going to plan. Then, with roughly four hours remaining, a driveshaft failure ended it all.

The Mercedes-AMG GT3 No. 80 crew — Maro Engel, Luca Stolz, Fabian Schiller, and Maxime Martin — claimed overall victory, giving Mercedes-AMG its first win at the Nürburgring in 10 years. For Verstappen and his team, the result was a painful DNF after they had led comfortably with a gap of more than 30 seconds over the chasing pack.

Commanding, Then Gone

The Verstappen team had controlled large portions of the race and built a lead that should have been more than sufficient to absorb typical endurance-racing variables — safety car periods, strategy gaps, minor damage. Thirty seconds in hand at a circuit as unpredictable as the Nordschleife represented a dominant position.

The driveshaft failure did not care about any of that. A mechanical issue at the worst possible moment ended Verstappen's debut run before he could taste a victory his team had earned. The retirement came with just under four hours remaining in the 24-hour event.

Mercedes-AMG Takes the Victory

The winning No. 80 car carried Maro Engel, Luca Stolz, Fabian Schiller, and Maxime Martin across the finish line after the race's final hours saw the attrition of other frontrunners. It was a first Nürburgring 24 Hours victory for the Mercedes-AMG team in a decade, underlining the brand's current dominance across motorsport disciplines — from Formula 1 with Antonelli's championship lead, to the Nordschleife in endurance racing.

For the four drivers who shared the winning car, the victory was earned through a combination of pace, reliability, and race management — everything the sport demands of endurance racers. Their effort deserves full recognition even if the headlines have naturally focused on the dramatic ending to Verstappen's challenge.

Context: A Difficult 2026 for Verstappen

The Nürburgring heartbreak arrives at a challenging time for Verstappen in his F1 career. The Red Bull RB22 has been a difficult car to extract pace from in 2026, and Verstappen currently sits seventh in the drivers' championship with limited points from the opening four races. His exit clause — which would allow him to leave Red Bull if he is not in the top two of the championship at the time of the Hungarian Grand Prix in late July — remains in play.

Verstappen's decision to race at the Nürburgring alongside his F1 commitments reflected both his love of motorsport in all forms and his desire to keep competing at the highest level. The result was not the one he wanted, but the performance — leading a 24-hour race by more than 30 seconds — demonstrated that his racecraft extends well beyond the F1 paddock.

What's Next

Verstappen and Red Bull head to Montreal this week for the Canadian Grand Prix, a sprint weekend running May 22-24 at Circuit Gilles-Villeneuve. The championship situation at Red Bull remains complicated, and this week's race — combined with the sprint format that offers additional points — gives Verstappen an opportunity to begin clawing back toward the top two positions he needs to retain by the Hungarian Grand Prix in July. The Nürburgring DNF is in the past. Canada is now the focus.
