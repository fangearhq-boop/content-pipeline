Can Kimi Antonelli Win Four in a Row? The Record Books Have Never Seen This

By Elena Voss | F1 Fanrecap | May 19, 2026

The numbers surrounding Kimi Antonelli's 2026 Formula 1 season are becoming difficult to contextualize because the sport has no prior reference point for them. Three races entered. Three pole positions. Three race victories. Four races total — including a Miami sprint win — and the championship lead by 20 points.

He has never started an F1 race from anywhere other than the front row. He has never finished a race in worse than fourth. He is 19 years old and from Bologna, Italy, and he is doing things to the record books that nobody expected from someone at the very beginning of his career.

Canada is next. The question is whether any of this stops in Montreal — or whether the streak extends to something even more extraordinary.

The Record No One Held Before

Antonelli's achievement of winning his first three F1 starts from pole position is unprecedented. The sport has seen dominant world champions — Michael Schumacher, Sebastian Vettel, Lewis Hamilton, Max Verstappen — who accumulated consecutive victories at the peak of their careers. But starting a career with a 3-from-3 pole-to-victory record? That had never been done.

The reasons why it is so rare are straightforward. New drivers typically need time to learn circuits, acclimatize to the machinery, and build the race management skills that convert qualifying pace into sustained race pace over a full Grand Prix distance. The transition from junior formulas — even dominant ones — does not come with a guarantee that the talent scales immediately to the demands of Formula 1.

Antonelli has made the transition look effortless. Whether it is effortless is almost certainly not the case — but his ability to conceal the difficulty is itself a remarkable attribute.

What Canada Tests

Circuit Gilles-Villeneuve will present Antonelli with a different challenge than the previous rounds. The stop-start nature of the circuit, with heavy braking zones into slow corners and then maximum-acceleration exits onto long straights, requires specific car behavior that differs from the power-circuit demands that favored Mercedes in the early season.

The Wall of Champions at the final chicane has caught out champions and frontrunners across decades — a concrete barrier waiting at the exit of the complex that ends races without warning. Maintaining composure under lap-after-lap of pressure at a circuit with no margin for error is a different psychological test than the flowing high-speed layouts where Antonelli has won so far.

And this week adds the sprint format complexity — two separate qualifying sessions, a sprint race Saturday afternoon, and the main race Sunday — meaning Antonelli must manage not just tire preparation but also the mental energy of six days of compressed intensity at a circuit thousands of fans attend specifically to watch their home race.

The Championship Context

Beyond the streak itself, Antonelli's 100-point lead in the championship (with Russell second on 80) gives him a working cushion heading into Canada. He does not need to win in Montreal — but the momentum advantage of winning four consecutive races would send a message to every rival that the 2026 season has an overwhelmingly dominant force at its front.

Russell's circuit knowledge and historical results make him the most credible threat on paper. McLaren's improving form means Norris and Piastri cannot be dismissed. Ferrari's Leclerc will push hard at a circuit that has historically produced strong results for the Scuderia. Antonelli's focus must be on controlling the race from the front — the same approach that delivered his first three wins.

What's Next

Sprint qualifying begins Friday, May 22 at Circuit Gilles-Villeneuve in Montreal. The sprint race is Saturday at 5:00 PM ET. The Canadian Grand Prix main event starts Sunday at approximately 2:00 PM ET. Four races in, the 2026 season has produced one driver with an unprecedented record. Canada will show whether the streak continues — or whether Russell, Norris, Leclerc, or someone else finally finds the answer to Antonelli's dominant start.
