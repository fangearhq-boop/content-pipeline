FIA's Mid-Season MGU-K Fix Debuts in Miami — F1's Most Controversial Rules Get Their First Patch

By Ryan Calloway | May 1, 2026 | F1 Fanrecap

The 2026 Formula 1 season opened with a dramatic new technical formula — and an almost equally dramatic series of complaints from drivers about what those regulations produced. Starting this weekend in Miami, the FIA is responding with the first significant mid-season tweak to the new power unit rules.

New MGU-K deployment limits take effect from the Miami Grand Prix, in what represents the first formal acknowledgment from the FIA that the opening races under the new regulations produced outcomes that required adjustment.

What's Changing and Why

The core of the change centers on when and how much electrical energy the MGU-K — the motor-generator unit on the kinetic side of the power unit — can deploy during a lap.

Under the previous rules, the unit could deploy at its full 350 kilowatts at any point on the circuit. The revised rules maintain 350kW deployment in what the FIA calls "key acceleration zones" — the straights and areas where overtaking is most likely to occur — but limit output to 250kW elsewhere on the lap. The aim is to reduce the harvesting behavior that has caused sudden power variations mid-corner, a phenomenon that drivers dubbed the "superclip."

Max Verstappen was the most vocal critic of the regulations, calling the situation "fundamentally wrong" at multiple points during the first three race weekends. The Red Bull driver said after the Japan GP that the FIA's tweaks were insufficient, and indicated he hopes for "bigger changes" for 2027.

All Stakeholders Agreed

The FIA confirmed that all teams and stakeholders agreed to the new rules. A more radical proposal — to cut the maximum MGU-K output from 350kW all the way down to 200kW — was rejected during the stakeholder consultation process. The outcome is a compromise that aims to preserve the spectacle of the 350kW acceleration while smoothing the erratic energy management behavior that created the problematic driving characteristics.

Whether this will prove sufficient is an open question. Mercedes, who have dominated the early championship with their W16, have appeared most comfortable with the 2026 regulations. McLaren and Ferrari have been more competitive than Red Bull and Aston Martin, though both trailing teams have cited the power unit dynamics as a key factor in their early-season struggles.

Miami as the Laboratory

The Miami International Autodrome is in some ways a fitting circuit for this experiment. It features multiple long straights where 350kW deployment will remain available, and a technically demanding mid-section where the 250kW limit will apply. If the new rules work as intended, the racing in those mid-sector sections should feel more planted and consistent for drivers — and more predictable for viewers.

Teams have had limited time to model the impact of the changes. The sprint format this weekend compresses the data-gathering window, meaning Friday's single 90-minute practice session will be critical for teams to understand how the new energy management profile affects their car's behavior and race strategy.

What's Next

The proof will be in the racing. Sprint Qualifying today and the Sprint on Saturday will provide the first competitive data on how the new rules play out in practice. Drivers' feedback from those sessions will shape the conversation heading into Sunday's Grand Prix. If Verstappen and other critics are still complaining after Miami, pressure for the larger 2027 changes will intensify. If the racing improves, the FIA's measured approach will have been vindicated.
