Antonelli Arrives in Miami Leading the World Championship at 19 — F1's New Star Chases Hat-Trick

By Marcus Cole | May 1, 2026 | F1 Fanrecap

A teenager is leading Formula 1's World Championship. And he's just getting started.

Kimi Antonelli, 19 years old and in his second Formula 1 season with Mercedes, arrives at the 2026 Miami Grand Prix weekend as the Drivers' Championship leader with 72 points — nine clear of team-mate George Russell, 23 ahead of Ferrari's Charles Leclerc, and 31 ahead of Lewis Hamilton. At his age, Antonelli holds the distinction of being the youngest driver ever to lead the Formula 1 Drivers' Championship.

Two Wins and a Points Lead

The season has unfolded in a fascinating way. Russell took the opening round in Australia, but Antonelli answered with back-to-back victories in China and Japan. Those two wins, combined with consistent points finishes across the first three rounds, have given the Italian teenager a championship advantage that is small in raw numbers but enormous in narrative terms.

"I feel more in control," Antonelli said ahead of this weekend's Miami sprint weekend, showing a composure that has become his hallmark. He acknowledges a "fundamental" issue with his race starts that he's working to address — a rare vulnerability in what has otherwise been a near-flawless championship campaign.

Mercedes Dominance and the Challenge Ahead

Behind Antonelli's individual success sits a broader story of Mercedes supremacy. The Silver Arrows lead the Constructors' Championship with 135 points — 45 ahead of Ferrari (90 points) and 79 ahead of McLaren (56 points). Red Bull, struggling with their 2026 machinery, trail in fourth with just 16 points.

Miami represents an opportunity for Antonelli to extend that advantage. McLaren has been fast at this circuit in recent seasons, and Ferrari's Russell will push hard to close the gap. But Antonelli has demonstrated in China and Japan that when the pressure arrives, he knows exactly what to do with it.

The Magnitude of the Moment

Context is useful here. When Ayrton Senna — the driver to whom Antonelli has inevitably been compared — was 19, he was two years away from his F1 debut. When Michael Schumacher was 19, he was still racing karts and junior formulae. When Max Verstappen was 19, he had recently become the youngest race winner in F1 history, but the championship lead was still years away.

Toto Wolff, Mercedes' team principal, has been careful not to let the Senna comparisons build unchecked, noting that they "will not help him win the F1 title." The warning is well-intentioned: the fastest way to derail a young champion is to load them with expectation before they've earned it.

Antonelli seems to understand. He's racing the car, not the headlines.

What's Next

The Miami Grand Prix is a sprint weekend, meaning today includes both Free Practice 1 (90 minutes) and Sprint Qualifying. Saturday features the Sprint and full Qualifying, with the Grand Prix itself on Sunday, May 3 at 4PM ET. A third straight victory would give Antonelli a commanding 33-point lead and effectively announce himself as the championship favorite entering the European swing. The teenager from Bologna is at the wheel of the fastest car in Formula 1 — and he knows it.
