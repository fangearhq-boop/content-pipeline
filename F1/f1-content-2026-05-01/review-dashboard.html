Thunderstorm Alert — 88% Chance of Rain Could Disrupt Miami Race
NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post The 2026 Miami Grand Prix could be facing a dramatic disruption on race day — and not the kind that comes from wheel-to-wheel combat. According to AccuWeather's latest forecast, Sunday's race faces an 88% chance of rain and a 53% probability of thunderstorms. That combination is more than just weather talk — it carries genuine legal implications. Florida law mandates that major outdoor public events must be halted when lightning approaches within eight miles. There is no discretion in that rule: if lightning is close, the race stops. The FIA has acknowledged the threat and confirmed a contingency plan is in place, promising to minimize disruption to the on-track programme. But minimizing disruption and eliminating it are two very different things when tropical thunderstorms are in the forecast. The good news is the rest of the weekend looks glorious: Friday and Saturday are expected to be hot and dry, with temperatures reaching 33-34°C and track temperatures potentially exceeding 56°C during FP1. Teams will get their running in before the storm arrives. The Miami Grand Prix sprint weekend schedule: FP1 (90 minutes) today, Sprint Qualifying today, Sprint and Qualifying on Saturday, Grand Prix on Sunday. Three sessions and a sprint race before the clouds roll in. ### Image Post Caption 88% chance of rain. 53% chance of thunderstorms. FIA's New MGU-K Rules Debut in Miami — F1's Most Controversial Regs Get a Fix
NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post The most debated aspect of the 2026 Formula 1 season gets its first official fix this weekend in Miami. After months of driver criticism — led most loudly by Max Verstappen, who called the new regulations fundamentally broken and said there was "something wrong" at every race weekend — the FIA has implemented new MGU-K deployment rules that take effect from the Miami Grand Prix. The changes work like this: the electric motor can still deploy at its full 350 kilowatts in key acceleration zones — the straights and main overtaking opportunities — but is limited to 250 kilowatts on other parts of the circuit. The goal is to reduce the uneven harvesting behavior, particularly the controversial "superclip" effect that has created sudden power variations mid-corner. A more aggressive proposal — to cut from 350kW down to 200kW — was rejected by stakeholders. What emerged is a compromise that aims to preserve the spectacular acceleration while smoothing out the moments that made cars feel unpredictable. Verstappen has signaled that he views these tweaks as insufficient, saying he hopes for "bigger changes" for 2027. For now, the Miami Sprint weekend is the first live test of whether the revised rules improve the racing product. The FIA says all teams and stakeholders agreed to the changes. ### Image Post Caption F1's most controversial regulations get their first mid-season fix in Miami. New MGU-K rules: full 350kW on straights, limited to 250kW elsewhere. Verstappen wants more. The racing will decide. ⚡ #F1 #MiamiGP

Honda Brings "Countermeasures" to Miami for Aston Martin
NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post Honda is in Miami with hardware, and for the Aston Martin team, that represents at least a glimmer of hope after one of the most challenging starts to a season the team has ever experienced. After the Japan Grand Prix, Honda engineers inspected the Aston Martin chassis at their Sakura headquarters in Japan, specifically targeting the severe vibration issues that have plagued Fernando Alonso and Lance Stroll throughout the opening races of the 2026 season. The problem became so serious that team principal Adrian Newey warned in Melbourne that drivers risked "permanent nerve damage" if they completed too many consecutive laps with the current vibration levels. Honda has brought what it calls "countermeasures" to Miami — hardware changes designed to reduce the vibrations and improve reliability. However, the Japanese manufacturer is managing expectations carefully. A Honda spokesperson cautioned that while some progress has been made, the changes "will not have a visible impact on power unit performance" on track. In other words: Alonso and Stroll may have a more comfortable ride. But they won't suddenly be fighting for podiums. This is a foundational step in Aston Martin's recovery, not a breakthrough. The important thing is that Honda is responding, the communication channels between manufacturer and constructor are open, and the two parties have a clear diagnosis of the problem. Solutions take time. ### Image Post Caption Honda brings hardware countermeasures to Miami after Aston Martin's nightmare start to 2026. Newey warned of "permanent nerve damage." This weekend is a small but meaningful step forward. #F1 #MiamiGP #AstonMartin

### Long-Form Post Mercedes has a problem most teams dream of: two drivers genuinely capable of winning the World Championship, separated by nine points. George Russell arrived in Miami having won the season opener in Australia. Kimi Antonelli has won the two races since. Now they head into a sprint weekend in Florida with both men in the conversation for the 2026 title — and the team's strategy department about to face some very difficult decisions. Russell, for his part, is playing it cool. Asked about Antonelli's nine-point lead, he said it is "not even a consideration" for him right now. Antonelli, for his part, has identified a key weakness he's working to correct: a "fundamental" issue with his race starts that has cost him positions at the beginning of several races.
