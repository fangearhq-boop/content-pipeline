Bahrain and Saudi GPs Cancelled: Why F1's 2026 Calendar Just Got Shorter and What It Means

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap

April 8, 2026

The 2026 Formula 1 season was scheduled to be 24 races long. It will now be 22. The Bahrain and Saudi Arabian Grand Prix events have been cancelled due to the ongoing conflict in the Middle East, creating the five-week gap between the Japanese GP and the Miami GP that fans have been wondering about — and reshaping the mathematical landscape of the championship fight.

Why the Cancellations Happened

Both Bahrain and Saudi Arabia had been on the F1 calendar for several seasons, with the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia hosting the Jeddah Corniche Circuit race and Bahrain's Sakhir circuit serving as the traditional season opener in many years. The ongoing regional conflict made staging both events untenable from a safety and logistical standpoint, and Formula 1's commercial rights holders confirmed the cancellations.

The result is an unusual gap in the early part of the season. Three races were held — Japan, and the two preceding rounds — before the entire paddock went home for five weeks with no events on the calendar until Miami on May 1.

What Teams Are Doing With the Break

Teams are making productive use of the enforced downtime. Ferrari is preparing what multiple sources describe as a sizeable aerodynamic upgrade package for Miami — the first race with their new ADUO power unit's development arc underway. Mercedes is running simulator sessions to debrief the opening three rounds. Red Bull, under pressure from Verstappen's public criticism of the RB22, is working through what changes are possible within the 2026 technical regulations.

For engineers, a five-week break mid-season provides a rarer opportunity than it might seem. Factory development cycles continue, but the absence of race weekend obligations allows full focus on wind tunnel work, CFD analysis, and the kind of deep strategic review that is difficult to complete between back-to-back race weekends.

The Championship Mathematics

Two fewer races means every point carries more weight. Antonelli's nine-point lead over Russell — equivalent to roughly finishing one position ahead across a race weekend — is more meaningful in a 22-race season than it would have been in a 24-race one. The championship is also compressed into a tighter window, with the final rounds carrying greater mathematical significance.

For Verstappen and Red Bull, the shortened calendar may actually create more urgency. The exit clause window (August-October) falls in the middle of a shorter season, meaning less time to accumulate the points needed to climb back into the top two. Every race from Miami forward matters more than it would have in the original 24-race structure.

What's Next

Miami returns Formula 1 to action on May 1, and all of the developments from the April break — rule changes, upgrades, contract negotiations — arrive at the circuit simultaneously. The 22-race season's second quarter begins with a Sprint weekend, meaning double the points-scoring pressure in a single weekend.

The shortened calendar is an adjustment, not a disaster. But it changes the calculus of every team and every driver for the rest of 2026.
