Bearman Cleared for Miami — But 10 of 12 Penalty Points Means One Wrong Move Could End His Season

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap

April 8, 2026

Oliver Bearman has been medically cleared to race in Miami. The 50G crash at Suzuka left him with nothing worse than a bruised knee, and by any physical measure, he walked away from one of the most violent impacts in recent Formula 1 memory in remarkable shape. What he did not escape was the penalty points — and those, unlike a bruised knee, will not heal before Miami.

The 50G Crash That Changed the Season

The sequence at Suzuka's Spoon Curve has become the defining safety moment of the 2026 season. Bearman, during an ERS deployment phase, caught another car at a closing speed differential of approximately 50 km/h — a product of the violent power spikes inherent in the current 2026 power unit regulations. The impact registered at 50G. The FIA estimated the crash forces were among the highest seen in a contemporary Formula 1 car.

Bearman's physical recovery is, by all accounts, complete. His medical team has confirmed he is fit to compete. The crash was the exhibit that pushed the FIA to schedule its April 9 emergency summit, and the proposed rule changes — raising super clipping from 250kW to 350kW, reducing energy recovery — are a direct response to the conditions that produced that impact.

The Penalty Point Problem

At Suzuka, Bearman accumulated points that brought his total to 10 of 12 on the FIA penalty points tally. Under the current system, 12 points triggers an automatic one-race ban. He is two points from that threshold.

In a normal calendar configuration, some of those points would expire before Miami — penalty points have a twelve-month rolling window, and intermediate races provide opportunities for older points to age off. But with Bahrain and Saudi Arabia both cancelled, there is no race between Japan and Miami. Bearman arrives at the Miami International Autodrome with 10 points and no relief valve.

The Haas team has confirmed he will race in Miami. The sporting pressure on him will be considerable. Any incident that attracts steward attention — a collision, an unsafe release, a track limit violation with consequences — could push him to 11 or 12 points. A race ban in the middle of his first full season at Haas would be a significant setback.

What's Next

Bearman's position going into Miami is straightforward in theory and genuinely precarious in practice: finish the race weekend without adding penalty points, and he survives with his racing license intact. The Sprint format adds an additional session with points implications, doubling the number of competitive moments where incidents can occur.

He is 20 years old, racing in his first full Formula 1 season, for a midfield team, on a circuit where passing is difficult and contact is almost inevitable. The pressure to stay clean while racing at the limit is one of the more challenging situations any young driver could face.

That he is physically capable of doing it is confirmed. Whether he can manage the on-track complexity that comes with it is the question Miami will answer.
