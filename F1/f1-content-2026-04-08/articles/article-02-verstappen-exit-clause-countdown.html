Verstappen's Exit Clause Countdown: The Clock on F1's Biggest Story

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap

April 8, 2026

Four months. That is roughly how long Formula 1 has before the window opens on the most consequential contract clause in the sport's recent history.

Max Verstappen's Red Bull contract contains an exit clause he can activate between August and October 2026 — but only if he finds himself outside the top two in the drivers' championship at that point. After three races in an RB22 he has publicly described as "terrible," "Mario Kart," and "a joke," he is already far from that threshold. And the gap isn't closing.

The Public Criticism

Verstappen's frustration is not a private matter. He has made his feelings about the 2026 formula clear in multiple public forums, calling the car fundamentally uncompetitive and the racing experience one he does not enjoy. He finished eighth at the Japanese Grand Prix — a result that would have been unthinkable for the dominant champion of 2021 through 2024.

Sky Sports commentator David Croft put it plainly: "The retirement threat is not empty." Multiple credible sources have reported that a sabbatical in 2027 is under active discussion, though the distinction between sabbatical and outright retirement from the sport has not been confirmed. What has been confirmed is that Verstappen is at what he described as "the beginning of the period" where he is deciding his future.

The Contractual Mathematics

The exit clause is not a guarantee of departure — it is a conditional option. To be triggered, Verstappen needs to be outside the top two in championship standings during the August-October window. Right now, he is already far behind Mercedes' Andrea Kimi Antonelli (72 points) and George Russell (63 points). If that gap persists into summer, the clause becomes live.

The FIA's proposed rule changes, if voted through on April 20, could theoretically improve the RB22's competitiveness. But catching Mercedes in a single development cycle is unlikely. Red Bull would need significant performance gains across multiple race weekends while simultaneously navigating a driver situation that is consuming considerable management bandwidth.

Miami begins the stretch that matters. Every result from May onward feeds directly into the championship picture that will determine whether that August-October window becomes a live question or a footnote.

What's Next

The FIA's April 20 vote is the nearest pivot point. If rule changes improve the 2026 cars materially, Verstappen's calculus may shift. If the RB22 remains where it is — uncompetitive, frustrating, and far from the front — then the summer conversation about that exit clause becomes unavoidable.

Formula 1 has rarely faced a situation quite like this: its most dominant champion of the modern era publicly unhappy, contractually positioned to leave, and facing a rival team's machinery that may be a generation ahead. The sport's biggest story is still in its early chapters.
