FIA Summit Today: Six Proposed Fixes to F1's Broken 2026 Rules

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap

April 8, 2026

The FIA is convening its emergency summit on April 9 to address the mounting concerns around the 2026 Formula 1 regulations — a set of rules that have been called dangerous, unbalanced, and fundamentally broken within the first three races of the season. Today's session is a presentation and discussion meeting; the binding vote, requiring unanimous agreement from all teams, follows on April 20.

The goal: get changes in place before the Miami Grand Prix on May 1-3.

What's on the Table

The centerpiece proposal is raising the super clipping threshold from 250 kilowatts to 350 kilowatts. Super clipping is the mechanism that limits how much power can be deployed through the rear wheels on straights, and the current 250kW cap has created violent power spikes that make the cars unpredictable at high speed. Raising it to 350kW would smooth out power delivery and reduce dangerous closing speed differentials between cars.

The second major proposal is reducing the energy recovery cap per lap from 9 megajoules to 6 megajoules — or possibly as low as 6MJ depending on the source. This addresses the same underlying problem from a different direction: by limiting how much energy can be harvested and deployed, the FIA aims to reduce the sudden, dramatic speed changes that contributed to Oliver Bearman's 50G crash at Suzuka's Spoon Curve.

That crash — which left Bearman with a bruised knee and the paddock with a serious safety question — has become the defining moment driving urgency at the summit. A closing speed differential of approximately 50 km/h during an ERS deployment phase is not a driver error. It is a systemic problem with the rules themselves, and the FIA has acknowledged as much.

The Timeline

Today's meeting is a presentation, not a vote. Teams will hear the formal case for each proposed change, ask questions, and voice objections. The critical session is April 20, when the binding vote takes place. If unanimity is achieved — a high bar in a sport where teams have competing technical interests — the changes would need to be drafted, codified, and circulated to all teams before Miami.

That is a tight window. But the FIA and the teams appear to share a common interest in resolving these issues quickly. The alternative — continuing into a Miami Sprint weekend under rules that produced a 50G crash three races in — is not a palatable option for anyone.

What's Next

Watch the April 20 vote closely. Any single team blocking the unanimous requirement could delay changes until after Miami. If the vote passes, Miami becomes the first race of a subtly different F1 season — one where the car characteristics that have dominated the first three rounds are partially corrected.

Drivers have been vocal about the 2026 rules since pre-season. The FIA is responding. Whether the fixes are sufficient — or whether today begins a longer negotiation — will become clear in the next twelve days.
