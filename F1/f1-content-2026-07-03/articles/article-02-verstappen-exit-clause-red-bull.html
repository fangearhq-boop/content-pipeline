The Countdown Begins: Max Verstappen Could Leave Red Bull Before Summer Break

By Marcus Cole July 3, 2026 F1 Fanrecap

The Clause That Changes Everything

Max Verstappen holds a Red Bull contract that runs through the end of the 2028 Formula 1 season. On paper, that is a statement of long-term commitment from both sides. In practice, one specific provision buried within that agreement is now the most consequential clause in all of motorsport. It is performance-based: if Verstappen finds himself outside the top two in the drivers' championship standings when the Formula 1 summer break officially begins, he has the contractual right to activate an exit clause and leave Red Bull for the 2027 season.

That clause is active right now. After eight rounds of the 2026 World Championship — through the Austrian Grand Prix on June 28 — Verstappen sits seventh in the standings with 113 points. Kimi Antonelli leads the championship with 171 points. George Russell is second with 131. Verstappen is 18 points behind Russell in second place and 58 points behind championship leader Antonelli. With the summer break looming, the numbers are stark.

The Three-Race Window

Three races remain on the calendar before the summer break begins. This weekend's British Grand Prix at Silverstone is the first. The Belgian Grand Prix follows on July 17-19. The Hungarian Grand Prix closes the pre-summer sequence on July 24-26. That is three opportunities — 25 points per race available in the main event alone, plus sprint race points this weekend — to move from seventh into the top two.

The mathematics are not impossible but they require near-perfection from Verstappen and near-disaster from those above him. Bridging an 18-point gap to Russell over three races is theoretically achievable, particularly if sprint weekend points at Silverstone are added to the equation. But reaching second also requires Verstappen to overhaul drivers currently above him — a task that compounds the difficulty. And the clause itself requires second place specifically, not a general improvement in performance.

Verstappen's Manager Sends a Message

The situation has prompted public comment from Verstappen's management team. The message has been clear: the desire is to remain with Red Bull, but that desire is conditional. The team must demonstrate the ability to compete for wins and championships. A driver of Verstappen's calibre — a four-time world champion — will not accept simply finishing seventh with a team that cannot provide him with a winning car. The manager's position frames the exit clause not as a threat but as a logical safeguard in a sport where car performance defines outcomes.

Red Bull's 2026 season has been difficult. The new technical regulations that came into force this year changed the competitive order significantly, and Red Bull have not yet found the form that made them dominant across 2021-2024. Verstappen has been extracting results from machinery that is not at the front of the pack — but seventh in the championship reflects the reality of where Red Bull currently stand.

The Buyout Talks

Reports from within the F1 paddock indicate that Red Bull are exploring an alternative to a Verstappen departure: buying out the exit clause entirely. Figures described as being in the "low double-digit millions" have been cited in reporting. A buyout would effectively nullify the clause, removing the trigger and binding Verstappen to the team through 2028 regardless of championship position at the summer break.

Such a buyout would make sense from Red Bull's perspective if they believe their car development trajectory will improve significantly in the second half of 2026 and into 2027. Losing Verstappen would cause immense disruption to the team, both operationally and commercially. From Verstappen's perspective, accepting a buyout would require confidence that Red Bull can deliver a competitive package — and right now that confidence is being tested.

The 2027 Market Fallout

The stakes extend well beyond Red Bull's garage. Seventeen Formula 1 drivers are currently in the final year of their contracts in 2026. If Verstappen activates his clause and enters the open market, it would trigger a cascade of moves across the grid that could reshape the entire starting lineup for 2027. Two teams are reportedly already in discussions with Verstappen's management about the possibility of securing his services. The identity of those teams has not been confirmed publicly, but the speculation has already begun reshaping how fans and industry observers think about next season's grid.

Toto Wolff at Mercedes has been publicly clear: he is not pursuing Verstappen and is committed to Kimi Antonelli and George Russell. But with 17 contracts expiring and a four-time world champion potentially on the move, every team principal in the paddock is running scenarios in the background.

What's Next

The British Grand Prix sprint weekend begins today, and Verstappen's performance over the next three weekends will answer the most pressing question in Formula 1: will he activate the clause, or will Red Bull find a path to keeping him? The answers may come before a single lap of the British GP is run, if a buyout is agreed. Or they may play out across three increasingly intense weekends leading into the summer break. Either way, Max Verstappen's future is the story that hangs over everything else in the 2026 season.
