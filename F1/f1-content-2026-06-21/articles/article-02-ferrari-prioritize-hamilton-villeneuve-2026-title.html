Ferrari Must Prioritize Hamilton for 2026 Title, Says Villeneuve — Can Maranello Make the Call?

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap | June 21, 2026

Jacques Villeneuve has never been known for diplomatic silence, and his assessment of Ferrari's 2026 championship situation is characteristically blunt: the Scuderia must prioritize Lewis Hamilton if they want to win the title. Full stop.

The 1997 world champion made the statement publicly, pointing to what he sees as the only logical path forward for a team holding two drivers in the championship standings — one of whom is alive in the title fight, one of whom is not.

The Math That Drives the Argument

Hamilton sits 41 points behind championship leader Kimi Antonelli with 15 races remaining. That gap is manageable for a driver winning races — 41 points is less than two grand prix victories in the modern points system. Hamilton himself has said "nothing is impossible" when asked about his title chances, and the Barcelona win proved Ferrari can execute at the highest level.

Charles Leclerc occupies P4 with 75 points — 81 behind Antonelli. The math isn't impossible, but it requires near-perfection and significant misfortune for multiple rivals simultaneously. Villeneuve's argument is straightforward: Hamilton is Ferrari's title candidate, and the team's strategy, pit wall decisions, and resource allocation should reflect that reality.

The Political Minefield

Asking Ferrari to prioritize Hamilton over Leclerc is not a simple request. Charles Leclerc has been the face of Ferrari's future for half a decade. He took massive paycuts and remained loyal during years when the car was uncompetitive. He is arguably the more naturally gifted qualifier, the driver the tifosi adore. The idea of telling him to move aside for a seven-time champion who joined the team in 2025 would represent a stunning reversal of the power structure at Maranello.

Ferrari has form on team orders — they've used them to protect Leclerc before, most notably when his teammate Carlos Sainz was asked to cede position in the closing races of 2024. But using those orders in reverse, against Leclerc rather than for him, would test the limits of what the team is willing to do and what Leclerc is willing to accept.

Hamilton's Diplomatic Position

Hamilton himself has been careful publicly. He has not called for team orders. He has spoken about wanting Ferrari to "work together" without specifying what that means in a race situation where he and Leclerc are competing for the same result. But actions in the pit lane speak louder than press conference statements — the Austrian GP could be the first real test of whether Ferrari's strategy reflects a true prioritization or continues with a theoretical "best man wins" approach.

Sky Sports noted that Mercedes, for their part, are "counting Hamilton in" to the title fight — a phrase that carries weight given Toto Wolff's close relationship with the seven-time champion and knowledge of his capabilities at this stage of a season.

What's Next

The Austrian GP at the Red Bull Ring (June 26-28) will be the next flashpoint. If Ferrari's strategy at Spielberg clearly favors Hamilton — in tire strategy, in pit call sequencing, or in an explicit instruction — the narrative will shift significantly. Leclerc will be watching Austria as carefully as any race he's competed in. The team's decision over the next five race weekends will determine not just the 2026 title, but the future of Ferrari's internal dynamics heading into 2027.
