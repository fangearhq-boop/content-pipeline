Forty-Five Points Clear: How Antonelli Turned a Three-Way Fight Into a Title March

By Marcus Cole

July 20, 2026

Three months ago, the 2026 Formula 1 championship looked like an open contest. Kimi Antonelli, Lewis Hamilton, and George Russell were all in realistic contention. Ferrari, Mercedes, and McLaren each had the pace to challenge on different circuits. The second half of the season stretched out ahead as anyone's to win.

After the Belgian Grand Prix, the picture has changed. Antonelli leads by 45 points over Hamilton. Russell sits third, 50 back. Leclerc is fourth, 78 behind. The three-way fight at the front has become a coronation in progress — if Antonelli can maintain the form he has shown in recent races.

How the Gap Grew

The transformation from contested championship to dominant lead did not happen in a single race. It happened across a run of events in which Antonelli consistently scored while his rivals took turns giving points away.

Hamilton's 5-second penalty at Spa dropped him from third to fourth in the race result. Russell's retirement eliminated him entirely. Leclerc, for all his pace at Ferrari, has yet to string together the consistent scoring runs needed to mount a real title challenge from 78 points behind.

And through it all, Antonelli kept finishing at or near the front. He converts poles to wins. He avoids incidents. He does not panic under pressure. For a 19-year-old in his first season as a genuine title contender, that consistency is the most impressive part of his résumé.

Can Anyone Close the Gap?

Hamilton is the most realistic threat. He is in second place, 45 points behind, and driving for Ferrari — a team that has shown genuine race pace in 2026. If Hamilton can string together wins while Antonelli encounters reliability issues or on-track incidents, the gap could close.

But the summer break arrives at a moment when momentum is firmly with Antonelli. A 45-point lead is not insurmountable — a bad weekend can shred 25 points from a lead in an instant — but it requires Antonelli to falter, and he has given little evidence that is in his nature.

Russell, from 50 points back, faces an even steeper climb. His Spa retirement was not just a lost race. It was a momentum shift heading into the break — one that the second half of the season will struggle to reverse.

What's Next

The summer break is a reset button for the entire paddock. Teams will work on performance upgrades, drivers will recover and refocus, and the 2026 championship will resume with the standings locked where Belgium left them.

For Antonelli, the break arrives as a gift. He goes into it as the clear favorite for his first world title. He does not need to dominate every session from here — he needs to be consistent, avoid incidents, and let the points tally do the rest. Forty-five points is a platform. Now he has to protect it.
