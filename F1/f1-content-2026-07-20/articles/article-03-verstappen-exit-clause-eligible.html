Verstappen's Red Bull Exit Clause Is Now Eligible. Will He Use It?

By Ryan Calloway

July 20, 2026

The numbers are in, and they say what many expected: Max Verstappen's Red Bull exit clause is now eligible to be triggered.

The clause embedded in Verstappen's contract — which runs through 2028 — allows him to leave Red Bull early if he is not in the top two of the Formula 1 championship standings at the summer break. After the Belgian Grand Prix, Verstappen sits seventh. The clause can be exercised in October.

Whether he exercises it is a different question entirely.

What We Know

The Belgian GP weekend produced the latest installment in what has become one of F1's most watched transfer storylines. In the Spa press conference, Verstappen was asked directly about his future plans. His response was a single word: "No."

It was not a denial. It was a refusal to engage — a distinction Verstappen clearly understands and deliberately maintains. He did not say he is staying. He did not say he is leaving. He said he would not discuss it.

His manager, Raymond Vermeulen, was marginally more forthcoming. Vermeulen issued a statement reiterating that Verstappen wants to honor his Red Bull contract and "finish what he started" with the team. He acknowledged that a clause existing "doesn't mean they'll invoke it."

Meanwhile, reports from PlanetF1 and SportBible claim McLaren talks with Verstappen are in "closing stages" — suggesting a 2027 deal is near. ESPN's paddock sources offered a different view, calling those reports "wide of the mark" and suggesting the McLaren negotiations are not as advanced as portrayed.

The Red Bull Context

A year ago, Verstappen was the reigning world champion and the dominant force in Formula 1. Red Bull was the unquestioned constructor leader, and Verstappen appeared settled in a long-term partnership that had produced four consecutive titles.

The 2026 season changed that picture. Red Bull's car has not been competitive enough at the front of the grid, and Verstappen has spent much of the year in the midfield. His P3 at the Belgian GP was one of his stronger results in 2026 — and he is still seventh in the championship.

For a driver of Verstappen's ambition and competitive record, seventh place is not an acceptable destination. The exit clause exists precisely for this scenario: a performance-based off-ramp from a contract that no longer serves his championship objectives.

What's Next

The summer break gives all parties time to assess their positions. Verstappen and his management will evaluate whether Red Bull's 2026 improvements in the second half of the season justify remaining. McLaren, if the talks are real, will be watching the same results with interest.

October is the window. Between now and then, Verstappen's on-track results will either strengthen or weaken the case for staying. If Red Bull shows consistent pace improvements and Verstappen climbs the standings, the conversation may shift. If he spends September and October in the midfield again, the clause is there for a reason.
