Verstappen and Red Bull's Season Keeps Getting Worse — P8 at Japan, Five Weeks to Find Answers

By F1 Fanrecap Staff | March 30, 2026

It has been twelve months since Max Verstappen finished two points short of a fifth consecutive world championship. Now, three races into the 2026 season, he sits ninth in the standings with 12 points — and no clear route back to the front.

Verstappen's Japanese GP weekend was, by some measures, his least bad of the year: P8 at the flag compared to P9 and P6 in the two preceding races. But Suzuka should have been a circuit that suited the Red Bull more than Australia or China. It is a high-downforce track with flowing corners that reward mechanical grip and aerodynamic stability. Instead, the RB22 remained as uncooperative as ever.

Verstappen was eliminated from Q2 in qualifying — outpaced by rookie Arvid Lindblad of Racing Bulls, the second time this season a junior team driver has beaten him in Saturday's session. He started 11th and spent the race grinding through the midfield, eventually finishing eighth and half a second behind Alpine's Pierre Gasly in a straight fight he couldn't win.

The Car Is the Problem

Verstappen has been uncharacteristically vocal in describing the RB22's faults, and at Suzuka his frustration reached a new level. "Everywhere is just s***," he told engineers during one exchange. Post-race, he was only slightly more measured: "I'm not even frustrated anymore. I'm beyond that."

The fundamental issue is a car that swings unpredictably between understeer and snap oversteer, sometimes within the same corner, and shows different characteristics from session to session and lap to lap. Red Bull introduced new bodywork at Japan, their first major upgrade, but even that failed to deliver the expected improvement. "I'm driving with a different aero package this weekend, yet it seems like that's not working," Verstappen said.

His assessment of where the problem lies pointed specifically at the chassis rather than the power unit: "From the car side, we are really struggling at the moment and also to keep it consistent. There are problems that we know are in the car and we are trying to fix."

Team principal Laurent Mekies offered the most honest — and concerning — diagnosis of all: "It's important to first understand what we are trying to address as a limitation, then the development race will start." Three races in, Red Bull are still in the diagnosis phase.

Five Weeks to Turn It Around

The cancellation of the Bahrain and Saudi Arabian rounds means Formula 1 does not return until the Miami Grand Prix on May 3. For most teams, the five-week gap is a development opportunity. For Red Bull, it is something more urgent than that.

With Verstappen on 12 points and championship leader Antonelli already at 72, the mathematical gap is significant but not yet insurmountable. With 19 races and 475 points remaining, a major turnaround in form could still bring Verstappen into title contention. But every race that passes without a podium makes the arithmetic more difficult.

Verstappen acknowledged the break at least provides time: "With the month off, we can analyse our problems and get on top of them." The question is whether Red Bull can fix a car that their own team principal cannot yet fully diagnose.

What's Next

Red Bull will spend the coming weeks conducting intensive analysis at their Milton Keynes factory, looking at the aerodynamic and mechanical data from all three race weekends. The expectation is that significant updates will arrive in Miami, though whether those updates address the root cause of the RB22's instability remains to be seen.

For Verstappen, the next five weeks are a test of patience. The driver who has won in every car configuration Red Bull has presented him over the past four years is now asking fundamental questions about machinery that refuses to cooperate. He will arrive in Miami needing a points haul — and a car that he can trust again.
