Red Bull Believes April Break Can Win Back Verstappen — But the Exit Clause Is Real

By Elena Voss April 2, 2026

Four Weeks to Save a Championship Partnership

Red Bull team principal Laurent Mekies chose his words carefully, but the message was clear. "We have a lot of work to do," Mekies told reporters, "but I'm sure by the time we give him a fast car, he will be a much happier Max."

The statement is simultaneously a commitment, an acknowledgment, and a plea. The commitment: Red Bull will use the 5-week break between Japan and Miami to fix the RB22. The acknowledgment: the car, as it currently stands, is not good enough for a four-time world champion. The plea: stay patient, Max.

The Numbers Behind the Frustration

Max Verstappen's 2026 season begins with 12 points from three races. His best result is sixth place at the Australian Grand Prix — a result that, in isolation, would represent a poor weekend for any driver of his caliber. Red Bull is tied for sixth in the Constructors' Championship with Alpine, also on 16 points, having achieved none of the results their resources and historical record should produce.

The RB22 has been described internally as a car that goes "from one extreme to another" — reactive to setup changes in unpredictable ways that make it difficult to optimize for any individual circuit. In China, Verstappen struggled with tire degradation that the Mercedes drivers, benefiting from the PU advantage that reduces mechanical stress on the rear tyres, barely experienced. At Suzuka, rookie Isack Hadjar outqualified him. The pattern has been consistent enough that Verstappen's language in the paddock has become openly despairing.

The Exit Clause

ESPN confirmed that Verstappen's contract contains an exit clause specifically tied to the performance of the 2026 regulations. The clause is not a hypothetical — it is an active contractual tool, and whether it can be triggered depends on a performance threshold that only Verstappen and his management team know. The "quits immediately" headlines that circulated in tabloid outlets this week were not supported by credible sources and should not be taken at face value. But the exit clause is real, and it is not secret.

Red Bull's plan — a three-stage development push from Miami through Imola — is designed to deliver a materially improved car before the clause becomes relevant. The no-shutdown April break means engineers are working now. The team has identified the RB22's understeer characteristics and floor efficiency as the primary targets for the Miami update.

What's Next

Miami will be the first verdict. If Red Bull arrives with a car that can fight for a top-5 position consistently, the narrative changes — a struggling champion in a repairable car is a comeback story. If the Miami update disappoints and Verstappen continues to be outpaced by midfield teams and his own rookie teammate, the exit clause conversation will intensify in a way that Mekies' public confidence cannot contain. Four weeks is a short time to rebuild the car and the relationship. But it is what Red Bull has.
