One Week Until the FIA's April 9 Meeting — Can Sainz Get Safety Changes Before Miami?

By Marcus Cole April 2, 2026

The Crash That Changed Everything

Oliver Bearman was traveling at approximately 186 miles per hour when he approached the Spoon curve at Suzuka. Franco Colapinto's Alpine, just ahead of him, was in the energy harvesting phase — decelerating at a rate dictated by the 2026 hybrid system's charging requirements. The closing speed between the two cars was approximately 45 kilometers per hour. The impact registered at 50G. Bearman walked away. The conversation hasn't stopped since.

The FIA's response — a confirmed technical working group meeting on April 9 with all team technical directors — represents the sport's most significant regulatory review since the 2026 rules were introduced. It takes place one week from today.

What's on the Table

The April 9 agenda is understood to cover four primary areas: super-clipping in qualifying, where extreme power differentials between cars in harvest mode and deployment mode create unpredictable overtaking dynamics; dangerous closing-speed differentials in racing conditions; battery status warning systems that would allow trailing drivers to know when a car ahead is harvesting energy; and potential long-term changes to the electric-to-thermal power ratio for 2027.

GPDA director Carlos Sainz has been the most vocal driver pushing for changes to be implemented before Miami. His position is simple: the Bearman crash was a warning. The next accident at a similar closing speed may not end the same way.

The Skeptics and the Process

Lewis Hamilton offered a more cautious assessment. "Drivers have no power with the FIA," he said, acknowledging that his skepticism is born from experience rather than cynicism. F1's regulatory process requires unanimous team agreement for any mid-season changes — a high bar given the competing interests of teams that benefit from the current rules and those that suffer under them.

The FIA's own statement confirmed that "a number of meetings are therefore scheduled in April to assess the operation of the new regulations and to determine whether any refinements are required." The language is measured. The stakes are not.

What's Next

The April 9 meeting is the first answer to whether Formula 1's governing body can move quickly enough to address a safety concern that has the explicit support of the drivers' association. If changes are approved and fast-tracked for Miami — whether to qualifying procedures, warning systems, or harvest thresholds — the meeting will be remembered as the moment the 2026 era corrected course. If it produces only a follow-up meeting, with real changes deferred to Imola or beyond, the pressure on the FIA from drivers, teams, and the public will only grow. The clock is running.
