The Teammate Problem: Will Mercedes Show Favouritism Toward Antonelli Over Russell?

By Ryan Calloway April 2, 2026

Nine Points and a Question

Kimi Antonelli leads George Russell by nine points in the 2026 Formula 1 Drivers' Championship. Nine. One race's worth of points at the wrong moment, one safety car that falls badly, one technical failure — and the order reverses. They drive for the same team. They share data. They share engineers. And now, with a 5-week break before Miami, the question that Mercedes has been doing its best to avoid is being asked out loud.

Former F1 driver Johnny Herbert raised it publicly this week: will Mercedes start showing favouritism toward Antonelli as the season develops? The comment, published by Motorsport Week, was not idle speculation. Herbert has decades of paddock experience and understands the internal pressures that come when a constructor's two drivers become genuine championship rivals.

A Scenario Mercedes Hoped to Avoid

When Mercedes signed Antonelli alongside Russell for 2026, the vision was a young talent developing under an experienced driver. What the team got was different: a 19-year-old who has won two of the first three races of the season, set two pole positions, and emerged as the clear championship frontrunner — while his senior teammate sits just 9 points behind, hungry and capable of winning at any circuit.

Autosport is now formally asking whether Antonelli is the outright 2026 title favourite. The answer, based on pace, consistency, and the margin over the field, leans heavily toward yes. But there is a complication built into the very structure that made his success possible: Russell, 28, is no supporting act. He won the opening race of the season in Australia and has not finished outside the top four.

The Historical Precedent

Intra-team F1 championships have a complicated history. Red Bull managed the Vettel-Webber era with increasing awkwardness before settling on a clear No. 1. Mercedes navigated Rosberg-Hamilton in 2016 with a championship that went to the final lap before one driver prevailed. Ferrari imploded over Schumacher-Barrichello. The playbook for managing two drivers who can both win is unwritten — and team orders, when they arrive, tend to leave one half of the garage and much of the fanbase unhappy.

Team principal Toto Wolff has been consistent in stating that both drivers will be free to race. Whether that position holds through to the final rounds of a championship that is increasingly competitive at the front will be one of the defining storylines of the 2026 season.

What's Next

The Miami GP (May 1-3) will be the first opportunity for Mercedes to signal, through their in-race strategy, how they intend to manage the battle within their own garage. A team order — even a subtle one — will be the story of the weekend. No team order, and the race between Antonelli and Russell continues organically, with 19 rounds still to run in the 2026 championship. The most interesting rivalry in F1 right now has both drivers wearing silver.
