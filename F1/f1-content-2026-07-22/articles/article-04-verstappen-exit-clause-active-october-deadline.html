Verstappen's Red Bull Exit Clause Is Active — Manager Issues Loyalty Statement as October Deadline Ticks

By Elena Voss

July 22, 2026

Max Verstappen can now formally leave Red Bull Racing. His exit clause — built into a contract that runs through 2028 — was triggered when his DNF at the British Grand Prix made it mathematically impossible for him to finish in the top two of the drivers' championship before the summer break. He currently sits seventh with 91 points, 113 behind Antonelli.

The clause gives him until October 2026 to decide whether to exercise it. Summer break begins Sunday night. The timing is not coincidental.

Vermeulen's Statement

On Tuesday, Verstappen's manager Raymond Vermeulen issued his most direct public statement on the situation to date: "We have a contract until 2028. Of course there are exit clauses; there always have been. But we've never exercised one. On the contrary, we've always been loyal and will remain so. We want to continue on this path with Red Bull and for Max to end his career here — but, of course, with the chance to win."

Broken down, the statement does three things: it acknowledges the clause exists, it points to a history of not using it, and it attaches a condition — "with the chance to win" — that Red Bull's 2026 form may not currently satisfy. That last phrase is the most significant. It is not a guarantee that Verstappen stays; it is a statement of what a stay would require.

Red Bull sits fifth in the constructors' championship. Verstappen is seventh in the drivers' standings. The car that won four consecutive championships is not the car he drove this year.

Where Could He Go?

The realistic destinations have narrowed since the start of the season.

McLaren is effectively closed. Oscar Piastri answered a direct question at Spa last Sunday with a single word — "Yes" — when asked if he would remain a McLaren driver in 2027. His manager Mark Webber confirmed Piastri is contracted through at least 2028. The McLaren door has been shut from the inside.

Mercedes is complicated. Toto Wolff has said publicly that the team has "two drivers with whom we have long-term, multi-year contracts" — Kimi Antonelli through 2029, George Russell through at least the end of 2027. To create a seat, Mercedes would need to buy out one of those contracts. The financial and reputational cost of doing so — particularly for Antonelli, who leads the championship at 19 — would be extraordinary.

Aston Martin is the most intriguing option. Adrian Newey, who designed every one of Verstappen's four championship-winning Red Bull cars, now leads Aston Martin's technical operation. That connection is not abstract — it is a working relationship built across a decade of success that defines both men's legacies. Aston Martin has the financial backing of Lawrence Stroll's investment and a new technical campus. The B-spec chassis debuting at Hungary represents Newey's first full design contribution to the team. If it works, the pitch becomes: Newey built the car that will win you another championship. Come drive it.

Ferrari remains a possibility, described by insiders as "less likely but not impossible." Charles Leclerc is contracted and Hamilton arrived this year. A third rotation would be unusual.

The Red Bull Position

Reports from inside Red Bull indicate that senior figures are split on how to respond to the clause. Some want to attempt to buy it out or otherwise persuade Verstappen that a longer-term rebuild is worth staying for. Others see the competitive trajectory more clearly.

The summer break is when these conversations move from background noise to actual negotiation. Three weeks of paddock quiet typically means the phone lines between team principals, managers, and driver camps are anything but.

What's Next

The October deadline is not far off. Verstappen will race at Hungary this week — his fourth race weekend since the British GP triggered the clause — and then disappear into the summer break alongside everyone else. When the paddock reconvenes for the Dutch Grand Prix at Zandvoort, the conversation will have moved significantly one way or the other.

Whether Verstappen ultimately stays or goes, the clause being active and his manager publicly conditioning loyalty on "the chance to win" means this story is no longer theoretical. Something will happen by October.
