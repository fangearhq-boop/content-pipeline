Mercedes Confirms Software Bug Hurt Both Cars at Spa — And Hungarian GP Starts Thursday

By Elena Voss

July 22, 2026

The questions surrounding Mercedes' performance at the Belgian Grand Prix have been answered — and the answer is worse than expected. Technical director Simone Resta confirmed Tuesday that a software coding error caused both Kimi Antonelli and George Russell to run with compromised energy delivery throughout race weekend at Spa-Francorchamps.

The bug was over-delivering energy from the MGU-H system early in each lap, which caused the system to exhaust its available power budget before reaching critical sections of the track. Between Turns 1 and 5, both cars were losing boost they should have had. Russell suffered an additional secondary deployment deficit heading into the final chicane that cost him 0.2 to 0.25 seconds per lap in qualifying alone.

"It's a deeper coding problem," Resta said, according to RaceFans. "Over-delivering energy early in the lap, meaning it ran out early."

Two Weeks of Misdiagnosis

The severity of this revelation is compounded by its timeline. The software glitch did not originate at Spa — it traces back to Silverstone, the British Grand Prix held two weekends earlier. Mercedes identified unusual pace differences between the two cars at that race and initially attributed the problem to driving style variations between Antonelli and Russell.

Russell was asked to modify his driving technique based on that diagnosis. He did. The performance deficit remained. It took another race weekend and post-race data analysis at Spa to identify the actual source: a software layer that was mismanaging the energy harvest cycle, causing both cars to perform below their potential at multiple points during each lap.

The misdiagnosis cost the team two full race weekends of optimization. At Spa, Mercedes still managed a race victory through Antonelli — but with a better-functioning energy system, the pace and qualifying positions could have been meaningfully different.

The Hungarian GP Problem

Formula 1 opens practice at the Hungaroring on Thursday. Mercedes has not confirmed whether the bug will be fully resolved before FP1.

That uncertainty carries real championship weight. Antonelli arrives in Budapest with a 45-point lead over Lewis Hamilton — built over six wins from ten races — but the software issue creates a performance floor that is difficult to quantify without clean data. If the bug persists into qualifying, Russell's lap time deficit of more than 0.2 seconds per lap becomes a compounding problem on a circuit where the difference between pole and P3 is often measured in tenths.

The Hungaroring is a high-downforce circuit where smooth delivery of power through slow, technical corners is essential. Energy deployment reliability matters enormously there. A car that runs out of MGU-H power at the wrong moments is not a car built for Budapest's particular demands.

Toto Wolff's pre-event statement — "We need to execute a clean, reliable weekend" — now reads as something more than a standard pre-race message. He may have already known what Resta confirmed publicly on Tuesday.

What's Next

The immediate priority for Mercedes is deploying a software fix before Thursday's FP1 session begins. If the team resolves the issue cleanly, the damage from Spa and Silverstone is limited to lost points that cannot be recaptured. If it persists into the Budapest weekend, Antonelli's ability to extend his lead before the summer break is genuinely at risk — and a dominant first half of the season could end on a compromised note heading into a three-week shutdown.

Thursday will tell the story. Mercedes has the resources and talent to fix a software problem in the time available. Whether they have fixed it already is the question the Hungaroring will answer.
