McLaren's "Macarena" Wing and Aston Martin's Full B-Spec: The Biggest Upgrade Day of the 2026 F1 Season

By Marcus Cole

July 22, 2026

Thursday's opening practice session at the Hungaroring will feature two of the most significant technical debuts of the 2026 Formula 1 season arriving at the same time, from different teams, with very different levels of pressure attached to both.

McLaren brings an experimental upside-down rear wing to FP1. Aston Martin brings an entirely new car. One is exploratory. One is existential.

McLaren's "Macarena" Rear Wing

McLaren's headline item at Budapest is a revised floor package that addresses what the team has publicly described as being "two to three months behind in aero development" relative to their rivals. The floor is the priority. But the wingwork is the story.

Accompanying the floor upgrade is an experimental rear wing that McLaren engineers have nicknamed the "Macarena" — because it runs inverted compared to conventional rear wing design. Traditional rear wings generate downforce by pressing air downward; the Macarena's unconventional orientation produces a different airflow interaction with the rear of the car, the details of which McLaren has not publicly disclosed.

The Macarena will only appear in FP1. McLaren has confirmed it will not race at Hungary and will not appear at Zandvoort after the summer break. The session will function as a controlled data collection exercise — FP1 data informs the longer-term development roadmap, not this weekend's race strategy.

F2 champion Andrea Fornaroli will take one of McLaren's FP1 seats as part of their young driver programme, adding another data point to what is shaping up as one of the busiest single practice sessions of the year for the McLaren garage.

Aston Martin's B-Spec Overhaul

For Aston Martin, the Hungarian Grand Prix carries weight that goes beyond a single race. The team has not meaningfully upgraded the AMR26 since it was unveiled at pre-season testing in Bahrain — ten consecutive race weekends ago. During those ten rounds, rival teams brought upgrade after upgrade. Aston Martin brought essentially nothing. The gap between the AMR26's debut specification and the competition's current pace has grown steadily with each passing weekend.

The B-spec package that arrives at the Hungaroring is the team's attempt to stop that slide and potentially reverse it. Led by Adrian Newey's technical group, the changes are comprehensive: a new nose design, revised rear suspension geometry, reworked aerodynamic surfaces across the entire bodywork, significant weight reductions to both the chassis and gearbox, and rehomologated crash structures that required formal FIA recertification.

Team principal Mike Krack confirmed the team has sufficient spare components to run the complete B-spec package on both Fernando Alonso's and Lance Stroll's cars from the start of the weekend. There is no staged introduction — both drivers get the full package on Thursday.

Stroll provided the bluntest assessment of the stakes: "If the Hungary package goes wrong, a new engine won't be enough." Honda is providing a power unit update later in the season, but Stroll's message was clear: chassis upgrades and power unit upgrades need to compound, not compensate. If the B-spec fails to deliver, the Honda update alone cannot rescue the season.

Why Both Upgrades Matter This Weekend

The Hungaroring is one of the highest-downforce circuits on the calendar — slow, technical, with corners that reward aero efficiency and mechanical grip above raw top speed. It is a track where proper downforce management can overcome raw power deficit, making it theoretically ideal for testing upgrade packages that target aerodynamic efficiency rather than straight-line performance.

McLaren's floor upgrade is directly targeted at high-downforce circuits: their press materials explicitly cite improved braking and cornering performance as the package's design goal. If the floor delivers its projected gains, the Hungaroring is a better test environment than most.

For Aston Martin, the internal projections on the B-spec are ambitious — some estimates cited internally suggest up to two seconds of lap time improvement across the full package. Whether those projections translate to race pace will define whether Newey's team enters the second half of the season as a competitive force or continues its slide.

What's Next

FP1 on Thursday is the first data point for both packages. McLaren will assess the Macarena wing results overnight and decide how to proceed with their aero development programme post-summer. Aston Martin will know within two sessions whether the B-spec has delivered what Newey's team promised.

Either way, Thursday afternoon will provide more technical clarity about the second half of the 2026 F1 season than any practice session of the year so far.
