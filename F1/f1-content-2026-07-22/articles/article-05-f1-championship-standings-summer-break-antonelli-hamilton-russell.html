F1 Championship Standings at Halftime: Antonelli 45 Clear, Hamilton and Russell Separated by Only 5 Points

By Ryan Calloway

July 22, 2026

As Formula 1 prepares for its final race before the summer shutdown, the 2026 championship picture after ten rounds is simultaneously clarifying and tightening in all the most interesting places. One driver is running away with it. Two others are fighting each other for relevance. And a four-time champion sits seventh with an exit clause in his pocket.

Antonelli's Dominant First Half

Kimi Antonelli leads the drivers' championship with 204 points. He has won six races and started from pole position six times in ten rounds. He is 19 years old.

The historical context is worth repeating because it will be relevant for years: Antonelli is the youngest championship leader in Formula 1 history. He set the youngest pole position record at the Chinese Grand Prix earlier this year. If he wins the 2026 title, he will become the first teenager ever to win the Formula 1 World Championship, taking the record from Sebastian Vettel, who won at 21 in 2010.

His first-half form would be remarkable at any age. He has a 45-point lead with eight races remaining — 200 points still available. The math is in his favor in every scenario except a catastrophic mechanical or personal performance collapse.

Hamilton vs. Russell — Five Points

The more combustible story heading into the summer break is the five-point gap between Lewis Hamilton and George Russell for second and third place in the drivers' championship.

Hamilton sits at 159 points. Russell at 154. They are Mercedes teammates driving for different constructors — Russell with Mercedes, Hamilton now with Ferrari — and they share a recent history that includes a significant first-lap collision at Spa where Russell clipped Hamilton's front tyre and retired on the spot while Hamilton served a five-second time penalty and finished fourth.

The Hungaroring is where they resume that competition this Sunday. Hamilton has won here eight times — more than anyone in the sport's history. Russell won two races here during his dominant Mercedes years. At a circuit where the top two or three qualifying positions often determine the race result, their relative pace heading into Saturday's session may determine where both of them stand when the summer break begins.

What makes the battle more pointed is that their constructors are also separated at the top: Mercedes leads Ferrari by 73 points in the constructors' championship. Hamilton's points count for Ferrari; Russell's count for Mercedes. Every point between them is also a point between their respective teams.

The Rest of the Picture

Charles Leclerc sits fourth at 126 points — firmly in the championship mathematically but needing significant help to close a 78-point gap to Antonelli over eight races. Lando Norris and Oscar Piastri trail in fifth and sixth for McLaren, with the team's big Hungarian upgrade package potentially shifting their competitive position in the second half.

Max Verstappen is seventh with 91 points — 113 behind Antonelli. His exit clause from Red Bull is now formally active. His manager issued a loyalty-but-conditions statement Tuesday. The summer break may decide where he races in 2027.

In the constructors' standings: Mercedes holds 358 points, Ferrari 285, with a 73-point margin between them. Mercedes is chasing its first constructors' title since 2021 and what would be its ninth overall.

A note from Spa: Ferrari were fined €30,000 by the FIA following the incident in which a mechanic was struck during Hamilton's pit stop. The stewards found no fault with Hamilton and noted he suffered a "sporting disadvantage" from the resulting delay. €10,000 of the fine was suspended, conditional on Ferrari submitting a safety protocol report to the FIA within 14 days.

What's Next

The Hungarian Grand Prix runs July 24-26 at the Hungaroring in Budapest. After Sunday's race, the entire paddock enters the four-week summer break. When Formula 1 returns, it returns to Zandvoort for the Dutch Grand Prix — and whatever conversations happened in the quiet weeks will have shaped the second-half storylines in ways that will only become apparent once the engines fire up again.

The championship is Antonelli's to lose. But eight races is a long way to go, and the five-point battle inside the top three promises to make the second half as interesting as the first.
