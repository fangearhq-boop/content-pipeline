Is Max Verstappen Done With Red Bull? A Champion's Patience Is Running Out

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap | July 7, 2026

There are several ways to interpret Max Verstappen's recent public statements. The most generous reading is that he's venting — a competitive athlete frustrated by circumstances outside his control, saying more than he means in the heat of the moment. The less generous reading is that he is accurately describing what he sees, and what he sees is a Red Bull Racing team that can no longer give him what he needs.

After crashing out at Silverstone — apparently with the same rear wing DRS failure that also caused him trouble at the Austrian Grand Prix — Verstappen said he "doesn't want anything to do with it for a while." Earlier in the season, he called the 2026 F1 cars "Formula E on steroids" and likened the driving experience to "Mario Kart." He has said directly: "I'm definitely not having fun, at all, with these cars."

Verstappen is seventh in the championship with 76 points. He is 103 points behind Kimi Antonelli. Red Bull is fourth in the constructors' standings, more than 200 points behind Mercedes.

The Contract and the Exit Clause

Verstappen signed a seven-year extension with Red Bull through 2028 — a contract that seemed, at the time of signing, to be one of the great certainties in Formula 1. His dominance from 2021 to 2024, winning four consecutive world championships, made the arrangement logical for both sides. Red Bull needed Verstappen. Verstappen had built his identity within Red Bull's structure.

The critical detail that changes the calculus: the contract contains performance-based exit clauses. If Red Bull misses specific performance targets relative to the competition, Verstappen is entitled to explore his options before the deal expires. The exact thresholds of those clauses are not public, but the framing is clear enough — this is a contract designed to keep Verstappen only as long as Red Bull gives him a car worth driving.

Right now, they are not. Red Bull's fourth-place constructors' position reflects a fundamental gap in performance that the team's engineers have not yet closed. The new 2026 technical regulations — entirely new power units, new aerodynamic philosophy, new energy management demands — appear to have exposed Red Bull's relative disadvantage in ways that won't be quickly fixed.

What Would a Verstappen Move Look Like?

The realistic alternatives for Verstappen, if he chose to activate an exit clause, are limited by availability. Mercedes is set with Antonelli and Russell. Ferrari has Hamilton and Leclerc. McLaren has Norris and Piastri. There is no obvious vacancy at a top team for a driver of Verstappen's profile to slot into cleanly.

The more likely scenario, if things don't improve, is that Verstappen pressures Red Bull into a major technical effort in the second half of the season and into 2027 planning — using the public frustration as leverage to accelerate the team's development timeline. Champions of his caliber rarely leave winning environments willingly. The question is whether Red Bull can credibly present itself as a winning environment again.

What's Next

The Belgian Grand Prix at Spa is on July 17 — a high-speed, power-sensitive circuit that will tell us a great deal about where Red Bull's package actually stands relative to the top teams. If Verstappen is fighting for the top five at Spa, the conversation looks different than if he's battling for seventh and eighth. Red Bull needs a technical breakthrough. Verstappen's patience has a finite limit — and right now, that limit looks closer than anyone would have predicted at the start of 2026.
