Cadillac F1's Rocky Debut: America's New Team Is Learning the Hardest Lessons in Motorsport

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap | July 7, 2026

American motorsport has waited a long time for this moment. General Motors' Cadillac brand joined Formula 1 in 2026 as the grid's 11th team, signing Valtteri Bottas and Sergio Perez as its driver pairing and announcing its ambitions with the kind of optimism that new F1 entrants always bring and experience always tempers.

Nine races into their debut season, Cadillac F1 is sitting toward the back of the constructors' standings. Bottas finished 16th at Silverstone. Perez was 14th. The cars are competitive — in the sense that they are completing race distances and occasionally outrunning other cars — but they are not yet making the kind of headlines that their corporate backers had hoped for by July.

None of this is surprising to anyone who has watched F1 for long enough. The gap between announcing an F1 team and being genuinely competitive is one of the sport's most reliably underestimated challenges.

The Structural Challenge

Formula 1 teams spend decades accumulating knowledge. The aerodynamic understanding, the simulation infrastructure, the supplier relationships, the engineer development pipelines — all of it takes time to build. Red Bull, now a four-time constructors' champion, began as a relatively modest project in 2005. Mercedes' dominant period was the result of years of investment that predated even their technical dominance of the hybrid era.

Cadillac entered in 2026, a year when the entire sport adopted entirely new technical regulations: new power units with a strict 50-50 split between combustion and electrical energy, a radically different aerodynamic philosophy, and the removal of the MGU-H component that defined hybrid F1 for over a decade. Every team was rebuilding from scratch in some sense. But established teams rebuilt from a foundation of accumulated infrastructure, data systems, and institutional knowledge. Cadillac built from nothing, simultaneously.

Their results are what the structural reality predicts. That's not a failure of execution — it's an accurate outcome of the difficulty of the undertaking.

The Driver Situations

Valtteri Bottas and Sergio Perez bring different stories to the Cadillac garage. Bottas spent years as one of the fastest second drivers in F1 at Mercedes, delivering results that would have made him a champion at most other teams. At Cadillac, his role is different: he's being asked to lead a technical development effort from the cockpit, providing feedback that shapes how a team learns to compete at the highest level. It's quieter work than podium finishes, but it matters.

Perez, who had a long stint at Red Bull that included race wins and podiums but ultimately ended with a parting of ways, brings championship experience and the specific kind of knowledge that comes from years inside a top team. His ability to communicate precisely what a car needs — and what it's missing — is a resource that Cadillac will rely on as they develop their 2027 package.

What's Next

The first points score for Cadillac F1 will be a milestone worth watching for. At circuits that historically compress the field — street circuits, technical layouts where raw power matters less — a Cadillac in the top ten is not impossible. The team's development curve will determine whether that happens before the end of 2026 or has to wait for 2027's updates. What's certain is that America has its team in Formula 1, the foundation is being laid, and the gap between entry and competitiveness is being closed — race by race, lap by lap.
