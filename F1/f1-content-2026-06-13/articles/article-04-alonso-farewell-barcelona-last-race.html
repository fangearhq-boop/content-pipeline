Fernando Alonso's Farewell: 'Probably My Last Barcelona Race in Formula 1'

By Elena Voss | F1 Fanrecap | June 13, 2026

Fernando Alonso has given us a lot of moments in 25 years. He has given us championship battles that defined eras, wheel-to-wheel fights that became folklore, and a competitive intensity that has never fully dimmed regardless of the car beneath him. This week, at a circuit he has raced on more times than almost any other, he gave us something else: a goodbye.

"It's going to be a special weekend," Alonso told reporters ahead of the 2026 Spanish Grand Prix. "Probably my last Barcelona race in Formula 1. So, I want to say thanks to everyone. I will try to enjoy the weekend."

Why This Weekend Is Different

Alonso was careful to note that he is not formally announcing his retirement. He is still contracted to Aston Martin through the end of the 2026 season, and nothing in his statement forecloses the possibility of racing in 2027 if the right opportunity presents itself.

But the circuit calendar has made a specific point: Barcelona will not host a Formula 1 grand prix in 2027. Starting next year, the Circuit de Barcelona-Catalunya will rotate with the Belgian Grand Prix at Spa, meaning the next race in Spain won't be until 2028 at the earliest. Alonso, who will turn 45 in July of this year, acknowledged that simple arithmetic makes a 2028 appearance in Montmelo unlikely — regardless of what happens with his contract, his health, or his competitive desire.

This is his last race at the circuit that has, in many ways, defined his relationship with the sport. He began his Formula 1 career in an era when Barcelona was the season's proving ground. He has won here. He has fought here. He has watched rivals celebrate victories he believes he should have had. And through all of it, the Catalan crowd has treated him as one of their own — a Spanish champion, a local legend, a racer who never gave up.

The Career in Numbers

Alonso holds 32 Formula 1 race victories and two World Championship titles, claimed in 2005 and 2006 with Renault. He is 44 years old and still competing, which says everything about his dedication to the sport. He raced for Minardi, Renault, McLaren, Ferrari, Alpine, and now Aston Martin — a journey through virtually every major team and era of modern Formula 1.

The records tell only part of the story. What Alonso brought to the sport was an attitude: that every session, every lap, every circuit on the calendar deserved maximum effort regardless of the car's performance. He won two titles in an era dominated by Ferrari. He pushed McLaren machinery beyond its limits in 2007. He squeezed podiums out of cars that had no right to be on the podium.

What's Next

Whatever happens in Sunday's race, Alonso will leave Barcelona knowing he said thank you. The crowd will return the sentiment, as it always has. And Formula 1 will have had another reminder that the characters who make this sport worth watching do not last forever — not even the ones who seem immune to the passage of time.
