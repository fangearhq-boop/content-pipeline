Verstappen's Engine Gamble: New Power Unit, Zero Wins, and an Exit Clause on the Horizon

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap | June 13, 2026

At this time last year, Max Verstappen was the reigning Formula 1 World Champion and the dominant force in the sport. At Circuit de Barcelona-Catalunya this weekend, he arrives as a driver searching for his first win of 2026 — seventh in the Drivers' Championship, 113 points behind the championship leader, and carrying a new power unit in a car that has been unreliable all season.

The Monaco Grand Prix was the lowest point yet. On lap 1, before the field had even completed a full circuit, Verstappen's Red Bull stalled due to a power unit failure. He was classified last. While Kimi Antonelli won his fifth consecutive race, Verstappen scored zero points and watched his championship deficit grow even further beyond mathematical competitiveness.

A Fresh Start in Barcelona

Red Bull has responded by fitting a new power unit to Verstappen's RB20 for the Barcelona-Catalunya weekend. Verstappen described the upgrade as "a step in the right direction" after free practice on Friday — the most positive assessment he has given of his car's trajectory in several rounds.

The early practice data suggests improvement relative to Monaco's catastrophic showing. Red Bull appeared more competitive in the mid-field at Barcelona than they had been at the street circuit, though they remain well behind Mercedes' pace and still face the same underlying challenges with the 2026 regulations that have hamstrung them all season. One new engine component does not fix a fundamentally compromised car design.

The Clock Is Ticking

The urgency behind Barcelona extends beyond a single race result. Verstappen's Red Bull contract contains a performance-linked exit clause that can be triggered on July 27. That date falls after the Austrian Grand Prix, scheduled for June 26-28 — the race immediately following Spain.

The clause exists because Verstappen's contracts have historically included provisions allowing him to leave if Red Bull cannot provide a championship-competitive car. After zero wins in 2026 and a string of poor results, the conversation about whether Red Bull qualifies as "championship competitive" is a live one. If Barcelona and Austria do not produce dramatically better results, the pressure on Verstappen to exercise his options — and on Red Bull's rivals to make their pitch — will intensify sharply.

The four-time champion is not someone who accepts mediocrity. The new power unit is a tangible step. What he needs is a race result that demonstrates it was enough of a step to matter.

What's Next

Sunday's race will offer Verstappen a chance to score competitive points, rebuild confidence in the technical direction Red Bull has taken, and send a message ahead of the Austrian GP exit clause window. A strong result in Barcelona — a podium, or something near it — would fundamentally change the narrative. Another disappointing finish would accelerate the timeline on one of Formula 1's most consequential off-track decisions in years.
