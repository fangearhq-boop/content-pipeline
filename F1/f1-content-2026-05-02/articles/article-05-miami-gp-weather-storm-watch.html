Miami Storm Watch — 88% Rain, Thunderstorms, and a US Law That Could Stop the Race

By Marcus Cole | May 2, 2026 | F1 Fanrecap

Formula 1 races in bad weather all the time. Wet tracks, safety cars, slick strategies — these are the elements that create some of the sport's most memorable moments. But Sunday's Miami Grand Prix faces a weather scenario with a dimension that most F1 races don't have to consider: the law.

Current forecasts put the chance of precipitation on Sunday at 88%, with a 53% probability of thunderstorms during the race window. Those numbers alone would make Sunday's 4 PM ET start at the Miami International Autodrome one of the most weather-dependent races of the 2026 season. Add the Florida legal element, and the complexity increases dramatically.

The US Law Factor

Under Florida state law, outdoor public events must be halted if lightning is detected within eight miles of the venue. This is not an FIA regulation — it is a legal requirement that supersedes sporting authority. If thunderstorms bring lightning to the Miami area during the race, the event will be suspended regardless of what the FIA might otherwise decide. Officials cannot simply choose to race through it.

The FIA confirmed it has contingency plans in place for the Miami Grand Prix, including provisions for red flags, restart procedures, and the possibility of the race being declared official if more than half the distance is completed. But those contingencies only apply to interruptions. A sustained storm with persistent lightning would force a halt that even the most determined race director cannot override.

How Weather Changes Everything

Even setting aside the lightning risk, an 88% rain forecast rewires the entire competitive picture for Sunday's race. McLaren arrived in Miami with their most comprehensive upgrade package of the 2026 season — a new floor, new aero, new bodywork — specifically designed to improve their dry-weather performance. A wet race would largely neutralize that investment, at least in terms of comparative advantage.

Mercedes, by contrast, has shown exceptional consistency across different conditions throughout 2026. Championship leader Kimi Antonelli and George Russell both have the experience and car balance to manage a wet or mixed-condition race. If the track is wet from the start, Antonelli may find his path to a Miami victory significantly clearer.

For drivers like Max Verstappen and Charles Leclerc — who have been battling for podiums without the dominant cars they might prefer — rain is an opportunity. Wet conditions increase the premium on driver skill and reduce the aerodynamic advantage that separates the top teams in dry conditions. A safety car in mixed conditions could shuffle the deck entirely.

What's Next

Sunday's Miami Grand Prix starts at 4 PM ET on ESPN. The meteorological situation will become clearer as Sunday approaches, but as of Saturday, the rain forecast remains significant and the thunderstorm probability is real.

Whatever happens on Sunday, the Miami weekend has already delivered drama: Norris breaking Mercedes' qualifying dominance, McLaren's sweeping upgrade, and the ongoing championship battle between Antonelli and Russell. The weather just adds one more unpredictable variable to what is already the most compelling weekend of the 2026 F1 season to date.
