Ferrari's Three-Point Miami Upgrade Plan: Monza Test April 22, 'Macarena' Wing Ready to Race

By Elena Voss | F1 Fanrecap | April 5, 2026

Ferrari is not sitting still during the five-week break. While the rest of the paddock plans and prepares, the Scuderia is executing a structured, three-element upgrade program designed to transform the SF-26's competitiveness before the Miami Grand Prix on May 3.

On April 22, Ferrari will conduct a 200km test at Monza — a rare opportunity afforded by the unusual break in the calendar following the cancellation of the Bahrain and Saudi Arabian Grands Prix. Most of the planned Miami upgrades will be fitted to the car for that test, giving Charles Leclerc, Lewis Hamilton, and the engineering team real track data ahead of race weekend.

The Three-Point Plan

The first and most straightforward element is a revised floor. This aerodynamic component, which was originally intended to debut at the Bahrain Grand Prix before that race was cancelled, targets increased downforce for the SF-26. The team has been managing a deficit in mechanical and aerodynamic grip that has prevented Leclerc and Hamilton from fully challenging Mercedes in race conditions, and the floor revision is designed to address that gap directly.

The second element is a software overhaul for the power unit, specifically targeting Ferrari's management of super clipping — the energy harvesting mode that allows recovery under full throttle. The current SF-26 has been particularly affected by the speed differentials that super clipping creates, losing significant pace on straights when the car enters harvest mode. The new software aims to smooth this transition and recover lost straightline performance.

The third and most intriguing element is the 'Macarena' rear wing. Ferrari's unconventional design, which is reported to flip completely inside out to reduce drag at specific points, has been run in practice sessions during the early 2026 races but has never appeared in qualifying or the race. Miami could be its competitive debut.

The Engine Gap and ADUO

Underlying all of this aerodynamic and software work is an estimated 20-horsepower deficit to Mercedes in raw power unit performance — a gap that aerodynamics alone cannot fully bridge. Ferrari has submitted an application to the FIA under the Additional Development and Upgrade Opportunities (ADUO) framework, which allows power unit manufacturers to develop and upgrade their engines under specific controlled conditions during the season.

If the ADUO application is approved, Ferrari would be permitted to begin closing the power unit gap through in-season development — a process that, combined with the aerodynamic and software upgrades, could make the SF-26 a genuine Mercedes rival by mid-season rather than late-season.

The Miami Context

Miami was originally scheduled as the sixth round of the 2026 season. With Bahrain and Saudi Arabia cancelled, it is now the fourth. That calendar shift has compressed Ferrari's development timeline but also created the Monza test opportunity — a chance to validate all three upgrade elements in a controlled environment before race conditions.

Mercedes has dominated the 2026 season so far, with Kimi Antonelli leading the championship at 72 points and the constructors' lead standing at 45 points over Ferrari. The Miami upgrade is Ferrari's most focused attempt yet to change that dynamic.

What's Next

April 22 is the key date — the Monza test will determine how ready Ferrari's upgrades are and what refinements may be needed before they arrive in Florida. If the test goes well, Miami on May 3 could mark a genuine shift in the 2026 competitive order. If it reveals issues, Ferrari faces another race weekend trying to maximise a car they already know needs work.
