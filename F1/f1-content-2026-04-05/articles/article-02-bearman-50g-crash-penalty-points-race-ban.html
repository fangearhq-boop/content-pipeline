Bearman's 50G Suzuka Crash: 10 Penalty Points, 2 From a Race Ban, and a Warning F1 Cannot Ignore

By Marcus Cole | F1 Fanrecap | April 5, 2026

Oliver Bearman walked away from the accident. He was lucky. And the fact that he was lucky is, in itself, a problem.

At the Japanese Grand Prix at Suzuka, Bearman's Haas was travelling at 308 kilometres per hour when he encountered a 45km/h closing speed gap with Franco Colapinto's Alpine at the entry to the Spoon Curve. The difference in speed was not caused by an error from either driver. It was caused by the 2026 regulations — by the fact that different manufacturers deploy and harvest battery energy at different points on the circuit, creating invisible, lethal pockets of speed differential.

Bearman swerved onto the grass to avoid a collision with the slower Alpine. He skidded back across the track. He hit the barrier at 50G. He walked away.

The Numbers

Fifty Gs is the kind of force that ends careers and ends lives. Bearman experienced it and stood up. The crash reinforced something the barriers and the halo and the modern safety standards have achieved — but it also revealed the limit of what passive safety can address.

If the speed differential had been slightly larger, or the angle slightly different, the result might not have been a driver walking to the medical car. This is why 93 percent of F1 fans in a PlanetF1 poll called for rule changes after the incident. This is why safety has been placed at the top of the April 9 FIA summit agenda.

The Penalty Points Picture

Separate from the safety question is the regulatory one: Bearman now has 10 penalty points on his licence following the Japanese Grand Prix. At 12, an automatic one-race ban is triggered.

He needs two more points to miss a race — and Miami is the next event on the calendar, beginning May 3. Whether the incidents that drove Bearman's points total to 10 were his fault, or consequences of a regulatory environment that creates dangerous situations, becomes a legitimate philosophical question when the penalty system is in play.

Haas team boss Ayao Komatsu has been measured in his response. He acknowledged the crash and its implications but cautioned that Formula 1 "cannot make a knee-jerk reaction" in changing the regulations. His concern — shared by others in the paddock — is that rapid changes could create new problems before the existing ones are fully understood.

The Root Cause

The 2026 regulations introduced a new power unit format with significant battery-electric components, and with it, a new dynamic: teams and manufacturers make different choices about when to deploy power and when to harvest energy. On a given straight, one car might be on full deployment while another is in harvest mode — and the speed difference between those two states can be dramatic.

Drivers have been raising this concern since the start of the season. The Bearman crash at Suzuka gave those concerns a concrete, visible, undeniable shape. The FIA April 9 summit will look directly at how energy harvesting and deployment can be regulated to reduce, if not eliminate, these dangerous closing speed situations.

What's Next

Bearman continues his 2026 season with Haas, approaching Miami with the knowledge that he is two points from a race ban — and that the FIA is reviewing the regulatory environment that put him in that crash to begin with. The April 9 summit outcome will be closely watched by every driver on the grid. For Bearman, the stakes are more personal than most.
