FIA April 9 Summit: Six Potential Fixes to the 2026 F1 Regulations Revealed

By Ryan Calloway | F1 Fanrecap | April 5, 2026

Four days from now, Formula 1's most consequential off-track meeting of the 2026 season takes place. Team technical chiefs, engine manufacturer representatives, and the heads of the FIA and Formula 1 will gather on April 9 to confront what has become an urgent question: what needs to change about the 2026 regulations, and how quickly can it happen?

The backdrop is not abstract. Oliver Bearman's 50G crash at Suzuka — caused in large part by the dangerous speed differentials that the new energy deployment rules create — put the safety conversation at the front of the queue. Drivers have been raising concerns since the season opener. The April 9 summit is the formal response.

Six Potential Solutions

According to reporting from The Race and other outlets, six possible solutions are currently under evaluation. They include:

The first proposal would raise the super clipping energy limit from its current 250kW to 350kW — the same level available under lift and coast harvesting. This would make super clipping the preferred method of energy recovery, reducing the reliance on lift and coast and significantly cutting the dangerous speed differentials that emerge when different manufacturers harvest at different points on track.

A second proposal calls for reducing the total energy available per lap, from the current 9MJ to 7MJ. This would cost approximately one second per lap in performance, but a further reduction to 6MJ could double that loss. The trade-off is between competitiveness and safety.

A third proposal — described as unconventional — would scrap the fixed activation zones for active aerodynamics entirely, giving teams the freedom to adjust their wing settings at any point on track, similar to the early days of DRS. This could help extend battery life and smooth out the energy deployment patterns that contribute to the speed gaps.

Three additional proposals are under consideration alongside these primary fixes, with the expectation that adjustments in one area could benefit others simultaneously.

The Safety Imperative

The April 9 meeting was always on the calendar — an agreed review point after the opening phase of the 2026 season. But Bearman's crash gave the gathering a sharper edge. A 50G impact at 308km/h, caused by a 45km/h closing speed difference that was a direct product of energy harvesting disparities, is not an acceptable racing incident. It is a regulatory consequence.

Safety is confirmed to be at the top of the April 9 agenda. The FIA has been clear that it wants to respond proportionately — quickly enough to address genuine safety risks, carefully enough to avoid overreacting to the complexity of a new regulatory framework.

The Unanimous Agreement Challenge

Any changes agreed upon at the April 9 summit will require unanimous acceptance from all teams and engine manufacturers before they can be implemented. This is a significant hurdle. Not all manufacturers are affected equally by the current regulation problems, and those who benefit from the current framework have less incentive to support rapid changes.

The goal is to have any agreed fixes ready for implementation by the Miami Grand Prix on May 3 — the fourth round of the 2026 season and the next race following the current five-week break.

What's Next

April 9 will determine whether Formula 1 can agree on a path forward quickly enough to implement meaningful changes before Miami. If the summit produces consensus, the 2026 season could look significantly different from May 3 onward. If it does not, the pressure will only intensify — and the safety conversation will remain open, unresolved, through another race weekend.
