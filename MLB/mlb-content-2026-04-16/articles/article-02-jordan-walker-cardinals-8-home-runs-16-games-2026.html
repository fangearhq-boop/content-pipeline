Jordan Walker Is Doing Something No Cardinal Has Done Since Mark McGwire

By Jake Torres April 16, 2026

The Numbers Are Not a Typo

Jordan Walker leads Major League Baseball with eight home runs through 16 games. He is slashing .333/.394/.767 with a 219 wRC+ and a 97.1 miles-per-hour average exit velocity that leads the entire sport. His hard-hit rate of 70 percent is second-highest among qualified batters. On Tuesday, the Cardinals beat the Cleveland Guardians 5-3, with Walker at the center of the offense again.

Those are not the numbers of a player running hot for a week. That is a historically dominant start to a season by a player who has become the most dangerous hitter in baseball through the first three weeks of April.

The Company He Is Keeping

Walker is now the fourth player in Cardinals franchise history to hit seven or more home runs in the team's first 15 games. The other three on that list are Albert Pujols, Scott Rolen, and Mark McGwire. Those three names represent some of the most accomplished power hitters the Cardinals have ever put on the field, and Walker has joined them before his 24th birthday in what is being called his breakout season.

He also became the first Cardinals outfielder since Ray Lankford in 1997 to have a grand slam, a stolen base, and at least three hits in the same game — a combination that requires both power and athleticism in equal measure.

Where He Was Just One Year Ago

The context around Walker's 2026 performance matters. In his first two major league seasons — spanning 527 at-bats across 2024 and 2025 — Walker hit 11 total home runs and slashed .211/.270/.324. He was selected No. 1 overall in the 2021 MLB Draft and came to the Cardinals with enormous expectations. Those expectations, through two full seasons, had not been met. Questions about his development were becoming louder as spring training began.

Something changed. The exit velocity numbers tell part of the story — 97.1 mph is not a number you generate by accident. The launch angle and fly ball rate tell another part. Walker is hitting the ball harder and elevating it more consistently than at any point in his professional career. Whatever adjustment he made during the offseason or spring training is producing results that belong in the historical record.

What's Next

The Cardinals continue their schedule with Walker as the anchor of the lineup. The question every baseball observer is now asking is whether this pace is sustainable — April samples can be misleading, and pitchers will adjust. But the underlying quality indicators (exit velocity, hard-hit rate, launch angle) suggest this is more than luck. St. Louis has a potential superstar, and the rest of the NL Central is aware of it.
