Lucas Giolito Still Available — Cubs and Padres Circle as Pitching Injuries Mount

By Marcus Cole April 16, 2026

The Market Is Coming to Giolito

Lucas Giolito is still a free agent. That sentence would have surprised most people in November, when the right-hander completed a 3.41 ERA season covering 145 innings for the Boston Red Sox and looked like a reasonable mid-tier starting pitcher on the open market. Now, in mid-April 2026, the surprise has faded — but the opportunity may be arriving.

Pitching injuries have mounted across the league in the early weeks of the season. Multiple contenders are staring at rotation questions they did not anticipate. According to The Athletic, the Chicago Cubs and San Diego Padres are the teams generating the most interest in Giolito, monitoring a situation involving a one-time All-Star who remains available at a price that the right franchise can justify.

What Giolito Brings

At his best — which the 2025 season with Boston represented — Giolito is a veteran innings-eater who limits damage, keeps his team in games, and provides the kind of rotation stability that contenders need through September. His 3.41 ERA across 145 innings was the kind of performance that deserves a multi-year contract in a healthy market.

The complication is arm history. Giolito missed the entire 2024 season following UCL surgery, then dealt with UCL discomfort again at the end of 2025 before the Red Sox shut him down. Teams willing to spend on his market value — estimated around $20.4 million annually according to Spotrac — are also betting that the arm holds up through a full season. That is not a small risk for a multi-year commitment.

Giolito's Perspective

Giolito has been straightforward about his situation in public. He told a podcast that he has been maintaining arm fitness with 75-pitch bullpen sessions while awaiting a deal, and his request is simple: be paid close to his market value. He is not asking for a hometown discount. He is waiting for a team willing to make a real offer on a pitcher who has demonstrated he can contribute at the major league level when healthy.

That combination — clear market value, obvious need across the league, and a pitcher staying ready — typically produces a signing. The Cubs and Padres are not the only teams paying attention, and rotation injuries have a way of accelerating decision timelines.

What's Next

A deal for Giolito could come at any point between now and the July trade deadline, depending on how teams evaluate their pitching depth. For Cubs fans, a mid-rotation upgrade from a name pitcher would be significant. For the Padres, adding Giolito would address a real need. And for Giolito, signing before May would give him a full season to rebuild his market for 2027. The waiting game is almost certainly near its end.
