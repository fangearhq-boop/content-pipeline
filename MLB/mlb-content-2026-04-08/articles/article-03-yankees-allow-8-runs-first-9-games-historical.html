The Yankees Have Allowed 8 Runs Through 9 Games — Matching a Record From 1943

By Ryan Calloway

April 8, 2026

The last time a baseball team started a season allowing as few runs as the 2026 Yankees, the United States was in the middle of World War II.

Through nine games, New York's pitching staff has allowed just 8 runs — a figure that ties the 2002 San Francisco Giants and 1993 Atlanta Braves for the fewest runs surrendered through any team's first nine games in the modern era of baseball. Through five games, they allowed just 3 runs, matching the fewest by any team in the first five games of a season since the 1943 St. Louis Cardinals.

The Yankees are 8-2. Their pitching staff is making history.

Max Fried's Historical Start

The tone was set in the season's first two starts by Max Fried, who pitched 6.1 scoreless innings in his opener and followed it with 7 more scoreless innings in his second start — 13.1 consecutive scoreless innings to begin the year. He became the first Yankee to accomplish that since Mel Stottlemyre, who opened the 1967 season with back-to-back complete-game shutouts.

The context matters: Stottlemyre's back-to-back shutouts in 1967 are legendary. Fried didn't throw complete games, but 13.1 scoreless innings across two starts puts him in that same company — Yankees pitchers who set the tone for a season in extraordinary fashion from the very first week.

Cam Schlittler's Remarkable Run

The other name earning attention is Cam Schlittler, a rookie who wasn't a household name entering the season but opened 2026 in spectacular fashion. His 21-inning scoreless streak — dating back to 2025 — was finally snapped on Tuesday night when Oakland's Nick Kurtz doubled in the 3rd inning. But before that, Schlittler had given the Yankees something rare: a second starter performing at an elite level alongside Fried.

In his first two starts, Schlittler had thrown 11.2 scoreless innings, allowed just 3 hits, walked no one, and struck out 12. He became the first Yankee ever to combine 15+ strikeouts, zero runs allowed, and fewer than 5 baserunners over any two-game span.

Doing It Without Cole and Rodon

Perhaps the most staggering aspect of the Yankees' start is that they've done all of this without their two highest-profile off-season acquisitions. Gerrit Cole is on the injured list. Carlos Rodon is on the injured list. Three of their nine games have been shutouts.

What this means, practically, is that even when those two return, a pitching staff that is already one of the most historically dominant in memory gets significantly better. The Yankees' rotation depth — even without their top two — has been good enough to set records that haven't been touched in 83 years.

What's Next

The Yankees continue their early April schedule with a full week of games that should tell us whether this pace is sustainable or a statistical aberration. History suggests that nobody holds this rate over a full season. But history also suggests that teams with this kind of starting pitching tend to be competitive into October. New York is 8-2, their rotation is healthy (enough), and they are making baseball history in real time.
