Chase Burns (0.82 ERA) and Rhett Lowder (1.30 ERA) Are Making History for the Cincinnati Reds

By Jake Torres

April 8, 2026

The Cincinnati Reds rank last in all of baseball in OPS with runners in scoring position. They are tied for last in runs scored per game. By every offensive measure, they are one of the worst hitting teams in the majors right now.

They have been sweeping opponents anyway.

The reason is two young starting pitchers who are simultaneously posting numbers not seen in over a century.

Chase Burns: 0.82 ERA and a Fastball That Sounds Like a Warning

Through his first two starts of 2026, Chase Burns carries a 0.82 ERA. His average four-seam fastball clocks at 98.3 mph — the third-fastest average velocity among all MLB starters. Against the Texas Rangers in his most recent outing, he struck out 9 batters, induced 21 swings-and-misses, and pitched into the 7th inning for the first time in his big league career.

Burns is doing what the best pitchers in baseball do: he's not just throwing hard, he's spinning it, locating it, and making hitters look uncomfortable at every speed. The early returns suggest a pitcher who has made a significant developmental leap from the 2025 season into 2026.

Rhett Lowder: The Most Historically Rare Start to a Career

If Burns's ERA is impressive, Lowder's is something else entirely. Through eight career starts, Rhett Lowder carries a 1.30 ERA — the lowest ERA through any Reds pitcher's first eight career starts since earned runs became an official statistic in 1913. In the expansion era (since 1961), only four pitchers have begun their careers with a lower ERA: Fernando Valenzuela, Cisco Carlos, Shota Imanaga, and Phil Niekro.

To put Lowder in that company — as a Reds pitcher, in 2026, eight games into his career — is not something that happens often. It may not happen again for a long time.

Winning Despite the Offense

The Reds recently swept the Texas Rangers — a team with one of the better offenses in the American League. They did it by holding the Rangers scoreless or near-scoreless across multiple starts. The formula is straightforward: get six or seven innings from Burns or Lowder, give up one or two runs, and let the offense scratch out enough to win.

For a team with last-place offensive numbers, it's working. The Reds are not a great offensive team. They have two starters carrying ERAs that belong in the record books.

What's Next

The Reds will continue their 2026 season with both arms taking regular turns in the rotation. The question now isn't whether Burns and Lowder are good — the early evidence is unambiguous. It's whether the team around them can eventually provide enough offense to make those starts count in a division race. The early-season answer is yes. The long-season answer will become clearer by June.
