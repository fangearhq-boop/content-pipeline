Mason Miller's 34⅔-Inning Scoreless Streak Ends on a Controversial Umpire Call

By Marcus Cole

April 28, 2026

Mason Miller's historic consecutive scoreless innings streak is over — and the way it ended will generate debate for weeks. The San Diego Padres closer's 34⅔-inning run came to a halt Monday against the Chicago Cubs when home plate umpire Dan Merzel called a visibly foul ball fair, awarding Cubs batter Matt Shaw an infield hit that ended the streak in one of the most controversial moments of the 2026 season so far.

Despite the circumstances, the record stands: Miller owns the Padres franchise record for consecutive scoreless innings, surpassing Cla Meredith's 33⅔-inning mark that had stood since 2006. The Padres still won the game. But the baseball world is understandably frustrated that one of the most compelling individual pitching narratives of the season ended on a blown call.

The Streak in Context

Miller's dominance in 2026 has been nothing short of extraordinary. Across 14.1 innings pitched this season, he owns a 1.26 ERA and a 0.56 WHIP with a 28:2 strikeout-to-walk ratio and 10 saves. The consecutive scoreless innings streak — which dated back to late last season — built steadily throughout the spring as Miller established himself as the most unhittable closer in the National League.

The record Miller was chasing was Gregg Olson's 41 consecutive scoreless innings, set with the Baltimore Orioles in 1988 — the all-time MLB record for a reliever. With the streak ending at 34⅔, Miller finished 6⅓ innings short of that historic mark. The opportunity to break Olson's record, which has stood for 38 years, will have to wait for another time.

The Controversial End

The play that ended the streak was as frustrating as any could be for Miller and Padres fans. Multiple angles confirmed that the ball from Shaw's bat went foul — but Merzel ruled it fair, and the call stood. The infield hit broke the streak. The official MLB review process did not overturn the call.

It is a reminder that even the most elite performances in baseball are subject to the unpredictability of the sport — including the human element of officiating. Miller, traded from the Oakland Athletics in July 2025, has made the most of his opportunity in San Diego and has done nothing but dominate since arriving.

What's Next

Miller's bounce-back performance will be closely watched in the coming days. His track record of sustained excellence suggests a brief stumble will not affect his effectiveness as the Padres push through their NL West schedule. San Diego remains in playoff contention, and Miller's health and performance are central to those prospects.

The streak is over. The legacy of what Miller accomplished — a Padres franchise record and the longest such stretch by any major league reliever in recent memory — is not. He's still the most dominant closer in the National League, and his next outing will have every baseball fan watching.
