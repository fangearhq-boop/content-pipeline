Red Sox Fire Alex Cora, Name Chad Tracy Interim Manager After 10-17 Start

By Ryan Calloway

April 28, 2026

The Boston Red Sox have fired manager Alex Cora and five members of his coaching staff following a disappointing 10-17 start to the 2026 season. The organization named Chad Tracy, previously the manager of Triple-A Worcester, as interim manager. It is the first time the Red Sox have dismissed a manager during an active season since they let go of Jimy Williams in August 2001 — 25 years ago.

The firing sent shockwaves through the clubhouse. Multiple players were reportedly in tears when news broke. Cora, who won the 2018 World Series with Boston in a dominant seven-game series, had been one of the most respected managers in the American League. His dismissal represents a stark reckoning with a season that has gone wrong in ways few anticipated.

What Led to the Decision

Red Sox chief baseball officer Craig Breslow acknowledged in a Sunday morning press conference that the decision was painful but necessary. Also let go alongside Cora were hitting coach Pete Fatse, third-base coach Kyle Hudson, bench coach Ramón Vázquez, assistant hitting coach Dillon Lawson, and Major League hitting strategy coach Joe Cronin.

Boston's 10-17 record through the first month of the season reflects a team that has underperformed across every area. The pitching staff has struggled with consistency, the offense has failed to capitalize on scoring opportunities, and the defense has been unreliable. The cumulative effect created a situation the front office felt required immediate action.

Cora first managed the Red Sox from 2018 to 2019, winning the franchise's most recent World Series title. He was suspended for the 2020 season due to his role in the Houston Astros sign-stealing scandal while he served as a bench coach there. Boston rehired him ahead of the 2021 season, and he managed the club through 2025 before the difficult 2026 start proved to be his undoing.

Chad Tracy Steps In

Tracy's managerial debut under the new circumstances came Sunday against the Baltimore Orioles at Camden Yards. He has extensive experience in the Red Sox organization, having developed players at the Triple-A level in Worcester. Whether Tracy can provide the spark the roster needs — or whether more substantive organizational moves follow — remains to be seen.

The Red Sox entered 2026 with legitimate postseason expectations. A talented roster built for contention was supposed to be the foundation for a playoff push. Instead, Boston joins the New York Mets and Philadelphia Phillies as the most prominent disappointments of the early 2026 season — and now they are doing what the Mets have so far resisted: making a decisive in-season managerial change.

What's Next

The Red Sox have 130+ games remaining in 2026. Tracy's first challenge is establishing a winning culture and stopping the organizational freefall. Whether a new manager can arrest the slide depends on whether the underlying talent performs to its potential — something it has not done through the first month of the season.

The AL East remains competitive, and Boston has the roster to compete for a Wild Card spot if the players respond to a new voice. That response begins now. With Fenway Park and an expectant fan base waiting, the Red Sox under Tracy have everything to prove and very little margin for continued failure.
