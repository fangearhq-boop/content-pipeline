Jordan Walker Was Losing the Home Run Derby. Then He Hit Six in a Row.

By Jake Torres | Ballpark Banter | July 15, 2026

At some point during Sunday night's Home Run Derby at Citizens Bank Park, the crowd started turning. Kyle Schwarber — a Philadelphia Phillie, a Philly native by adoption, the guy who leads baseball in home runs — had built an 11-6 lead over Jordan Walker in the championship round with just three swings remaining.

The crowd could smell it. The hometown favorite was about to win the Derby in his home park, in front of his home fans, in the most anticipated All-Star week Philadelphia had hosted in 30 years.

Then Walker hit six in a row.

The Comeback

Walker was trailing 11-6 when he entered his final three regulation swings. He hit three more home runs to tie it at 11-11 and force a bonus period. Then he hit three more in the bonus, finishing with 12 total to Schwarber's 11.

Final: Walker 12, Schwarber 11.

It was the kind of finish the Derby rarely produces — a genuine comeback in a format that usually produces steady output rather than dramatic swings. Walker, 24 years old, pulled it off in Schwarber's home stadium, against the MLB home run leader, in front of a crowd that was absolutely not pulling for him.

The Historical Context

Walker became the first Cardinals player to win the Home Run Derby since the event was established in 1985. That's a list that includes Mark McGwire, Albert Pujols, Matt Holliday, and Marcell Ozuna — none of them won it. Walker did.

The $1 million prize he took home exceeded his entire 2026 salary with the Cardinals. He was asked about the win afterward and told ESPN that he hoped his performance would inspire Black kids to take up baseball — a statement that resonated well beyond the event itself and drew national attention the next morning.

For what it's worth on the rest of the field: Alejandro Caminero hit a 491-foot home run — the longest of the night — and still lost to Walker in the semifinals, 6-5. Bryce Harper, the local legend and 2018 Derby champion, was eliminated in the first round with 8 home runs, losing to Schwarber 10-8.

What's Next

Walker returns to the Cardinals outfield for the second half of the 2026 season with a new trophy and a new story attached to his name. He's 24 years old. He just out-homered the MLB home run leader in a championship round at one of the most hostile settings the Derby has ever produced.

The Cardinals don't open the second half as a postseason favorite. But Walker enters it as someone who performed in the biggest moment the weekend had to offer — and then some.
