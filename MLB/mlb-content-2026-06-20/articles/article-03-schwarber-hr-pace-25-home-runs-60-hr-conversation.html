The 60-HR Conversation: Schwarber at 25 and a Historic Pace That Demands Attention

By Ryan Calloway | Ballpark Banter | June 20, 2026

The number 60 has not been a realistic point of discussion in a single-season home run conversation in a long time. Kyle Schwarber is making it one.

The Philadelphia Phillies designated hitter leads all of baseball with 25 home runs in 2026 — the first player in the major leagues to reach that mark this season. Through 315 plate appearances, Schwarber is batting .249 with 43 RBIs and 46 walks. The strikeouts are there, the walks are there, and the home runs are very much there. His pace for the full season projects to 65 or more home runs, a number that places him in company so rarefied it stops the conversation cold.

Only two players in MLB history have hit more than 65 home runs in a single season. Barry Bonds hit 73 in 2001. Mark McGwire hit 70 in 1998. That is the entire list. Schwarber is currently pacing to sit just behind both of them.

What the Numbers Actually Say

Schwarber's 25 home runs in 315 plate appearances translate to a rate of one home run every 12.6 plate appearances. Extended over a full 700-plate-appearance season, that rate produces a projection in the neighborhood of 65 to 66 home runs — which would tie or surpass Sammy Sosa's 66 from 1998 and land Schwarber at No. 3 on the all-time single-season list.

Projections have limits. Players get hot, players get cold. Pitchers make adjustments. Injuries happen. The second half of any season is full of variables that projections cannot account for. All of that is true, and none of it changes the mathematical reality of where Schwarber is sitting today — the halfway point of the 2026 season has not yet arrived, and he already has 25 home runs.

For context, the players who finished a season with 60 or more home runs were almost always at or above this pace at this point on the calendar. Schwarber is running right alongside that history.

Alvarez Is Watching

The home run race has company. Yordan Alvarez of the Houston Astros has been the closest pursuer all season, a generational power hitter who has matched Schwarber homer for homer in stretches. Alvarez trailing Schwarber is not a sign of weakness — it is a sign of how extraordinary Schwarber's pace has been.

The two play in different leagues, which means head-to-head matchups are limited to the All-Star events in July. That divergence has shaped the home run race into something more like a dual pursuit than a direct competition — both players chasing history simultaneously, neither able to look directly at the other's work until Philadelphia in July.

If Schwarber continues to pace at 65-plus, the question becomes whether Alvarez can close the gap or whether the race stays where it is now: Schwarber alone at the front, Alvarez close enough to keep the pressure on.

Citizens Bank Park in July

The Home Run Derby is July 13 at Citizens Bank Park in Philadelphia. Schwarber's home ballpark.

The setup is almost too good. The player leading baseball in home runs, in the middle of one of the most discussed individual offensive pursuits in years, competing in a home run hitting contest in the stadium where he has spent years launching balls into the left-field seats — in front of a Phillies crowd that will make the roof rattle for every swing he takes.

A player in Schwarber's position, with the Derby coming to his home park, almost certainly participates. If he does, it will be one of the most anticipated Derby appearances in recent memory.

The Phillies as a team sit at 30-28, nine-plus games behind the Atlanta Braves (46-27) in the NL East. The standings picture is challenging. The individual story is not.

What's Next

Schwarber's immediate target is 30 home runs before the All-Star break — a milestone no player has reached in the pre-break stretch in years. At his current rate, it is not a reach. It is a genuine possibility inside the next three weeks.

The Home Run Derby on July 13 provides the theatrical midseason showcase for everything the Schwarber story has built toward. After that, the second half of the season begins and the real question takes shape: can he sustain this pace long enough to make 60, 65, or something beyond that a legitimate conversation at the finish line?

Alvarez will not slow down. The competition is real. But the 60-home-run conversation belongs to Schwarber right now, and it is only getting louder.
