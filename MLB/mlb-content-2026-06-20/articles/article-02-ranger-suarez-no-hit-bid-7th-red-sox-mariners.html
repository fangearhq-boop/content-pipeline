Ranger Suarez Takes No-Hitter Into the 7th, Red Sox Top Mariners 6-2

By Jake Torres | Ballpark Banter | June 20, 2026

Ranger Suarez had T-Mobile Park buzzing — or more precisely, going quiet — in all the right ways Friday night.

The Boston Red Sox left-hander carried a no-hit bid into the seventh inning against the Seattle Mariners before Josh Naylor lined a one-out double to break up the gem. Suarez finished with 6⅔ innings of one-hit ball, striking out five and walking three, as Boston rolled to a 6-2 victory. It was Suarez's first win since April 27 against the Toronto Blue Jays — nearly two months between victories — and it came with the kind of dominant performance that makes the wait feel irrelevant.

For seven innings' worth of baseball, Ranger Suarez looked like the best pitcher in the American League.

How the No-Hit Bid Unfolded

Suarez was locked in from the opening pitch. He retired the first 10 Mariners batters he faced — a clean run through Seattle's lineup that had the crowd at T-Mobile Park aware of exactly what they were watching by the time the fourth inning arrived.

The first crack in the no-hitter came with a walk in the fourth inning, breaking the perfecto bid while keeping the no-hitter intact. It is the kind of small distinction that matters enormously in the moment: a walk ends the perfect game conversation, but the no-hit bid lived on. Suarez got back to work.

Through six innings, he had surrendered nothing but that walk. Zero hits. A Seattle lineup that had scored runs all season was chasing Suarez's stuff without much to show for it.

The seventh inning began the same way. One out, still no hits. Then Naylor turned on a pitch and laced it into the gap for a double. The stadium sound shifted — the held-breath silence that comes with a no-hit bid replaced by the ordinary noise of a baseball game. The bid was over at 18 outs.

Suarez finished the inning, exited after 6⅔, and handed a 6-2 lead to the bullpen. Boston closed it out from there.

Suarez at 30 — Still One of the Game's Better Lefties

There is something worth noting about what Suarez did Friday beyond the no-hit drama. The 30-year-old Venezuelan left-hander has never been a name that generates the same attention as some of Boston's other arms, but his profile is genuinely interesting. He mixes his pitches with feel rather than raw velocity, works the corners, and induces weak contact when he's locating well.

Friday night, he was locating exceptionally well. The final line — 6⅔ innings, one hit, five strikeouts, three walks — understates how dominant the start felt in real time. The three walks were the blemish, but Suarez worked out of trouble each time and never allowed Seattle to put a crooked number on the board.

The win ended a stretch of nearly two months between victories. Whether that gap reflects poor run support, a few tough-luck outings, or something in Suarez's approach that he's since corrected is a question worth watching in his next few starts. Friday offered a strong piece of evidence that he's figured something out.

Seattle's Quiet Night

The two runs the Mariners eventually scored came after the no-hit bid was over — a footnote performance against a bullpen managing the final innings. For a Seattle offense that has had stretches of genuine production in 2026, Friday night was a reminder that the right pitcher can make anyone look ordinary.

The Mariners got no-hit into the seventh inning at their own ballpark. That is not a comfortable headline for a team looking to stay relevant in the AL West race. Seattle's offensive struggles in this game were complete — 10 consecutive outs to open the game, then five more innings of nothing before Naylor finally found a barrel.

What's Next

Suarez's next start becomes one of the more interesting appointments on Boston's schedule. Friday's outing was his best of the season by any measure — the kind of start that can kick-start a run of consistent pitching if the momentum carries. For a Red Sox rotation that needs depth behind its frontline arms, a Suarez who looks like Friday-night Suarez is significant.

Boston's second-half positioning is also worth watching. A win like Friday's — on the road, dominant, against a team in the AL West race — is exactly the kind of game that matters when the calendar turns toward August and the standings tighten.

For the Mariners, the offensive struggles are a storyline that will not go away after one rough night. Seattle needs its lineup to show up consistently in the second half, and a game where they were no-hit through six innings is not the kind of performance that inspires confidence.

Ranger Suarez went to Seattle on Friday night and nearly made history. He walked away with a win instead. For a pitcher who hadn't won since late April, that's more than enough.
