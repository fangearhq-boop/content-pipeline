Glasnow to 60-Day IL: The Dodgers Are Running a Rotation Without Two of Their Aces

By Jake Torres June 8, 2026

The Problem With Los Angeles' Rotation

The Los Angeles Dodgers built one of the deepest pitching staffs in baseball this offseason. The expectation was that the combination of Shohei Ohtani, Tyler Glasnow, Blake Snell, and the supporting cast behind them would give the Dodgers an almost unfair advantage in the National League West. The expectation has not matched the reality.

Glasnow was officially shifted to the 60-day injured list on Saturday after a back injury that first appeared in early May has not cleared him to resume a throwing program. Manager Dave Roberts offered a blunt assessment: "I think he wants to get cranking again, but then the doctors just are not allowing for it and the body is not allowing for it." Snell has been unavailable since needing bone spur surgery. The rotation that was supposed to dominate is now running on Ohtani and whatever depth the organization can provide.

Glasnow's Timeline and What Comes Next

The earliest Glasnow can return under IL rules is July 6. That date, however, is a floor — not a projection. Roberts' language on Saturday suggested that even the July 6 timeline carries uncertainty. The key step that must happen before Glasnow can begin working toward a return is medical clearance to resume throwing, which had not been granted as of the announcement.

The Dodgers made a corresponding move by adding pitcher Nick Frasso to the 40-man roster, acquired in a trade with the Chicago Cubs. Frasso will remain in Triple-A Oklahoma City for now, but his addition to the 40-man gives Los Angeles flexibility as they manage the roster around these absences.

How the Dodgers Are Staying Afloat

The obvious answer is Shohei Ohtani. The two-way superstar is pitching at an ERA that belongs in the history books — a mark that ranks among the best for starting pitchers in the modern era. He has been the unquestioned anchor of a rotation that would otherwise be in crisis, and his performance is the primary reason the Dodgers are 42-23 despite missing two starting pitchers of Glasnow and Snell's caliber.

Beyond Ohtani, the Dodgers have leaned on their depth system — a collection of arms developed or acquired for exactly these situations. The organization's investment in pitching depth over multiple seasons is now paying off in a practical sense: they have enough bodies to fill innings even when the headliners are unavailable.

The Question the Rest of the NL Is Asking

The Dodgers are 42-23. They are in first place in the NL West. By the traditional measures, the rotation crisis has not yet manifested in the standings. But baseball seasons do not end in June, and the wear on a rotation — especially one leaning on a single elite arm to carry the load — has a way of accumulating over time.

The rest of the National League West is watching. The Braves and other NL contenders are aware that a healthy Dodgers rotation is a more formidable opponent than what Los Angeles is currently fielding. If Glasnow and Snell return in July, the arithmetic in the pennant races changes. If they don't, the path through October for Los Angeles runs almost entirely through Ohtani.

What's Next

The Dodgers' next key update on Glasnow will come when — or if — he receives medical clearance to begin throwing again. Without that step, July 6 is an optimistic baseline rather than a firm target. Snell's recovery timeline remains separate and will be updated as the summer progresses. For now, Ohtani carries the load, and the Dodgers hold their breath on the injury reports.
