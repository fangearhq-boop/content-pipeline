Judge Down. Murakami Down. Schwarber Leads the 2026 HR Race Alone.

By Marcus Cole June 8, 2026

One Player Left Standing

For most of the first two months of the 2026 season, the home run race had three names worth following: Kyle Schwarber of the Philadelphia Phillies, Aaron Judge of the New York Yankees, and Munetaka Murakami of the Chicago White Sox. Three different stories. Three different paces. Three different reasons to pay attention.

As of this week, two of those names are on the injured list. Judge went down with a right rib stress fracture on June 6. Murakami has been out since approximately May 31 with a Grade 2 hamstring strain. Both face timelines measured in weeks, not days. Both will return — eventually. But right now, Kyle Schwarber is alone at the top of the 2026 home run leaderboard, and the runway in front of him is open.

The Numbers That Got Him Here

On May 15, Kyle Schwarber hit his 20th home run of the season. The date matters: May 15 is the earliest any player in Major League Baseball history had reached 20 home runs in a single season. The previous record fell. The pace required to match it is extraordinary.

Schwarber has continued adding to his total since that milestone. The trajectory puts him on pace for a season total that would comfortably exceed 50 home runs — and depending on how the second half goes, the range could extend toward territory that is genuinely rare in baseball history.

The 60-HR Club and What It Actually Means

The 60-home run club is baseball's most exclusive territory. It has been reached only four times in the sport's history: Barry Bonds with 73 in 2001, Mark McGwire with 70 in 1998 and 65 in 1999, and Sammy Sosa with 66 in 1998, 64 in 2001, and 63 in 1999. Three players. No one since 2001. The distances involved make 60 home runs a number that requires sustained, elite power production over the course of a full season.

Schwarber is not there yet. The mathematical path to 60 requires keeping the pace alive through August and September — months when pitchers have adjusted, slumps happen, and the grind of a full season starts to wear on every hitter. Historical pace races rarely end where they begin in June. What matters now is the foundation he has built and the advantage that early lead provides.

The Irony of an Open Road

There is something unusual about the way Schwarber has taken the lead: not by outrunning his competitors, but by watching them get sidelined. Judge was chasing him. Murakami, the White Sox rookie sensation, was one of the most compelling individual stories in the sport. Now both are on the IL, and Schwarber adds to his total every night against a leaderboard with no one immediately behind him.

Historic home run seasons have always been better when they involved a race. Bonds in 2001 had context. McGwire in 1998 had Sosa. What Schwarber faces now is something different: the race against history itself, with no active rival to define the urgency. Whether that makes the pursuit more or less compelling depends on what happens over the next four months.

What's Next

Judge is expected to return in late July at the earliest. Murakami's timeline from a Grade 2 hamstring strain suggests a return in late June or early July. When both return — and when other AL and NL sluggers potentially heat up — the home run race will have more participants. Until then, every Schwarber at-bat is a chance to pad the lead against history.
