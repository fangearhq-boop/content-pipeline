Masyn Winn Delivers First Career Walk-Off as Cardinals Edge Mets 2-1 in 11 Innings

By Jake Torres | April 2, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

The ball blooped off Masyn Winn's bat and dropped into right field just in front of a diving Carson Benge. JJ Wetherholt, the automatic runner at third base with two outs in the bottom of the 11th inning, scored without breaking stride. Cardinals win 2-1. Series win for St. Louis. First career walk-off RBI for the 23-year-old shortstop.

It was not elegant. It was not a towering home run or a line drive that rattled off the wall. It was a bloop single — the kind of hit that frustrates pitchers and defenses in equal measure, the kind that looks like luck until you realize the hitter made contact in a two-out, extra-inning situation and put the ball where it needed to go.

The result was the same: Cardinals 4-2 on the season, winners of their first two series of 2026, and the Mets heading home to New York with a 3-3 record and a RISP problem that is becoming impossible to ignore.

The Play, the Moment, and the Series

The Cardinals had played clean baseball for nine innings. Matthew Liberatore started and went six innings, allowing one run on three hits with two strikeouts and one walk — a controlled, efficient outing from a lefthander who handled a deep Mets lineup with precision. The Cardinals tied the game in the later innings and sent it to extras.

Wetherholt, one of baseball's most exciting young players, was placed at third base as the automatic runner to open the 11th. Two outs, no margin for error. Winn put a pitch into right field and it fell. Walk-off. Series complete.

Gordon Graceffo earned the win in relief. The Cardinals bullpen held the Mets scoreless through the extra frames. Juan Soto had hit a solo home run in the sixth inning for New York's only run — the kind of individual offensive production that the Mets have come to rely on and that the team around him is not yet matching.

The Mets' RISP Problem

The numbers tell a troubling story for New York. The Mets went 0-for-11 with runners in scoring position in Wednesday's finale. Over the course of the three-game series against St. Louis, they went 1-for-29 with RISP. Through six games on the season, the team is batting .162 with runners in scoring position.

This is a lineup built to score runs. Soto is a generational offensive talent. The surrounding pieces were assembled with the explicit goal of providing run production depth. Six games does not make a trend — but 1-for-29 in a three-game series is the kind of stretch that can shape how a season is perceived before it really begins.

The Mets are 3-3. They have time to fix it. But April is when habits form, and right now the habit is leaving runners stranded.

A Cardinals Team Worth Watching

St. Louis has won its first two series of 2026 and is 4-2 with contributions from different players each night. The Cardinals have not needed any one player to carry them — Liberatore pitched six quality innings, Winn delivered the walk-off hit, and the bullpen protected the lead. That kind of team baseball is what Cardinals fans have come to expect, and what opposing teams have come to fear.

Winn's emergence as a walk-off threat at shortstop adds another dimension to a lineup that was already deeper than it looked on paper heading into the season. At 23, he has already shown the kind of clutch instincts that take most players years to develop.

What's Next

The Cardinals will continue building on their strong start as they move into a second week of play. Winn's walk-off moment will likely be the play of the early season in St. Louis, and the confidence that comes from winning tight games tends to compound over time.

The Mets will need to address their RISP struggles quickly. A 3-3 start is manageable. A RISP line of .162 through the first week is a warning sign that needs to become a conversation before it becomes a pattern.
