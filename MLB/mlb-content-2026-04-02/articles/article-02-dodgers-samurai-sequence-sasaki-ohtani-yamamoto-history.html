Sasaki, Ohtani, Yamamoto: The Dodgers' Samurai Sequence Made MLB History

By Jake Torres | April 2, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

For three days in Los Angeles, baseball witnessed something it had never seen before. Monday: Roki Sasaki. Tuesday: Shohei Ohtani. Wednesday: Yoshinobu Yamamoto. Three consecutive games. Three starting pitchers. All born in Japan.

Per the Elias Sports Bureau, it has never happened in Major League Baseball history. A franchise has never started three straight games with Japanese-born pitchers. The Dodgers just did it in the opening week of the 2026 season, and the "Samurai Sequence" — as it has been called — is officially in the record books.

The Cleveland Guardians won Wednesday's finale 4-1, ending the series. But what the Dodgers accomplished transcends a single game result, or even a three-game series. What happened this week in Los Angeles was a statement about the global reach of baseball and the unprecedented collection of international talent that the Dodgers have assembled.

Three Starts, Three Lines

Sasaki opened the sequence Monday and gave the Dodgers a quality start, establishing the tone. Tuesday belonged to Ohtani, who delivered one of the most efficient outings of the early season: six innings, one hit, zero runs, six strikeouts, 87 pitches. The Dodgers won 4-1. It was the kind of performance that reminded everyone why Ohtani's return to the mound was the most anticipated event of the 2026 season.

Yamamoto closed it out Wednesday. He went six innings, allowing two earned runs on four hits with two strikeouts. He induced key double plays with his signature splitter and escaped a jam in the sixth inning. The Guardians scored on a Daniel Schneemann double that was aided by an error and a Gabriel Arias solo home run, but Yamamoto kept his team in the game through six.

The Guardians took the finale 4-1. The Dodgers are 4-2 and have demonstrated, in three days, that their starting pitching depth is unlike anything in baseball.

Ohtani's Other Numbers

While the historic sequence dominated the narrative, Ohtani's individual numbers deserve their own acknowledgment. His scoreless pitching streak stands at 22.2 innings to open the 2026 season — the longest active streak in Major League Baseball. He has not allowed an earned run in a regular season start this year.

As a hitter, Ohtani has reached base in 36 consecutive games — the longest active on-base streak in the majors. These are the kinds of numbers that define Hall of Fame careers. Ohtani is posting them simultaneously, as a two-way player, in what is supposed to be the most demanding season of his career.

The 2026 season was described, before it started, as the year Ohtani would attempt a full two-way workload for the first time in years. Through the first week, he is doing exactly that — and doing it at an elite level on both sides of the ball.

The Larger Significance

The Sasaki-Ohtani-Yamamoto sequence is notable for the record. But it also reflects something broader about the evolution of the sport. Japanese baseball has been sending elite players to the majors for decades — but never at this volume, and never to the same team at the same time.

All three pitchers are legitimate rotation anchors. None of them is a project or a fill-in. The Dodgers built this rotation intentionally, signing and posting each of these players with the understanding that they were acquiring three of the best pitchers in the world. The payoff, this week, was a historic footnote and a three-game glimpse at what that investment looks like in action.

What's Next

The Dodgers will continue rotation through their schedule and set up for the next time through the order. Ohtani's next mound start will again be appointment viewing — with a 22.2-inning scoreless streak on the line, every start carries heightened stakes.

The question for the Dodgers is not whether the talent is there. It is whether the three-peat bid can hold together over 162 games. The Samurai Sequence gave them a historic start. Now the real work begins.
