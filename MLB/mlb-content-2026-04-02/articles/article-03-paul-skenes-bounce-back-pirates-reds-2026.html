After a Nightmare Opening Day, Paul Skenes Returns to Form Against the Reds

By Ryan Calloway | April 2, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

The 2025 National League Cy Young Award winner lasted 0.2 innings in his 2026 debut. Five runs, 37 pitches, and a pulled early exit that felt like the kind of start that follows a pitcher for weeks. That was Paul Skenes on Opening Day against the Mets.

Six days later, he went out against the Cincinnati Reds and looked like himself again.

Five innings. One earned run. Five strikeouts. 77 pitches, 51 of them strikes. The Pittsburgh Pirates won 8-3. The redemption arc, at least for now, is written.

What Happened on Opening Day — and Why It Didn't Stick

The Opening Day disaster against the Mets requires context. Skenes walked into an inning that spiraled partly through error-aided chaos. The Mets scored five runs on a combination of hits, walks, and Pittsburgh defensive miscues that compounded quickly. Skenes was pulled after recording just two outs.

For most pitchers, that kind of outing triggers legitimate concern. For Skenes, the concern was real but the perspective was clear: this is a pitcher with a 2.10 career ERA through 57 starts — the fourth-best mark by any pitcher in Major League Baseball since 1920. He is the only pitcher in MLB history, per records going back to 1913, to post a sub-2.00 ERA and 175 or more strikeouts in his first 25 major league starts. One bad outing, however ugly, does not redefine that resume.

He is also the reigning Cy Young winner, finishing 2025 with a 1.97 ERA and 216 strikeouts in a 10-10 season that demonstrated how little wins and losses reflect individual pitcher performance.

The Bounce-Back by the Numbers

Against the Reds, Skenes went back to what made him dominant in 2025. His fastball had its velocity, his command was sharp, and he induced soft contact when he needed it. Five strikeouts, one walk, and one run through five innings on a pitch count that allowed the Pirates to manage his workload responsibly coming off a short Opening Day outing.

Skenes is now 5-0 with a 0.53 ERA in career starts against Cincinnati. The Reds, despite being a team with legitimate offensive weapons, have not solved him. This may be a matchup the Pirates look forward to scheduling every season.

Oneil Cruz provided the offensive support, hitting his third home run in two games — a three-run shot that gave Pittsburgh the cushion they needed. Cruz has been one of the more dangerous hitters in the early going, and his presence in the lineup behind Skenes gives Pittsburgh a real chance to compete on any given night.

The Season Ahead for Skenes

The broader question for Skenes in 2026 is not whether he is capable of dominant performances. He answered that Tuesday. The question is whether the Pirates can support him consistently enough that his season-end numbers reflect the quality of his pitching.

A 10-10 record with a 1.97 ERA last year was the clearest possible demonstration of how little a pitcher controls his win-loss record. If the offense and defense hold up behind him in 2026, the NL Cy Young conversation could start with his name again in October.

What's Next

Skenes will line up for his next start on regular rest. The Pittsburgh rotation is built around him, and after the bounce-back against Cincinnati, the Pirates have the kind of momentum they need heading into the second week of the season.

The Mets will get another crack at Skenes later in the schedule. That rematch will be worth watching.
