### STORY 1: Dodgers Shut Out Cubs 6-0

#### Long-Form Post
Window: 8:00 AM ET

The defending World Series champions just reminded everyone who runs the National League.

Sunday night in Los Angeles, the Dodgers shut out the Cubs 6-0 to win their three-game series — and in doing so, ended Chicago's MLB-best 10-game winning streak.

This wasn't just a loss. This was a statement.

Justin Wrobleski was outstanding: six scoreless innings, multiple traffic jams navigated, zero damage. And then Shohei Ohtani put the exclamation point on the night in the seventh inning with his sixth home run of the season — a 109.8 mph laser that lit up the Dodger Stadium scoreboard.

The Cubs, for their part, played exactly the wrong way to end a winning streak. They left 12 runners on base. They went 0-for-8 with runners in scoring position. When you get those kinds of opportunities against the Dodgers and can't convert, the result is a shutout loss.

But here's the context: The Cubs went 10-0 on a run that included beating the defending champions twice. They're 16-9 and still very much in the NL Central race. This loss hurts, but it doesn't define their season.

What it does tell us is that the NL's two best teams — the Dodgers and the Cubs — are going to see a lot of each other in October, assuming both get there.

NL West? Dodgers 18-8, tied with San Diego. NL Central? Reds and Cubs tied at 16-9. The NL is a mess right now, and that's exactly what makes it must-watch baseball.

### STORY 2: Murakami Historic Start / Extension Question

#### Long-Form Post
Window: 10:30 AM ET

The question circling the Chicago White Sox organization right now: What do you do with Munetaka Murakami?

Here's the situation. Murakami signed a 2-year, $34 million deal before the 2026 season — widely viewed as an aggressive ask by his reps. Three weeks into his MLB career, that contract looks like the steal of the century.

Through 25 games:
→ 11 home runs (tied for the MLB lead with Yordan Alvarez)
→ .394 OBP, .992 OPS
→ 19 RBIs
→ Most HRs by a Japanese-born player through 24 MLB games in baseball history

He homered in five consecutive games when he first got going — tying an MLB rookie record. He and teammate Colson Montgomery became the first pair of teammates in MLB history to each homer in four consecutive games simultaneously.

And now the White Sox (10-15) have reportedly NOT initiated any extension talks.

On one hand, that makes business sense — you don't lock someone up after 25 games. On the other hand, with each passing week, Murakami's market value only increases. If he sustains this pace, a deal that looked expensive in February would look like a bargain by the All-Star Break.

The White Sox are rebuilding. Murakami is the first sign that the rebuild might be arriving ahead of schedule. How they handle the next 18 months of his contract could define the organization's next decade.

Baseball's most interesting roster management story is happening on the South Side of Chicago.

### STORY 3: Mason Miller's Record Chase

#### Long-Form Post
Window: 12:30 PM ET

You might have missed this one amid all the big headlines. That would be a mistake.

Mason Miller, the San Diego Padres' closer, has now thrown 34 2/3 consecutive scoreless innings — a Padres franchise record. But the more significant number is the one he's chasing:

41.

That's the all-time MLB record for consecutive scoreless innings by a reliever — set by Gregg Olson of the Baltimore Orioles back in 1988. That record is 36 years old and has survived every dominant closer the sport has produced since.

Miller is 6 1/3 innings away.

The 27-year-old righty — who was traded from the Oakland Athletics to San Diego last July — has been virtually untouchable: 0.00 ERA, 0.35 WHIP, 27 strikeouts in just 11.1 innings this season. And the streak doesn't just cover 2026. It dates back into last season, which makes the consistency even more remarkable.

Context for the record: At a typical closer's pace (one inning per appearance, 3-4 appearances per week), Miller is perhaps 6-7 more outings away from breaking the record.

Nobody in baseball has been more dominant over the last year. The record just needs a few more zeros on the scoreboard.

### STORY 4: Paul Skenes' Near-Perfect Game

#### Long-Form Post
Window: 3:00 PM ET

On Friday night in Pittsburgh, Paul Skenes put on one of the best pitching performances of the 2026 season.

Twenty consecutive batters sat down. Twenty. One through six, through the order twice, and halfway through a third time — all without a hit, a walk, or any trouble.

Then Jake Bauers stepped up in the seventh inning with two outs and ripped a single to center field on the 20th at-bat Skenes had faced.

The perfect game was gone.

But Skenes kept going, finished the seventh, and the Pirates won 6-0 in a one-hit shutout. His final line: 7 innings, 1 hit, 0 runs, 0 walks, 7 strikeouts on 93 pitches. A masterpiece.

This is the same Paul Skenes who allowed five runs before recording two outs on Opening Day against the Mets. He was roughed up in his season debut, then bounced back like a reigning Cy Young Award winner does — which he is.

Skenes is 3-1 with a 3.27 ERA. The Pirates are 14-11. In the NL Central, that's alive. Pittsburgh hasn't been a legitimate contender in years. With Skenes at the top of the rotation, they might actually be this year.

The next time Skenes takes the mound, keep an eye on those 20 consecutive batters. He very nearly made history. He might get another chance.

#### Long-Form Post
Window: 3:00 PM ET

It's the home run race nobody expected.

Yordan Alvarez (Houston Astros): 11 home runs, .347 batting average, 26 RBIs, 1.245 OPS. Playing for a 10-16 team.

Munetaka Murakami (Chicago White Sox): 11 home runs, .394 OBP, .992 OPS, 19 RBIs. Playing for a 10-15 team.

Through roughly 25 games of the 2026 season, two of the most prolific hitters in baseball are locked in a dead heat for the major-league home run lead. And both of them are doing it while their teams struggle around them.

Alvarez's numbers tie Lance Berkman's Astros franchise record for home runs through 26 games. He's the best hitter in baseball, doing it with peak efficiency on a club that can't hold its own in the AL West.

Murakami is making history as the most productive Japanese-born player through his first 24 major-league games — ever. His first season in America has been everything the White Sox front office hoped for and more. Even if his team hasn't matched the production.

The question going forward: Can either team get things turned around before their star slugger's efforts feel completely wasted?

For now, just enjoy the race. Through April, it doesn't get better than this.
