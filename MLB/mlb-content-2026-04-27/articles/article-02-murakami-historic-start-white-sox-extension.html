Murakami Is Making History Every Week — and the White Sox Don't Have an Extension Deal Yet

By Jake Torres | April 27, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

Through 25 games of his MLB career, Munetaka Murakami is putting together one of the most remarkable debut months any player has had in baseball history.

Eleven home runs. Tied with Yordan Alvarez for the major-league lead. A .394 on-base percentage. A .992 OPS. Nineteen RBIs. And a résumé of historical firsts that gets longer by the week: most home runs by a Japanese-born player through 24 MLB games ever; first rookie to homer in five consecutive games (matching the MLB rookie record); first player alongside teammate Colson Montgomery to homer in four consecutive games simultaneously — a combination never before seen in MLB history.

And the Chicago White Sox have not started extension talks.

The Contract Situation

Murakami signed a two-year, $34 million deal before the 2026 season — a contract widely viewed as aggressive at the time. The White Sox were coming off a historic losing season. Murakami was a star in Japan's Nippon Professional Baseball, but no one had seen what he could do against major-league pitching.

Now we know. And the $34 million deal looks like one of baseball's greatest bargains.

Multiple reports confirmed this week that the White Sox have not initiated extension discussions with Murakami. From a pure business standpoint, that's understandable — you don't lock a player up after 25 games on a multi-year extension without seeing what the full season looks like. But there's a counter-argument: with each passing week of this production, Murakami's asking price in any future negotiation only goes up.

If Murakami finishes the year anywhere near this pace — even if he slows significantly in the second half — the White Sox will face a decision on whether to extend him through his arbitration years and potentially lock him up long-term, or let the market determine his value.

The HR Race and Team Context

Tied with Alvarez for the MLB lead at 11 home runs, Murakami is carrying the White Sox offense almost entirely. Chicago is 10-15 — tied with the Astros for the worst record among teams with a slugger putting up top-three MVP-level numbers.

That's the strange paradox of Murakami's spring. He arrived as the hope for a rebuilding franchise. He's overdelivered on every projection. And yet the White Sox are still losing games because the supporting cast isn't where Murakami is. Montgomery has been terrific (homering in four straight games himself), but the rest of the lineup and the pitching staff remain works in progress.

Murakami is the acceleration the White Sox rebuild needed. The question is whether the organization builds around him fast enough to take advantage of his peak years.

What's Next

The White Sox continue their 2026 campaign this week with Murakami in the lineup every day. At some point, extension talks will begin — the only question is when and at what price. For now, Murakami is focused on the one thing he can control: hitting baseballs very far, very hard, and very often. He's doing exactly that, and the baseball world is watching.
