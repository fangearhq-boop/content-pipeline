MLB Week 4 Standings: The NL Central Is Doing Something It's Never Done Before

By Ryan Calloway | April 27, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

The MLB Power Rankings for the week of April 27 are out, and the early-season standings picture confirms what fans watching daily have suspected: this is an unusually chaotic, unusually entertaining start to a baseball season.

Here's the state of baseball through roughly 26-27 games, division by division.

NL Central: Historic

For the first time in the Divisional Era — which dates back to 1969 — all five National League Central teams are simultaneously over .500. The Reds and Cubs are tied atop the division at 16-9. The Cardinals are 14-10. The Pirates are 14-11. Even the Brewers are 13-11.

The combined record of NL Central teams: 73-48 (.603 winning percentage). That means these five teams are beating the rest of baseball at a collective rate usually reserved for one or two elite clubs.

The implications are significant. Postseason Wild Card spots fill up fast when an entire division is performing at this level. Teams from the NL East or NL West that slip for even a month could find themselves scrambling for October access by June. This division is eating everyone's lunch right now.

NL West: A Dead Heat

The Los Angeles Dodgers (17-8) and San Diego Padres (17-8) are tied for the NL West lead. This is the NL's most closely watched division — the defending World Series champion Dodgers against a Padres team that started 2-5 and has won at a torrid pace since.

Dodgers manager Dave Roberts publicly said he's not worried about San Diego. The Padres keep winning anyway. Shohei Ohtani is hitting at an elite pace (6 HRs, contributing on both sides of the ball), and Mason Miller is locking down the ninth inning with historic efficiency for the Padres. This race will define October in the National League.

NL East: Atlanta's Lead Is Growing

The Braves (18-8) have the best record in the National League and lead the NL East by 5.5 games over the Marlins (12-13). The Mets (9-16) and Phillies (8-17) are having dismal starts. The Nationals are young and showing flashes, but aren't yet equipped to sustain a division race.

Atlanta has been the NL's most consistent team all April. Their pitching has been strong, their offense balanced, and their division rivals have largely underperformed expectations.

American League

In the AL East, the New York Yankees lead at 16-9. Aaron Judge is on a ~66-home-run pace and the lineup has been productive. Tampa Bay at 13-11 and Baltimore at 12-13 keep the race competitive for now.

The AL Central is tight: Detroit and Cleveland are tied at 14-12. The AL West has Oakland and Texas tied at 13-12, with Houston (10-16) significantly underperforming despite Yordan Alvarez's historic individual production.

What's Next

The NL Central race — and the broader question of which teams from this historically good division will reach October — is the defining story of the 2026 season's first month. Every game involving Cincinnati, Chicago, St. Louis, Pittsburgh, and Milwaukee carries playoff-race weight that typically doesn't arrive until August. Buckle up. The most interesting division in baseball is going to get better before it gets worse.
