Mason Miller Is 6 Innings From Rewriting History — and Almost Nobody Is Talking About It

By Ryan Calloway | April 27, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

In 1988, Gregg Olson of the Baltimore Orioles threw 41 consecutive scoreless innings as a reliever. That record has survived 36 years, four generations of dominant closers, and every elite bullpen arm the sport has produced since.

Mason Miller, the San Diego Padres closer, is 6 1/3 innings away from breaking it.

Miller has thrown 34 2/3 consecutive scoreless innings — a Padres franchise record. His 2026 numbers are frankly absurd: a 0.00 ERA, a 0.35 WHIP, and 27 strikeouts against just two walks in 11.1 innings this season. The streak extends into 2025, meaning the consistency runs across two calendar years.

And yet, with Murakami making history, the Dodgers and Cubs dominating headlines, and the NL Central doing unprecedented things, Miller's chase has flown almost entirely under the radar.

Context for the Record

Olson's record is the all-time benchmark for reliever dominance. Set in his rookie season, it remains the longest scoreless streak ever assembled by a pitcher working out of the bullpen. To approach it, a pitcher needs not just performance but luck: no cheap infield hits, no errors leading to unearned runs, no inherited runners scoring.

Miller has had all of it for 34 2/3 innings.

The 27-year-old was traded from the Oakland Athletics to San Diego on July 31, 2025, and immediately became the Padres' closer. He's been the team's best reliever since arriving — a premium arm paired with elite command for a team that needed both.

At a typical closer's workload (one inning per appearance, three to four appearances per week), Miller is roughly six to seven outings from the record. Each save opportunity now carries the weight of history alongside the standard high-leverage pressure.

Why This Matters for the Padres

San Diego is tied with the Dodgers at 17-8 for the NL West lead — the most surprising early-season development in the National League. The Padres started the year 2-5 and have been one of the sport's hottest teams since.

Miller's reliability at the back of their bullpen is a significant part of that turnaround. When the ninth inning arrives, Padres managers know it's over. That's a rare luxury. That confidence cascades through the roster — relievers protect smaller leads when the ninth is locked up, starters can exhaust their pitch counts knowing the closer won't blow it.

If Miller breaks Olson's record, it would be one of the most significant individual pitching achievements of the 2026 season, regardless of how much attention it's receiving right now.

What's Next

Miller's next save opportunity comes whenever the Padres next hold a lead entering the ninth. Given their performance this season, that could be as soon as Monday. The countdown to 6 1/3 innings has begun. Whether anyone is paying close enough attention is a different question — but when that record falls, nobody will be able to say they weren't warned.
