### STORY 1: Yamamoto Throws First Complete Game of His MLB Career

#### Long-Form Post — 9:00 AM ET

Yoshinobu Yamamoto had never gone the distance in a major league regular-season game.

Not in his first start. Not in his Cy Young season. Not in any of the 65 regular-season appearances before Sunday.

Then he went to Yankee Stadium for Game 1 of a rain-makeup doubleheader and threw nine innings.

The line: 4 hits, 2 runs, 0 walks, 7 strikeouts, 102 pitches. The final score: Dodgers 8, Yankees 2. His record moves to 10-6. And somewhere in the record books, there is now a complete game next to his name.

The timing was not accidental. Saturday's rainout rescheduled the game as a split doubleheader. The Dodgers needed someone to eat innings in Game 1 to protect their bullpen for the nightcap. Yamamoto didn't just do that. He rendered the bullpen unnecessary.

It was the first Dodgers complete game against the Yankees since Jerry Reuss did it in Game 5 of the 1981 World Series. First Dodgers CG in two years. Sixty-six starts to get there.

He is that good.

### STORY 2: Red Sox Win 13th Straight — Longest Since 1948

#### Long-Form Post — 11:00 AM ET

The Boston Red Sox beat the Tampa Bay Rays 6-1 Sunday afternoon.

That's 13 wins in a row. A four-game sweep of Tampa Bay to close out the home stand. And a win streak the franchise hasn't seen since Harry Truman was in the White House.

The 1948 Red Sox won 13 in a row. That's when this happened last. That team had Dom DiMaggio and Bobby Doerr. This team has a 13-game win streak and a record of 50-48.

The Red Sox franchise record is 15 consecutive wins. They are two wins away from that. The Baltimore Orioles come to town today.

This started as a streak. Then it became a franchise-defining hot run. Now it is a record chase. The Red Sox haven't given anyone a reason to stop watching.

### STORY 3: Ohtani's July 22 Start Is Canceled — Left Knee Irritation

#### Long-Form Post — 1:00 PM ET

Shohei Ohtani is not pitching Wednesday.

The Dodgers announced Sunday that his scheduled July 22 start against the Philadelphia Phillies has been canceled. The reason is left knee irritation. Ohtani received a lubricant injection in the knee just before the All-Star break and has not felt 100 percent since while throwing.

Manager Dave Roberts did not offer a specific return date. He said it would be "some time" — and then elaborated that "if it's two weeks or three weeks, then that's kind of why we're trying to do this, so we don't have to worry about it throughout the end of the season."

The encouraging part: Ohtani is still hitting. He went 2-for-5 with two RBI as the designated hitter in Sunday's Game 1 against the Yankees. No knee pain hitting. The two-way career is not in jeopardy. The pitching schedule is.

The Dodgers will navigate the next two to three weeks without him on the mound. They have the best record in baseball and depth in their rotation. But they want their ace healthy before October. The clock is running.

### STORY 4: Skubal Trade Deadline — Five Teams In, Price Still "Outrageous"

#### Long-Form Post — 3:30 PM ET

The Tarik Skubal trade deadline situation has more companies in the room.

The Cubs, Braves, and Rays were the established suitors. Now the Dodgers and Phillies are confirmed active as well — five teams pursuing the same pitcher, all with 14 days remaining before August 3.

Detroit's asking price has not changed. Per Jon Heyman, the Tigers are seeking controllable pitching and athletic position players close to big-league ready. One NL executive described that price as "outrageous."

Skubal is a rental — he is a free agent after the 2026 season. The Tigers are not getting certainty in return no matter what they ask for. What they are getting is a premium on someone else's urgency, and the five teams willing to call them suggest the market is willing to pay it.

The question — 14 days out — is whether someone blinks.

Montgomery is one of the most interesting stories in the American League this season. The AL Central lead in their back pocket.

This team was 41-121 in 2024. They are 51-46 now.
