First Time in 66 Starts: Yoshinobu Yamamoto Throws a Complete Game

By Ryan Calloway | Ballpark Banter | July 20, 2026

Yoshinobu Yamamoto had not thrown a complete game in the major leagues. Not in 2024, when he made his Dodgers debut and finished among the best pitchers in baseball. Not in 2025, when he built on that and established himself as a genuine ace. Not in any of the 65 regular-season starts he'd made before Sunday afternoon at Yankee Stadium.

On Sunday, he threw nine innings.

The Dodgers beat the Yankees 8-2 in Game 1 of a split doubleheader — a game that existed only because Saturday's contest was rained out. The rainout forced the rescheduling. The rescheduling created a doubleheader. And the doubleheader handed Yamamoto a moment that his résumé didn't have yet: a complete game, in New York, on the biggest stage the regular season offers.

The Numbers

Nine innings. Four hits. Two runs, both earned. Zero walks. Seven strikeouts. One hundred and two pitches, 69 for strikes.

He allowed a run in the first inning and another in the third, and then he locked down. From the fourth inning through the ninth, no Yankee crossed the plate. Yamamoto was not dominant in the flashy, strikeout-every-batter sense — but he was efficient, commanding, and precisely what a No. 1 starter is supposed to be when a team needs him to go deep.

The Context

The complete game was not just a personal milestone. It was operationally significant.

With two games in one day, the Dodgers needed to protect their bullpen. Their relievers did not throw a pitch in Game 1. Yamamoto handed them nine innings of rest before Game 2 started. The timing was what made the performance feel like something larger than an individual accomplishment — it was the right pitcher delivering exactly the right game at the right moment.

He is the first Dodgers pitcher to throw a complete game against the Yankees since Jerry Reuss did it in Game 5 of the 1981 World Series. The first Dodgers complete game in any game, against any opponent, in two years. In 66 career regular-season starts, it is the first time he has gone the distance.

Yamamoto's Place in This Season

His record stands at 10-6. The Dodgers entered Sunday's games as the best team in baseball, and Yamamoto has been a primary reason why. The complete game adds a layer to what has already been a strong season — it shows the endurance and the ceiling that scouts and evaluators talked about when the Dodgers signed him.

Shohei Ohtani went 2-for-5 with two RBI in the same game as the designated hitter. The Dodgers scored eight times. Every element of the game worked. Yamamoto happened to be the piece that made all of it more efficient.

What's Next

The Dodgers continue their series against the Yankees in New York. Yamamoto's next start will be set up normally — he threw 102 pitches Sunday and will need his usual rest. The complete game is the headline. The 8-2 win is the result that matters for the standings. And the fact that this happened at Yankee Stadium, in front of the largest possible audience, in the highest-profile game on the July schedule, gives the milestone the weight it deserves.
