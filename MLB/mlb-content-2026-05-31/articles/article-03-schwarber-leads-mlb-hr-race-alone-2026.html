The 2026 home run race was supposed to be a three-way story. Kyle Schwarber, the Phillies' designated hitter, had been setting records. Munetaka Murakami, the White Sox rookie, had been matching him homer for homer. Aaron Judge, because it is always Judge, was lurking.

Now the story has changed shape.

With Murakami on the injured list Saturday following a Grade 2 right hamstring strain, Schwarber holds the major league home run lead alone. He had 20-plus home runs entering Sunday. His pace projects to approximately 66 over a full 162-game season. He already owns the record for the earliest date any player has ever hit his 20th home run in major league history — a milestone that had belonged to Luis Gonzalez since 2001.

The race continues. But it looks different now than it did Friday morning.

The Record That Made History

Schwarber's 20th home run came on a date that had never happened before in major league history. Luis Gonzalez's May 17, 2001 mark had stood for 25 years as the earliest any player had reached 20. Schwarber passed it, erasing a benchmark from one of the most memorable offensive seasons of the steroid era and planting himself in the record books for something no one in the modern game had managed to replicate.

The significance of the earliest-date record is what it implies about the pace. A player doesn't set the record for earliest-ever 20 home runs without also being on one of the more extraordinary home run trajectories in recent memory. Schwarber's pace entering June is not just good for 2026 — it is historically unusual by any modern standard.

The Path to 60

Sixty home runs in a season. The number has not been reached since Barry Bonds hit 73 in 2001. Before Bonds, there was the 1998 season — McGwire with 70, Sosa with 66 — and the years that followed: Sosa again at 65 and 63, McGwire again at 70. The 60-home-run club is extraordinarily exclusive, limited entirely to Bonds, McGwire, and Sosa, and all of it clustered within a few years that baseball has spent two decades coming to terms with.

No one since Bonds in 2001 has hit 60. The current projection for Schwarber has him at approximately 66 — a number that would place him in that company.

Projections at this point in the season always carry significant uncertainty. Players slump. Injuries happen, as Murakami just demonstrated. Pitchers adjust. The second half is not a linear extension of the first. But those caveats exist for every projection, and Schwarber's 2026 performance has been consistent enough that dismissing the projection entirely would be dishonest.

He is on pace. Whether he sustains it is a different question, and it is the question that will make this summer worth watching.

Murakami's Absence and the Race's Shape

The most compelling version of the 2026 home run race was the one running through Saturday morning: Murakami vs. Schwarber, two players at 20 home runs on different sides of the league, one a rookie sensation from Japan and one a veteran slugger with a long track record of power. Their parity made the race electric.

Murakami's hamstring injury doesn't end his participation in the race — it pauses it. He is expected to return in approximately four weeks, which means he should be back by early July. The gap Schwarber opens during those four weeks will depend on how he continues to hit.

If Schwarber hits three or four home runs per week while Murakami is out, the deficit becomes real. If Schwarber cools off and the race is still close when Murakami returns, the summer could get dramatic in a way that would be genuinely exciting for baseball.

Judge remains in the picture as well. The Yankees' outfielder has been behind Murakami and Schwarber this season, but he has never required a large sample to close a gap. His place in the race depends on the next several weeks.

What Schwarber Needs to Do

The short answer is what he has already been doing: keeping the swing short, staying disciplined in the zone, and not letting the narrative of the race change how he approaches at-bats. Schwarber has dealt with attention and expectation throughout his career. He has been in a World Series. He has been traded. He has been a free agent. The home run race is pressure of a specific kind, but it is not pressure he hasn't navigated before.

The Phillies as a team are also playing meaningful baseball. They beat the Dodgers 4-3 on Saturday in a game that tied the series and showcased what their lineup can do. Schwarber being in a productive lineup environment helps him see better pitches. It helps the entire offense function. His performance and the team's performance are intertwined in ways that matter for both.

The home run race is entering a new chapter. Schwarber is ahead, alone, and the history books are within reach. Whether the story ends in August or September with something extraordinary is what the next three months are for.
