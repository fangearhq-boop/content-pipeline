For 13 consecutive appearances, Tanner Scott had been unhittable. He had not allowed a run since April 24. His ERA stood at 1.14. He had struck out a third of the batters he faced in 23.2 innings. The Dodgers' closer had become one of the most reliable relievers in the National League, and that reputation was about to be tested in the most visible possible setting.

It didn't hold.

Scott entered in the eighth inning Saturday night with the Dodgers ahead 3-1, Roki Sasaki having delivered five-plus innings of excellent work against one of the NL's best offenses. What happened next gave Phillies fans something to celebrate and reopened conversations about a chapter of Scott's career he had been working hard to leave behind.

Edmundo Sosa hit a two-run home run. Bryce Harper added an RBI single. Philadelphia scored three times with two outs in the eighth and held on to win 4-3, snapping the Dodgers' six-game winning streak and tying the series at one game apiece.

The Eighth Inning That Changed Everything

The sequence that ended Scott's run of dominance unfolded quickly. He allowed a pair of singles to put two runners on base with two outs. It was still manageable. The count was against him. The situation was tight but not lost.

Then Sosa connected.

The two-run home run cleared the left field wall — just over, but over enough. The Dodgers' 3-1 lead evaporated instantly into a 3-3 tie. Harper followed with an RBI single that gave Philadelphia a 4-3 advantage it would not surrender.

It was the first blown save of 2026 for Scott. After 13 appearances without an earned run, one inning had unraveled the streak.

Sasaki's Excellent Work, Wasted

The frustrating context for the Dodgers was how well their starter had pitched. Roki Sasaki went 5.1 innings, allowed three hits, struck out seven batters, walked one, and gave up only a solo home run to Alec Bohm in the second inning. The 406-foot blast gave the Phillies a 1-0 lead they held briefly, but the Dodgers recovered to take a 3-1 advantage by the seventh when Mookie Betts delivered an RBI single.

Sasaki has been building toward this kind of performance for most of 2026. Seven strikeouts against a lineup that includes Schwarber, Harper, and Bohm is not a small thing. The 23-year-old right-hander looked like a legitimate top-of-the-rotation arm Saturday night, and none of it showed up in the win column because of what happened in the eighth.

That's the cruelty of baseball. A starter can be excellent and still lose. The credit for a great start exists in the stat line; it doesn't always exist in the outcome.

The 2025 Shadow Returns

Scott's 2025 season included 10 blown saves — a number that had made him one of the more discussed relievers in baseball for the wrong reasons. The Dodgers retained him and watched him respond with a dominant opening to 2026. Thirteen appearances, zero runs allowed, a 1.14 ERA that ranked among the best in the bullpen.

One blown save does not erase any of that. The statistical record still shows what he had done through mid-May. One inning is not a collapse, and it is not fair to read the arc of 2025 back into a single 2026 outing.

But baseball fans have long memories, and the brief flashback to his worst moments will be unavoidable. Scott will be back in games this week. How he responds will matter as much as the blown save itself.

Series Tied, Rubber Game Today

The Phillies and Dodgers now stand at one game each with a rubber match scheduled Sunday at Dodger Stadium. The Dodgers won Game 1 by a score of 4-2, with their offense providing the margin. Philadelphia answered in Game 2 through clutch at-bats in the eighth inning and a late lead it protected.

Sasaki won't be available to start Game 3 after pitching 5.1 innings the night before. The Dodgers will need to piece together their pitching for the series finale. For the Phillies, the momentum from Saturday's comeback carries into Sunday's first pitch.

Schwarber and Harper in a Dodger Stadium environment with momentum is not a bad position to be in. The Phillies have won two-game series many times this season by doing exactly what they did Saturday night — grinding through a tough at-bat with two outs in a late inning and making the other team pay.

Game 3 will sort out which team gets the bragging rights from one of May's best series. For now, the Phillies walk out of Saturday with a split and the advantage that the blown save gave them.

Tanner Scott will carry the memory of this eighth inning into his next outing. How long that memory lingers depends on what he does with it.
