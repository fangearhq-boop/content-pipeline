Kyle Schwarber Hit His 20th Homer Four Days Faster Than Bonds Did in His Record Season

By Jake Torres

May 20, 2026

Barry Bonds hit his 20th HR in his record 73-HR season on May 19. Kyle Schwarber hit his 20th on May 15. The pace is no longer just remarkable — it's historic.

On May 15, 2026, Kyle Schwarber hit his 20th home run of the season. It was the earliest date in Major League Baseball history that any player had reached that mark.

Six days before that, in a different era, Barry Bonds hit his 20th home run of the 2001 season — on May 19th. Bonds would go on to hit 73 home runs that year, the most in the history of the game.

Schwarber is four days ahead of Bonds' pace from that record-setting season. The comparison is no longer merely hypothetical. The math is real, and the implications are extraordinary.

What the Numbers Actually Mean

Reaching 20 home runs by mid-May is something very few players in baseball history have done. Schwarber is just the eighth player ever to hit 20 home runs in his team's first 45 games. That list is short by design — it takes a combination of extraordinary power, consistent contact, and a favorable run of pitch sequences that rarely lines up for any hitter for that long.

Schwarber's current pace projects to approximately 67 to 70 home runs by the end of the 2026 season. Only two players in the history of Major League Baseball have hit 70 or more home runs in a single season: Bonds, with 73 in 2001, and Mark McGwire, with 70 in 1998.

To be clear about what this means: maintaining any portion of this pace through the dog days of summer and into September is extraordinarily difficult. Every player who has entered May on a record-setting pace has had to fight through mechanical slumps, opposing adjustments, weather, fatigue, and the accumulated weight of a full season.

But right now, the pace is what it is. And what it is, is historic.

The Phillies' Complicated Situation

The Philadelphia Phillies are 21-23. Their team record does not reflect their best player's individual achievement — a situation that creates an unusual tension around following this story. Schwarber is carrying the offense in a way that would be even more celebrated if the team around him were performing at an equivalent level.

Aaron Judge is second in the MLB home run race with 16. Schwarber leads him by four, a margin that reflects how separated this performance has been from the rest of the league. The rest of the power landscape looks normal. Schwarber does not.

What's Next

The milestone to watch now is 25 home runs — when does Schwarber reach it, and how does that date compare to the pace of Bonds' 73-HR season? Every Phillies home game from here through May is a potential landmark. The Bonds comparison will intensify or fade based on what Schwarber does over the next month.
