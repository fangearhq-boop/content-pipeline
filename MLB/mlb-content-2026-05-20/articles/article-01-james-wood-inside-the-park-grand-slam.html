James Wood Hits First Inside-the-Park Grand Slam Since 2022, Powers Nationals' Comeback Win

By Marcus Cole

May 20, 2026

Wood's ball caromed off an outfielder's glove and rolled to center. He never stopped running. Washington rallied from a 5-run deficit to beat the Mets 9-6.

Inside-the-park grand slams arrive so rarely in baseball that the sport sometimes goes years between them. The previous one in Major League Baseball happened in 2022. Tuesday night in Washington, the wait ended.

James Wood, the 22-year-old Washington Nationals outfielder, drove a ball to left-center field that caromed off outfielder Morabito's glove and rolled past Tyrone Taylor toward center. Wood never slowed down rounding the bases, sliding across home plate before the Mets fielders could relay the ball home. He emerged from the dirt with his first career grand slam — and one of the rarest plays in baseball — in the books.

The Nationals were trailing the Mets 5-0 when it happened. They won 9-6.

How Rare Is an Inside-the-Park Grand Slam?

Regular home runs happen multiple times per game across the majors. Inside-the-park home runs are comparatively rare — a combination of speed, opportunistic ball placement, and fielding miscues that all have to align at once. Inside-the-park grand slams require all of that plus a bases-loaded situation where everything breaks perfectly for the hitter.

The last one before Wood's came in 2022. In the intervening four-plus years of professional baseball — hundreds of thousands of plate appearances, thousands of balls hit to the wall — no one had done it. Until Tuesday.

Wood went 3-for-4 on the night, contributing throughout the lineup. Jose Tena added a home run for Washington as the Nationals completed a dramatic five-run deficit reversal. The Nationals had lost their two previous games before Tuesday's win.

Wood at the Center of Washington's Future

Wood's moment arrives as the Nationals continue building toward what they hope will be a competitive future. The 22-year-old has shown the combination of tools — speed, power, arm — that made him a highly-regarded prospect. Moments like Tuesday's are what scouts projected when they identified him as a future centerpiece.

Inside-the-park grand slams do not define careers. But they are the kind of plays that get replayed for years, that fans reference when they talk about what a player was capable of at his best. Wood's Tuesday night highlight will be in that category.

The Mets had opened a 5-0 lead that looked comfortable. It was not. Washington's 9-6 final was the result of an offense that caught fire at exactly the right moment, sparked by one of the stranger and more spectacular plays in recent baseball memory.

What's Next

The Nationals and Mets continue their series with Game 3 on Wednesday. Washington enters with momentum from Tuesday's comeback. Wood leads the Nationals in the offensive spotlight — and baseball fans now have a very specific clip they'll be watching on highlight shows for some time.
