Cam Schlittler's Historic Start: No Pitcher Has Done This Since Walter Johnson in 1913

By Jake Torres

May 20, 2026

A 1.35 ERA, 59 strikeouts, 9 walks, and 1 home run allowed through 9 starts. The last time a pitcher opened a season with those numbers was 1913.

Walter Johnson pitched for the Washington Senators from 1907 to 1927. He is widely considered one of the two or three greatest pitchers in the history of baseball. His name appears in the record books the way Mount Everest appears on maps: as an endpoint, the outer boundary of what is possible.

Through his first nine starts of the 2026 season, Cam Schlittler is pitching like Walter Johnson in 1913.

Schlittler's numbers: 1.35 ERA, 59 strikeouts, 9 walks, 1 home run allowed, 5-1 record. No pitcher in the century-plus that has passed since Walter Johnson completed his 1913 campaign has opened a season with that same combination of strikeout rate, walk rate, home run rate, and ERA through nine starts.

The gap is 113 years. No one has closed it. Until now.

What Makes the Numbers So Remarkable

The combination of statistics that defines Schlittler's accomplishment — more than 50 strikeouts, fewer than 10 walks, no more than one home run allowed, and an ERA below 1.50 — is not about any single category being extreme. It is about all four categories being elite simultaneously.

Pitchers occasionally approach one of these thresholds early in a season. They manage a low ERA for a few starts before the walks climb. Or they strike out batters at a high rate but give up home runs at a higher clip. Or they are stingy with the home run ball but struggle to miss bats consistently enough to suppress their ERA.

What Schlittler has done — maintaining all four categories at elite levels simultaneously through nine starts — is the specific combination that baseball has not seen since the dead ball era, before home runs were commonplace, before the modern pitching workload existed, before virtually everything about the game had changed.

A 25-Year-Old in Uncharted Territory

Schlittler is 25 years old. The New York Yankees knew they had a promising arm when they developed him through their system, but "first pitcher since Walter Johnson" is not a comparison anyone makes lightly or early.

It takes nine starts of sustained excellence to earn a comparison to Walter Johnson. Schlittler has provided those nine starts. The Yankees rotation has found its anchor. Whether Schlittler sustains this level over the full season will determine where exactly this performance fits in the broader historical narrative — but the beginning of the narrative is already written.

What's Next

Schlittler's next start will bring his total to 10 — and with it, another opportunity to maintain or improve upon the statistical combination that no modern pitcher has matched. The ERA is at 1.35. The strikeout-to-walk ratio is exceptional. The season is young enough that the historical comparison will only intensify as the months pass.
