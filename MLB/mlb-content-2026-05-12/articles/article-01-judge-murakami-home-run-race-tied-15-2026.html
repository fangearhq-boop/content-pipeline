Aaron Judge and Munetaka Murakami Are Tied. The 2026 Home Run Race Just Got Real.

By Marcus Cole | Ballpark Banter | May 12, 2026

Murakami hit a solo shot on May 9 to tie Judge for the MLB lead at 15. Two very different players, two very different paths, same number on the board.

For the first 37 or so games, Aaron Judge had the MLB home run lead to himself. Fifteen home runs. Batting .273. On pace for a number that would challenge his own 2022 American League record. Nobody had caught him.

Then on May 9, Munetaka Murakami stepped in against a pitcher and put a solo home run into the seats. His 15th. He drew even with Judge. The board reads 15-15.

The 2026 home run race has a co-leader, and the pair of players sharing that spot is one of the more compelling narratives the sport has produced in a while.

Two Different Players, Same Number

Judge is the Yankees captain and one of the best hitters in the game's recent history. He won the American League MVP in 2022 with 62 home runs, breaking Roger Maris's AL single-season record. He entered 2026 with 383 career home runs. The 400-HR milestone is coming — it's a question of when, not if. Every home run he hits this season adds to a career that is already one of the best in franchise history.

Murakami arrived from Japan this past December on a two-year, $34 million deal. He won the NPB Triple Crown in 2022 with the Yakult Swallows, hitting 56 home runs. He signed with the White Sox as a first baseman with generational power and a question mark over how he would adjust to major league pitching. Forty games in, the adjustment looks complete. He has 15 home runs off 15 separate pitchers. No multi-homer games yet. Every shot has come from a different arm, meaning opposing pitchers have not yet solved how to retire him with the same approach twice.

The Records Murakami Already Holds

Before his first double or triple in the major leagues, Murakami had 15 home runs. That is the MLB record for most home runs before a first double or triple in league history. It is not the kind of stat that appears in the morning box scores. It is the kind that analysts discover when they run unusual queries through historical databases.

Murakami's first MLB season is also setting records for Japanese-born players. His power production in the first six weeks surpasses any comparable start from a Japanese player making his MLB debut. The comparisons to past Japanese imports have largely been to contact hitters and pitchers. Murakami represents a different profile entirely.

The Pace and What It Means

Both players are on pace for approximately 60 home runs over a full 162-game season at their current rate. Judge has done 60-plus before. Murakami has never played 162 MLB games before this year. Whether either player sustains the pace into August and September is the question that will keep this story alive all summer.

The 2026 season had a compelling storyline in Judge's solo chase of the 400-HR club. Now it has something more immediate: two elite power hitters competing for the same leaderboard spot in real time. Every game, every at-bat from either player in the national conversation. That's the kind of thing that makes people who hadn't checked the standings in weeks start paying attention again.

What's Next

Judge and the Yankees are playing the Orioles on Tuesday night. Murakami and the White Sox have their own game on the Tuesday slate. Watch the home run ticker. Both players have shown they can hit in any environment, against any type of pitcher. The tie may last a week or it may last a month. But either way, the race is on and the sport is better for it.
