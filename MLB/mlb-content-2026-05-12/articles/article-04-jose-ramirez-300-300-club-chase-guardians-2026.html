José Ramírez Is 9 Home Runs From the 300-300 Club — A Conversation About What He's Built

By Marcus Cole | Ballpark Banter | May 12, 2026

On May 2, Ramírez reached 300 career stolen bases. He now has 291 career home runs. Nine more puts him alongside eight of the most exceptional players in baseball history.

On May 2, in the fifth inning of a Guardians win over the Athletics, José Ramírez stole third base. It was his 300th career stolen base, making him only the second player in Cleveland franchise history to reach that milestone after Kenny Lofton. Postgame, a reporter ran the obvious follow-up calculation.

Ramírez has 291 career home runs.

Nine more home runs put him in the 300-300 club. The club with eight members. The club that includes Willie Mays.

The 300-300 Club and What It Means

The 300 home run and 300 stolen base combination is one of the most exclusive achievements in baseball history. To reach both milestones requires elite power output sustained over a long career, elite speed sustained over that same career, and the health and longevity to play enough games to accumulate those numbers. Players who do one well often sacrifice the other. Players who do both are extremely rare.

The eight current members of the 300-300 club are Willie Mays, Barry Bonds, Alex Rodriguez, Andre Dawson, Carlos Beltran, Bobby Bonds, and two others. That is a list of Hall of Famers, perennial MVP candidates, and players who defined their eras. Ramírez has a shot at joining it.

Ramírez's Career in Context

The underappreciation of Ramírez's career is a legitimate analytical conversation. He has spent his entire career in Cleveland, a mid-market franchise that does not generate the same national coverage as teams in New York, Los Angeles, or Boston. His numbers have been excellent for more than a decade. He wins Silver Sluggers, appears on MVP ballots, and performs in the playoffs when the Guardians get there.

At 33, Ramírez is still producing. His 2026 season has been solid offensively and his stolen base numbers remain active. Nine home runs is a pace that a healthy Ramírez can cover in a matter of weeks. If he reaches 300 home runs this season, he does so before his 34th birthday.

The Hall of Fame Question

Membership in the 300-300 club does not guarantee a Hall of Fame plaque. But Ramírez's combination of sustained elite production at third base, defensive value, and these counting stat milestones puts him in a conversation that grows stronger with each season. Third basemen in Cooperstown include players with lower career numbers than what Ramírez is building.

The case doesn't require the 300-300 club to be compelling. But the 300-300 club would make it undeniable.

What's Next

Ramírez and the Guardians continue their season in the AL Central. Each home run he hits moves the counter forward. 291 to 292. 292 to 293. Keep watching. When he hits number 300, it will be one of the quieter historical moments of the 2026 season — a player doing something only eight others have done, in Cleveland, on a Tuesday night, probably to less attention than it deserves.
