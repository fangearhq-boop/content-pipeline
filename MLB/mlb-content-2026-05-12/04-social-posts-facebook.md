### STORY 1: Judge vs. Murakami HR Race Tied at 15

#### Long-Form Post — 8:00 AM ET

The 2026 MLB home run race has a co-leader.

Aaron Judge came into May with the league lead at 15 home runs, putting him on pace for one of the most historically dominant power seasons in recent memory. Then, on May 9, Munetaka Murakami stepped into the box and hit a solo home run to left field to draw even. Both players: 15 home runs. Both players on pace for 60-plus over a full season.

The pairing is one of the more interesting story matchups you could construct. Judge is the captain of the New York Yankees, a veteran superstar who won the AL MVP in 2022 with 62 home runs, the American League single-season record. He entered this season with 383 career home runs and is chasing the 400 club. Every time he hits a home run, it means something beyond the box score.

Murakami is a first-year player who signed a 2-year $34 million deal last December after winning the NPB Triple Crown in Japan with 56 home runs in 2022. He arrived with enormous expectations and has exceeded all of them. He has 15 home runs off 15 pitchers with no multi-homer games yet — every shot has come from a different arm. And he owns the MLB record for most home runs before a first double or triple in league history.

This is not a fluke. Murakami came to the major leagues ready.

The question now is who breaks the tie first, and whether either player can sustain this pace deep into the summer. The last time the league had a prolonged, nationally compelling home run race was an era ago. This one is just getting started.

### STORY 4: Ramírez 300-300 Club Chase

#### Long-Form Post — 3:00 PM ET

On May 2, José Ramírez stole third base in the fifth inning of a Guardians win over the Athletics. It was the 300th stolen base of his career. He became only the second player in Cleveland franchise history to reach that milestone — the first since Kenny Lofton.

That night, reporters did the math. At 291 career home runs, Ramírez now needs 9 more to join the 300-300 club.

The 300-300 club is eight members long. Willie Mays. Barry Bonds. Alex Rodriguez. Andre Dawson. Carlos Beltran. Bobby Bonds. Three more names that share that particular category of sustained excellence over a long career.

Eight members since baseball began. Ramírez has a shot at being No. 9.

There's nothing flashy about how he's building this case. Ramírez doesn't have a signature moment that non-fans can point to. He doesn't have a 62-HR season like Judge or a household nickname like Bonds. What he has is 12 years of elite, consistent production for one franchise in a mid-market city. Third base. Clutch hitting. Smart base running. Year after year.

He has 300 stolen bases and 291 career home runs at age 33. The math works.

If Ramírez gets to 300-300, the conversation about where he ranks all-time at the third base position gets a lot more interesting.

#### Long-Form Post — 6:00 PM ET

But the historical comparison that stands out: De La Cruz is the first shortstop since 1900 to post 5 home runs and 5 stolen bases through 16 games of a season. That's not a 2026 record. That's a 126-year record. The shortstop position has produced hundreds of Hall of Fame players since the turn of the last century. None of them did what De La Cruz did this spring.

He is 24 years old.

The raw tools that scouts raved about in the minors are translating. He swings with elite bat speed, makes contact to all fields, and covers ground at shortstop with range that few players at any position can match. His average exit velocity is among the best in baseball. He doesn't just hit home runs — he destroys baseballs.

The Reds made the right choice building around De La Cruz long-term. This is what that investment looks like when it's paying off.
