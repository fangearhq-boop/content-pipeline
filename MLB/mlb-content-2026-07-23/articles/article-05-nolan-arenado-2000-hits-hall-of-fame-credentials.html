Nolan Arenado Is the 6th Member of the Most Exclusive Club in Baseball. The Other 5 Are All in Cooperstown.

By Jake Torres | Ballpark Banter | July 23, 2026

On Monday night at Chase Field in Phoenix, Nolan Arenado stepped into the box against Oakland Athletics right-hander Jeffrey Springs in the fifth inning and hit a double to left field. The Chase Field crowd gave him a standing ovation. He tipped his helmet from second base and jogged back toward the dugout when the inning ended the way he always does — without ceremony.

It was his 2,000th career hit. It made him the 297th player in MLB history to reach the milestone. The Diamondbacks lost to Oakland that night, 5-2. None of that is the important part.

The important part is what the 2,000th hit did to his résumé.

The Club No One Else Is In

Nolan Arenado is now the sixth player in Major League Baseball history to combine 2,000 or more career hits, 350 or more home runs, and 10 or more Gold Gloves. The first five members of that club are Ken Griffey Jr., Willie Mays, Mike Schmidt, Johnny Bench, and Al Kaline.

Every one of them is in the Hall of Fame.

Arenado is 35 years old and in his first season with the Arizona Diamondbacks. He is the only active player in this club. He is the only third baseman in this club. He is, as of Monday night, the company that Griffey and Mays and Schmidt and Bench and Kaline keep.

Building the Full Case

The case for Arenado's eventual induction in Cooperstown does not rest on a single milestone or a single statistical threshold. It rests on a body of work across 14 major league seasons that has been consistent in both its offensive production and its defensive excellence in ways that very few players ever achieve simultaneously.

His career batting line sits at .280/.337/.503. He has 366 home runs and 1,228 RBIs. He has scored 1,038 runs. He has 424 doubles. He is an eight-time All-Star. He has five Silver Slugger awards. His 10 Gold Gloves at third base — by most evaluations — represents a defensive standard at the position matched only by Brooks Robinson in the history of the game.

The offense would be enough for most third basemen to reach Cooperstown. The defense, layered on top of that offense across 14 seasons, makes the case different in kind rather than in degree. There are productive third basemen. There are elite defensive third basemen. There are a handful of players in the history of the position who were both at the same time for more than a decade.

Arenado is in that group. Monday's double made it official in statistical terms.

The Arizona Chapter

Arenado joined the Diamondbacks this season after his time with the Rockies and Cardinals, coming home in a sense to a National League West market where he can play meaningful games in a park that fits his left-handed swing and his defensive skill set at the hot corner. The 2,000th hit came in Arizona. The second half of his career, and perhaps the final push toward the numbers that make a Hall of Fame case undeniable, is being written there.

He is not at the finish line. At 35, with his health intact and his production still at a high level, he is mid-race. The finish line is Cooperstown, and it is coming into view.

What's Next

Nolan Arenado and the Arizona Diamondbacks continue their 2026 season. Arenado stands at 366 career home runs, 1,228 RBIs, and now 2,000 hits. His Hall of Fame candidacy will be officially measured when he retires, but the statistical markers he set on Monday have already placed him in the company of five players that voters have never left out of Cooperstown.
