Ohtani Threw Wednesday. Prior Said It Looked Good. Roberts Still Has No Return Date.

By Jake Torres | Ballpark Banter | July 23, 2026

The news is better than it was a week ago. It's not as good as it could have been.

Shohei Ohtani threw an approximately 31-pitch bullpen session at Citizens Bank Park in Philadelphia on Wednesday, July 22 — his first mound work in roughly two weeks and the first significant progress update on the knee injury that has kept him out of the Dodgers' rotation since July 3. The session came ahead of the originally targeted July 25-26 window, which is itself an encouraging sign.

Pitching coach Mark Prior kept the assessment brief: "It looked good."

Dodgers manager Dave Roberts was equally direct on the other half: Ohtani will not return to the rotation during the team's current road trip. No timetable was announced. The return date is unknown.

What the Session Means

An early bullpen session with a positive report from Prior is the best possible outcome short of a specific return date. Before Wednesday, the question was whether Ohtani could throw at all without setback. The answer, after 31 pitches in Philadelphia, appears to be yes. The Athletic's Fabian Ardaya reported Ohtani appeared to throw his full pitch arsenal during the session — not a cautious limited-velocitiy version of himself, but the real thing.

That matters for the Cy Young case, which has been paused since July 3. Before the knee forced him off the mound, Ohtani was 8-2 with a 1.79 ERA and 95 strikeouts in 85.2 innings — a first-half performance that put him firmly in the Cy Young conversation. Every week without a start is a week without new data for the voters, and the Dodgers need him healthy in October whether he wins the Cy Young or not.

The Dodgers Without Him Pitching

The Dodgers were 64-38 entering the week. They have been winning through the knee issue because their rotation is deep enough to absorb the absence and because Ohtani has continued to hit as the designated hitter throughout his recovery. The team has not collapsed without him on the mound. That context allows the organization to take his return timeline cautiously rather than rushing a decision.

But the postseason picture is what makes the return date matter most. The Dodgers are built for October. Ohtani healthy in October, in the rotation, with the full arsenal he showed briefly on Wednesday, is a different proposition than Ohtani as a DH who last started in July. The organization knows this. Every interview Roberts gives on the subject is a careful balance between optimism and caution, because the cost of rushing is significantly higher than the cost of waiting.

The Orthovisc Injection and the Timeline

During or after the All-Star break, Ohtani received an Orthovisc injection — a lubricant designed to reduce friction and inflammation in the knee joint. The injection required the roughly two-week window before throwing that led to Wednesday's bullpen session. In that sense, Wednesday was always the target. The fact that it came before the originally stated July 25-26 window means the injection did its job on schedule or slightly ahead of it.

The next step after a bullpen session is a live batting practice session, followed by a simulated game, followed by a minor league rehab appearance or a direct return to the rotation. How long that progression takes depends on how the knee responds in the days after Wednesday's throwing. If Prior's "It looked good" holds up and there's no setback, the timeline is measured in weeks, not months.

What's Next

Ohtani continues as the Dodgers' designated hitter while the knee recovers. The team has not announced a return date or a target for the next step in his throwing progression. The Dodgers are on the road through the end of the current trip. Any update on a potential return to the rotation is expected when the team has more information following Wednesday's session.
