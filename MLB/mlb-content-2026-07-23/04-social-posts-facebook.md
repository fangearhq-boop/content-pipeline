### STORY 1: Red Sox Streak Ends at 15 — Franchise Record Tied, Not Broken

#### Long-Form Post — 8:00 AM ET

The Red Sox got one of the two games they needed Wednesday. Game 2 was one game too far.

Boston beat the Baltimore Orioles 6-3 in the first game of Wednesday's doubleheader at Fenway Park. That win extended the Red Sox's winning streak to 15 games — tying the 1946 franchise record, the longest streak in the history of the franchise before this one. The 1946 record was set by a team with Ted Williams, Johnny Pesky, Bobby Doerr, and Dom DiMaggio. It stood for 80 years.

Then the Orioles came back and won Game 2, 5-1. The streak ended at 15.

The 1946 record was tied. Not broken.

What the streak did for this team's season cannot be understated. The Red Sox started it at 37-48. They finish it at 52-49. In 15 games they went from a team headed toward another losing season to a team with a winning record and a foothold in the AL Wild Card race. The streak did not make them a championship contender. But it made them relevant in a way that they weren't on the day it started.

The longest Red Sox win streak since 2006 is over. Their second half is just getting interesting.

### STORY 2: Ohtani Bullpen Session — Ahead of Schedule, Still Without a Date

#### Long-Form Post — 10:30 AM ET

Shohei Ohtani threw on Wednesday. The context matters.

Ohtani threw an approximately 31-pitch bullpen session at Citizens Bank Park in Philadelphia on July 22 — his first mound work in roughly two weeks since receiving an injection in his left knee during the All-Star break. The session was ahead of the originally targeted July 25-26 window. Pitching coach Mark Prior kept his assessment simple: "It looked good." The Athletic's Fabian Ardaya reported Ohtani appeared to be throwing his full pitch arsenal in the session.

The good news stops there for anyone hoping for a quick return.

Dodgers manager Dave Roberts confirmed that Ohtani will not return to the starting rotation during the team's current road trip. No return timetable was set beyond that. Ohtani last pitched on July 3. He has missed two July starts and the All-Star Game. He has continued to hit as a designated hitter throughout the knee issue.

Before the knee forced him off the mound, Ohtani was 8-2 with a 1.79 ERA and 95 strikeouts in 85.2 innings — numbers firmly in the Cy Young conversation. Those numbers are frozen right now. The Dodgers are 64-38 and have been winning without him pitching, which means the urgency of a specific return date is more about October than August.

The bullpen session is a step. The next step is a start. When that start happens is the only question that still needs an answer.

### STORY 3: MLB Trade Deadline 11 Days Away — The Market Is Moving

#### Long-Form Post — 12:30 PM ET

August 3. That's the MLB trade deadline. Eleven days from today.

The early action is already in the books. In the past week: the Kansas City Royals picked up right-hander Nate Pearson from Houston (July 22). The Dodgers added righty Seth Halvorsen from Colorado (July 20). The Cubs grabbed Aaron Civale from Oakland (July 18). These are roster-filling moves by teams with bigger ambitions — the setup before the headline.

The headline is Tarik Skubal. The Detroit Tigers ace is in his final contract year, and the question of whether he moves before August 3 has defined the early weeks of deadline season. ESPN's Jeff Passan reported the Tigers are "far more likely than not" to move him. But rival executives around the league are increasingly telling reporters a different story — that Detroit may hold. The Tigers have the fourth-best record in MLB since June 1. They're still within range of the AL Wild Card. Skubal himself wants the Tigers to be buyers. The asking price for him is described as "enormous," which has kept most clubs at a distance.

The New York Mets have made a dramatic shift — the majority of their roster is available. The Los Angeles Angels are making starting pitchers Reid Detmers and Jose Soriano available. Luis Arraez is expected to move. So is Aroldis Chapman.

Eleven days. The market is heating up and nothing significant has happened yet. That gap tends to close fast.

### STORY 4: Chicago White Sox — The Rebuild Faces Its Biggest Test Yet

#### Long-Form Post — 3:00 PM ET

Kyle Teel was the proof of concept.

When the White Sox's top catching prospect arrived and immediately started contributing this season, it felt like the confirmation that the rebuild was working — that the front office's decision to let 2024 become historic in the worst possible way (121-41, the worst record in MLB history) was going to pay off. Teel was the return on that patience.

Then he hurt his left ankle applying a tag earlier this week, went on the injured list, and is expected to miss six to twelve weeks — likely for the rest of the season.

Edgar Quero is the replacement. The 22-year-old switch-hitter was called up from Triple-A Charlotte. He is part of the same rebuild pipeline that produced Teel and Colson Montgomery, who has hit 21 home runs this season — becoming the first player in MLB history to record 20 or more home runs in his first 275 at-bats in each of his first two major league seasons.

The White Sox are still leading the AL Central by one game over the Cleveland Guardians. They have the second-best run differential in the American League. The system is deeper than any one player, which is the whole point of a rebuild.

The rebuild started from the worst season in franchise history. Today it leads the AL Central with six weeks left until the trade deadline.

### STORY 5: Nolan Arenado's Hall of Fame Case Keeps Building

#### Long-Form Post — 6:00 PM ET

Nolan Arenado joined the most exclusive club in baseball history on July 20, and the five other members are all in Cooperstown.

Arenado's 2,000th career hit — a double to left field off Jeffrey Springs in the fifth inning at Chase Field in Phoenix — made him the sixth player in MLB history to combine 2,000 or more hits, 350 or more home runs, and 10 or more Gold Gloves. The other five members of that club: Ken Griffey Jr., Willie Mays, Mike Schmidt, Johnny Bench, Al Kaline.

Every one of them is in the Hall of Fame. Arenado is the only active player in the club.

The statistical case for Arenado's eventual induction does not rest on a single milestone. His career batting line sits at .280/.337/.503 with 366 home runs, 1,228 RBIs, and 1,038 runs. He is an eight-time All-Star. He has won 10 Gold Gloves at third base. He has five Silver Sluggers. Defensively, he is considered among the best third basemen in the history of the position.

He is 35 years old, in his first season with the Arizona Diamondbacks, and the Diamondbacks lost 5-2 to Oakland the night he got his 2,000th hit. That last detail is the kind of thing that gets forgotten in most milestone recaps. Arenado tipped his helmet to a standing ovation from the Chase Field crowd and then jogged back to the dugout, the same way he always does.

There is no rush. He's building a case a line drive at a time.
