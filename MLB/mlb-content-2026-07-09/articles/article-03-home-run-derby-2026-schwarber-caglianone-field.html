Home Run Derby 2026: Schwarber's Back in Doubt, Caglianone Is the New Name to Know

By Ryan Calloway | Ballpark Banter | July 9, 2026

Four of eight spots are confirmed. Four are still open. The T-Mobile Home Run Derby is Monday night at Citizens Bank Park, and the most interesting question isn't who's competing — it's whether Kyle Schwarber will even show up.

The field is taking shape, though. And it includes a name that most casual fans should get to know before Monday's first swing.

The Confirmed Field (4 of 8)

Junior Caminero, Tampa Bay Rays (26 HR): Caminero is back for his second Derby after finishing as the runner-up last year. He's fourth in MLB in home runs this season and brings legitimate thunder to the competition. Expect him to be among the favorites.

Ben Rice, New York Yankees: A first-time All-Star in his third major league season, Rice makes his Derby debut. He's been one of the more pleasant surprises in the AL this year and carries the quiet confidence of a hitter who knows exactly what he can do.

Jac Caglianone, Kansas City Royals: This is the name. The 22-year-old from the Florida Gators was one of the most talked-about power hitters in college baseball last season, and scouts have been salivating over his raw power since before he was drafted. He has 14 home runs in his first MLB season — not a massive number, but the exit velocities and distances have been eye-opening. A Derby is exactly the stage where a hitter like Caglianone can announce himself to the mainstream audience. Watch for him.

Willson Contreras, Boston Red Sox: A four-time All-Star making his first Derby appearance. Contreras brings the experience and charisma to carry a competition, even in a field full of bigger names. His showing up as a first-time Derby participant at this stage of his career makes him a sentimental pick for many fans.

The Schwarber Question

Kyle Schwarber leads MLB with 31 home runs. He plays his home games at Citizens Bank Park — the venue hosting this Derby. He was the 2025 All-Star Game MVP. He is the perfect star for this event in this city.

He also has a back issue.

Schwarber spoke to Sports Illustrated about his Derby status this week: "If this back deal calms down, then I'll probably do it. But just gotta make sure, one, I'm there, and then two, that this back thing kind of calms down."

That's a conditional yes. Which is not the same as a yes.

If Schwarber participates, he becomes the story — the hometown slugger, the defending MVP, the most prolific home run hitter in baseball right now, swinging at Citizens Bank Park in front of his own fans. The narrative writes itself. If he can't go, the Derby loses its most compelling angle but still has a genuinely interesting field.

The New Format

MLB scrapped the timer format entirely for 2026. Here's how it works now:

Round 1: each player gets 20 swings. Knockout rounds: 15 swings. The key wrinkle — and it changes strategy significantly — is this: if you homer on your final swing, you keep going until you miss. A hot streak on your last swing becomes an unlimited extension.

No bonus time. No 30-second clock. Just swings and home runs.

The change should produce different outcomes than the timer format, which rewarded hitters who could maintain a pace over two minutes. The swing-count format rewards precision and peak power on a fixed number of attempts, with the last-swing extension adding a moment-of-truth element that could produce unforgettable sequences.

MLB also unveiled a 2026 Home Run Derby chain that pays tribute to the Liberty Bell — a fitting touch for a competition in Philadelphia's backyard.

What to Watch For

Four spots remain, and the names announced between now and Monday will continue to shape expectations. The Schwarber update is the headline. The Caglianone debut is the sleeper. And the new format is the wild card that makes predicting a winner more complicated than usual.
