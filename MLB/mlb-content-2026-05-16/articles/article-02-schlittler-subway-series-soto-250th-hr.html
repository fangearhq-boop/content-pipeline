Schlittler Dominates Subway Series Opener; Soto's 250th Career HR Headlines 5-2 Yankees Win

By Jake Torres | May 16, 2026

The 2026 Subway Series opened with two performances that deserve to exist in separate conversations — one from each side of the scoreboard.

Cam Schlittler was masterful for the New York Yankees, carrying a one-hit shutout into the seventh inning and striking out nine batters over 6.2 innings. Juan Soto delivered one of the most significant at-bats of the season, hitting his 250th career home run in the seventh inning. The Yankees won 5-2. And Citi Field gave the full-capacity crowd enough to process on both sides of the rivalry.

Schlittler's Subway Series Statement

At 25 years old, pitching in the biggest game of his early career against the crosstown rival in a packed ballpark, Cam Schlittler was in complete control. His 96-99 mph fastball kept Mets hitters off balance all night. He walked two, struck out nine, and allowed one hit through 6.2 innings — holding his AL-best ERA at 1.35.

The Mets' offense has struggled against high-velocity pitching this season, and Schlittler attacked that weakness from the first pitch. He worked quickly, threw strikes, and made the at-bats look routine that were anything but. When you carry a one-hit shutout into the seventh in a Subway Series game, you've made a statement that outlasts the scoreboard.

Jazz Chisholm Jr. opened the scoring with a two-run double off former Yankees closer Clay Holmes in the third inning, part of a three-run frame that gave Schlittler the cushion he needed. Ben Rice added his 14th home run of the season in the ninth, padding the lead.

Soto's 250th: A Milestone With Historical Weight

In the seventh inning, down 5-0, Juan Soto stepped into the batter's box against Schlittler with two outs and the count at 0-2. The situation called for a strikeout. Soto had other ideas.

He hit a fastball 393 feet to dead center field at 102.6 miles per hour. It landed in the seats and the crowd gave him the moment it deserved — because no matter which team you root for in New York, 250 career home runs at age 27 is a number worth standing for.

The list of players who reached 250 home runs and 95 stolen bases before turning 28 is among the shortest in baseball history. Alex Rodriguez. Ken Griffey Jr. Mickey Mantle. Mike Trout. Frank Robinson. Andruw Jones. And now Juan Soto — the man who signed a $765 million contract with the Mets last offseason, who came back to face his former team, and who hit the milestone off the opponent's best pitcher in the team's signature rivalry series.

He hit it on an 0-2 pitch. The best hitters always find a way.

What's Next

The Subway Series continues Saturday with Game 2 at Citi Field. The Yankees lead the series 1-0 and will look to the pitching matchup for the next chapter of a weekend that is already delivering at the highest level. Both teams know what's at stake — not just series momentum but city-wide bragging rights through the summer. Schlittler set an early standard for excellence in this series. Soto reminded everyone that even in a losing game, he can author the night's defining moment. Saturday's game promises more of the same.
