10 Home Runs, 15 Stolen Bases, 39 Games: Oneil Cruz Is Writing Baseball History

By Marcus Cole | May 16, 2026

You may not be watching Pittsburgh Pirates games. That's understandable — the Pirates are not a playoff team, and national baseball coverage follows the standings. But inside the PNC Park box scores, Oneil Cruz is putting together a statistical line that almost no player in the history of the sport has matched this early in a season.

Through his first 39 games of 2026, Cruz has 10 home runs and 15 stolen bases. The combination of those two marks before a player's 40th game has been accomplished by only two players in major league history: Eric Davis of the Cincinnati Reds in 1987, and Lou Brock of the St. Louis Cardinals in 1967.

Cruz is the third. And he plays shortstop.

What Makes This So Unusual

Power and speed at this rate is historically rare. Eric Davis's 1987 season — where he hit 37 home runs and stole 50 bases — is considered one of the great five-tool seasons in baseball history. Lou Brock was a Hall of Fame outfielder in his prime. Their early-season power-speed combinations have stood alone in the record books for decades.

Cruz doesn't play outfield. He plays shortstop. He's 6 feet 7 inches tall. There is no player in baseball history who looks like Oneil Cruz, and there are very few who have produced like him at this position at this stage of a season.

The combination of size, athleticism, speed, and power that Cruz brings to the game is genuinely unique. His home runs don't just leave the ballpark — they leave the ballpark dramatically. His stolen bases come from a man who shouldn't be able to move like that. He defies every conventional expectation for what a shortstop is supposed to look like.

The Team Context

The Pirates have not been a playoff contender in recent years, and 2026 does not appear to be different. That means Cruz's individual excellence often goes unacknowledged at the national level. When a team is losing, the highlights get buried.

But individual milestones don't require a winning record. They require effort, talent, and consistency — and Cruz has provided all three through the first six weeks of the season. Regardless of where Pittsburgh finishes, Cruz's first 39 games of 2026 will appear in baseball history books alongside Eric Davis and Lou Brock. That's not a consolation. That's a distinction.

What's Next

Every game Cruz plays from here adds to one of the more compelling individual narratives of the 2026 season. Whether he maintains this power-speed pace over the full year determines whether this becomes an all-time statistical season or an extraordinary hot start. History suggests that sustaining a 40-home-run, 50-steal pace over 162 games is extraordinarily difficult. But through 39 games, Cruz has done everything the record books required — and then some. Keep watching the Pittsburgh box scores. There's something worth seeing there.
