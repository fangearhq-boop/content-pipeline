Aaron Judge Is 29 Home Runs From a Historic Milestone — The 400 HR Chase Begins

By Jake Torres | April 14, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

Aaron Judge had 371 career home runs as of early April — passing Gil Hodges for sole possession of 83rd place on the all-time list. The number itself is less interesting than what it represents: the three-time AL MVP is closing in on 400 career home runs, a milestone that only 59 players in MLB history have reached.

This is not a story about this season's home run total. Judge hit 62 in 2022. He's hit 50+ in four different seasons — tying Mark McGwire, Babe Ruth, and Sammy Sosa for the most 50-home run seasons in MLB history. At 33 years old, he is past the peak power years but still elite, still productive, and still the most feared hitter in the American League lineup.

The question for 2026 — and 2027, and however long Judge plays — is whether he can maintain the pace that gets him to 400. The math is encouraging. Twenty-nine home runs at his average production rate is achievable in a season and a half.

The Early 2026 Season

Judge's start to 2026 was bumpy. He struck out four times in five plate appearances in his season opener, a rough debut that generated questions about his mechanics and approach. Those concerns were answered quickly: Judge homered in his very first at-bat at Yankee Stadium for the home opener, giving the crowd the moment they came to see.

The early-season pace has been careful and patient, consistent with the approach of a veteran who understands that April production matters less than postseason production. Judge has never been a fast starter — his career April numbers are solid but not as overwhelming as his July and August numbers when he historically peaks.

The Yankees' success in 2026 will depend significantly on whether Judge can stay healthy through the full season. He has dealt with injuries in multiple recent campaigns that have cost him games and plate appearances. When he plays a full year, the numbers are historic. When he misses time, the Yankees are a different team.

What 400 Would Mean

Four hundred career home runs is a threshold that places a player in elite historical company. Only 59 players have reached it — a list that includes Babe Ruth, Mickey Mantle, Barry Bonds, Hank Aaron, and every other name that comes up in "greatest of all time" conversations.

Judge has already secured his place in the conversation through other means: the 2022 season in which he hit 62 home runs broke the American League record and placed him in the pantheon of single-season sluggers. His back-to-back MVP awards in 2024-25 added to a résumé that is already Hall of Fame worthy.

But 400 career home runs would be a milestone that transcends advanced metrics and era-adjusted comparisons. It's a number. A round, undeniable, historically significant number.

What's Next

Judge will continue accumulating. The Yankees face a schedule that includes plenty of hitter-friendly parks and pitchers he has historically produced against. By midseason, the 400 HR chase will be a genuine tracking conversation in the baseball media. For now, it's a number on the horizon — but a compelling one. At 371 and counting, Aaron Judge is writing the final chapters of a career that belongs in any conversation about the greatest power hitters the game has produced.
