Tied at 14: Aaron Judge and Munetaka Murakami Are Racing Toward History

By Jake Torres | May 6, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

The 2026 Major League Baseball home run race has two leaders, a dead heat, and one of the most compelling storylines of the early season.

Aaron Judge of the New York Yankees and Munetaka Murakami of the Chicago White Sox are both sitting at 14 home runs — tied for the major league lead entering May 6. Both are on a pace that would project to more than 60 home runs over a full 162-game season. Both represent fundamentally different kinds of greatness — and that contrast is making the race all the more compelling.

Aaron Judge: The King Defending His Crown

Judge enters this moment as the most celebrated home run hitter in recent American League history. In 2022, he set the AL record with 62 home runs. He has since remained one of the most feared hitters in the game — a 6'7" right-handed slugger who combines historic power with elite plate discipline.

This spring, he has been in one of the hottest stretches of his career. He has hit 11 home runs in his last 21 games — a pace that would simply be unsustainable for most hitters but has become a point of expectation for Judge when he's locked in. His 14th home run came May 4, a two-run shot in the first inning off Shane Baz, as the Yankees completed a four-game sweep of the Baltimore Orioles that wasn't particularly close (the Yankees outscored Baltimore by 29 runs over the four games). New York is 24-11, the AL East leaders.

Munetaka Murakami: A Phenomenon Making History

Murakami's story is different, and in some ways more remarkable for its novelty. The Japanese-born White Sox slugger has established himself as one of the most dangerous hitters in the American League in his first extended run in the majors — and his start to 2026 has been historically unusual even by those standards.

Consider: of Murakami's first 12 extra-base hits this season, all 12 have been home runs. There is no recorded instance of that happening in Major League Baseball going back to 1900. His five-game home run streak — which pushed his total to 14 — tied the White Sox franchise record. The rate at which he is making history is remarkable: each week seems to bring a new record attached to his name.

Murakami also surpassed Shohei Ohtani's own record for home runs by a Japanese-born player through his first 21 MLB games. He is not just a story in the context of the White Sox or the AL — he is a story within the history of the game itself.

What's Next

A home run pace in the mid-60s over a full season would challenge Roger Maris and, eventually, Judge's own AL record. Whether either player can maintain this rate over a full 162 games is a separate question — baseball history is full of April and May paces that don't survive the summer grind. But through May 6, the evidence says both men are capable of something extraordinary.

The Judge-Murakami race is the kind of story that sustains an entire baseball summer. Two different players, two different teams, two different stories — and 14 home runs each. The race is officially on, and it isn't close to being decided. Follow along.
