Ohtani Gives Everything — 7 IP, 8 K, 0 BB — and the Dodgers Lose 2-1 to Houston Anyway

By Jake Torres | May 6, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

There is only so much a pitcher can do.

Shohei Ohtani delivered the longest start of his Dodgers career this season on Tuesday — seven complete innings in Houston, 89 pitches, eight strikeouts, zero walks. It was, by nearly every measure, Ohtani at his best. And yet Los Angeles walked out of Minute Maid Park with a 2-1 loss, their offense having been held scoreless through seven innings by Astros pitcher Peter Lambert.

Ohtani's ERA is 0.97 through six starts and 37 innings. The Dodgers' season record is something less than their lineup was supposed to produce.

The End of a Streak

Ohtani entered Tuesday having faced 122 batters this season without allowing a home run. It was one of the more remarkable early-season streaks in the game — the NL Pitcher of the Month for March and April holding the ball in the yard game after game, building on one of the best starts any pitcher has had in recent memory.

Christian Walker ended that in the second inning, lining a solo home run off Ohtani into the seats. It was a clean, well-hit ball. Braden Shewmake added another solo shot later in the game. Two solo home runs. That was all the damage the Astros needed against the best pitcher in the National League.

The home runs don't tell the real story of what Ohtani did Tuesday. He was sharp, efficient, and dominant for seven innings. The first completed 7-inning start of his Dodgers career this season — his first 7-inning outing since 2023 — was precisely the kind of workload management and performance quality that the Dodgers have needed from their $700 million pitcher. He gave it to them. They gave him one run.

The Offense and the Problem It Represents

Peter Lambert, a former minor league journeyman now pitching for Houston, silenced the Dodgers lineup for seven innings. Los Angeles managed zero runs through Lambert's start, finally scratching one across in the eighth inning against the Houston bullpen. It wasn't enough.

The Dodgers were built to score. Their offseason additions and returning talent were supposed to make them one of the most formidable offensive teams in the National League. Tuesday in Houston was another night where that expectation didn't meet reality.

There is still a lot of season remaining, and Dodgers offensive slumps have a way of resolving themselves — the talent is too deep for it to remain suppressed indefinitely. But the pattern of leaving their best pitcher without run support is one that can't continue if Los Angeles intends to contend in the NL West.

What's Next

Despite the loss, Ohtani's 0.97 ERA remains one of the most historically impressive early-season pitching marks in recent memory. The NL Pitcher of the Month award he received for March and April is well-deserved — he has been, game for game, the best pitcher in the league.

The Dodgers continue their series against Houston. They need their offense to match the standard their ace has already set. Tuesday was a night that belonged to Ohtani in every way that mattered except the final score.
