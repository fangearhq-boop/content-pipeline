### STORY 1:

#### Long-Form Post -- 8:00 AM ET

Last night in Cincinnati, Elly De La Cruz did what Elly De La Cruz does — he made a game unforgettable.

De La Cruz went 3-for-6 with two home runs, five RBI, a stolen base, and three runs scored as the Reds beat the Tampa Bay Rays 12-6 Tuesday. Cincinnati scored the first nine runs of the game and never looked back.

Cincinnati extended its win streak to five games and improved its road record to a major-league-best 10-2. They're in first place in the NL Central, and this is a team that's starting to look like a genuine contender.

The Reds didn't just beat the Rays Tuesday. They announced themselves.

### STORY 2:

#### Long-Form Post -- 10:30 AM ET

In 37 innings this season, Shohei Ohtani had not allowed a single home run. He had faced 122 batters. Nobody had touched him deep. The question was never whether it would happen — only when.

Tuesday night in Houston, Christian Walker answered first, lining a solo home run off Ohtani in the second inning. Braden Shewmake added another later. Two solo shots. That was it.

And yet — Ohtani's ERA is 0.97. He went 7 innings on 89 pitches, striking out eight and walking no one. It was the longest start of his Dodgers career this season, the first time he completed seven innings since 2023. By any measure, this was Ohtani at his best.

The problem was the Dodgers' offense. Los Angeles managed zero runs against Astros pitcher Peter Lambert through seven innings. When they finally scratched one run in the eighth against the Houston bullpen, it was too little, too late. Astros 2, Dodgers 1.

You could argue that Ohtani pitched a near-perfect game and still lost. You could also argue that the Dodgers' offense — which was supposed to be one of baseball's most formidable — is struggling to live up to expectations.

Either way: when your pitcher has a 0.97 ERA through six starts and you lose 2-1, the story isn't the pitching.

### STORY 3:

#### Long-Form Post -- 8:00 AM ET

The Atlanta Braves entered Tuesday with the best record in baseball. They had won six consecutive games. They looked, frankly, unstoppable.

Washington said no.

The Nationals beat the Braves 11-4 Tuesday night, snapping Atlanta's six-game winning streak. James Wood homered. Luis García Jr. collected four hits. The Nationals' offense delivered the kind of performance that should remind everyone: no team is invincible over a 162-game season.

The Braves are still 25-11. They still lead the NL East by more than eight games. They are still one of the most well-rounded teams in baseball — Matt Olson, Ozzie Albies, Drake Baldwin, and Michael Harris II have all put together MVP-caliber starts to the season. One loss doesn't change the picture.

But it does add an interesting subplot: Washington is quietly building something. James Wood, a young outfielder of considerable talent, has been one of the more exciting players to watch in the early going. Luis García Jr. hit his way back into the conversation Tuesday. The Nationals have quietly improved, and Tuesday they showed they can hang with the big boys.

For the Braves, Tuesday is a footnote. For Washington, it might be a statement.

### STORY 4:

#### Long-Form Post -- 12:30 PM ET

When the 2026 season began, most casual observers expected the Reds to be competitive but not dominant. What they have instead is something rarer: a generational rookie.

Sal Stewart was named the National League Rookie of the Month for March and April on Monday. The Cincinnati third baseman hit .281 with nine home runs and 29 RBI in 31 games during that stretch — leading all rookies in RBI and ranking among the best numbers in the entire league.

The RBI total, in particular, stands out. Rookies producing 29 RBI in a month-and-a-half of play at that level of consistency is extraordinarily rare. Stewart wasn't just keeping up with veterans — he was outpacing them.

His .726 slugging percentage at one point ranked among the best in MLB. His OPS topped 1.100 for an extended stretch. He has home run power, the ability to put the ball in play, and — evidenced by Tuesday's three-RBI performance in the Reds' 12-6 win — clutch timing.

The NL Rookie of the Year race is his to lose. Through May 6, Stewart is the clear frontrunner. The question isn't whether he can sustain it — the question is whether any other rookie can match it.

### STORY 5:

#### Long-Form Post -- 6:00 PM ET

The best home run race in baseball right now isn't happening in any single ballpark. It's happening across the AL.

Aaron Judge of the Yankees and Munetaka Murakami of the White Sox are tied at 14 home runs — the MLB lead heading into May 6. Both are on a pace that would project to 60 or more home runs over a full 162-game season. Both represent completely different stories.

Judge is the reigning home run king, a 6'7" force of nature who set the American League record in 2022. He's responded to Murakami's emergence by going on a tear: 11 home runs in his last 21 games. The Yankees swept the Baltimore Orioles four games to zero (outscoring them by 29 runs) over the weekend, and Judge was at the center of it all — including a first-inning two-run shot off Shane Baz on May 4.

Murakami brings his own remarkable stat line to the competition: of his first 12 extra-base hits this season, all 12 have been home runs. That has never happened in the MLB record books going back to 1900. His 14 HRs came in a 5-game streak that tied the White Sox franchise record.

Two players. Two teams. One lead. This is one of 2026's most compelling narratives, and it's only getting started.

Who wins the 2026 MLB home run title — Judge or Murakami?

### STORY 6:

#### Long-Form Post -- 10:00 PM ET

Going into 2026, the Detroit Tigers were supposed to have one of baseball's most exciting rotations: Tarik Skubal (back-to-back AL Cy Young), Framber Valdez, Jack Flaherty, and a deep staff.

That plan has unraveled fast.

Skubal is out 2-3 months after arthroscopic surgery to remove loose bodies from his left elbow. His arm locked up during a bullpen session. The two-time Cy Young winner — who was projected to command the largest pitching contract in free agency history at year's end — will miss at least half the season.

It gets worse. Casey Mize is also on the injured list. Justin Verlander is unavailable. Reese Olson missed the entire season after shoulder surgery in February. Jackson Jobe only just threw his first bullpen session after Tommy John surgery.

What's left: Framber Valdez, Jack Flaherty, Keider Montero, and Ty Madden stepping in for Skubal.

That's still a functional rotation — but it's not the rotation Detroit envisioned. The AL Central race is now wide open in ways it wasn't a week ago. And the free agency implications for Skubal are significant: the question is no longer how much money he'll make in November, but whether he can get healthy enough in August to prove he belongs on the highest of all the contracts.

The Tigers' season just got a lot harder.
