Three Starters Down, Still Winning: Atlanta's Rotation Crisis and the Men Holding It Together

By Ballpark Banter | April 1, 2026

Before the first pitch of the 2026 season, the Atlanta Braves lost three of their five projected starting pitchers. Spencer Schwellenbach and Bryce Waldrep are both on the injured list and not expected back until summer. Spencer Strider, still working his way back to his pre-Tommy John form, began the year dealing with an oblique strain. Joey Wentz tore his ACL covering first base before the calendar turned to April. And shortstop Ha-Seong Kim is out after tearing a tendon in his right middle finger — an injury sustained when he slipped on ice in January.

This is the kind of start to a season that breaks franchises. It has not broken the Braves.

On Wednesday, Atlanta beat the Oakland Athletics 4-0. The win moved them to 4-1 on the season. They are winning games they have no business winning given the shape of their pitching staff.

Sale and Lopez: The Load-Bearing Walls

When the projected rotation collapsed, two pitchers stepped into the gap: Chris Sale and Reynaldo Lopez.

Sale is 37 years old. He has been through injuries of his own — multiple stints on the IL, Tommy John surgery, the long road back to sustained effectiveness. But he pitches with the intelligence and precision of a veteran who has been in situations like this before, and his presence in the rotation gives Atlanta a trustworthy No. 1 option on days when the team cannot afford to lose.

Lopez has developed into one of the more consistent starting pitchers in the National League. He works with a reliable four-pitch mix, commands the zone, and has the kind of durability that makes him a genuine workhorse option if the rest of the rotation stays shelved. He and Sale, together, form a top-two pairing that most teams would envy — even if the Braves' situation around them is difficult.

The Silver Lining

Atlanta's lineup remains dangerous. The offense has been doing the heavy lifting while the pitching staff repairs itself, and the Braves have shown an ability to manufacture runs that masks some of the rotation's thin depth. The 4-0 win over Oakland was a shutout — which means whoever took the ball on Wednesday delivered exactly what Sale and Lopez have been providing: competitive, scoreless baseball.

The realistic best case for Atlanta: Strider's oblique heals ahead of schedule, and Schwellenbach or Waldrep makes it back in July rather than August. That would give them a four-man rotation for the second half — which is manageable for a contending team.

What's Next

The Braves' season is being built month by month rather than planned all at once. The question for April: can Sale and Lopez give them enough to stay competitive while the cavalry heals? Through the first week of the season, the answer is yes. What happens in May and June will determine whether this story ends in October.

Watch Atlanta closely. This is the most interesting injury narrative in baseball right now — and somehow, they're winning.
