José Ramírez at 33 — How the Guardians' Legend Is Making Franchise History While Playing His Best Baseball

By Jake Torres | April 25, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

José Ramírez leads Major League Baseball in stolen bases. He is 33 years old and plays third base.

That sentence contains a fact that would have seemed implausible earlier in his career, when Ramírez's power was the headline and his baserunning was a secondary weapon. In 2026, he is running with the best of them — 11 stolen bases through the first weeks of the season — and doing everything else that has made him one of the most complete players in the game for more than a decade.

The Guardians beat the Blue Jays 8-6 on Friday in a performance that encapsulated what Ramírez brings to this team: production at every phase of the game, leadership that elevates those around him, and a relentlessness that makes him a problem for opposing pitchers in every plate appearance. He was named AL Player of the Week for the most recent award period. At 33, the accolades have not slowed.

A Month of Franchise History

April 2026 has been remarkable for Ramírez in ways that go beyond statistics. On April 6, he appeared in his 1,620th career game with the Guardians — breaking the franchise's all-time games played record, previously held by Terry Turner since 1918. Turner's mark had stood for 108 years. It fell to a player who has spent his entire 13-year career in Cleveland, never leaving, never chasing a bigger payday or a championship contender elsewhere.

Five days later, on April 11 in Atlanta, Ramírez hit a home run against Braves starter Martin Perez. It was, by any conventional measure, a routine at-bat in a regular-season game. But it was also the home run that made Ramírez the first Cleveland Guardian to homer against all 29 other MLB teams. Every opponent. Every ballpark. Every pitching style. He has hit a ball over the fence against all of them.

His 287 career home runs — all with Cleveland's franchise — rank second in franchise history, behind only Jim Thome's 337. He is in his 13th season and showing no sign of finishing any time soon.

Why Age Is Not the Story

For most professional athletes, 33 is the age at which the decline becomes visible. Reaction time slows. Injuries accumulate. The gap between what a player was and what he is becomes a subject for feature articles and radio debates. For Ramírez, the opposite has happened. His stolen base totals have climbed. His plate discipline has remained elite. His defensive range, which was always underrated, continues to hold up at a position that is unforgiving to aging legs.

Part of the explanation is physical: Ramírez has always been compact, explosive, and efficient in his movements rather than relying on raw athleticism that fades with age. Part of it is mental: he processes the game at a level that allows him to compensate for any physical adjustments that come with a longer career. And part of it is simply the kind of professional approach that produces longevity in a sport that is designed to find and expose weaknesses.

Whatever the reason, the result is a player who is making franchise history at 33 and showing every indication that he intends to make more of it before he is finished.

What's Next

The Guardians are quietly building the case that they are a legitimate playoff contender in the American League. Ramírez is the reason the argument is taken seriously. With the regular season still in its early weeks, every game Ramírez plays adds another entry to a franchise record book that already bears his name in multiple places. When the Hall of Fame conversation for Ramírez begins in earnest — and it will, probably very soon — this April will be a month worth remembering.
