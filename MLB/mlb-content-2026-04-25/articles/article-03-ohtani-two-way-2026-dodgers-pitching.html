Shohei Ohtani's Two-Way 2026: Pitching at 0.50 ERA While Batting Every Day — The Dodgers Are Doing It Again

By Ryan Calloway | April 25, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

Shohei Ohtani's 53-game on-base streak ended last week. He went 0-for-4 as a hitter in a 3-0 loss to the San Francisco Giants. It was, by any measure, a bad day at the plate.

On the same night, Ohtani threw six scoreless innings and struck out seven batters as a pitcher.

That is Shohei Ohtani's 2026 baseball season in one paragraph. When one part of his game steps back, another steps forward. He is 2-0 with a 0.50 ERA and 18 strikeouts through his first three pitching starts, averaging six innings per outing. As a designated hitter, he is batting .258 with an .854 OPS. The Los Angeles Dodgers are 21-6, the best record in baseball.

The Pitching Performance in Context

A 0.50 ERA through 18 innings is not a number that requires heavy statistical context. It means Ohtani has allowed one earned run in three starts spanning six innings each — against major league competition, in a lineup that will see him again two days later as a hitter. The novelty of the two-way approach still generates the questions: can you really do both at an elite level for a full season? Through April, the answer is yes, definitively.

The Dodgers have spaced his starts at every six or seven days rather than the standard five-day rotation. The plan calls for roughly 25 pitching starts over the full season, protecting his arm while maximizing his value in the lineup every other day. Manager Dave Roberts has been deliberate about managing Ohtani's workload — no back-to-back starts, no exceeding six innings unless the situation is extraordinary.

The early returns suggest the plan is working. Ohtani's 18 strikeouts through 18 innings (an exactly 1.0 strikeouts-per-inning rate) reflect the same elite stuff he's brought throughout his career, with the command and pitch selection of a pitcher who has been preparing for this role for years.

The Japanese Rotation That Has Never Happened Before

The Dodgers accomplished something unprecedented in early April: they started three consecutive Japanese-born pitchers in the same rotation turn. Roki Sasaki on Monday. Ohtani on Tuesday. Yoshinobu Yamamoto on Wednesday. The first time in Major League Baseball history that a team started Japanese-born pitchers in three consecutive games.

Sasaki, whose new pitch is generating significant interest around the league, has been effective in his own right. Yamamoto has been the steady, reliable presence the Dodgers have counted on since signing him to his record contract. Together with Ohtani, the three represent the most formidable Japanese-born pitching staff ever assembled on a single roster — and one of the most formidable pitching staffs in recent baseball history, regardless of nationality.

The Dodgers lead baseball with a 1.05 team WHIP. Their opponent batting average of .193 is the best in the sport. Every element of their pitching is exceptional, from the starters through the bullpen.

What's Next

Ohtani's next pitching start is expected within the week. Every start he makes carries the same dual narrative: a historic performance as a pitcher who is also the team's most productive designated hitter. The Dodgers, defending back-to-back World Series champions, look like the best team in baseball through April. If they sustain this level of performance into October, Ohtani could be pitching in the World Series while also batting in the middle of one of the best lineups in the sport. That story has never existed before in baseball. It is happening right now in Los Angeles.
