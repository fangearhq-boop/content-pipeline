Every NL Central Team Has a Winning Record — Baseball Has Never Seen a Division Like This

By Jake Torres | April 25, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

Through April 24, 2026, every team in the National League Central has a winning record.

The Cincinnati Reds are 15-8, leading the division. The Pittsburgh Pirates, Chicago Cubs, and St. Louis Cardinals are all 13-9. The Milwaukee Brewers, the division's "worst" team right now, are 12-9. Five teams. Five winning records. One division with a cumulative record of 66-44 and a .600 winning percentage.

In the entire history of Major League Baseball's Divisional Era — dating back to 1969, when the sport split into divisions — no division has ever finished a full season with all of its teams above .500. We are watching something unfold that has never reached the finish line before. And it is happening in Cincinnati, Pittsburgh, Chicago, St. Louis, and Milwaukee.

What the History Says

The closest the sport came to this feat was the 2005 National League East, when the Atlanta Braves, Philadelphia Phillies, New York Mets, Florida Marlins, and Washington Nationals all finished at exactly .500 or better. Not everyone was above .500 — some finished exactly at the line — and none of them matched the current NL Central's collective winning percentage through the same point in the season.

Every other year in the 57-year history of divisional play, at least one team in every division has finished below .500. Sometimes significantly below. The NL Central has produced some of baseball's most prolonged stretches of futility from the era of teams tanking for draft picks — and now, in 2026, it is producing the most collectively excellent division start in baseball history.

The reasons for this remarkable performance vary by team. Cincinnati has been building toward this for three seasons, with Elly De La Cruz developing into the most electric player in the National League and a pitching staff anchored by one of the best young rotations in the league. Pittsburgh's young core has arrived faster than anticipated. The Cubs and Cardinals continue to be capable of competing at a high level when healthy. And Milwaukee, the defending NL Central champion, remains a well-managed, well-constructed club that does not lose easy games.

What Happens When They Play Each Other

The most fascinating element of an all-winning-record division is that teams in this division face each other 90 times over the course of the season — and that means the division's winning percentage will, by mathematical necessity, erode as the season progresses. Every NL Central team that wins a divisional game beats another NL Central team. The math of an internally competitive division means that the collective winning record cannot sustain itself indefinitely.

But it can sustain itself longer than any previous division in history has managed. And the race within the division — no team more than three games behind the Reds' pace right now — will be one of the most compelling divisional stories of the entire 2026 season. Every NL Central series, for the next four months, carries the weight of a race that has no room for prolonged losing streaks.

What's Next

The NL Central standings will shift with every series played. But the division's collective excellence — already historically unprecedented through April — means that whatever team emerges as division champion in October will have earned it against genuine competition. If you are not watching NL Central baseball right now, you are missing the most balanced and historically remarkable division race in baseball history. The question is not whether this will remain unbroken through October — it almost certainly will not. The question is how long it lasts. Every day it continues is another day of baseball history being made.
