Munetaka Murakami Hits HR No. 11, White Sox Rookie Tied for MLB Lead With Alvarez

By Marcus Cole | April 25, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

Munetaka Murakami's home run Friday night did not just win a game. It tied him for the major league lead.

The Chicago White Sox designated hitter launched his 11th home run of the season in the team's 5-4 win over the Washington Nationals on April 24, matching Yordan Alvarez of the Houston Astros for the most home runs in baseball through April 25. A Japanese-born rookie on a rebuilding team is tied for the MLB home run title in late April. That is not a sentence you have ever read before.

Murakami's 11 HRs through his first 25 games are the most by any Japanese-born player through that threshold in MLB history — surpassing the pace Shohei Ohtani set when he came to the United States. Through April 23, Murakami had also tied the MLB rookie record by homering in five consecutive games. He was the first rookie in baseball history to reach 10 home runs before May 1.

The White Sox Found Something Special

Chicago signed Murakami to a two-year, $34 million contract during the offseason after his run in Nippon Professional Baseball. The deal looked reasonable at the time — a measured bet on a power hitter making the jump from NPB to MLB. It now looks like one of the most valuable contracts in baseball.

The White Sox have been rebuilding for multiple seasons now, and for most of that time, "interesting" and "Chicago White Sox" have not been frequent companions. Murakami has changed that equation dramatically. His 5-4 win over the Nationals on Friday was the kind of victory that is easy to overlook in a 162-game schedule — but it is wins like this, powered by Murakami's extraordinary power display, that shift rebuilding timelines and change how a franchise is perceived.

Adding to the intrigue is Colson Montgomery, Chicago's other young power bat, who has six home runs of his own. The two together represent the most surprising rookie power combination in the sport. If Murakami and Montgomery maintain anything close to their current pace, the White Sox will have the offensive foundation of something genuinely compelling.

The Historical Context

Murakami's 11 HRs through 25 games put him on a 71-home run pace over a full 162-game season. No one will sustain that pace, but the underlying production is real. His .256 batting average with a 1.026 OPS and 19 RBIs tells the story of a hitter who is connecting for power and doing it consistently, not just getting lucky in a week of games.

For Japanese-born players in MLB, the home run records carry particular significance. Murakami passed the 24-game threshold with more HRs than Ohtani ever hit through the same point — the player who has been the gold standard for Japanese-born power in the major leagues. That comparison will not last a full season, but it is real right now, and it represents something meaningful for what Murakami is building.

What's Next

Murakami is tied for the MLB home run lead at 11, alongside Yordan Alvarez. Alvarez is one of the most feared hitters in the sport, and Murakami — a 25-year-old playing his first major league season — is keeping pace. The race between them for the home run title, if it extends into May and June, will be one of the most compelling storylines of the 2026 season. Watch Murakami's at-bats closely this weekend: history is happening, one swing at a time.
