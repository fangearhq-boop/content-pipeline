## STORY 1: Murakami HR No. 11 — Tied for MLB Lead

#### Long-Form Post — 8:00 AM ET
The Chicago White Sox are not supposed to be one of the most exciting teams in baseball right now.

They lost 121 games two years ago. They're rebuilding. They're supposed to be years away. And yet: Munetaka Murakami hit his 11th home run of the season Friday night, a shot that put the White Sox ahead in a 5-4 win over the Nationals and tied him with Yordan Alvarez for the major league home run lead.

A Japanese-born rookie on a rebuilding team tied for the MLB home run lead in late April. Let that land for a moment.

Murakami's 11 HRs in his first 25 games are the most by any Japanese-born player through that threshold in MLB history, surpassing the pace set by Shohei Ohtani. Through April 23, he had tied the MLB rookie record by homering in five consecutive games. He was the first rookie ever to reach 10 HRs before May 1.

The White Sox signed him to a two-year, $34 million deal during the offseason. It may already be the most valuable contract in the sport. And Colson Montgomery — the team's other young power bat — has six home runs of his own. The most unlikely power duo in baseball is playing on Chicago's South Side.

## STORY 2: José Ramírez Feature

#### Long-Form Post — 10:30 AM ET
José Ramírez is 33 years old, and he might be playing the best baseball of his career.

He leads all of Major League Baseball in stolen bases — with 11. At 33. As a third baseman. That alone is remarkable enough for a story. But in April 2026, Ramírez has done things that go well beyond a stat line.

On April 6, he appeared in his 1,620th career game with the Guardians, surpassing a franchise games-played record held by Terry Turner since 1918. Let that sit for a second: a mark that had stood for 108 years, shattered by a 33-year-old who shows no signs of slowing down.

Five days later, on April 11, he hit a home run against the Atlanta Braves — becoming the first Cleveland Guardian to homer against all 29 other Major League teams. That's franchise history, accomplished by a player who has spent his entire 13-year career with one organization.

The Guardians beat the Blue Jays 8-6 on Friday, and Ramírez's presence remains the engine of a Cleveland team that quietly looks like a legitimate playoff contender.

When someone asks you why baseball matters, tell them about José Ramírez. He's one of a kind.

## STORY 3: Ohtani Two-Way Power

#### Long-Form Post — 12:30 PM ET
Shohei Ohtani's on-base streak ended at 53 games last week. And on the very same night it ended, he threw six innings of shutout ball.

That's Ohtani's 2026 in miniature. When one thing goes sideways, something extraordinary replaces it.

Through his first three pitching starts of the season, Ohtani is 2-0 with a 0.50 ERA and 18 strikeouts in 18 innings. As a hitter, he's batting .258 with an .854 OPS. And the Dodgers are 21-6 — the best record in baseball.

The Dodgers have also done something baseball has never seen before: they started three consecutive Japanese-born pitchers in the same rotation turn — Roki Sasaki on Monday, Ohtani on Tuesday, Yoshinobu Yamamoto on Wednesday. First time in MLB history.

Ohtani is expected to make about 25 starts this season, pitching every 6-7 days. The plan is to protect his arm while keeping his bat in the lineup every day. So far, the plan is working — spectacularly.

The defending back-to-back World Series champions have the best pitcher, best team, and most unique roster construction in baseball. And they're not slowing down.

## STORY 5: NL Central All Over .500

#### Long-Form Post — 6:00 PM ET
Baseball history is unfolding quietly in the National League Central, one win at a time.

Every team in the NL Central has a winning record right now. That's the Reds (15-8), Pirates (13-9), Cubs (13-9), Cardinals (13-9), and Brewers (12-9) — five teams, five winning records, zero losers.

It sounds ordinary until you look at the history. In the entire Divisional Era — going back to 1969 — no division has ever finished a full season with every team above .500. The closest anyone got was the 2005 NL East, when all five teams finished at exactly .500 or better. Nobody has ever finished every team over .500.

The 2026 NL Central is 66-44 collectively, a .600 winning percentage. That's remarkable. Every game within the division matters. Every series has playoff implications. The NL Central may be headed toward the most competitive intra-division race in baseball history.

We're watching something unprecedented. And it's happening in Cincinnati, Pittsburgh, Chicago, St. Louis, and Milwaukee.

## STORY 6: Early-Season Trends

#### Long-Form Post — 8:00 PM ET
Twenty-five games into the 2026 MLB season, the early data is telling a story.

Walk rates are down across the board — batters are swinging more in early counts, likely as pitchers have adjusted their approach after last year's changes in plate discipline. The result is shorter at-bats and more contact early. Whether that's a real trend or April noise, we'll know in June.

Home run production is up. Multiple hitters are on pace for 50-plus home runs — Murakami, Alvarez, Judge, and others are all hitting at rates that would historically rank among the season's best. The ball is flying in April.

Andy Pages leads all of baseball with a .409 batting average through 25 games — a number almost certain to regress but remarkable for now.

The Mets, after a 12-game losing streak that ended April 22, are now 8-16 — and Francisco Lindor is dealing with a calf injury. The Royals, picked by many to contend, have also struggled early. Not every team that was supposed to be good is good.

The teams that look like the real deal right now: Dodgers, Yankees, Guardians, Reds. Keep watching.

## STORY 7: Dodgers Dynasty

#### Long-Form Post — 10:00 PM ET
It's one thing to win a World Series. It's another to win two in a row. And it's something entirely different to come back in Year 3 looking even better.

The Los Angeles Dodgers are 21-6 — the best record in baseball — and the numbers they're putting up as a team are staggering. They lead the majors in home runs, batting average, on-base percentage, and slugging percentage. They also lead in WHIP (1.05) and opponent batting average (.193). They are, by almost every meaningful metric, the best team in both offense and pitching.

Shohei Ohtani is pitching at 0.50 ERA. Yoshinobu Yamamoto has been outstanding. Roki Sasaki has introduced a new pitch that's generating buzz around the league. Together, they represent the most dominant Japanese-born pitching staff in baseball history — and they all pitch for the same team.

The Dodgers won the 2024 and 2025 World Series. They're playing like they expect to win the 2026 one, too. In Los Angeles, dynasty isn't a word they're shying away from.
