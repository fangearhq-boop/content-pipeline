## STORY 1: Ohtani's 53-Game On-Base Streak Ends

#### Long-Form Post — 8:00 AM ET

Shohei Ohtani's 53-game on-base streak is over.

The streak that began on August 24, 2025 — spanning an entire offseason and into a new season — ended Wednesday night at Oracle Park in San Francisco. Ohtani went 0-for-4 against the Giants and the Dodgers lost 3-0.

But here's the thing about how it ended: Ohtani pitched six scoreless innings in the same game. He couldn't get on base at the plate. He couldn't be scored upon on the mound. That's the two-way contradiction that makes Ohtani unlike anyone who has ever played the game.

By the numbers, the streak was historic. At 53 consecutive games reaching base, Ohtani set the record for the longest such streak by any Japanese-born MLB player — surpassing Ichiro Suzuki's 43-game mark. He also tied Shawn Green for second in Dodgers franchise history. Only Duke Snider's 58-game streak from 1954 stands higher in the Dodgers record books.

On Tuesday, Ohtani extended the streak to 53 with an infield single against the Giants, tying Green. On Wednesday, the same Giants team ended it.

Dodgers manager Dave Roberts is now reportedly mulling whether to rest Ohtani more often on his pitching days, given the physical toll of appearing in the lineup as a designated hitter on the same night he throws 100+ pitches. That workload management conversation will now take center stage.

The streak is over. The legend of Shohei Ohtani keeps growing. ⚾

## STORY 2: Mets Snap 12-Game Losing Streak

#### Long-Form Post — 10:30 AM ET

The New York Mets won a baseball game Wednesday night.

Yes, really.

Mark Vientos delivered a go-ahead RBI single in the eighth inning, Luke Weaver got the final out, and the Mets beat the Minnesota Twins 3-2 to snap a 12-game losing streak — the longest in franchise history since 2002.

It was the Mets' first victory since April 7. Fourteen days without a win. The losses included gut-punching extra-inning defeats, blown leads in the late innings, and an offense that averaged just 1.83 runs per game during the skid while the rest of baseball averaged at least three.

Juan Soto returned to the lineup for the first time in weeks, providing a boost to the order. The crowd at Citi Field reacted with genuine relief.

And then Francisco Lindor exited in the fourth inning with left calf tightness. Of course he did.

The Mets are 8-16 after the win. The historical context is brutal: no team in the World Series era has ever made the postseason after losing 12 straight games in April. The climb is steep, and it starts with Lindor's health now in question.

But the Mets won. For one night, that's enough. ⚾

## STORY 3: Aaron Judge 9 HRs, On Pace for 66

#### Long-Form Post — 12:30 PM ET

Aaron Judge has figured something out in the first inning.

Through 22 games of the 2026 season, Judge leads all of MLB with nine home runs. That's notable on its own. But the statistic inside the statistic is what makes it remarkable: five of those nine home runs have come in the first inning.

In first-inning plate appearances this season, Judge is batting .450. His OPS is 1.726. His RBI total is eight. These numbers, in a 27-out game, are coming before the lineup has even turned over once.

The effect on the Yankees is measurable. New York is 11-3 when they score first. Judge is often the reason they do.

His Statcast numbers back up the performance: 91.3-91.7 mph average exit velocity, 28.3% barrel rate. He's hitting the ball hard, in the air, and frequently. At his current pace, Judge is on track to hit 66 home runs over a full 162-game schedule.

The Yankees are 13-9 and leading the AL East. Judge is the biggest reason why. ⚾

## STORY 5: Murakami 7 HRs — Japanese-Born Record

#### Long-Form Post — 5:30 PM ET

Yoshinobu Murakami is quietly rewriting one of baseball's most watched record categories.

The Chicago White Sox infielder has hit seven home runs through his first 21 MLB games — more than any Japanese-born player through the same point in their career. He surpassed Shohei Ohtani, who hit five home runs in his first 21 MLB games.

In a season that has already been a showcase for Japanese baseball talent — Ohtani's now-ended 53-game on-base streak, Yoshinobu Yamamoto's continued development on the Dodgers' pitching staff — Murakami is writing his own chapter.

He's doing it for the White Sox, which makes the milestone even more meaningful for a franchise that has struggled to provide fans with much to celebrate in recent seasons. Seven home runs in 21 games gives Murakami one of the most electric starts of any international player in recent memory.

Keep an eye on him. The 2026 season is becoming a Japanese baseball history book. ⚾
