Shohei Ohtani's 53-Game On-Base Streak Ends — He Also Threw 6 Scoreless Innings That Night

By Marcus Cole | April 23, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

Shohei Ohtani went 0-for-4 on Wednesday night. He also pitched six scoreless innings. The Dodgers lost 3-0. And the most remarkable individual hitting streak of the 2026 season came to an end.

Ohtani's on-base streak ended at 53 consecutive games against the San Francisco Giants at Oracle Park, finishing a run that dated back to August 24, 2025. The streak crossed an entire offseason, carried into a new season, and ended not with a thunderclap but with a quiet 0-for-4 night that still featured one of the great two-way performances anyone can produce when he throws six scoreless innings in the same game he can't get on base.

By the numbers, the streak was a piece of history. At 53 games, Ohtani set the record for the longest on-base streak ever by a Japanese-born MLB player, surpassing Ichiro Suzuki's mark of 43 games. He also tied Shawn Green for second in Dodgers franchise history — Green reached that mark in 2000. Only Duke Snider's 58-game streak from 1954 sits higher in the Dodgers record books.

How It Ended — And the Twist

The night before — Tuesday, April 21 — Ohtani had extended his streak to 53 with an infield single against the same Giants team, tying Green. He then took the mound Wednesday in what became a 3-0 loss, and the lineup delivered nothing. Four at-bats, four outs. The streak was over.

But the same night the bat went quiet, the arm was dominant. Six innings, no earned runs, the kind of start that would be the centerpiece of the story for any other pitcher. For Ohtani, it was the backdrop to the end of a hitting milestone. That is the impossible duality of what he does.

Dodgers manager Dave Roberts acknowledged after the game that he is now considering whether to rest Ohtani more often on his scheduled pitching days, given the physical toll of appearing in the lineup as a designated hitter on the same night he is working through 100 or more pitches on the mound. That workload management question — one that Ohtani's unique profile forces every manager to confront — will be a continuing conversation throughout 2026.

What the Streak Meant

For the better part of eight months, Ohtani reached base in every game he played. The streak covered multiple pitching starts in 2025, all of the 2026 spring, and 23 games of the new season. It outlasted weather delays, lineup shuffles, the start of a new chapter with a new rotation, and the arrival of an entirely new cast of opponents studying how to get him out.

The historical peer group for a 53-game on-base streak is small. The 2026 version of Ohtani — fully committed to pitching again after his Tommy John recovery — was producing this streak while also working as a starter every five days. No player in the history of the game has ever combined two-way output at this level with this consistency.

What's Next

The Dodgers are 16-7 and remain the class of the National League. Ohtani's next start on the mound will draw significant attention as Roberts evaluates whether to limit his lineup appearances on those days. The streak may be over, but the conversation about Ohtani's place among the greatest players in baseball history will never fully pause — not with what he continues to do every time he steps on the field, or the mound, or both.
