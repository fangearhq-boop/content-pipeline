Yoshinobu Murakami Sets Japanese-Born HR Record with 7 in First 21 Games

By Jake Torres | April 23, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

Yoshinobu Murakami has done something no Japanese-born player had ever done through the first 21 games of an MLB career.

The Chicago White Sox designated hitter entered Wednesday with seven home runs through 21 games — the most by any Japanese-born player through that stretch in Major League history, surpassing Shohei Ohtani's five home runs through the same threshold. The record places Murakami in an exclusive conversation almost immediately upon his arrival in the American League.

The White Sox, who entered the 2026 season as a rebuilding franchise still working through the consequences of one of the most difficult stretches in recent baseball history, have watched their new designated hitter transform individual at-bats into a running historical reference point. Seven home runs through 21 games is elite production by any measure. As a record for players crossing from Japan to MLB, it is a statement about what Murakami's power can mean at the highest level.

The Record in Context

Ohtani's five home runs through his first 21 games stood as the benchmark for Japanese-born players transitioning to MLB power production. That comparison carries significant weight given what Ohtani has become — a two-way player who set the American League single-season home run record in 2022 and has redefined what is possible for players of his background in this league.

Murakami reaching seven through the same sample means he entered Wednesday's games having outpaced one of the most celebrated power hitters in the world during the exact same early-career window. The immediate caveat is that Ohtani was a two-way player even in those early games, managing his pitching workload alongside his hitting. Murakami arrives in Chicago as a pure designated hitter, able to focus entirely on his offensive output. That distinction matters when making direct comparisons.

What matters more than the comparison is the production itself: seven home runs through 21 games is a pace of approximately 54 over a full 162-game season. Sustained or not, the rate reflects elite raw power translating from Japan's NPB to the American League without the adjustment period that frequently slows power hitters in their first months.

What It Means for the White Sox

Chicago's rebuilding timeline has been painful and prolonged. The White Sox are not a playoff contender in 2026, and their roster reflects a franchise still in the process of restocking its talent base. Murakami's production does not change their standings position in the American League Central, but it offers something valuable: a genuine offensive centerpiece around which the next phase of the rebuild can be constructed.

The White Sox signed Murakami this offseason as part of a broader international talent acquisition strategy. His first 21 games have validated that investment ahead of any reasonable schedule. A player capable of hitting home runs at this rate — in a new league, against unfamiliar pitching, in an unfamiliar environment — is a rare asset regardless of the team context around him.

What's Next

The record is established. The larger question is whether Murakami's power production can sustain through the second month of the season as opposing pitchers develop plans to limit his damage. The adjustment period that follows a hot start is the true test of any new MLB hitter, and Murakami will face increasingly sophisticated approaches as the league accumulates data on his tendencies. For now, through 21 games, he has cleared a historical bar that no Japanese-born player had previously reached, and that accomplishment belongs in the record books regardless of what comes next.
