Mets Snap 12-Game Losing Streak with 3-2 Win Over Twins — But Lose Lindor to Calf Injury

By Jake Torres | April 23, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

The New York Mets won a baseball game on Wednesday, April 22. That sentence sounds simple, but for a franchise that had not won since April 7, it carried weight that goes beyond the box score.

Mark Vientos delivered a go-ahead RBI single in the eighth inning, Luke Weaver closed it out, and the Mets defeated the Minnesota Twins 3-2 to end their 12-game losing streak — the longest in franchise history since 2002. Juan Soto returned to the lineup, providing a welcome boost. Citi Field breathed a collective sigh of relief.

And then Francisco Lindor exited the game in the fourth inning with left calf tightness, turning a moment of celebration into something more complicated.

How the Win Happened

Weaver's performance on the mound gave the Mets a chance. He pitched with the kind of efficiency and composure that had been absent from too many Mets outings during the losing streak. The eighth inning was the turning point — the game was tied, the crowd was restless, and Vientos stepped in and delivered exactly the kind of clutch hit that had been so elusive during the preceding two weeks.

The final score was 3-2. It was not a blowout, not a statement, not a sweep. It was one win after 12 consecutive losses, and in that context, it meant everything.

The historical weight of this moment deserves acknowledgment: the Mets' 12-game losing streak was the fourth-longest in franchise history and the longest in 24 years. No team in the World Series era has ever made the postseason after losing 12 straight games in April. That statistic does not mean the Mets' season is over — seasons have been rebuilt from worse starting points — but it defines the scale of the challenge ahead.

The Lindor Shadow

Francisco Lindor is the soul of the Mets roster. He is the captain, the shortstop, the tone-setter, and the face of a franchise that paid him accordingly. His departure from Wednesday's game in the fourth inning with left calf tightness cast an immediate shadow over the celebration.

The Mets had managed just 1.83 runs per game during the losing streak. With Soto's return and the lineup gradually reassembling, there was reason to believe the offense was about to turn a corner. Lindor's health is now the central concern. A significant absence from their best position player would make the already steep climb toward playoff relevance even steeper.

The Mets will evaluate Lindor's status over the coming days. How long he is out will go a long way in determining whether Wednesday's win was the start of a turnaround or a brief interlude in a difficult season.

What's Next

The Mets are 8-16. No team with a 12-game losing streak in April has made the postseason, but the history books don't prevent teams from writing new chapters. The schedule ahead, Lindor's health, and whether the offense can approach anything resembling its preseason potential will determine whether this win is the beginning of something or just a break in the difficulty. At minimum, the Mets can wake up Thursday knowing they won. In a season that has had precious few of those moments, that matters.
