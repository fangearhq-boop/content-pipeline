Aaron Judge Leads MLB with 9 Home Runs — 5 in the First Inning, On Pace for 66

By Ryan Calloway | April 23, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

Aaron Judge is doing something specific in the first inning of baseball games that goes beyond ordinary early-game production.

Through 22 games of the 2026 season, Judge leads all of Major League Baseball with nine home runs. That top-of-the-leaderboard position is notable. But the statistic within the statistic is what defines his impact: five of those nine home runs have come in the first inning. The game opens, the lineup hasn't turned over, and Aaron Judge is already making pitchers and pitching coaches regret their decisions.

In first-inning plate appearances this season, Judge is batting .450. His on-base plus slugging percentage is 1.726. He has driven in eight runs before the rest of the stadium has fully settled in. The Yankees are 11-3 when they score first, and Judge's first-inning production is a primary engine of why.

The Numbers That Define the Season So Far

Judge's Statcast profile explains the production. His average exit velocity sits between 91.3 and 91.7 miles per hour, with a 28.3 percent barrel rate — among the highest in baseball. He is hitting the ball hard, in the air, and in the direction that matters. Home runs are not accidental at these numbers; they are a predictable output of elite contact quality.

At his current rate of nine home runs through 22 games, Judge is on pace to hit 66 over a full 162-game schedule. That would place him in historically elite company and raise immediate comparisons to his 2022 season, when he set the American League record with 62 home runs. That comparison is premature — 22 games is a very small sample, and the pace rarely sustains perfectly — but it reflects how extraordinary the early production has been.

The Yankees, at 13-9, are positioned well in the AL East. Judge's bat is the biggest reason why. When he comes to the plate in the first inning, opposing pitchers already know they are likely to face the most dangerous hitter in the game at maximum leverage, with the lineup ahead of him having had zero plate appearances.

What It Means for the Yankees

New York entered 2026 with championship ambitions, as it has most seasons, and Judge's performance through the first month is a reason to believe those ambitions are grounded in reality rather than wishful thinking. The Yankees' 11-3 record when scoring first suggests that Judge's early-inning home run pace isn't just a counting stat — it translates directly into wins.

The challenge, as always, is sustaining it. Judge has dealt with injury in past seasons, and the demands of a 162-game schedule are real. But through 22 games, there is no player in baseball more valuable to his team's chances of winning each night.

What's Next

Watch Judge's first-inning at-bats as a bellwether for the Yankees' overall performance. His continued health and production through April will set the tone for the season. With the AL East competitive and the Yankees in position to lead it, every Judge home run — particularly those that come before the lineup has turned over — defines the ceiling of how good this team can be. The pace of 66 home runs is theoretical. The nine he already has are real.
