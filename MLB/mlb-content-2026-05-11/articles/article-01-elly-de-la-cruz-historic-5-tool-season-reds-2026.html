Elly De La Cruz Is Becoming a 5-Tool Force — Leading All MLB Shortstops in WAR in 2026

By Marcus Cole | Ballpark Banter | May 11, 2026

The Cincinnati Reds shortstop has 10 home runs, 8 stolen bases, and leads every shortstop in baseball in Wins Above Replacement through the first six weeks of the season.

Elly De La Cruz is having the kind of season that reminds you why the hype was always justified.

The 24-year-old Reds shortstop leads all Major League Baseball shortstops in Wins Above Replacement this season with a 1.9 WAR, ahead of Kansas City's Bobby Witt Jr. at 1.8. He's doing it with 10 home runs, 8 stolen bases, a .265/.337/.510 slash line, and an average exit velocity of 94.4 mph — a number that reflects not just power, but consistent quality of contact across a full lineup card.

On Sunday, he went 3-for-3 with two runs scored in a 5-0 Cincinnati win over the Houston Astros — the kind of contribution that makes the Reds' offense click.

The Historic Start at the Position

Baseball has a way of recording firsts, and De La Cruz has been accumulating them quietly. He was the first shortstop since 1900 to record five home runs and five stolen bases within 16 games of a season — a combination that speaks to the rarity of a true power-speed threat at the most demanding defensive position on the infield.

Shortstops who hit for power tend to sacrifice speed. Shortstops who steal bases tend to sacrifice power. De La Cruz doesn't choose — he does both, and the numbers reflect it across a full season of plate appearances rather than just a hot start.

The Approach Is Evolving

What's different about De La Cruz in 2026 isn't just the numbers — it's the approach. His 150 OPS+ and .412 weighted on-base average suggest a hitter who has made tangible improvements in pitch selection and plate discipline. He has always had the physical tools; the refinement is what's making those tools translate into elite production.

De La Cruz put together a 25-homer, 67-steal season in 2024 and followed it with 22 homers and 37 steals in 2025. In 2026, the power numbers are trending back upward while the approach at the plate has improved. The combination is what separates a good player from a great one.

He's only 24. Players at this position, at this age, with this skillset don't stop improving.

Sunday Was the Story in Miniature

Cincinnati's 5-0 shutout of Houston on Sunday afternoon was a perfect microcosm of what De La Cruz brings. He didn't homer — but he went 3-for-3, reached base every time up, and scored twice. His at-bats set the table for the run production that followed. He singled in a fourth-inning rally that started the scoring, then scored on a Bleday triple that made it 2-0. His energy in the lineup makes the Reds' offense harder to navigate.

That kind of contribution — not always measured in home runs — is exactly what WAR is designed to capture. De La Cruz contributes with his bat, his speed, his defense, and his baserunning. All of it adds up to 1.9 WAR, and all of it is why the Reds are watching him become something significant in 2026.

What's Next

The Reds continue their road trip following the Houston series. De La Cruz, as the engine of Cincinnati's lineup, will be the focal point of opposing pitching plans everywhere they go. He's a two-time All-Star at 24, leading his position in the most comprehensive individual metric in the game. If his development trajectory continues — and there's no reason to think it won't — the Reds have one of the most exciting players in the National League building toward the prime years of a potentially great career.
