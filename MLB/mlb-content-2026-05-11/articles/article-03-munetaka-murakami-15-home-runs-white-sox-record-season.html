Munetaka Murakami Has 15 Home Runs in 40 Games — The White Sox's Record-Breaking Rookie Is Just Getting Started

By Ryan Calloway | Ballpark Banter | May 11, 2026

The Japanese slugger is carving out a historically unusual first MLB season, setting multiple records while chasing one of the most productive power campaigns the sport has seen.

The Chicago White Sox paid Munetaka Murakami $34 million over two years in December. If you're doing the math on what that money has bought so far, start with 15 home runs in 40 games.

Murakami made his MLB debut March 26 and has not stopped hitting home runs since. He homered in each of the first three games of his career — making him just the fourth player in MLB history and the first White Sox player ever to accomplish that — and has been setting records at a rate that's difficult to track in real time.

Among his record-setting accomplishments: he hit the most home runs in MLB history before recording a first double or triple in a career. He owns the most appearances in the first game of consecutive series in recent history. He has positioned himself as one of the most unusual and captivating hitters in baseball's modern era.

What He Did in Japan

For anyone wondering where this production came from, Murakami's NPB resumé is instructive. In 2022, playing for the Tokyo Yakult Swallows, he hit 56 home runs and became the youngest Triple Crown winner in Japanese professional baseball history at age 22. That season made him one of the most coveted players in the world — a hitter whose raw power translated across the largest possible sample in Japanese baseball's top league.

The concern, as always, was the adjustment from NPB to MLB. That concern has been answered in 40 games. Murakami's swing has not required significant modification; his approach translates; and the pitching he's facing, while different, hasn't slowed him down.

Comparisons to Kyle Schwarber — a left-handed power hitter who punishes mistakes with extraordinary consistency — have followed Murakami since spring training. Through 40 games, those comparisons look reasonable.

The Pace and the Possibilities

Fifteen home runs in 40 games projects to roughly 60 home runs over a full 162-game season — a number that would be among the sport's elite single-season totals in any era. Maintaining that pace over a full major league season is extraordinarily difficult; players slow down, pitching adjusts, fatigue accumulates.

But Murakami has given no indication of slowing down. The White Sox, a team that hasn't had much to celebrate in recent years, are watching a player who is not just exciting — he's historically interesting. The record book keeps getting referenced because he keeps doing things nobody has done.

What's Next

Murakami continues in the White Sox lineup as Chicago navigates a competitive AL schedule. His home run total will continue drawing attention — every power milestone brings the comparison numbers back into focus. At 15 home runs through 40 games, the projection for a 50-plus HR season is entirely plausible. Fantasy experts have been weighing in on whether 50 is the ceiling or just a checkpoint. For White Sox fans, the more immediate question is simple: when does he hit No. 16?
