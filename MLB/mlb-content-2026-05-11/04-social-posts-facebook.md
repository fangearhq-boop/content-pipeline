He's only 24. He's leading his position in WAR. He's in the conversation for the best shortstop in the National League.

The emergence is real — and it's just getting started.

### STORY 3: Munetaka Murakami — Record-Breaking HR Season

#### Long-Form Post — 12:30 PM ET

When the Chicago White Sox signed Munetaka Murakami to a two-year, $34 million deal in December, the question was whether the two-time Central League MVP and the man who hit 56 home runs in a single NPB season could replicate that production against Major League pitching.

The answer, through his first 40 games, has been an emphatic yes.

Murakami now has 15 home runs — a pace that would put him at roughly 60 for a full season. He homered in each of the first three games of his career, becoming just the fourth player in MLB history to accomplish that feat and the first in White Sox history. He set a MLB record for the most home runs hit before recording a first double or triple in a career. He has homered in the first game of multiple consecutive series in a way that has the record books scrambling to keep up.

His comparisons to Kyle Schwarber — a pure, power-first hitter who punishes mistakes — are apt, but Murakami might have more raw power than any hitter currently in the game. His NPB resumé isn't a fluke: in 2022, he became the youngest Triple Crown winner in Japanese professional baseball history at age 22.

The White Sox paid $34 million for two years. At his current rate, they're getting that back in marketing alone, let alone wins. Murakami is must-see baseball in 2026.

### STORY 4: José Ramírez — 300 Steals, 300-300 Club in Sight

#### Long-Form Post — 3:00 PM ET

José Ramírez already has his place in Cleveland baseball history. Now he's chasing a spot in baseball history, period.

On May 2, Ramírez stole his 300th career base — swipping third in the fifth inning of a 14-6 Guardians win over the A's at Sutter Health Park. It was a lead he lost his helmet to slide for, and a milestone nobody could take away: Ramírez became only the second player in Cleveland franchise history to reach 300 career stolen bases, joining Kenny Lofton.

But the more remarkable number is what it means for the 300-300 club.

At the time of his milestone steal, Ramírez had 291 career home runs. That means nine more home runs — a realistic ask even in a matter of weeks — would make him the ninth member of the 300-300 club in baseball history. The club's membership list reads like the Hall of Fame itself: Willie Mays. Bobby and Barry Bonds. Andre Dawson. Alex Rodriguez. Carlos Beltran. Starling Marte. Trea Turner.

Ramírez fits that company. He is one of the most complete players of his generation: a power-speed combination that's been elite for a decade, a consistent presence at third base, and a leader for every Cleveland team he's suited up for.

"I set my standards a little higher than those numbers," Ramírez said after the milestone steal.

Nine home runs to go. The 300-300 club is waiting.
