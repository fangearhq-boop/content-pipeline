That is the context of what Schwarber is doing in 2026. The Philadelphia Phillies left fielder has 20 home runs through 46 games, the earliest any player in Major League Baseball history has ever reached that mark. He's already four home runs ahead of Aaron Judge (16) in the MLB home run race, with Byron Buxton and Munetaka Murakami tied at 15 in third. At his current pace, Schwarber is on track to hit approximately 67-70 home runs this season. Only two players in baseball history have ever hit 70 or more: Barry Bonds (73 in 2001) and Mark McGwire (70 in 1998). Schwarber is running ahead of both of them right now. There is a catch: the Phillies are 21-23. The team has relied almost entirely on Schwarber's production — they're 10-5 when he homers, 10-17 when he doesn't. That math tells the whole story. He is an absolute force of nature surrounded by a lineup that hasn't consistently supported him. It doesn't diminish what he's doing. We may be watching one of baseball's most historic individual seasons in real time. The closer's heart rates in San Diego were through the roof for those few minutes, but Miller delivered. For Yamamoto, it was a tough-luck loss. The Dodgers right-hander pitched quality baseball, and Shohei Ohtani had two hits. But Andujar's first-inning blow was all San Diego needed. Game 2 of this three-game series is Tuesday night.
