Schwarber's HR Pace Is Ahead of Bonds. Here's What That Actually Means.

By Jake Torres | Ballpark Banter | May 19, 2026

Barry Bonds reached his 20th home run of the season on May 19, 2001 — the year he hit 73 to set the all-time single-season record. Kyle Schwarber reached his 20th home run on May 15, 2026. Four days earlier than Bonds was at that checkpoint.

Let that settle in for a moment.

The Record Context

Schwarber's 20 home runs through 46 games are the most by any player on the earliest date in Major League Baseball history. The previous record belonged to Luis Gonzalez, who reached 20 on May 17, 2001 — the same year Bonds was chasing his 73.

At Schwarber's current pace, he is projected to hit somewhere between 67 and 70 home runs in 2026. The range depends on methodology and whether he maintains his current clip through the dog days of summer. Even at the conservative end of 67, it would represent the third-most home runs in a single season in MLB history.

Only two players have ever hit 70 or more: Bonds with 73 in 2001, and Mark McGwire with 70 in 1998. Roger Maris held the record from 1961 to 1998. Aaron Judge's 62 in 2022 currently stands as the American League record. Schwarber is running ahead of all of them except Bonds.

The Phillies Problem

There is a frustrating reality hiding behind the headline numbers. Philadelphia is 21-23. The team has been carried almost entirely by Schwarber's historic pace — they are 10-5 when he homers, and 10-17 when he doesn't. Those numbers reflect a team that has not given its franchise slugger meaningful support.

Schwarber leads all of Major League Baseball in home runs. Aaron Judge sits four back at 16, with Byron Buxton and Munetaka Murakami tied at 15 for third. The gap at the top is not close. What is missing is the lineup around Schwarber that would allow his individual brilliance to produce team wins consistently.

What's Next

The real question now is sustainability. Schwarber has built an insurmountable early lead in the home run race, and he is playing in one of baseball's best hitting environments at Citizens Bank Park. The second half of the season will be the true test — whether he can maintain this pace through August and into September, or whether the long season normalizes his numbers.

Even if he falls to 50 or 55 by season's end, it will have been one of the most extraordinary stretches of power hitting in baseball history. And if he keeps going at this rate? The record books are right in front of him.
