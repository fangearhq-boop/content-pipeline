Schwarber vs. Judge: MLB's Most Compelling Home Run Race Is Just Getting Started

By Ryan Calloway

June 3, 2026

Kyle Schwarber leads MLB with 22 home runs and is on pace for 66. Aaron Judge trails by five but is on his own historic pace. Two players tracking toward 50-plus seasons in early June — the last time MLB had this was 2001.

The Fastest Start in MLB History

Kyle Schwarber reached 20 home runs on May 15. That is the earliest any player in Major League Baseball history has hit 20 home runs in a single season. The previous record-holders — Barry Bonds, Josh Hamilton, Mark McGwire — all did it later. Schwarber did it first.

By June 3, the Philadelphia Phillies left fielder has 22 home runs and leads all of MLB. His season pace, maintained over 162 games, projects to approximately 66 home runs — which would be among the highest totals in the sport's history. Schwarber is not going to hit 66. Nobody expects that. But the pace is real, the production is real, and the lead he has built is real.

Judge Is Coming

Five home runs behind Schwarber is Aaron Judge, who has 17 on his own historic pace. As detailed elsewhere, Judge is tracking toward what could be his fifth career 50-HR season — a milestone nobody has ever achieved. The Yankees captain is not in headline-generating territory yet, but the arc of his career suggests he does not slow down in June, July, and August.

The gap between 22 and 17 sounds large. Over a 162-game season, it is virtually nothing. Schwarber and Judge play in different leagues, for different teams, in different environments — they will rarely share a box score — but they are competing against the same history and the same standard.

The Last Time This Happened

The last time two players were on 50-plus home run paces simultaneously this early in a season was 2001, when Barry Bonds (who finished with 73) and Sammy Sosa (64) were both tracking toward historic totals in early June. That season produced a dual pursuit that captivated the sport all summer.

Whether Schwarber and Judge can both sustain their current paces is genuinely uncertain — most projections regress even elite power hitters to the mean over a full season. But the possibility exists. And the possibility alone is worth paying attention to.

The Phillies' Context

Schwarber's production doesn't exist in isolation. The Phillies have gone 10-3 under new manager Don Mattingly, transforming their early-season struggles into something that looks like a genuine contender. The lineup behind Schwarber — featuring Bryce Harper, Trea Turner, and others — creates the kind of protection that allows a power hitter to see pitches worth driving rather than getting constantly worked around.

Schwarber is not simply capitalizing on a hot stretch. He is doing what he has always done — hit the ball very hard, very often — at a pace that has never been quite this fast.
