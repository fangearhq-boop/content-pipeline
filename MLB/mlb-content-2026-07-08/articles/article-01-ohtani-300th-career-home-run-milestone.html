Shohei Ohtani Hits 300th Career Home Run: The Legend Keeps Growing

By Marcus Cole | July 8, 2026

There are moments in a baseball season that stop the scroll. Tuesday night in Denver was one of them.

Shohei Ohtani led off the game against Colorado's Michael Lorenzen and drove a 2-0 pitch 409 feet to center field. Home run No. 300 of his Major League career. The first Japanese-born player in MLB history to reach the milestone. And just like that, the conversation shifted from "one of the best" to something harder to define — and easier to just watch.

It was his 20th homer of the 2026 season. It took him 1,121 games to get there.

The Company He Keeps

Speed matters in the 300 home run conversation, because everybody who plays long enough can eventually get there. What separates Ohtani's path is how fast he walked it. Among all players in MLB history, only four reached 300 home runs in fewer games: Aaron Judge (955 games), Ralph Kiner (1,087), Ryan Howard (1,093), and Juan Gonzalez (1,096). Ohtani, at 1,121 games, is fifth on that list.

There's another piece of trivia tucked into Tuesday night's homer: Ohtani hit his 300th as a leadoff shot, making him just the second player in MLB history to do so. Steve Finley is the other, having done it in 2006. The details keep piling up.

He is also the leader in career strikeouts among the 300-homer club, with 765 career Ks. The comparison the numbers keep forcing is Babe Ruth — the only other player who hit 300 home runs while also pitching at the Major League level for an extended period. And even that comparison breaks down, because Ohtani's two-way career has lasted longer than Ruth's pitching tenure, and the context of the modern game is incomparable.

All-Star Week Context

In one week, Ohtani will be at Citizens Bank Park in Philadelphia for the 2026 MLB All-Star Game. He leads all players in All-Star voting. He is 32 years old and has his best baseball ahead of him, if the trajectory of his power numbers is any guide.

The 300-homer club isn't just a counting statistic. It's a statement about sustained excellence over a career. For Ohtani, hitting the milestone as the best player on the best team in baseball — in a season where he's also the unanimous leader in All-Star votes — it lands differently than it does for a player in the twilight of a long career. This isn't a retrospective achievement. It's a marker on a path that's still being built.

The Dodgers are 60-32, the best record in MLB. Ohtani has 20 home runs and is entering the second half of the season. The legend keeps growing.

What's Next

Ohtani heads to Citizens Bank Park for MLB All-Star Week starting July 12. The All-Star Game is July 14 at 8 PM ET on FOX. The next milestone to track: at his current pace, Ohtani could reach 35-40 home runs this season, which would be a career high as a full-time hitter. The 400-homer club is a long way off, but with 300 now in the books and Ohtani's career trajectory, it's a conversation that's no longer premature.
