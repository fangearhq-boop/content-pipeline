Five Starters Down: The Blue Jays' Rotation Has Collapsed and Dylan Cease Is the Latest Casualty

By Jake Torres

May 26, 2026

Toronto built what was supposed to be one of the most formidable starting rotations in the American League. In the span of several weeks, that rotation has been almost entirely dismantled by injuries — and the ace is now the fifth starter on the shelf.

The Toronto Blue Jays placed Dylan Cease on the 15-day injured list Monday with a left hamstring strain. Cease, in his first season with the club on a seven-year, $210 million contract, left Sunday's game against the Pittsburgh Pirates in the fifth inning after feeling the injury.

The official diagnosis carries the word "mild." The roster context surrounding it is not mild at all.

The Scale of the Problem

When Cease's name was added to the injured list Monday, he joined four other Blue Jays starting pitchers already on the shelf: José Berríos, Max Scherzer, Shane Bieber, and Cody Ponce.

Five starting pitchers. Simultaneously. In the same rotation that Toronto assembled to compete for an AL East title.

Cease has been the rotation's best piece. He entered Sunday's game 3-3 with a 3.05 earned-run average and a major-league-leading 92 strikeouts in 11 starts. That strikeout total — more than any other pitcher in baseball through late May — speaks to the quality of what Toronto is missing. This is not an ordinary arm going down. This is an elite pitcher at the top of his performance curve who now must sit while his hamstring heals.

A Contract Under Scrutiny

The seven-year, $210 million commitment Toronto made to Cease this winter was one of the offseason's most debated investments. Cease is 30 years old. Long-term contracts for starting pitchers at that age carry inherent risk. The hamstring injury that arrived in start number 11 does not mean the deal is a mistake — hamstring strains happen to young, healthy pitchers too — but it does accelerate the timeline of scrutiny for an organization that bet heavily on rotation depth.

For reference, Cease's 92 strikeouts lead baseball. His 3.05 ERA is respectable in the current run environment. On performance alone, the investment is paying off. The issue is that the performance is paused, and the Jays have no depth to absorb the absence.

The Compounding Factor

Vladimir Guerrero Jr. is also day-to-day after being struck by a pitch on his elbow during the Pittsburgh series. Guerrero is Toronto's franchise cornerstone — the hitter the offense is built around. His absence from the lineup even for a handful of days compounds a situation that was already dire.

The Blue Jays built this roster around the expectation that Cease would anchor a rotation, Guerrero would anchor a lineup, and the organization would compete for an AL East division title. Both of those anchors are now unavailable, at least temporarily, and the team faces a stretch of its schedule without either of them healthy.

Some of the other rotational injuries may resolve before Cease returns. Bieber has missed time before and found his way back. Scherzer, at his age, is managed carefully. Berríos has pitched through adversity before. Ponce, as the lower-profile arm on the list, has less organizational urgency attached to his return.

But five starters down at the same time is a depth test no organization should have to pass this early in a season, and Toronto is now in the middle of it with its best pitcher unavailable.

What's Next

Cease's 15-day IL designation means he is eligible to return in early June at the earliest, assuming the hamstring heals without complication. The Blue Jays will need to patch together a rotation in the interim with whatever depth remains in Triple-A Buffalo. The AL East remains competitive, and every game missed with this much pitching on the shelf makes the path to October steeper. Toronto's front office will need to make decisions quickly about whether additional external help is available — and whether this situation qualifies as the emergency it appears to be.
