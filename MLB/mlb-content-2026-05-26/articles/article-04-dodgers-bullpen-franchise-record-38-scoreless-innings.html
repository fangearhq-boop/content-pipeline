38 Scoreless Innings and Counting: The Dodgers' Bullpen Is Setting Franchise Records While Everyone Else Is Hurt

By Marcus Cole

May 26, 2026

Tyler Glasnow is hurt. Blake Snell is hurt. Edwin Diaz, the All-Star closer the Dodgers signed in the offseason, is on the injured list. The Los Angeles bullpen looked at all of that and decided not to care.

The Los Angeles Dodgers' bullpen has thrown 38 consecutive scoreless innings. That is a franchise record in the modern era, the longest such streak in the major leagues since the 2017 Cleveland Indians reached 38.2 consecutive scoreless innings out of the bullpen.

Twelve different relievers have contributed to the streak. None of them is the player the Dodgers signed to anchor the unit. Edwin Diaz, the All-Star closer who came to Los Angeles on a multi-year deal, landed on the injured list early in the season with a loose body in his elbow and is not expected back until the second half. The bullpen responded by building a historic scoreless streak without him.

The Context: A Rotation Under Siege

To appreciate what the Dodgers' bullpen has accomplished, it helps to understand what the rest of the pitching staff is going through.

Tyler Glasnow, one of baseball's most talented starters when healthy, has not been available. Blake Snell, who joined Los Angeles with a track record of excellence, is also unavailable. Jack Dreyer, a promising arm in the rotation mix, has been lost as well. Diaz was supposed to close games cleanly; he is on the IL instead.

The Dodgers have been piecing together their pitching from pieces that were not part of the original plan. In that context, the bullpen's 38-inning scoreless stretch is not just an impressive statistic — it is the reason the team has remained in contention despite losing multiple high-profile arms.

Kyle Hurt: The Name Nobody Knew

The standout performer in the Dodgers' bullpen during this stretch is Kyle Hurt. The right-hander has made 14 appearances this season and has not allowed a run in any of them. His earned-run average for the year sits at 0.60. His WHIP is 0.87. Hurt is not a pitcher who appeared on many fantasy baseball draft boards in April. He is now one of the most effective relievers in baseball.

These are the kinds of stories the Dodgers' organizational pitching depth generates consistently. When the proven arms get hurt, players who were developed within the system step in and perform at a level that allows the team to keep winning. Hurt is the latest example — and perhaps the most dramatic one in recent memory.

Tanner Scott and the Collective Effort

Beyond Hurt, Tanner Scott has been the bullpen's veteran presence. Scott has not allowed a run since April 24, a stretch of 10.1 consecutive scoreless innings that has made him one of the most reliable late-inning options in the National League. His experience — and his continued ability to miss bats in high-leverage situations — has been a stabilizing force for a group that needed steadiness when the stars went down.

The other 10 contributors to the streak have ranged from seasoned veterans to previously unknown arms that the Dodgers cycled through as needed. That is the hallmark of this organization's approach to pitching: have enough depth that any single injury — or group of injuries — cannot dismantle the entire operation.

The streak eventually ended over the weekend, but the Dodgers still won the game. Winning despite that breach is its own statement.

What's Next

The question for Los Angeles is whether this level of bullpen performance is sustainable over a full season, and whether Glasnow, Snell, or any of the other missing arms return in a timeframe that allows the rotation to stabilize before October. Edwin Diaz's return in the second half could turn a very good bullpen into an elite one. For now, what the Dodgers have built without him is remarkable enough — a franchise record, a streak that stands with the sport's best in recent years, and proof that this organization's pitching depth runs deeper than anyone assumed when the injuries started piling up.
