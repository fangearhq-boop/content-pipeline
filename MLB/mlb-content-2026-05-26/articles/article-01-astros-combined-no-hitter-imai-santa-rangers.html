Astros Throw First MLB No-Hitter Since 2024: Imai Leads, Santa Makes History on His Debut

By Marcus Cole

May 26, 2026

The first no-hitter in Major League Baseball since September 2024 arrived Monday night in Arlington — courtesy of a shaky start, two crucial double plays, and a 23-year-old pitcher making the most extraordinary debut in over a century.

Alimber Santa had never thrown a pitch in the major leagues before Monday night. He walked to the mound at Globe Life Park in Arlington, Texas, and proceeded to retire all six batters he faced across two perfect innings to complete a Houston Astros combined no-hitter. A 9-0 final. The first no-hitter in baseball since September 4, 2024.

Santa is the first pitcher since 1900 to appear in a no-hitter on his major-league debut. That fact alone is enough to make Monday's game historic. The circumstances surrounding it made the story even better.

How It Started — and How It Almost Ended Early

Tatsuya Imai did not look like a man about to throw a no-hitter in the first inning.

The Japanese right-hander, who signed a three-year, $54 million deal with Houston in January, walked three of the first four batters he faced. The bases were loaded with one out. One more walk forces a run. One hit ends the no-hit bid in the first inning and almost certainly changes the game's character entirely.

Instead, Imai induced a double play. The inning ended with the bases empty and no runs scored. He would not walk another batter.

From that point, Imai was dominant in a quietly efficient way. He threw 97 pitches over six innings, generating eight ground-ball outs and two additional double plays. He struck out two. The Rangers put balls in play, and those balls found gloves. Six hitless innings and one masterwork of contact management — Imai handed the ball to the Astros' bullpen with the no-hitter alive.

The Historical Debut

Steven Okert handled the seventh inning with three outs. Then Santa came in for the eighth.

The 23-year-old right-hander had pitched in the minor leagues. He had never thrown in a major-league game. He was making his debut, and he was making it in the eighth inning of a no-hitter with six outs standing between the Astros and history.

Santa retired all six batters he faced. Two innings. Six up, six down. Perfect from start to finish. The Houston Astros no-hit the Texas Rangers 9-0, and a pitcher who was not in the major leagues 24 hours earlier was responsible for the final out.

To find the last time a pitcher appeared in a no-hitter on his big-league debut, you have to go back to October 15, 1892, and a pitcher named Bumpus Jones. More than a century passed between that moment and Santa's two innings in Arlington. The history books were updated Monday.

The Context: 630 Days Without a No-Hitter

The last MLB no-hitter before Monday came on September 4, 2024, when the Chicago Cubs' Shota Imanaga, Nate Pearson, and Porter Hodge combined to blank the Pittsburgh Pirates.

The drought between that game and Monday stretched over 630 days — one of the longer gaps between no-hitters in recent baseball history. The sport had been waiting for one. When it finally came, it came from a starter who walked three in the first inning and a rookie who had never pitched in the majors.

This is the 17th no-hitter in Astros franchise history. No organization has thrown more combined no-hitters than Houston. The Astros' ability to develop and deploy pitching at every level of the game continues to generate the kinds of performances that draw the sport's attention.

Tatsuya Imai came to Houston with a reputation as one of Japan's best pitchers. Three All-Star selections. A 1.92 ERA in his final Japanese League season. The Astros gave him $54 million and a rotation spot, and Monday night he justified every dollar — even if it took a shaky first inning to get there.

What's Next

The Astros and Rangers continue their series Tuesday night in Arlington, with Houston seeking to build on Monday's momentum. For Alimber Santa, his debut night will be told and retold as long as baseball keeps records — which is to say, forever. For Tatsuya Imai, Monday was the kind of statement start that confirms organizational investment. And for baseball, the 630-day wait for a no-hitter is finally over.
