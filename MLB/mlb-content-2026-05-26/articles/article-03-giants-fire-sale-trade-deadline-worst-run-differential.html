The Giants Have MLB's Worst Run Differential. The Trade Deadline Teardown Has Begun.

By Ryan Calloway

May 26, 2026

San Francisco built one of the most expensive rosters in franchise history and expected to compete. With roughly 40 games played, the reality is the opposite — and the organization is already beginning to explore what it can salvage before summer's trade deadline.

The San Francisco Giants are exploring trades for starting pitchers Robbie Ray and Tyler Mahle and outfielder Harrison Bader, according to multiple reports. The moves represent the beginning of what could be a significant mid-season teardown for a club that invested heavily this offseason and is watching that investment fail in real time.

San Francisco's run differential sits at minus-42 through approximately 40 games — the worst figure in Major League Baseball. They have already made one notable trade, sending Gold Glove catcher Patrick Bailey to the Cleveland Guardians. The Bailey move was a signal. The exploration of Ray, Mahle, and Bader confirms the direction.

The Investment That Isn't Paying Off

The Giants entered 2026 with a roster that cost north of $200 million to assemble. Several significant multi-year contracts were signed with the expectation that the club would compete in the National League West. At approximately the one-quarter mark of the season, the results are among the worst in the sport.

The biggest contracts belong to players who are struggling. Rafael Devers. Willy Adames. Jung Hoo Lee. Matt Chapman. All four are underperforming relative to expectations and their contract values. All four are also largely untradeable in their current state — clubs in contention are not going to absorb large contracts for players who are not producing, and the market for struggling veterans is a thin one.

That leaves the Giants with a difficult reality: the players most likely to attract deadline buyers are the lesser-paid arms and role players, while the high-dollar commitments remain on the books regardless of what happens this summer.

What They Can Move

Robbie Ray is the most interesting trade chip. Starting pitchers with Ray's track record — he won the AL Cy Young Award in 2021 — attract attention from contending teams regardless of current-year performance, because a change of scenery can sometimes restore a pitcher's effectiveness. If the Giants can find a team willing to take on a portion of his salary, the deal may work.

Tyler Mahle and Harrison Bader represent lower-cost alternatives for teams that want to add depth without a major financial commitment. Both are the kinds of players that playoff-chasing clubs use to fill specific roster needs at the deadline.

Logan Webb, the Giants' ace, is a different category. Reports suggest the club might entertain offers for Webb if the losing continues — but moving the team's best pitcher would be a more significant organizational decision that management has not yet confirmed is on the table.

A Warning for Other Big Spenders

The Giants' situation in 2026 is an illustration of the risk inherent in high-spending offseasons. Building a roster around expensive multi-year contracts assumes that the players who earned those contracts will continue to perform at that level — and that they will do so simultaneously, in the same park, with the same teammates.

When the assumptions fail, as they have in San Francisco, the organization is left with major commitments, limited trade flexibility, and the uncomfortable prospect of rebuilding while paying for players who are supposed to be part of the foundation.

The Giants will have decisions to make before July's trade deadline. The process has already started. Where it ends depends on how much more the team is willing to sell — and whether any of the underperforming contracts find their form before the window closes.

What's Next

San Francisco will continue playing out the first half of the season while its front office assesses the trade market. The deadline is not until late July, which means there are still weeks of data to collect before major decisions become final. If the run differential stays near minus-40 and the record continues to decline, the moves will accelerate. Watch for Ray to be the first significant arm to change addresses — a contending team in need of rotation help will find a way to structure a deal before the summer ends.
