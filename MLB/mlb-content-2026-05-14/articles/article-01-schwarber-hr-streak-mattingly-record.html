Schwarber Goes for Six: The HR Streak, the Franchise Record, and His Manager's MLB Mark

By Marcus Cole | May 14, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

If you scripted a baseball story, you would not write this one. You would assume it was too neat, too obvious in its irony, too constructed to feel real.

Kyle Schwarber has hit home runs in five consecutive games. He tied the Philadelphia Phillies franchise record on Tuesday night at Fenway Park with a first-inning blast off Brayan Bello that sailed over the right-field wall. He now has 17 home runs, the most in Major League Baseball. Tonight he goes for his sixth consecutive game.

If he hits one, he will own the Phillies record alone. And if he keeps going? The MLB record of eight consecutive games with a home run is held by three men: Dale Long of the 1956 Pirates, Don Mattingly of the 1987 Yankees, and Ken Griffey Jr. of the 1993 Mariners.

Don Mattingly is Kyle Schwarber's current manager.

The Franchise Record He Just Tied

The five-game home run streak is significant in Philadelphia baseball history. Schwarber joins Dick Allen (1969), Mike Schmidt (1979), Bobby Abreu (2005), Chase Utley (twice in 2008), Rhys Hoskins (2017), Odúbel Herrera (2018), and Trea Turner (2023) as the only Phillies to reach the mark.

That is a list of serious hitters — a Hall of Famer in Schmidt, franchise centerpieces in Allen, Abreu, and Utley, and modern contributors in the others. Schwarber's entry into that company is deserved. He has been the most consistent power threat in the NL this spring, and his six home runs over the five-game stretch represent a hot stretch that would be notable by any standard.

Tuesday's win over the Red Sox was a 2-1 decision in which Schwarber's home run provided the decisive margin. That pattern — Schwarber's power changing the game outcome — has defined the Phillies' offensive identity under Mattingly's interim tenure.

The Manager Connection

Don Mattingly set the MLB record in 1987 as a New York Yankee, homering in eight consecutive games to tie the standard Dale Long had established in 1956. Griffey matched the record in 1993. No one has broken it in the decades since.

Mattingly, 65, was named Phillies interim manager on April 28 after Rob Thomson was fired following a 9-19 start. The Phillies have gone 11-3 under Mattingly since, riding Schwarber's power surge as a primary engine. His son, Preston Mattingly, serves as the Phillies' general manager — a family arrangement that makes the dugout dynamic unique in modern baseball.

The irony of Mattingly watching his own player chase the record he holds is not lost on the Philadelphia clubhouse. Whether Schwarber ultimately passes it matters less than the theater of the chase itself.

Tonight at Fenway

Getting to six straight home runs requires getting a good pitch to hit. Red Sox pitchers are fully aware of the target on Schwarber's back in this at-bat. Fenway Park's dimensions create specific challenges and specific opportunities — the Green Monster in left creates angles that favor pull hitters, but the Red Sox will work to avoid the inner half against a left-handed power bat.

If Schwarber homers, he stands alone in franchise history and the countdown to Mattingly's record begins in earnest. If he does not, the streak ends at five — tied for the all-time Phillies mark, and still the most home runs in baseball through mid-May.

What's Next

The Phillies wrap up their series at Fenway Park tonight before returning to Philadelphia for their next homestand. Schwarber's six-game bid is the night's most watched at-bat storyline across baseball. Should the streak extend, the conversation about Mattingly's eight-game mark will only grow louder. At 17 home runs, Schwarber is on pace for a historically significant power season regardless of how the streak resolves tonight.
