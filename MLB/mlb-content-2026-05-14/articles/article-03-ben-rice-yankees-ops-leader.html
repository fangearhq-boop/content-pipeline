Ben Rice Is Leading MLB in OPS and No One Is Talking About It

By Ryan Calloway | May 14, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

The story of the 2026 Yankees was supposed to be the home run race. Aaron Judge entered the season with 383 career home runs and championship aspirations. Munetaka Murakami arrived from Japan carrying a 60-homer season and the weight of being one of the most anticipated imports in recent memory. Both players are delivering.

But quietly, without the benefit of a narrative arc or a record to chase, New York first baseman Ben Rice has become the most productive hitter in baseball by one of the sport's most comprehensive offensive measures. His OPS — on-base plus slugging percentage — leads all of Major League Baseball at 1.214.

Not first among first basemen. Not first among Yankees. First in baseball.

The Numbers Behind the Number

An OPS of 1.214 is not sustainable at a season-long level — history suggests it will moderate as the sample grows. But the underlying profile suggests Rice is genuinely elite, not just running hot.

His average exit velocity is 94.5 miles per hour, placing him among the game's hardest hitters. His hard-hit rate of 59.6 percent means nearly six of every ten balls he makes contact with qualify as hard contact by Statcast's definition. His barrel rate of 21.3 percent is elite territory — most players who sustain above 15 percent are considered power hitters. Rice is nearly at 22.

His wOBA — weighted on-base average, which assigns appropriate value to different outcomes — sits at .467. The MLB average hovers around .320. Rice is producing at a level roughly 45 percent above league average, which is the kind of gap that separates stars from superstars.

He has 13 home runs, placing him fourth in baseball. Eight of those 13 have come off right-handed pitching, which eliminates the option for opposing managers to hide him in a favorable platoon matchup. He hits the ball hard against everyone.

The Third Story in the Bronx

Rice's emergence alongside Judge and Murakami creates a lineup depth that opposing pitching staffs have to navigate carefully. If you plan your bullpen around containing Judge and Murakami, Rice punishes you. If you focus your scouting on Rice, the other two will make you regret the pivot.

The Yankees built their lineup in the off-season around depth and balance — a philosophy that argues for multiple dangerous hitters at different spots in the order rather than one or two stars surrounded by lesser contributors. Rice is the embodiment of that philosophy working as designed.

He has received far less national attention than his production warrants. That is partly a function of New York's star-driven coverage environment, where Judge and Murakami fill most of the column inches regardless of who is actually hitting best on a given day. Rice has been too busy producing to complain about the imbalance.

What's Next

The Yankees continue their series in Baltimore on Thursday evening at Camden Yards. Rice's OPS lead will attract more attention as the season progresses and his sample size grows. Whether he finishes the year atop that leaderboard depends on health and sustained contact quality — but the underlying metrics suggest the performance is real. In a Yankee lineup generating significant offense from multiple spots, Ben Rice is proving to be the most productive hitter by the numbers. The Judge and Murakami story is real. So is this one.
