One Hit in 64 Batters: Rico Garcia Is the Most Untouchable Reliever in Baseball

By Jake Torres | May 14, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

Here is a number that requires a second read: 64 batters faced, one hit allowed. That is the 2026 season of Rico Garcia, the Baltimore Orioles reliever who has produced the most extraordinary start by any pitcher in the Expansion Era.

Garcia is the first pitcher since 1961 to allow at most one hit over his first 64 batters faced to start a season. He is also the first pitcher in MLB history — across any era — to allow at most one total hit in his first 20 appearances in a single season. His ERA is 0.47. His WHIP is 0.42. Batters are hitting .018 against him.

The one hit he allowed? A home run by the Royals' Michael Massey on April 21. Every ball in play against him before and after that moment has been recorded as an out. His BABIP — batting average on balls in play — is a perfect, impossible .000.

What Makes Garcia Unhittable

Garcia is not simply getting lucky. His strikeout rate is real — 22 strikeouts in his first season appearances, with seven walks, generating outs at a rate that Statcast's pitching run value metric ranks as the best in baseball. According to the metric, he has been more valuable than any other reliever in the sport this year, including closer Mason Miller of the Athletics.

The underlying pitch profile shows a reliever who has learned to locate, generate deception, and miss bats on demand. At 29 years old, Garcia is not a young prospect still developing. He is a finished product who figured something out — likely during his long, winding journey through the minor leagues and free agency — and arrived in Baltimore knowing exactly how to get hitters out.

The .000 BABIP will regress. Baseball's mathematics make some degree of regression inevitable. But a pitcher with genuine strikeout ability and command does not need luck to be excellent. When Garcia's BABIP normalizes to a reasonable level, his ERA will go up — but not by as much as the BABIP regression would suggest, because his actual pitching quality is real.

The Road That Got Him Here

The biographical context makes Garcia's 2026 performance more meaningful, not less. He was selected by the Colorado Rockies in the 30th round of the 2016 draft — the 906th pick overall. He worked through the Rockies system, got released, and signed elsewhere. He had Tommy John surgery in 2021, costing him nearly a full season of development and adding another obstacle to a career that had never been easy.

He has been a free agent seven times. He has been claimed off waivers four times, including three separate times during the 2025 season alone. Three times in one year, another organization decided to take a chance on him off the waiver wire — and three times, a different organization decided to let him go.

Baltimore took the fourth claim, kept him, and put him in a role where his specific skill set — deception, location, missing bats — could be deployed effectively. The results have exceeded every reasonable expectation.

What's Next

Garcia will continue to serve as one of Baltimore's primary leverage relievers through the 2026 season. The Orioles, who won their series against the Yankees on Tuesday 1-0, are in the AL East race with Tampa Bay and New York. Having an elite reliever at their disposal gives manager Brandon Hyde the kind of late-game flexibility that wins one-run games. Garcia is already the story of the Orioles' 2026 season. If his performance holds — or even partially holds — he will be one of the most remarkable pitching stories of the year across all of baseball.
