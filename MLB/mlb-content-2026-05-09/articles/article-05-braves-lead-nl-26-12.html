Atlanta Braves Lead the NL at 26-12 With the Best Offense in Baseball

By Jake Torres | Ballpark Banter | May 9, 2026

While the Cubs grab headlines and Judge dominates the HR race, the Braves are quietly putting together one of the most prolific offensive seasons in recent memory.

The Atlanta Braves don't need the attention. They just keep winning.

Atlanta sits at 26-12, leading the National League East and matching the Chicago Cubs and New York Yankees for the best records in all of baseball. They have done it behind the most prolific offense in the sport — averaging 5.61 runs per game, a pace that is historically excellent at any point in the season, let alone through the first 38 games.

In a week dominated by Aaron Judge's historic home run pace and the Cubs' back-to-back 10-game win streaks, the Braves are getting overlooked. That's fine with them.

A Machine That Produces Runs

Five-point-six-one runs per game. That's what the Braves are averaging, and it's the best mark in baseball. To put it in context: a team that scores 5.61 runs per game over a full 162-game season would score more than 900 runs — something that almost never happens in the modern era of pitching dominance and defensive shifts.

The Braves achieve this not through one transcendent individual performance but through lineup depth. From top to bottom, they put pressure on opposing pitchers. When one part of the lineup goes cold, another heats up. The run production has been remarkably consistent throughout the first 38 games.

NL East Control

Atlanta leads the NL East by a comfortable margin, and the teams behind them have shown little indication they can close the gap. The Phillies, Mets, and Marlins have all had stretches of quality play, but none have been able to keep up with the Braves' overall consistency.

For a franchise that has made the playoffs in multiple consecutive years, sustained excellence is the expectation, not the exception. The 2026 team appears to be living up to — and perhaps exceeding — that standard.

The National Conversation

Part of the reason the Braves are flying under the radar is geography and narrative. The Cubs' twin 10-game streaks are a better story. Aaron Judge's home run chase is appointment television. But baseball success doesn't care about narratives.

Atlanta's combination of run production, a 26-12 record, and NL East control is the resume of a team that expects to be playing meaningful baseball in October. Whether they get more attention or less between now and the playoffs matters far less than whether they keep performing.

And right now, they are performing.

What's Next

The Braves have a summer full of meaningful games ahead. The NL East race will tighten at some point — division races always do. The test for Atlanta is whether the offense can sustain this pace and whether the pitching holds together through the dog days of August.

If it does, the 2026 Atlanta Braves will be a legitimate threat to reach the World Series. Their 26-12 start says this team has the talent to do exactly that. The league would do well to pay attention.
