Schwarber Has 29 HRs and the Derby Is at His House — Why Is He Still on the Fence?

By Jake Torres | Ballpark Banter | June 24, 2026

Kyle Schwarber leads all of Major League Baseball with 29 home runs. He is hitting .255 with a .369 on-base percentage and .603 slugging percentage through 74 games. He was the first player to reach 25 home runs in 2026. He is on a pace that very few players have ever approached.

The Home Run Derby is July 14 at Citizens Bank Park in Philadelphia.

His home park.

And somehow, as of today, he still has not committed to participating.

The Historic Pace

To understand why this Derby conversation matters, consider what Schwarber's pace actually represents. A pace of 60-plus home runs in a season puts a player in extraordinarily rare company. The threshold has been reached only a handful of times in Major League Baseball history. Schwarber's 29 home runs in 74 games would project to more than 60 over a full 162-game season — a rate that has defined some of the most memorable seasons in the sport's history.

He has already had one of the signature individual games of the 2026 season, recording three home runs in a single game — his fifth career three-home run performance. The power is not a fluke. It has been consistent, and it has been historic.

The Derby Case

The argument for Schwarber participating in the Home Run Derby is almost too obvious to make.

He leads the majors in home runs. The Derby is literally at Citizens Bank Park, where the Phillies play and where Schwarber has spent the most celebrated years of his career. The Phillies faithful — who gave Schwarber perhaps the greatest individual reception any non-homegrown player has received in Philadelphia's history — would create an atmosphere unlike anything the Derby has seen in years.

When asked about it, Schwarber was noncommittal but not dismissive. "If that happens, we'll see what happens for the Home Run Derby." That's the kind of answer that leaves a door open without walking through it.

The Case for Skipping

There are legitimate reasons to skip the Derby, and players cite them regularly. The additional swings can alter a hitter's rhythm. The pressure of a home crowd — which sounds like a positive — can weigh on some players in ways that are hard to predict. Some hitters have reported mechanical issues in the weeks following a Derby appearance that they trace back to the format of the competition.

Schwarber is also leading All-Star voting at designated hitter. He will be in Philadelphia regardless of whether he participates in the Derby. The question is whether he adds 60-plus of the sport's most electric moments to that trip.

What's Next

Derby invitations and participant confirmations typically come out in the days following the All-Star roster announcement in early July. If Schwarber agrees to participate, the Derby at Citizens Bank Park becomes one of the most anticipated individual events in recent All-Star history. If he declines, Philadelphia will still give him a standing ovation when he's introduced — and quietly wonder what might have been. His answer should come within the next two weeks.
