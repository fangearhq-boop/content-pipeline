Freeman's Go-Ahead Homer Rescues Ohtani — Dodgers Complete Sweep of Rays

By Ryan Calloway | Ballpark Banter | June 18, 2026

Shohei Ohtani was not at his best Wednesday night. The Tampa Bay Rays made him pay for it — and then Freddie Freeman stepped in.

Freeman hit a two-run home run in the sixth inning, a 407-foot shot to center field off Tampa Bay reliever Kevin Kelly, that turned a 4-2 Dodgers deficit into a 5-4 lead. Los Angeles held it from there. The Dodgers won 5-4 and completed a three-game sweep of the Rays — their first sweep of Tampa Bay since August 2013 and just the second sweep of the Rays in franchise history.

Ohtani's Rough Inning

Ohtani was cruising until the fifth inning. Tampa Bay found multiple baserunners and scored four runs in the frame — a collapse that was notable in context for a pitcher who has been among the most dominant arms in the game over the course of his career. Five hits and four earned runs in a single inning is a departure from Ohtani's standard, and it left the Dodgers trailing when they needed him most.

The bullpen held from there. Ohtani was removed from the game, and Los Angeles needed offense to overtake what the Rays had built. The situation fell exactly to the spot in the batting order where Freddie Freeman was waiting.

Freeman's Clutch Moment

Kelly entered in relief for Tampa Bay and quickly found himself facing Freeman with runners on base and the Dodgers trailing by two. The ball left Freeman's bat at 407 feet, landing deep in center field and giving Los Angeles a 5-4 advantage that the Dodgers would not relinquish.

MLB.com captured the moment with a headline that said it cleanly: "Freddie just has that aura." That is accurate and well-earned. Freeman has been the clutch performer on this Dodgers team for years — the player who absorbs the big at-bat in the biggest moment and delivers a result the box score captures but the footage makes real.

The Dodgers trailed 4-2 entering the sixth with their best pitcher already out of the game. They left with a series sweep. Freeman is the principal reason the math worked.

The Sweep in Context

Sweeping the Tampa Bay Rays is not a routine accomplishment. The organization has spent two decades building teams that stay in games, play good fundamental baseball, and make it difficult for opposing offenses to put together extended runs. The Dodgers swept them anyway.

The historical note is real: Los Angeles completed just its second-ever sweep of Tampa Bay in franchise history. The previous one came in August 2013. Over a decade between sweeps of the same franchise speaks to how difficult it is to win three consecutive games against Tampa Bay's systematically constructed roster.

For the Dodgers, the sweep extends a stretch of baseball that has the team playing with confidence heading into the second half of the season.

What's Next

Ohtani will bounce back from Wednesday's fifth inning. The track record is too long and too strong for one rough frame to define his 2026 outlook. For Los Angeles, the result is what matters — and the result was a sweep, a comeback, and another Freddie Freeman highlight for the archives.

With the All-Star break approaching in July, the Dodgers enter the second half of the season having taken care of business against a quality opponent. Freeman's home run gives them the win they needed. Ohtani will get his next chance to remind everyone why the previous start matters less than the next one.
