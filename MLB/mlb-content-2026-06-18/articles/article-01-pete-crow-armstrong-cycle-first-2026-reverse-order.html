Pete Crow-Armstrong Hits for the Cycle in Reverse Order — First of the 2026 Season

By Marcus Cole | Ballpark Banter | June 18, 2026

Pete Crow-Armstrong did something no Chicago Cubs player had ever done before. He just made sure it happened in the hardest possible way first.

On June 15, the 24-year-old center fielder hit for the cycle against the Colorado Rockies in a 5-4 Cubs victory — but he did it in reverse order. Home run in the first inning. Triple in the third. Double in the fifth. Single in the seventh. The rarest variant of one of baseball's rarest accomplishments, and the first player in Cubs franchise history to pull it off.

It was the first cycle of the 2026 MLB season and only the fourth reverse-order cycle in the divisional era, dating back to 1969.

How It Happened

Crow-Armstrong led off the game against Colorado by sending a pitch over the wall for a solo home run. He followed that with a triple in the third, stretching his legs around the bases with the kind of all-out effort that defines his game. A double in the fifth kept the progression intact. Then came the single in the seventh — the "easy part" that is statistically the most likely hit a batter records, but in the context of completing a reverse-order cycle, the final piece of something exceptional.

Crow-Armstrong went 4-for-4 with two RBI and scored once. He was immediately picked off first base after recording the single, a lapse he acknowledged with candor afterward. "I did everything I could to help the team," he said, "but I also had a real lapse in focus."

The game still needed him. The Cubs entered the ninth trailing 4-3, needing a rally to avoid handing back what looked like a well-earned victory. Matt Shaw drew a bases-loaded walk to send Chicago home with a 5-4 win. The reverse-order cycle got the headline, but the walk-off delivered the result.

The Historic Context

Crow-Armstrong's cycle checks every box when it comes to rarity. At 24 years old, he is the youngest Cubs player to hit for the cycle since catcher Randy Hundley accomplished the feat in 1966. He is the 13th Cubs player in franchise history — a franchise that has now been playing Major League Baseball since 1876 — to hit for the cycle.

The reverse-order specificity makes it even more unusual. Completing the cycle in reverse means the most difficult hits — the home run and triple — come first, with the season requiring a player to have the discipline and circumstance to work backward through the standard progression. Only three players in the entire divisional era, going back to 1969, had done it before Crow-Armstrong. He is the fourth.

The first cycle of the 2026 MLB season arrived on June 15. The Cubs center fielder made sure it was one that baseball history would notice.

What the Day Says About Crow-Armstrong

Cubs fans have a complicated relationship with Crow-Armstrong, which is itself a sign of a player who has enough talent to inspire high expectations. When he is right, he is everything the franchise could ask for — elite defense, plus baserunning, a bat that punishes mistakes in the zone. When he struggles, the frustration is proportional to what he showed he could be.

June 15 was the version Cubs fans want to see more often. Four at-bats, four hits, four different categories of hit, against a team that needed no additional embarrassment in 2026. The cycle is a perfect statistical day — and for one afternoon in Chicago, Pete Crow-Armstrong delivered exactly that.

What's Next

Crow-Armstrong and the Cubs continue through the heart of the 2026 regular season with a roster that has shown the capability of competing but has not yet put together the kind of consistent run that turns early summer baseball into a genuine pennant race. Individual performances like the June 15 cycle provide moments — and the argument that this team has more in it than the standings may suggest.

The 2026 MLB cycle list stands at one. Crow-Armstrong owns it. Whatever comes next, that fact will not change.
