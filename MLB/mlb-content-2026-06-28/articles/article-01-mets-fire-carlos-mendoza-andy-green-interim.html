The Mets Fire Mendoza at 34-47 -- But the Problem Was Never the Manager

By Marcus Cole June 28, 2026

Out in Queens

Carlos Mendoza was fired as manager of the New York Mets on Friday, June 27, with the team sitting at 34-47 -- the second-worst record in the National League, ahead of only the Rockies and Giants. Andy Green, the Mets' VP of Player Development, takes over as interim manager. Green will return to the front office after the season ends; the search for a permanent manager will begin then.

The trigger for the move was six consecutive losses, including a Cubs doubleheader sweep in which the Mets committed six errors in the nightcap. Two years removed from leading New York to the National League Championship Series, Mendoza was gone before the All-Star break.

What Mendoza's Tenure Actually Looked Like

Mendoza was hired before the 2024 season. In his first year he led the Mets to the NLCS, where they lost to the Dodgers. That kind of first-year success is rare and legitimately impressive. The 2024 Mets overachieved relative to their preseason projections, and Mendoza received justified credit for holding the clubhouse together through a long and pressurized season.

In 2025 the Mets started 45-24 -- one of the best records in baseball through the first two months. Then came a collapse that no manager could have fully reversed. The team finished below .500 and missed the playoffs. In 2026, things have been worse from the start. At 34-47, the Mets are not a team that got unlucky in one-run games. They are a team that has been consistently outplayed.

What Green Inherits

Andy Green managed the San Diego Padres from 2016 to 2019 before moving into front office roles. He is experienced, he knows how to run a major league dugout, and his elevation to interim status reflects the Mets' desire for organizational stability rather than a dramatic external hire. He will not be the permanent manager. His job is to hold the roster together and see what the second half looks like before Steve Cohen's front office makes larger decisions.

The issue Green inherits is not tactical. It is structural. The Mets have a payroll built for contention and a roster that has not performed like one for two consecutive seasons. The combination of underperforming veterans and unresolved roster questions will not be solved by a different face in the dugout. They require the trade deadline -- which arrives August 3 -- and decisions about which direction this organization is actually heading.

What's Next

Green's first real test comes over the next three weeks before the deadline. If the Mets move toward a seller posture -- trading veterans for prospects and treating 2026 as a transition year -- the interim label on his title will feel appropriate. If Cohen decides to buy at the deadline in an attempt to salvage the second half, Green will be managing a team trying to go from 34-47 to playoff contender in six weeks. Both scenarios are possible. The front office has not committed to either one publicly. That decision, not the managerial change, is the real story of what happens next in Queens.
