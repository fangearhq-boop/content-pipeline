Murakami Surges to Sole MLB HR Lead at 13 — White Sox Rookie on Historic Pace

By Marcus Cole | May 2, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

Munetaka Murakami entered May having done something nobody in his first month of MLB baseball had ever done before — and he's showing no signs of slowing down. The Chicago White Sox slugger has taken sole possession of the MLB home run lead with 13 homers, passing both Aaron Judge (12, Yankees) and Yordan Alvarez (12, Astros) to sit atop the leaderboard alone.

At his current pace, Murakami is on track to hit approximately 65 home runs this season. The all-time MLB single-season record is 73, set by Barry Bonds in 2001. Nobody has reached 73 since. But Murakami is the kind of hitter who makes you wonder if anything is truly off the table.

The Extra-Base Hit Record

The number that keeps blowing analysts away isn't just the total — it's the quality. Murakami's first 12 extra-base hits in MLB history were all home runs. Not most of them. Every single one. That's a record going back to at least 1900, according to MLB.com, surpassing the prior benchmark of 10 consecutive XBH-as-HRs to start a career, set by Dae-ho Lee in 2016.

It means Murakami has never hit a double or a triple in his MLB career. Just home runs. It's a "three true outcomes" profile taken to an almost absurd extreme — he either walks, strikes out, or launches one over the fence. His .394 on-base percentage and .598 slugging percentage reflect this, as does his 98th percentile walk rate.

From Tokyo to the South Side

Murakami made his MLB debut on March 26, 2026, bringing with him enormous expectations. In Japan, he holds the NPB all-time single-season home run record with 56 (2022, Yakult Swallows), surpassing legendary slugger Sadaharu Oh. He also became the first Japanese-born player with seven or more home runs in his first 21 MLB games — passing Shohei Ohtani, who hit five in his first 21 games.

The White Sox, rebuilding after several difficult seasons, may have found the centerpiece around which their revival can be built. Murakami's debut has energized a fanbase that has been patient through years of rebuilding.

What's Next

The historic pace will draw comparisons all season. The questions will multiply: Can he maintain it over 162 games? Will pitchers adjust? Can he stay healthy? Those are legitimate concerns, but right now, Murakami is the best story in baseball — and the sport's most compelling home run chase since Judge hit 62 in 2022.

With 13 home runs in approximately 30 games and the entire summer ahead, the White Sox rookie is rewriting what we thought we knew about power hitting debuts. Keep your eyes on the South Side.
