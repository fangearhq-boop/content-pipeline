NL Central Enters May With All Five Teams Above .500 — Baseball Has Never Seen This Before

By Jake Torres | May 2, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

In 2004, the NL Central had all five teams above .500 as late as May 29 — the longest any full division had managed that feat in modern baseball history. Then it ended. And nobody had matched it since. Until now.

All five NL Central teams entered May 2026 above .500. The Cincinnati Reds lead the division, and the Cubs, Cardinals, Brewers, and Pirates are all winning. It's only the second time since the onset of divisional play that a full five-team division has kept this up so deep into the season — and there's a real chance it could last all year.

The Cruel Math

Here's the problem: only three NL Central teams make the playoffs. One division winner and two wild card spots. No matter how strong this division plays collectively, two teams with winning records are going to watch October baseball from home.

That's the extraordinary narrative tension here. Five competitive teams playing inside a division that can only give three of them the reward they're working toward. The NL Central is arguably producing the best sustained competition of any division in baseball right now — and it's going to break someone's heart in the fall.

Who's Standing Out?

The Reds have led the division behind a young roster energized by rookie contributions including Sal Stewart, whose breakout has given Cincinnati a new offensive weapon. The Cardinals have Jordan Walker's surprise emergence. The Cubs and Brewers have their own pieces. The Pirates, with Oneil Cruz asserting himself and rookie Konnor Griffin adding a spark, have been the most pleasant surprise in the group.

There isn't a dominant team in this division — and that's precisely what makes it great. Every series matters. Every game has playoff implications. The NL Central is going to be appointment viewing all summer.

What's Next

If the NL Central can sustain this through an entire season — all five teams finishing above .500 — it would be the first time in the history of divisional play (since 1969) that any division accomplished the feat. Baseball has never seen it. Whether this particular group delivers on that potential remains to be seen, but the early returns are extraordinary.

Check the standings every day. Five teams. Three spots. One of the most compelling divisional races in recent memory has barely started.
