We knew Munetaka Murakami was going to be different. We just didn't know he was going to be THIS different.

The Chicago White Sox slugger entered May with 13 home runs — enough to take sole possession of the MLB HR lead, passing both Aaron Judge (12, Yankees) and Yordan Alvarez (12, Astros) in the process. At his current pace, Murakami is on track to hit approximately 65 home runs this season.

Let that sink in for a second. Sixty-five home runs.

But the number that keeps blowing people away isn't just the count — it's the context. Murakami's first 12 MLB extra-base hits were ALL home runs. Not most of them. All of them. That's the longest such streak to start an MLB career since at least 1900, according to MLB.com, surpassing Dae-ho Lee's 10-homer run in 2016.

In Japan, Murakami is already a legend. He hit 56 home runs in a single NPB season in 2022, breaking Sadaharu Oh's record. Now, on the South Side of Chicago, he's rewriting MLB history too.

### STORY 2:

#### Long-Form Post -- 12:30 PM ET
Before the 2026 season, you probably couldn't have picked Ildemaro Vargas out of a lineup.

You definitely can now.

The Arizona Diamondbacks second baseman extended his hitting streak to 27 games on May 1 — and in doing so, broke the all-time MLB record for Venezuela-born players. Of those 27 games, 24 have come to open the 2026 season, making it the second-longest season-opening hitting streak since 1940.

The only player to open a season with a longer streak? Ron LeFlore of the 1976 Detroit Tigers, who hit in 30 straight games to start that year. Vargas is just 3 games away.

Meanwhile, Vargas is leading the NL in batting average at .378, ranking in the top three in slugging and OPS. He's not just hot — he's historically hot.

The Diamondbacks hammered four home runs in their 6-2 win over Milwaukee as Vargas extended the streak. Arizona may have found their identity this season, and it starts with No. 19.

Until now.

Aaron Judge (12 HRs) and Ben Rice (10 HRs) have become just the second pair of Yankees teammates to reach that milestone before game 30. The Yankees are 19-10, holding first place in the AL East, and both of their franchise cornerstones are swinging the bat like the postseason starts tomorrow.

Judge entered the season chasing history too — he needs approximately 20 more home runs to reach the 400-career HR milestone, a club that would put him alongside the all-time greats. At his current pace, he could get there well before September.

Rice, meanwhile, has emerged as a legitimate power threat — not just a nice story. The Yankees' lineup is deep, their pitching is coming together (Gerrit Cole and Carlos Rodon expected back soon), and they look like a genuine October contender.

May's going to be the real test — but Atlanta looks like they intend to make this a very short race.

### STORY 5:

#### Long-Form Post -- 3:00 PM ET
Something special is happening in the NL Central — and it may never happen again.

All five NL Central teams entered May above .500. The Reds lead the division. But the Cubs, Cardinals, Brewers, and Pirates are all winning too. This is only the second time since 1970 that a full five-team division has been above .500 this deep into the season — the last time was May 29, 2004.

Here's the catch: only three NL Central teams make the playoffs (one division winner, two wild cards). So two teams with winning records are going to go home in October. That's the painful math of a historic divisional race.

If all five teams stay above .500 through the end of the season, it would be the first time in MLB's divisional era (since 1969) that an entire division finished the year above .500. We're watching something that baseball has literally never seen before.
