### STORY 1: Skubal Returns Friday at Cleveland

**FB Long-Form Post**
Window: 8:00 AM ET

He's back.

Tarik Skubal, the Detroit Tigers' two-time Cy Young Award winner, is set to return to the starting rotation this Friday against the Cleveland Guardians — and it's hard to overstate how much this moment matters, both for the Tigers and for the rest of baseball.

Skubal underwent arthroscopic left elbow surgery on May 6. The initial timeline was two to three months. He's coming back in roughly five weeks. His first rehab start on June 7, at High-A West Michigan, produced five scoreless innings: six strikeouts, two hits, 54 pitches. Tigers manager A.J. Hinch saw exactly what he needed to see. "Very encouraged with Tarik," Hinch said. "He looked exactly kind of how he's looked in the live BPs. The velocity, the execution was good."

While Skubal was out, the Tigers went 6-21. That number tells you everything. He's not just their best pitcher — he's the gravitational center of everything they're trying to do. His presence in the rotation changes how opponents game-plan, how the bullpen is deployed, and how confident the team feels playing behind him.

But Friday's return isn't just a Tigers story. It's a trade deadline story.

Skubal is a free agent after this season. The Tigers are not currently in playoff contention. That combination has every team with a championship window — the Yankees, Dodgers, Braves, Brewers, Phillies — mapping out what it would take to acquire him before August 3. ESPN recently published nine blockbuster trade proposals involving Skubal, which tells you how seriously the baseball world is watching.

Here's the question the rest of baseball has to answer: do you go get the two-time Cy Young, or do you let a rival do it instead?

Friday, Cleveland. Skubal returns. And the most consequential pitching story of the trade deadline begins in earnest.

### STORY 2: Schwarber HR Race — 60-HR Pace

**FB Long-Form Post**
Window: 10:30 AM ET

Let's talk about what Kyle Schwarber is doing.

The Philadelphia Phillies designated hitter leads all of baseball with 23 home runs. Yordan Alvarez is second at 22. Aaron Judge is on the injured list with a rib stress fracture. Yoshinobu Yamamoto's rotation partner Shohei Ohtani is pitching, not mashing. Ben Rice is third at 18. The field has thinned dramatically, and Schwarber is running away with it.

But here's the number that should stop you in your tracks: his pace.

Schwarber hit his 20th home run through his team's first 49 games — the fastest anyone has reached that mark since Josh Hamilton in 2012. Projecting forward, his pace puts him on track for 60-plus home runs.

For context on how rare that is: only five players in major league history have ever hit 60 or more home runs in a season. Roger Maris. Sammy Sosa (three times). Mark McGwire (twice). Barry Bonds. Aaron Judge in 2022. That's it. In the entire history of professional baseball, five players.

Schwarber has 92 games remaining in the regular season. He has already proven he can go on home run tears — he does it every year. The question is whether the pace holds.

The interesting wrinkle is the absence of his competition. In a healthy season, Judge might be pushing Schwarber or even leading. But Judge is expected to miss two months. Schwarber is winning the race alone, against the history books, and the moment has a different kind of weight when you're not just beating other players — you're chasing a list that most assumed was closed.

Five players have ever done this. One of them might be playing right now.

### STORY 3: Yankees Win in Extras Without Judge

**FB Long-Form Post**
Window: 8:00 AM ET

Two games without Aaron Judge. Two wins.

The New York Yankees defeated the Cleveland Guardians 7-5 in 10 innings on Monday night, their second consecutive victory since Judge landed on the 10-day injured list with a right rib stress fracture. Cody Bellinger provided the winning blow — a walk-off two-run single off Shawn Armstrong in the 10th inning that sent the Yankees home with another win they badly needed.

Paul Goldschmidt and Ryan McMahon both homered for New York. Cleveland actually led 5-4 after Angel Martinez's two-run shot in the fifth, but Goldschmidt's RBI grounder in the eighth evened things up before Bellinger ended it.

The Yankees recall Spencer Jones from Triple-A to help fill the void in the outfield. Ben Rice has provided stability in the middle of the lineup. The team is rallying.

But the Yankees don't need to be told that winning two games without their best player isn't the same as winning two months without him. Judge is expected to miss roughly eight weeks with the stress fracture. That projection puts his return around late July or early August — coinciding with the August 3 trade deadline.

The AL East hasn't collapsed without Judge. The Yankees are still competitive. But the math of a pennant race changes when your franchise centerpiece is watching from the dugout in street clothes. Every series matters. Every extra-inning win counts.

For now, Bellinger is walking it off. Jones is adjusting to the big leagues. And the Yankees are proving, at least for a few days, that they're deeper than their star.

### STORY 4: Braves 45-21 — Most Dramatic Turnaround in Baseball

**FB Long-Form Post**
Window: 10:30 AM ET

Remember last October, when the Atlanta Braves finished 76-86 and missed the playoffs entirely?

Fast forward to June 2026, and those same Braves have the best record in baseball at 45-21. They lead the NL East by a wide margin. They are playing like a team that has been waiting a long time to remind everyone who they are.

The transformation is remarkable for how quickly it happened. First-year manager Walt Weiss took over a roster that underperformed significantly in 2025, with injury and inconsistency defining a disappointing season. Chris Sale, one of the best left-handed pitchers in the game when healthy, is anchoring the rotation and pitching like a legitimate Cy Young candidate. Ronald Acuña Jr., who missed most of 2025 recovering from injury, is back in the lineup and making up for lost time.

Going from 76 wins to a 100-win pace in a single season is historically exceptional. There are usually only a handful of teams per decade that manage a swing of this magnitude. The combination of a talented roster returning to health, a new voice in the dugout, and some early-season fortune can create exactly this kind of dramatic reversal — but it's still rare to see it happen this fast.

The Braves are not just good right now. They are the best team in baseball right now. Whether they maintain that distinction over 162 games is the question — but their run differential, their consistency across home and road, and the quality of their starting pitching all suggest this isn't just a hot streak. This is a team operating at its ceiling.

The National League goes through Atlanta this year. That wasn't something most people said in October.

### STORY 5: June Milestones Roundup

**FB Long-Form Post**
Window: 10:00 PM ET

Baseball is a sport defined by its history and its numbers. And June 2026 has been unusually good for both.

Here are three of the most unusual statistical stories in the sport right now:

**Cristopher Sánchez, Phillies — 50.2 Scoreless Innings**
The Phillies left-hander has extended his scoreless streak to 50.2 innings, breaking a franchise record that had stood for 115 years. The previous record belonged to Grover Cleveland Alexander — a name from a different era of baseball entirely. Sánchez is pitching in a different atmosphere than Alexander did, against better hitters with better data, and he is still finding ways to keep runs off the board at a historic rate.

**Detroit Tigers — 4 Walk-Off HRs Allowed While Leading**
The 2026 Tigers have now allowed four home runs in which they held the lead, only to see the opposing team walk it off with a go-ahead blast. That total already ties the single-season record for this specific and painful category, matching marks set by the 1985 Twins and 2006 Cleveland Indians. Coincidentally, the Tigers' own recent struggles — and Skubal's absence — are part of the same story.

**Cincinnati Reds — Winning Without Hitting**
The Reds are just the 4th team in MLB history to win all of their first 10 games decided by one or two runs. Their team batting average is .203. They are winning close games at a historic rate while hitting like they're in a slump that has lasted all year. It is one of the strangest team profiles in recent memory — and it's working.

Baseball gives you something new to talk about every day. That's why we love it.
