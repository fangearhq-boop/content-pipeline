June's Strangest MLB Stories — Sánchez's Historic Streak, Detroit's Walk-Off Nightmare, and the Reds' Anomaly

By Jake Torres | June 10, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

Baseball is a sport that generates numbers every day, and most of those numbers tell familiar stories. But every season produces a handful of statistical moments so unusual that they stop you mid-scroll and make you look twice. June 2026 has delivered three of them.

Cristopher Sánchez Is Rewriting a 115-Year-Old Record

The Philadelphia Phillies left-hander has extended his scoreless innings streak past the 50-inning mark, in the process shattering a franchise record that had stood since the deadball era. The previous Phillies record was held by Grover Cleveland Alexander — a name associated with a time when the game was played with a different ball, different rules, and dramatically different offensive environments.

Sánchez is pitching against modern hitters, facing lineups with more analytics-driven approach than any era before it, and he is still keeping runs off the board at a historic rate. A scoreless streak of this length does not happen because a pitcher is getting lucky. It happens because a pitcher is executing at a very high level, game after game, across multiple lineups and multiple ballparks. For a Phillies franchise with as much pitching history as any in the game, breaking Alexander's record is not a small thing.

The Tigers Cannot Stop Walk-Off Home Runs

The 2026 Detroit Tigers have now allowed four home runs in which the Tigers held the lead, only to surrender a game-ending blast to the opposing team. That total ties the single-season record for walk-off home runs allowed while leading, matching marks set by the 1985 Minnesota Twins and the 2006 Cleveland Indians.

This is the specific kind of misery that is almost impossible to recover from within the game — you are ahead, the game feels in hand, and then in a moment it is over and the other team is celebrating. The Tigers have experienced this four times in 2026. Given that Tarik Skubal just returned from injury and the team was 6-21 in his absence, the walk-off misery is simply one of many difficult storylines the franchise has been navigating.

The Cincinnati Reds Are Winning Games They Have No Business Winning

The Cincinnati Reds opened the 2026 season by winning their first 10 games decided by one or two runs. Their team batting average in that stretch was .203. That combination — winning the close ones at a perfect rate while hitting like a team in an extended collective slump — makes them the fourth club in major league history to achieve this specific anomaly, joining the 1987 Milwaukee Brewers, the 1966 Cleveland Indians, and the 1946 Boston Red Sox.

What it means over a full season is the more interesting question. Teams that win close games at an extreme rate tend to regress toward normal over time — the randomness that helps them in tight spots eventually distributes itself more evenly. But while it lasts, the Reds are enjoying one of the stranger winning stretches in recent memory.

What's Next

Sánchez's streak will end at some point — every scoreless streak does. The question is how long he can hold it and whether the Phillies can convert his excellence into a deep October run. For the Tigers, every walk-off loss is a chapter in the case for why Skubal's return matters so profoundly. And for the Reds, the statistical anomaly will eventually correct — but the memories of those close-game wins will outlast the regression.
