Schwarber Leads MLB With 23 HRs and a 60-HR Pace — Chasing Baseball's Most Exclusive Club

By Jake Torres | June 10, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

Kyle Schwarber leads all of baseball with 23 home runs, sitting one ahead of Yordan Alvarez and well clear of everyone else on the leaderboard. Aaron Judge is on the injured list with a rib stress fracture. The competition has thinned. And the number Schwarber is chasing is starting to come into focus: 60.

Schwarber hit his 20th home run through his team's first 49 games — the earliest any player had reached that mark since Josh Hamilton in 2012. At that pace, his projection stretched toward 66 home runs. He has 92 games remaining in the regular season. The math is still alive.

The Most Exclusive Club in Baseball

Hitting 60 or more home runs in a single season has happened exactly five times in major league history, by five different players. Roger Maris hit 61 in 1961, establishing the single-season record that stood for 37 years. Mark McGwire hit 70 in 1998 and 65 in 1999. Sammy Sosa hit 66 in 1998, 63 in 1999, and 64 in 2001. Barry Bonds hit 73 in 2001 — the all-time record. Aaron Judge hit 62 in 2022, establishing the American League record.

That is five players. In the entire history of professional baseball, across well over a century of play, five human beings have accomplished this specific thing. Whatever one might say about the era or the circumstances, reaching 60 home runs in a single season is among the rarest individual achievements in professional sports.

Schwarber, 32, is on pace to become the sixth.

What Makes This Different

The context around Schwarber's chase is unusual in at least one significant way: he is doing it largely alone. In recent years, the home run leader typically had company — Judge and Schwarber himself were both on aggressive paces in 2022. This year, both Judge and Yoshinobu Murakami are on the injured list, leaving Schwarber without the competitive pressure that historically fuels these chase storylines.

That cuts both ways. Without a rival pushing him, there may be less urgency. But without a rival, there is also no one stealing his spotlight, no comparable story splitting the public's attention. If Schwarber continues at his current pace through July, the conversation around him will become impossible to ignore.

He has 92 games to go. He needs 37 more home runs to reach 60. That is roughly two per week — a pace he is demonstrably capable of sustaining.

What's Next

The next meaningful milestone is No. 30, which would keep his pace firmly in 60-HR territory. Watch for Schwarber's production in June and July — the middle of summer is when home run hitters typically find their rhythm, and a hot streak of four or five HRs in a week could ignite the conversation in a way that even casual fans start paying attention. The list is short. The door is open.
