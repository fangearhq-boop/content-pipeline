### STORY 2: Star-Spangled Sunday / Yamamoto

#### Facebook Long-Form Post

On July 5, every Major League Baseball game aired on one company — NBC, Peacock, and NBCSN — for the first time in the sport's history.

That's all 15 games, every market, all at once. Star-Spangled Sunday lived up to its name.

And Yoshinobu Yamamoto gave the Dodgers the highlight of the day: 7 innings, 10 strikeouts, zero runs allowed in a 3-0 shutout of the San Diego Padres in Sunday Night Baseball. Freddie Freeman, hours after being announced as an All-Star starter for the tenth time, added a home run. The Dodgers' rotation — anchored by Yamamoto, with Blake Snell and Tyler Glasnow as the other options — is as dominant as any staff in the game.

Elsewhere on the Star-Spangled Sunday slate: the Atlanta Braves pummeled the New York Mets 10-3 in the free-to-broadcast noon game on NBC. And in the AL Central game of the day, the Chicago White Sox defeated the Cleveland Guardians 3-1 at Progressive Field, trimming Cleveland's division lead to just one game.

The idea behind Star-Spangled Sunday — every fan, every market, all on one platform, no blackouts — is the kind of accessibility experiment MLB should repeat. Twenty-nine of the 30 teams were in action. The holiday weekend delivered.

### STORY 3: Home Run Derby Field Update

#### Facebook Long-Form Post

The 2026 T-Mobile Home Run Derby field is taking shape — and the biggest name on the AL side just dropped out.

Aaron Judge, the New York Yankees slugger who leads AL outfielders in almost every offensive category, will not participate in the July 13 Home Run Derby at Citizens Bank Park. He's been on the injured list since June 5 with a stress fracture on the right side of his first rib. He made the All-Star roster as a starter, but the Derby is off the table.

That opens the door for Junior Caminero, the Tampa Bay Rays infielder who is the first confirmed Derby entrant. If the Derby had started tonight, he might be the favorite.

Kyle Schwarber of the host Phillies is conditionally in. He made the All-Star team (reserves and pitchers are announced July 6), and his participation in the Derby depends on whether his back, which has been giving him trouble, holds up. Schwarber leads all of baseball with 30-plus home runs, and swinging for the fences at a home-crowd event in Philadelphia would be appointment television.

The Derby itself is notable for two reasons this year: it's streaming on Netflix for the first time (July 13, 8 PM ET), and it's adopting a new swing-based format — 20 swings in Round 1, 15 swings in Round 2 and the Final — instead of the timed format.

### STORY 4: Skubal Trade Market

#### Facebook Long-Form Post

The Tigers swept the Yankees in New York this week. Three games in the Bronx, three wins for Detroit.

And yet — Tarik Skubal is still being traded.

The Tigers are 38-49 as of July 5. They're eight games behind the White Sox in the AL Central. They're sellers. A week of winning doesn't change the math, and Skubal — a free agent this fall, a two-time defending AL Cy Young Award winner, and one of the five best pitchers on the planet — is still the most coveted trade asset ahead of the August 3 deadline.

Bob Nightengale of USA Today named the Atlanta Braves as the frontrunner among rival executives. The Braves' urgency is real: Spencer Strider remains sidelined with an injury, leaving the rotation without its best arm. Atlanta has the organizational depth to match what Detroit is asking — two top-50 prospects, a top-10 organizational arm, and MLB-ready depth.

The Yankees, despite their series loss to Detroit, took themselves out of the running. A Yankees source told reporters: "No chance. None." The Dodgers are also currently out, though they could re-enter if Blake Snell or Tyler Glasnow misses more time.

Other teams on Skubal's trail: the Brewers, Cubs, Phillies, Padres, Rays, and Red Sox — according to Jon Heyman. ESPN placed the trade probability at 85%.

Twenty-nine days until the August 3 deadline.

### STORY 5: AL Central Tightens to 1 Game

#### Facebook Long-Form Post

The Chicago White Sox came back from 41-121 in 2024 to play meaningful July baseball against the Cleveland Guardians. And they're not done yet.

The White Sox beat the Guardians 3-1 on July 5 at Progressive Field, trimming Cleveland's AL Central lead to a single game. The Guardians stand at approximately 47-43; the White Sox at 46-43. One game separates two teams that, combined, have spent most of the last decade either rebuilding or contending — but never quite overlapping the way they are now.

The White Sox's turnaround is one of the best stories in baseball. Heading into 2025, they were coming off a 41-121 disaster — the most losses in a single MLB season in the modern era. What happened in one year to get them back into a division race? The emergence of Yoshida Murakami (seven home runs in 21 games, the most by any Japanese-born player over that span), a healthier rotation, and a culture reset led by a new front office.

The Guardians, meanwhile, are doing what the Guardians do: winning games they have no business winning, squeezing every point of efficiency out of a payroll that sits below the league median, and daring rival executives to underestimate them.

Now comes the trade deadline (August 3), and both clubs face a version of the same question: do we buy? Sell? Stand pat? With one game separating them, there's no obvious answer for either side.

What does the AL Central look like at the August 3 deadline? That may be the most interesting question in the American League right now.
