White Sox Win July 5, Sit One Game Back in AL Central: The Best Turnaround Story in Baseball

By Jake Torres

July 5, 2026

The Chicago White Sox beat the Cleveland Guardians 3-1 at Progressive Field on July 5, cutting the AL Central deficit to one game. The Guardians sit at approximately 47-43; the White Sox at 46-43.

One game. In July. In a division race that involves the Chicago White Sox — a team that went 41-121 in 2024.

The numbers demand some context.

From 41-121 to One Game Back

The 2024 White Sox were historically bad. They didn't just lose — they set the modern MLB record for losses in a single season, surpassing the 1962 Mets' 120-loss mark that had stood for 62 years. The rebuild began immediately after that season, but rebuilds in baseball typically take three to five years to bear fruit.

The 2026 White Sox are in first-place range by the All-Star break. The turnaround is not a full rebuild completion — it's an acceleration, powered in part by the emergence of Yoshida Murakami, who hit seven home runs in a 21-game stretch earlier this season, the most by any Japanese-born player over that span. The new front office and a healthier rotation did the rest.

The Guardians' Position

Cleveland's one-game lead is legitimately earned. The Guardians are doing what this franchise does consistently: winning games without the payroll advantages of larger markets, outperforming their preseason projection, and frustrating rival executives who underestimate them every year.

The Guardians took the AL Central lead from the White Sox on July 3 with a 4-3 win. That lead is now down to one game. Two games back to back — a lead change and then a challenge — set up an October-style intensity in early July.

The Trade Deadline Question

With 29 days until the August 3 trade deadline, both the Guardians and White Sox face a version of the same decision: buy, sell, or stand pat.

Neither team is a clear buyer or seller at one game apart. If the race tightens further — or if one team breaks ahead — the deadline calculation becomes cleaner. As of July 5, both franchises are in the gray zone: close enough to contend, not dominant enough to justify a win-now mortgage on the future.

What the Guardians and White Sox are doing in the AL Central right now is making the case that the American League's most interesting division race isn't in the East.

What's Next

Both teams return to their home markets for a series of divisional matchups before the All-Star break. The Guardians have won the division in recent years by staying consistent through the stretch run. The White Sox's challenge is proving that their surge is sustainable over the second half, not a hot July that fades in August. The trade deadline will tell both clubs something about their own ambitions — and whether they believe this race is real. Right now, with one game separating them, it is very real.
