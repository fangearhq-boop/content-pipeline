Schwarber's 29 HR Pace: What History Actually Says About a 60-Plus Season

By Ryan Calloway | Ballpark Banter | June 25, 2026

Kyle Schwarber has 29 home runs. He has played 74 games. His June slash line is .324/.439/.632 with 7 home runs in his last 18 games.

The math, carried out over a full 162-game season, produces a home run projection in the 63-65 range.

That number belongs to a very short list of players in Major League Baseball history.

The 60-Plus HR Club

Baseball's 60-plus home run club is famously small. Babe Ruth hit 60 in 1927. Roger Maris hit 61 in 1961. Mark McGwire hit 70 in 1998 and 65 in 1999. Sammy Sosa hit 66 in 1998, 64 in 2001, and 63 in 1999. Barry Bonds hit 73 in 2001. These are the names, and the list does not grow often.

When Schwarber's 29 home runs in 74 games enter that conversation, the natural first response is skepticism. Most pace-projections in June don't survive contact with a full season. Slumps happen. Injuries happen. Second-half fatigue is real.

But Schwarber's June numbers are not the output of a player on a hot streak. They are the output of a player who has been broadly excellent all season: a .324 batting average in a month when he hit seven home runs suggests genuine contact and power combined, not just a home run-or-nothing approach.

He's 33 years old, in his prime as a power hitter. The Phillies are competing. He is motivated. The conditions for historic production are as present as they can reasonably be.

The Derby Question

The Home Run Derby is July 14. At Citizens Bank Park. Kyle Schwarber's home park, in front of Kyle Schwarber's fans.

He has not said yes.

His stated position has been "We'll see" — a non-commitment that has fueled weeks of speculation. The argument against participating is well-established: the mechanics of hitting for maximum distance in a Derby setting can sometimes disrupt a batter's regular-season approach, particularly for left-handed hitters with large swings. Past participants have pointed to slumps immediately following the event.

The argument for participating is the moment itself. A historic home run pace. A home-crowd setting. A July 13 Derby night at the park where he plays 81 games a year. If Schwarber is going to have a historic season, a Derby win at Citizens Bank Park would be the perfect exclamation point.

Philadelphia has been loud in its preference. The city wants him in it.

What's Next

Schwarber's Derby decision is expected within the next week — participants are typically announced well before the event. His home run total through June 25 is 29. Whatever his final decision on the Derby, the second-half pace watch begins immediately after the All-Star break ends. If he hits 10 home runs in July, the 60-plus conversation becomes unavoidable. The pace to watch: Roger Maris reached 61 in a 162-game season. To match that, Schwarber needs 32 more in his remaining 88 games — slightly below his current pace. The math is real. The history is there to be made.
