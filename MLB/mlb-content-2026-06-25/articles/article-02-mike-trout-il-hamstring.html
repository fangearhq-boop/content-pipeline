Mike Trout on the IL: Hamstring Strain Interrupts His Most Promising Season in Years

By Jake Torres | Ballpark Banter | June 25, 2026

Mike Trout was placed on the 10-day injured list on June 18 with a right hamstring strain suffered in a routine play running to first base against the Arizona Diamondbacks.

The injury itself is considered relatively minor. Trout was characteristically upbeat after it happened, saying he felt "significantly better" within 24 hours. There is no specific return date, but the optimism in his tone and the Angels' lack of alarm suggest this is likely a short stay — the earliest possible return under the 10-day minimum would be around June 28-30.

But even a minor injury, in the context of Trout's recent career history, lands with a certain weight.

A History of Interruptions

Mike Trout is 34 years old. He won three AL MVP awards between 2014 and 2019 and was, for a substantial period of his career, considered the best player in baseball. Since 2021, the conversation has been different — not about whether he's elite, but about whether he can stay on the field.

He missed significant time with a calf injury in 2021. He had wrist surgery and missed the majority of 2023. Various other ailments limited his 2024 output. The through-line has been a player of rare gifts whose career has been repeatedly disrupted at a time when many peers were in their peak years.

2026 was shaping up differently. Trout was healthy, and by the time the hamstring strain occurred in mid-June, he was producing at a level that recalled his prime. The injury is minor. But it is also one more data point in a narrative that his most devoted fans have been watching with a combination of hope and apprehension for years.

What the Angels Need

The Los Angeles Angels have not made the playoffs since 2014. For most of Trout's prime — a prime that produced numbers comparable to the all-time greats — the franchise failed to build a competitive team around him. That story is well-documented and well-mourned.

At 34, with his career in its final chapters, the window for Trout to experience postseason baseball is narrowing. The Angels need him healthy for that to become possible, and every injury at this stage of his career raises the question of how much prime he has remaining.

The hamstring strain should not derail his season. Recovery from a Grade 1 strain is typically measured in days to weeks, not months. But every day he's not in the lineup is a day that matters when the calendar is moving forward.

What's Next

Trout's earliest return from the 10-day IL is approximately June 28-30. The Angels have not set a specific activation date, and the timeline will be driven by how quickly the hamstring responds to treatment. Watch for Trout to begin baseball activities in the coming days — hitting and running on the field would be the signal that an activation is imminent. His return to the lineup will be a significant story whenever it happens, because every game he plays at this stage of his career is one worth watching.
