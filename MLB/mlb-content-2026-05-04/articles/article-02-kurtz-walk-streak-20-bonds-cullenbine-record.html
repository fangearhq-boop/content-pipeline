Nick Kurtz Walk Streak Ends at 20 — Tied Barry Bonds, Two Short of the 1947 All-Time Record

By Jake Torres | May 4, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

Nick Kurtz's walk streak ended on Saturday without breaking the record. But the company he kept along the way is worth lingering on.

The Oakland Athletics first baseman failed to draw a walk in Oakland's 14-6 loss to the Cleveland Guardians, ending a run of 20 consecutive games with at least one walk. During that stretch, Kurtz passed Ted Williams — who walked in 19 straight games — and tied Barry Bonds at 20. He needed just two more games to break Roy Cullenbine's all-time MLB record of 22, set in 1947.

He didn't get them. But what he built was genuinely historic.

The Record He Was Chasing

Roy Cullenbine's 22 consecutive games with a walk has stood since 1947. To put that in context: it predates the integration of baseball, predates television, and predates most of the baseball stadiums currently in use. The record survived Bonds' legendary 2002-2004 walk seasons — when pitchers would not challenge the slugger under any circumstances — and it survived every elite plate discipline practitioner the sport has seen since.

Kurtz, 22 years old and in his first full MLB season, came within two games of ending it. He passed Williams (19 games) and tied Bonds (20 games) — two of the most feared hitters in baseball history. The A's first baseman is not currently a feared hitter in the same sense; he is a patient one, forcing pitchers to work and turning on-base percentage into something approaching an art form.

What the Numbers Mean

Kurtz entered May leading all of MLB with 33 walks — more before May than any player since Bryce Harper drew 38 in 2018. His on-base streak extended to 25 consecutive games, the second-longest active run in the big leagues. His OBP has been among the best in the American League throughout the early part of the season.

For a rebuilding Athletics team in their new ballpark era, Kurtz represents the kind of controllable, elite offensive profile that defines a genuine franchise cornerstone. His walk rate is not a fluke of small samples — it has been consistent across every level of the minor leagues and has translated immediately at the big-league level.

The End of One Streak, the Start of a Story

Walk streaks are not like hitting streaks. No one counts them in real time the way the sports world watches a 20-game hit streak. They reveal themselves in the record books, weeks after the fact, when someone notices a name next to Bonds and Williams and Cullenbine. That's what happened with Kurtz — quietly, across twenty games, he built something historic.

The walk streak is over. The plate approach that created it remains fully intact. And for the Athletics and their fans, the more compelling question is what Kurtz looks like at 25 or 28, with a full season of MLB pitching in his body and the confidence that comes with approaching the record books as a rookie.

What's Next

Oakland returns home this week for a series against an AL West opponent. Kurtz's on-base streak of 25 consecutive games remains active and is one of the longest in baseball. His 33 walks through early May will be watched as the season progresses — if he maintains anything close to that pace, he is on track for one of the highest single-season walk totals in the modern era. Roy Cullenbine's record remains at 22 games. For now.
