Murakami at 13 HRs — The White Sox Rookie on Pace for One of the Greatest Power Seasons in MLB History

By Jake Torres | May 4, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

The number that matters right now is 13. The number that matters by October might be something closer to 65.

Munetaka Murakami enters this week leading all of Major League Baseball with 13 home runs through his first 32 career games — a pace of 65.8 home runs over a full 162-game season. Barry Bonds holds the all-time single-season home run record at 73. The Chicago White Sox rookie from Japan is not targeting that record. But he is making the kind of start that belongs in the record book regardless.

Historic Territory From Day One

Murakami's first 32 career games have produced the third-most home runs of any player in that span in baseball history. Only Rhys Hoskins, who hit 16 in his first 32 career games in 2017, and Aristides Aquino, who hit 14 across a partial 2018-19 debut, rank ahead of him.

Then there is this fact: Murakami's first 12 career extra-base hits were all home runs. Every single one. No doubles. No triples. Just gone. That has never happened in at least 125 years of recorded MLB statistics, dating back to when individual game logs first became reliable. Not by Babe Ruth in 1914. Not by Bonds at his most feared. Not by anyone.

It is the kind of statistical quirk that feels almost impossible — and yet here it is, attached to a 26-year-old making his Major League debut after a dominant career in Japan's Central League.

The Profile: Three True Outcomes, Maximum Damage

Murakami's offensive profile is almost binary. He walks or strikes out or hits a home run. Very little happens in between. His 33 walks and .967 OPS underscore a plate approach built around not chasing, not putting weak contact in play, and waiting for pitches he can damage. When he connects, the results are dramatic. When he doesn't, he works the count until he finds a walk or swings and misses.

This is not a sustainable profile for a contact hitter. For a power hitter, it is exactly what elite production looks like. The White Sox understood when they signed Murakami that he would not be a .300 average guy — he would be a slugger, a run-producer, a lineup anchor built around the three-run homer.

Thirteen home runs in. He has delivered on every expectation, and then some.

What It Means for the White Sox

Chicago's rebuild has been one of the longer stories in recent baseball. The White Sox have been developing young talent for several years, and Murakami's arrival represents the most exciting individual performer the franchise has had since the prime of their last contending era. With 26 RBIs in 32 games and a lineup that is beginning to coalesce around him, the White Sox have reason to believe May and June will show whether this team can compete in the AL Central — not just develop one spectacular individual story.

What's Next

Murakami and the White Sox continue their 2026 season this week with a series in the American League. He remains the clear MLB home run leader heading into a stretch of games that will test whether opposing pitchers have begun to adjust or whether the historic pace continues. Aaron Judge (12 HRs) and Yordan Alvarez are the nearest challengers in the HR race. The individual battles are worth watching. But the larger question — can Murakami sustain anything close to this pace deep into a full MLB season? — is the storyline that could define the 2026 baseball season.
