### STORY 1:

#### Long-Form Post -- 8:00 AM
José Ramírez just added another chapter to one of baseball's greatest careers.

On Saturday, the Cleveland Guardians third baseman stole his 300th career base in the team's 14-6 victory over the Oakland Athletics. He joined Kenny Lofton as the only Guardians players to reach that milestone in franchise history. And with it, he moved into extremely exclusive territory.

Ramírez now needs just nine more home runs to join the 300 HR/300 SB club — one of baseball's most elite distinctions. Only eight players in the history of the sport have pulled it off: Barry Bonds, Alex Rodriguez, Willie Mays, Andre Dawson, Carlos Beltrán, Bobby Bonds, Reggie Sanders, and Steve Finley.

Think about that company for a second.

Ramírez enters the week with 291 career home runs and is on pace for around 30 this season. If that holds, he joins that list sometime in late summer or early fall. And he's already chasing a third consecutive 30-30 season — which would make him just the 9th player in ML history to accomplish back-to-back-to-back 30-30 campaigns.

In Cleveland. Quietly dominant. Making history one stolen base at a time.

### STORY 2:

#### Long-Form Post -- 10:30 AM
Nick Kurtz came within two games of rewriting baseball history.

The Oakland Athletics first baseman drew a walk in 20 consecutive games before his streak ended Saturday in a 14-6 loss to the Cleveland Guardians. During that run, Kurtz passed Ted Williams (19 games) and tied Barry Bonds (20) — two of the greatest hitters in the history of the sport. The all-time record, set by Roy Cullenbine in 1947, stands at 22 consecutive games. Kurtz needed just two more.

He didn't get them. But what he built was remarkable.

Kurtz entered May leading the majors with 33 walks — more than any hitter before May since Bryce Harper had 38 in 2018. His on-base streak stands at 25 consecutive games, the second-longest active run in the big leagues. At just 22 years old, his advanced plate discipline has made him one of the more interesting offensive profiles in the American League.

The walk streak is over. The player is not going anywhere.

### STORY 3:

#### Long-Form Post -- 11:00 AM
It happened in 1909. It happened in 1983. And it happened again on Saturday.

The Pittsburgh Pirates drew seven walks in a single inning against the Cincinnati Reds, tying the MLB record for most walks by a team in a single inning — an occurrence so rare it had only taken place twice before in the entire history of the sport.

Baseball is a game of precision and control. Pitching is prized above almost everything. And yet, somewhere in Cincinnati, seven consecutive Pittsburgh batters reached base on four balls in a single frame. No one who saw it could have predicted it would tie a mark that has existed since a time before World War I.

These are the moments that make baseball endlessly fascinating. The records no one is tracking until they're being broken — or tied — right in front of you.

### STORY 5:

#### Long-Form Post -- 1:00 PM
Munetaka Murakami is writing history on a daily basis.

The Chicago White Sox rookie enters this week leading MLB with 13 home runs in his first 32 career games. That's the third-most home runs anyone has hit in their first 32 career games — trailing only Rhys Hoskins (16 in 2017) and Aristides Aquino (14 in 2018-19). He's on a 65-home run pace.

And then there's this: his first 12 career extra-base hits were all home runs. That's never happened in at least 125 years of Major League Baseball. Not by Babe Ruth. Not by Barry Bonds. Not by anyone.

The 26-year-old from Japan — a multi-time Central League MVP — has adapted to MLB pitching with a ferocity that surprised even his biggest believers. His three-true-outcomes profile (home run or walk or strikeout, almost nothing in between) makes him one of the most unique offensive profiles in the sport. He's drawing 33 walks. He's posting a .967 OPS.

He's also playing for a White Sox team trying to turn the corner from a multi-year rebuild. If Murakami's historic pace holds — even partially — it accelerates that timeline considerably.

65 home runs. 125 years of history. All coming from a player most casual fans had never heard of eight months ago.

### STORY 6:

#### Long-Form Post -- 8:00 PM
The streak is over. The hitter is not.

Ildemaro Vargas went 0-for-4 Saturday as the Cubs' Shota Imanaga shut out the Diamondbacks 2-0, ending one of the most unexpected hitting streaks in recent baseball history at 27 games. He's the first Venezuelan-born player to hit in 27 or more consecutive games. He set the record for the second-longest hitting streak to open an MLB season since 1940. His 27-game run was the longest in the big leagues since Trea Turner hit in 27 straight games in 2021-22.

And yet on Sunday morning, he woke up still leading major league baseball with a .388 batting average.

The story was the streak. But the player is the story going forward. Vargas — 34 years old, a journeyman utility player who spent most of his career as a backup — is having the best offensive season of his life. What happens when his average is still .360 in July? .340 in September?

The streak wrote the headline. The bat will write the rest of the story.
