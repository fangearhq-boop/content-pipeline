Schwarber Has 23 HRs — And the Clearest Path to the 60-HR Club Since Barry Bonds

By Jake Torres | June 11, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

The 60-home run club is one of the most exclusive in baseball history. Its membership list contains exactly five names: Roger Maris (61, 1961), Mark McGwire (70 in 1998, 65 in 1999), Sammy Sosa (66 in 1998, 64 in 1999, 63 in 2001), Barry Bonds (73 in 2001), and Aaron Judge (62 in 2022). That's it. In the entire history of major league baseball, only five men have ever hit 60 or more home runs in a single season.

Kyle Schwarber, as of June 11, 2026, leads MLB with 23 home runs and is on the most credible pace toward that list since Judge's 2022 season. With Yordan Alvarez second at 22, Aaron Judge on the IL with a rib fracture, and Munetaka Murakami also sidelined, Schwarber stands alone at the top of the leaderboard. And the pace he's setting is not subtle.

What the Pace Means

Schwarber hit his 20th home run on May 15 — a date described at the time as the earliest any player had ever reached that milestone in MLB history. Entering June 11, he has added three more and shows no signs of slowing down. His approach at the plate remains consistent: patient when pitchers try to nibble at the edges, explosive when they put something in the zone. He is not a streak hitter in the traditional sense — he is a sustained force who produces at a rate that keeps compounding.

The math behind a 60-HR season is demanding. A player who hits 23 home runs in the first 63 or so games of the season is on pace for approximately 59 to 62 home runs over a full 162-game schedule — dependent on how the second half goes. The 60-HR bar has historically been broken only by players who maintained an almost impossible rate through July and August, when pitchers have more data and bullpen usage intensifies. Schwarber has proven himself capable of sustaining production through the grind of a long season.

The Competition Landscape

Alvarez at 22 home runs is close enough to keep the race interesting. He is a genuine 50-HR threat in any year his health holds, and if Judge returns from the IL on schedule in mid-to-late July, the three-way race between Schwarber, Alvarez, and a returning Judge could define the second half of the season.

For now, Schwarber is alone at the top. He is hitting in a ballpark — Citizens Bank Park — that rewards power, for a team that needs him to produce at a high level, with a lineup around him that gives pitchers no safe place to work around him. The conditions for a historic season are present.

What's Next

The next milestones to watch: home run No. 25 (approaching), No. 30, and the All-Star Break total, which will tell us whether Schwarber is truly on a 60-HR pace or whether regression is beginning. Every game he plays, every at-bat, every swing carries weight that other players' at-bats simply don't right now. The 60-HR club is historic, rare, and real. Schwarber is the closest thing baseball has to a genuine candidate since Judge did it four years ago.
