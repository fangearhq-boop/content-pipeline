Harris II Bases-Clearing Double Caps Comeback — Braves 3-2 Pirates, Best Record Continues

By Ryan Calloway | June 11, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

They trailed 2-0 heading into the seventh inning. They found a way to win. That is what the 2026 Atlanta Braves do.

Michael Harris II came to the plate Thursday with the bases loaded and a two-run deficit to erase, and he responded with a bases-clearing double off Pittsburgh Pirates reliever Evan Sisk. Three runs scored. The Braves led 3-2. They held on for the win. One more addition to what has become the most impressive record in baseball.

This is who the Braves are in 2026: a team that doesn't panic when it falls behind, that finds moments to exploit late in games, and that keeps building a win total that no one predicted when the season began. A year ago, Atlanta went 76-86 and missed the playoffs entirely. This year, under first-year manager Walt Weiss, they have the best record in major league baseball.

What Makes This Team Different

The comeback Thursday wasn't exceptional by the standards of this Braves team — it was typical. Weiss has built a culture in which getting behind doesn't change the approach. Players trust the lineup to produce. They trust the pitching to hold. They play clean baseball and put themselves in positions to win games that other teams might let slip away.

Chris Sale anchoring the rotation is a significant part of the story. When Sale is on the mound, the Braves are not going to give up five or six runs to a struggling opponent. He creates clean games — innings that don't require the offense to dig out of a significant hole. That reliability compounds across a 162-game schedule into exactly the kind of win total Atlanta is accumulating.

Ronald Acuña Jr., who missed significant time last season, has returned to form and is producing at the elite level that made him the best position player in the NL before his injury. His presence changes the way opposing managers construct their pitching plans — you can't ignore him, which means the players around him get better pitches to hit.

The Turnaround in Context

Going from 76-86 to the best record in baseball in one year is not a subtle improvement. It is dramatic, structural, and driven by something beyond just health and luck. Teams don't make this kind of leap simply because they stayed healthy — they make it because something about the way the team operates changed. The hiring of Walt Weiss appears to be that something.

Weiss hasn't managed before at the MLB level, which made the hire a risk. But first-year managers occasionally install exactly the culture a veteran roster was missing. The evidence, through Thursday, suggests that's what happened in Atlanta.

What's Next

The Braves continue to build their NL East lead. Their margin over the rest of the division has created a position where they can lose a series without panic. The question for the rest of the season is whether they can sustain this level of performance through summer — and whether Sale's workload can be managed effectively as the calendar turns to July and August. For now, Thursday was another win that looks unremarkable on its surface and extraordinary in context.
