From 2-8 to a Series Win: Boston's Season Finally Has a Pulse

By Jake Torres | April 12, 2026

It took thirteen games and two weeks of historically bad baseball, but the Boston Red Sox finally won a series.

Ranger Suárez pitched six scoreless innings. Willson Contreras drove in three runs. Boston beat St. Louis 7-1 on Saturday, capturing the series against the Cardinals and giving a fanbase desperately in need of something positive the first genuine reason for optimism since Opening Day.

The Red Sox are still 5-8. They still have significant ground to make up in the American League East. But after a start that matched the worst in 126 years of franchise history, winning two straight games — back-to-back for the first time since September 2025 — is not nothing. It's a foothold.

How Bad Has It Been?

The numbers have not been kind. Boston's 2-8 start through the first 10 games tied the worst 10-game opening in franchise history dating back to 1900. Manager Alex Cora, who has presided over championship seasons and never been someone to spin difficult realities, called the stretch "unacceptable." The team's batting average hovered around .208 for the first two weeks. They lost five games by two runs or fewer — games that could have gone the other way with a different bounce or a better at-bat with runners on base.

The offseason additions that were supposed to change Boston's competitive ceiling — Suárez, Sonny Gray, Contreras — had delivered almost nothing in the early weeks. Gray pitched well when it counted in the Milwaukee series win, but the overall picture was of a team that hadn't translated its offseason moves into on-field results.

Saturday Looked Like the Version Boston Paid For

The 7-1 win over St. Louis felt different. Suárez threw six scoreless innings with command, keeping a Cardinals lineup that had been productive early in the season off-balance throughout his time in the game. Contreras — one of the more expensive acquisitions of Boston's offseason — drove in three runs and showed the middle-of-the-order impact the Red Sox needed from him when they signed him.

Earlier in the week, Gray had delivered 6.1 innings of shutout ball in the series win over Milwaukee — the result that gave Boston consecutive wins for the first time all season. Both Gray and Suárez, on the same weekend, reminding the city of Boston why they were brought in.

The offense still has work to do. The team's struggles with runners in scoring position have been real, and one productive game doesn't erase two weeks of wasted opportunities. But when your rotation pitches well in back-to-back games, the offense tends to breathe easier — and that dynamic played out over the weekend.

What Needs to Happen Next

Boston needs this weekend to be the beginning of something, not a two-game interruption before the struggles resume. The bullpen has been inconsistent. The lineup still needs to perform with runners on base at a higher rate. And the early April schedule showed a team that was not executing in close games.

The American League East is competitive enough that falling too far behind in April creates real problems. The Yankees are at 8-5. Baltimore is fighting. Boston at 5-8 isn't eliminated from anything in mid-April, but the margin for error shrinks with each loss.

What's Next

The Red Sox continue their schedule after the Cardinals series with what they hope is a different identity than the one that defined their first two weeks. If Suárez and Gray keep pitching at their Saturday and Thursday levels, and the offense continues to build on the Cardinals win, Boston has the roster to make a run. The first series win of 2026 doesn't guarantee anything. But it confirms that the talent is there — it just needs to show up on a consistent basis.

For a team that tied a 126-year record for futility two weeks in, a series win is the first real evidence that the season isn't lost before it's started. That's worth something in April.
