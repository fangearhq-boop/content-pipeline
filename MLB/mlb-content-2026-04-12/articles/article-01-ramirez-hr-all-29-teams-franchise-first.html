In 125 Years of Cleveland Baseball, No One Had Done This Until Jose Ramirez

By Ryan Calloway | April 12, 2026

The Cleveland Guardians have been playing baseball since 1901 — through the Naps era, the Indians era, and the Guardians era — and in all that time, no player had ever hit a home run against every other team in Major League Baseball.

Jose Ramirez ended that streak on Friday night in Atlanta.

His first-inning home run off Braves starter Martin Perez completed the set: all 29 opponents, one home run apiece. The Guardians won 6-0, Parker Messick delivered 6.2 innings of shutout pitching, and Cleveland left Truist Park with a series split. But the number that will be remembered from April 11 is the one attached to Ramirez's name in the franchise record books: the first, in 125 years, to do this.

What Makes the Milestone Significant

The list of players who have worn a Cleveland uniform across 125 seasons is staggering. Larry Doby. Bob Lemon. Early Wynn. Rocky Colavito. Sam McDowell. Gaylord Perry. Frank Robinson. Albert Belle. Manny Ramirez. Jim Thome. Carlos Santana. None of them ever achieved the complete collection.

Part of what makes the achievement so difficult is straightforward geography and opportunity: to homer against all 29 opponents, you need to face all 29 — and you need to connect each time. The interleague scheduling that guarantees at least some exposure to every team was only introduced in 1997. Long careers, yes, but the sample sizes haven't always been there.

Ramirez also brings a particular wrinkle to this: he has spent his entire career in Cleveland. Not many players stay in one organization long enough to build this kind of cumulative achievement. His loyalty to the franchise adds a layer to the accomplishment that simple statistics can't fully capture.

April 2026 Is a Historic Month for Ramirez

The 29-team home run achievement is the third franchise milestone Ramirez has stacked in the first two weeks of the 2026 season. On April 6, he played his 1,620th career game as a Guardian, breaking the all-time franchise record for games played — the sole all-time leader. He is pacing for AL Player of the Month with a .342 average, .722 slugging percentage, 7 home runs, and 28 RBI in 21 games. His RBI total leads MLB by a significant margin.

The cumulative picture of what he has done for Cleveland's franchise — the offensive production, the postseason contributions, the decades of consistent excellence — is the kind of résumé that baseball historians point to when discussing where a player ranks among the all-time greats at their position.

Cleveland Is Right There With Him

The 6-0 win over Atlanta wasn't just a vehicle for Ramirez's milestone. Parker Messick's performance was a reminder of what the Guardians have quietly built in their rotation. Six and two-thirds innings of shutout ball against an Atlanta lineup that can cause problems is a legitimate statement from a pitcher still carving out his place in a major league rotation.

Cleveland is competing in the AL Central and has the talent to be a postseason team again in 2026. Ramirez's April output is the engine. The young arms around him are the supporting cast. It's a franchise that has learned to stay relevant without the payroll of the sport's biggest markets.

What's Next

There are no new entries to add to the 29-team home run list for Ramirez — the challenge is complete. What's left is the pursuit of individual milestones that carry even larger implications: 300 career home runs (he is approaching 300), 300 career stolen bases (within striking distance), and the continued case for a plaque in Cooperstown that grows more compelling every April.

Ramirez checked off a box on Friday night that no Guardian had ever touched. By Monday, he'll be focused on the next game, the next at-bat, the next chance to add to a legacy that is already unlike anything Cleveland baseball has produced before.
