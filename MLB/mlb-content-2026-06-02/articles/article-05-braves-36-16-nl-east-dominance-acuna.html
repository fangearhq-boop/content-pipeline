Braves Are 36-16 With a Nine-Game NL East Lead — When Does the Division Race End?

By Jake Torres | June 2, 2026

At some point in a baseball season, a division race stops being a race. The Atlanta Braves are not there yet — no division is technically over in early June — but at 36–16 with a lead in the NL East measured in nearly double digits, they are doing everything they need to do to get there before summer ends.

The Braves have the best record in the National League. They have 18 comeback wins. They have Ronald Acuña Jr. hitting grand slams. And they have Chris Sale, at 37 years old, striking out eight batters in five innings against his former team as though the calendar reads 2018.

What the Numbers Mean

A nine-game lead in early June is one of the most significant in baseball because of how it compounds. A team that is nine back in June faces a nearly impossible mathematical task: they need to gain roughly a game a week against a team that is also still trying to win.

The Braves have not won in spite of their schedule. They have won against it — building and maintaining a lead through the difficult portions of the early calendar that separate genuinely excellent teams from those that fade when the opposition gets harder.

The 18 comeback wins are the number that deserves the most attention. Winning games when you are behind requires a combination of bullpen strength, lineup depth, and the competitive character that organizations spend years trying to build. Atlanta has it. They find ways to put runs on the board late in games when lesser teams settle for the loss.

Acuña Back to Form

Ronald Acuña Jr.'s 2025 was interrupted by injury, limiting what many expected would be another MVP-caliber campaign. The 2026 version of Acuña appears to be healthy and dangerous again. His recent grand slam — his fourth career grand slam — came in a 10–2 win over Boston, the kind of statement performance that reminds the NL why he is one of the most feared players in the league when right.

Acuña hitting at full strength in the middle of an already loaded Atlanta lineup creates matchup problems that have no clean answer. Teams cannot pitch around the lineup because there is no easy out anywhere from top to bottom. They have to face Acuña, and when he is right, facing Acuña is a problem.

Sale's Remarkable Longevity

Chris Sale is 37 years old. His career has included multiple elbow surgeries, a Tommy John surgery, and the kind of physical disruption that ends most pitchers' careers as front-line starters. He is somehow still one. Eight strikeouts in five innings against the Boston Red Sox — his former team — is not the performance of a pitcher fighting to stay in a rotation. It is the performance of a pitcher who still has elite stuff and knows exactly how to use it.

Sale's ability to contribute as a mid-rotation starter on a championship-caliber team extends Atlanta's pitching depth in ways that matter in October. Playoff rotations compress; good teams need four or five starters who can pitch in high-leverage situations. The Braves have that.

What's Next

Atlanta's schedule in June will test whether this lead holds and grows or tightens slightly as opponents get more looks. The NL East rivals — the Phillies, Mets, and Marlins — are not going away, and the Phillies in particular have enough offensive firepower (including Kyle Schwarber's historic home run chase) to threaten if the Braves go through even a modest stretch of inconsistency.

But the Braves have shown all year that inconsistency is not part of their game. They lead this division for good reason, and if they play the second half the way they have played the first, the discussion about who wins the NL East will be over long before September. The conversation worth having now is who in October challenges them — because right now, no team in the National League has a convincing answer.
