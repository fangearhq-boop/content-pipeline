Kyle Schwarber Has 22 Home Runs and an Appointment With History

By Ryan Calloway | June 2, 2026

It is early June and Kyle Schwarber has 22 home runs. The math that follows that sentence is where the story gets remarkable.

The Philadelphia Phillies designated hitter leads all of Major League Baseball in home runs and is on a pace — through roughly 55 games — that projects to approximately 66 home runs for the full season. That number, if reached, would represent the highest single-season home run total in the sport since the era that baseball has spent two decades trying to move past.

To be clear about what that would mean: Schwarber, hitting in the modern era with modern equipment under modern rules, would be approaching the territory occupied only by Barry Bonds, Mark McGwire, and Sammy Sosa in seasons that carry asterisks in the public memory.

The Historical Context

Only 17 players in baseball history had hit at least 20 home runs through a team's first 49 games before Schwarber reached that milestone this season. He is the 18th, and the first to do so since Josh Hamilton in 2012.

The players with more than 20 through 49 games in history's landmark home run seasons: Mark McGwire in 1998 (en route to 70) had 25. Barry Bonds in 2001 (en route to 73) had 25. Every other player in the history of the sport has fewer than 20 through that point.

Schwarber is now at 22 and still climbing.

What It Actually Looks Like

The home runs themselves are not flukes. Schwarber has always been a power hitter, but the 2026 version has added a level of plate discipline and selectivity that makes his power sustainable rather than episodic. He is hitting the ball in the air at a rate that maximizes his elite exit velocity, and he is not chasing pitches the way a player swinging for records might be expected to do.

For the Phillies, Schwarber's home run pace is the most obvious piece of a broader offensive narrative. Philadelphia has the kind of lineup that creates pressure across nine batters, and Schwarber's production in the middle of that order — the threat of one swing ending an inning or a game — makes every at-bat around him more difficult for opposing pitchers to navigate.

The Pace Caveat

Pace projections are mathematical exercises that assume a consistent rate of performance through 162 games. Schwarber will not hit 66 home runs if he hits zero in the next month. He will not hit 66 if he enters a prolonged slump in August. Home run totals that climb early in the season almost always come back toward averages as pitchers adjust and fatigue accumulates.

What matters is not whether Schwarber finishes at 66 exactly. What matters is that he is producing at a rate — right now, in June — that has made him the most important offensive story in the sport. Every game, every at-bat, the question is whether this is the day he hits another one.

What's Next

Schwarber and the Phillies continue through the early-June schedule with the home run chase as the backdrop to every game. The questions to watch: Does the pace hold in June? Do pitchers begin to pitch around him more aggressively? Does the supporting cast behind him in the lineup continue to prevent that from happening?

The single-season home run record — 73, held by Barry Bonds since 2001 — is not within reach at the current pace. But 60 is. And 60 home runs in a single season, clean, in 2026, would be the most significant offensive performance in decades. Schwarber is already halfway to a number that has been reached only a handful of times in the sport's history.
