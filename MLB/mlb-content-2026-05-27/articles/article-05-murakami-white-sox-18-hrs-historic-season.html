Murakami's Historic Start: 18 HRs, 41 Walks, and a Record No One Has Ever Set in MLB

By Jake Torres

May 27, 2026

Munetaka Murakami is the first player in baseball history to hit 10 home runs and draw 20 walks in his first 25 career MLB games. Now at 18 HRs in 53 games, the White Sox rookie is redefining what a historic debut looks like.

The Chicago White Sox have struggled to generate positive headlines over the past several years. Munetaka Murakami has made that problem considerably easier to manage.

In his first 53 major league games, Murakami has 18 home runs, 37 RBIs, a .540 slugging percentage, and a .914 OPS. He leads the American League in home runs and ranks second in all of baseball. And buried inside those numbers is one line that no player in the history of the sport has ever produced: 10 home runs and 20 walks in his first 25 career major league games.

Nobody had done it before. Murakami did it before anyone realized it was possible.

The Combination That Makes the History

Power in baseball is one thing. The home run is the sport's most celebrated individual play, and the history of the position is full of players who hit the ball over the fence consistently from early in their careers. What separates Murakami is the plate discipline that accompanies the power.

His on-base percentage of .374 sits 139 points above his batting average of .235. That gap — the difference between getting on base and getting a hit — is almost entirely walks. Murakami draws them at a rate that would be remarkable for a veteran with full command of the strike zone. He is doing it in his first professional season in North America, against pitchers who are actively trying to discover and exploit his weaknesses.

So far, they have mostly failed. He walks more than he makes outs on balls in play. When he does make contact, a meaningful portion of it leaves the park. The combination of those two outcomes — walks and home runs — makes him one of the most difficult offensive players to manage in the American League, regardless of how much experience he has in the league.

He is also the fourth player in MLB history to hit that many home runs through his first 53 career games, placing him in historical company that includes some of the sport's most celebrated power hitters.

The Hot Streak Within the Historic Season

If Murakami's baseline production is historic, his recent form has elevated even that standard. Over his last six games, Murakami went 12-for-27 — a .444 batting average — with 10 RBIs. A player who was already producing at an unprecedented pace has become noticeably hotter at a point in the season when most players manage to stay consistent rather than improve.

The 18th home run pushed his total to a level where the comparison to the most significant rookie seasons in recent history becomes unavoidable. He is leading the AL at the position where the power hitters in the league traditionally cluster. The White Sox's decision to invest heavily in Murakami's acquisition from Japan is looking better with every game he plays.

The Bigger Picture for Chicago

The White Sox entered 2026 with questions about how quickly their rebuild would translate into wins. Murakami has been one of the most important answers to those questions. His production has given a young roster an immediate focal point and a legitimate star around whom the team can build its identity on the field.

The AL Rookie of the Year conversation is already happening, and it is happening with Murakami's name at the top. He is not just producing. He is producing in a way that has not been done before, in numbers that hold up against the best rookie seasons in baseball history, and doing it in a Chicago uniform that needed exactly this kind of lift.

What's Next

Munetaka Murakami continues his 2026 season with the White Sox as they push through the spring and into the summer schedule. Each at-bat carries the weight of a record-setting pace, and the question going forward is not whether he belongs in the major leagues — that question was answered emphatically — but how high the final numbers climb when the season ends in October.
