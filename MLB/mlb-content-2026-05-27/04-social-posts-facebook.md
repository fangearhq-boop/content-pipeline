### STORY 1: Jacob Misiorowski — Statcast Era Record

#### FB Long-Form — 8:00 AM ET

Let's start Wednesday morning with something you may have missed on Memorial Day.

Jacob Misiorowski threw 57 pitches at 100 miles per hour or faster on Monday night against the St. Louis Cardinals. That is not a typo, and it has never happened before — not once, in the entire history of MLB pitch tracking, which dates back to 2008.

The previous record was 47, set by Hunter Greene of the Reds in September 2022. Misiorowski didn't just break it — he threw ten more than anyone had ever thrown.

Here is what the outing looked like: 96 total pitches. 57 at 100+ mph. 40 at 101+. Nine at 103+. A top velocity of 103.4 mph, which he reached three times. Twelve strikeouts, matching his career high. Milwaukee won 5-1.

And Misiorowski is 24 years old and in just his second full season. He already has 100 strikeouts in 11 starts this year. When asked about the record-setting performance, the reaction was about as understated as the outing was the opposite. Four words, casual shrug, move on.

The Brewers are flying high in the power rankings right now, and this is a significant reason why. When a pitcher can sustain that velocity for nine innings with that kind of command, opposing lineups have no answer. You just take your strikeout and walk back to the dugout.

MLB has seen some great young arms in the past decade. Misiorowski is making a case to be the most electrically-dominant of them all. ⚾

### STORY 3: Corbin Carroll — One Triple From D-Backs Record

#### FB Long-Form — 12:30 PM ET

Corbin Carroll is one hit away from making Arizona history.

Carroll currently has 51 career triples — one shy of Stephen Drew's franchise record of 52, the most ever by a player in Diamondbacks history. He reached that total with a pair of triples against the Colorado Rockies on May 24, and now the D-backs are in San Francisco for a three-game series where that record could fall at any moment.

The pace at which Carroll has accumulated triples is almost unreasonable. He needed just 537 career games to reach 51. Drew, the franchise's previous record holder, needed 773 games to get to 52. Carroll has eight triples in 2026 alone — twice as many as any other player in baseball. He leads all active MLB players in career triples pace behind only Mike Trout and Starling Marte.

Carroll is 25 years old. He already holds the D-backs' top two single-season marks for triples (14 in 2024, 17 in 2025). At the rate he is going, he won't just tie this record — he'll eventually own it by a margin that future players won't approach for decades.

But first: the tie. Then the break. Oracle Park in San Francisco this week. Record watch is officially on.

### STORY 4: Blake Snell Surgery Update

#### FB Long-Form — 3:00 PM ET

For Dodger fans keeping an eye on Blake Snell's elbow, there is cautious good news out of Los Angeles.

Snell underwent surgery to remove three bone spurs from his left elbow, and Dodgers GM Brandon Gomes confirmed the procedure went "as expected." The team is expecting Snell to return this season — the question is how quickly.

There are two models on the table. In the optimistic scenario, Snell follows the path Tarik Skubal took after a similar procedure by Dr. Neal ElAttrache — Skubal threw his first bullpen session just 12 days after surgery. If Snell's recovery tracks the same way, a return before the All-Star break might be in the picture.

The more conservative comparison is Edwin Díaz, whose timeline after a related procedure ran closer to three months. That timeline would push Snell's return to late summer.

The Dodgers haven't sat still in the meantime. They acquired lefty reliever Eric Lauer from the Toronto Blue Jays as part of their response to the pitching depth issues, which also include Jack Dreyer being placed on the injured list and Brusdar Graterol going to the 60-day IL.

Snell made his 2026 season debut before the surgery, giving up four earned runs in three innings after dealing with shoulder fatigue earlier in the year. The Dodgers are clearly planning around his eventual return — they just don't know yet what "eventually" looks like.

### STORY 5: Murakami — Historic White Sox Season

#### FB Long-Form — 6:00 PM ET

The White Sox don't make a lot of baseball history. Munetaka Murakami is doing everything he can to change that.

In his first 25 career MLB games, Murakami hit 10 home runs and drew 20 walks. No one in baseball history had ever done that before — not in the history of the professional game. He became the first player ever to reach both thresholds in his first 25 games.

Now, in 53 games, he has 18 home runs, 37 RBIs, a .540 slugging percentage, and a .914 OPS. He leads the American League in home runs and ranks second in all of baseball. His on-base percentage is .374 — 139 points higher than his batting average of .235 — which means he walks more than he gets hits, and when he gets hits, a lot of them go over the fence.

He has been particularly scorching recently: 12-for-27 (.444) over his last six games with 10 RBIs. A player who was already producing at a historic pace has gotten hotter.

Murakami came from Japan as one of the most heralded international signings in recent years, and the White Sox needed him to be good. He has been historic. There's a significant difference between those two things, and Chicago is figuring out what to do with it.

AL Rookie of the Year race? He's winning it right now. ⚾
