Skubal Wants to Stay. The Tigers Are Actually Listening Now.

By Jake Torres

July 18, 2026

A few weeks ago, Tarik Skubal's trade felt like a formality. The Detroit Tigers were sellers. Skubal was a left-handed ace heading to a contender. Multiple outlets had trade probability estimates above 80 percent.

Now it is not so simple.

Skubal has told people close to him that he does not want to be traded — that he believes this Tigers team can win the World Series. And the Tigers, who have played outstanding baseball since June 1, are starting to take that seriously.

The August 3 deadline is 16 days away.

Why the Situation Has Changed

The Tigers have not been a good team for most of this decade. But since June 1 they have played at a level that resembles a genuine contender — winning far more than they have lost and positioning themselves in the AL Wild Card picture in a way that looked impossible earlier this season.

Skubal has been central to that run. He carries a 3.09 ERA, 0.95 WHIP, and 89 strikeouts across 75.2 innings in 13 starts — numbers that would rank him among the best pitchers in baseball if sustained. He missed time earlier this season after elbow surgery to remove a loose body, but has been dominant since returning.

When a pitcher at that level tells the organization he wants to stay — and the team has its own reasons to believe they can compete — the trade calculus shifts.

The Other Side of the Ledger

The suitors are still there. CBS Sports has identified the Chicago Cubs, Atlanta Braves, and Tampa Bay Rays as the strongest active suitors. An NL executive told Bleacher Report that the Tigers' asking price was "outrageous" — a word that either means Detroit is overvaluing Skubal or that they are using price as a way to justify keeping him.

Other outlets still project the trade as more likely than not, placing probability estimates above 80 percent. The single source that described Skubal as "all but confirmed staying" stands in contrast to the broader trade press coverage.

The situation is genuinely uncertain. Two sources can look at the same set of facts and come to opposite conclusions about where this ends up.

The Player's Voice

What is not uncertain is Skubal's own preference. He has made clear to people close to him that he wants to be in Detroit. That matters. Players who are traded over their explicit objection sometimes disengage in ways that can affect performance. For a team paying the asking price that Detroit has reportedly set, acquiring a pitcher who does not want to be there is a real risk.

That dynamic gives the Tigers leverage that is separate from Skubal's performance. They can hold firm on price and let Skubal's stated preference do some of the work for them.

What's Next

The August 3 deadline is the hard stop. Whatever happens with Skubal will be resolved in the next 16 days. The Cubs, Braves, and Rays are active. The Tigers' posture — whether they are genuinely entertaining staying put or using high price as a negotiating device — will become clearer as the deadline approaches. Skubal is scheduled to make his next start soon, and each performance adds more to the equation. By August 4, this will all be settled one way or another.
