Chase Burns Signs 7-Year, $105 Million Extension — the Reds Just Changed Everything

By Jake Torres

July 18, 2026

The Cincinnati Reds were supposed to be sellers this summer. That plan is officially over.

The Reds have signed right-hander Chase Burns to a seven-year, $105 million contract extension covering the 2027 through 2033 seasons, the team announced this week. The deal carries no options, no deferrals, and no complexity — just $15 million flat per year for seven years, locked in before Burns has reached his fourth year of major league service time.

It is the largest contract extension ever given to a pitcher with fewer than four years of MLB service time.

The Numbers Behind the Deal

Burns, who turns 24 this season, has been one of the most compelling young arms in baseball in 2026. He carries a 2.54 ERA with 118 strikeouts and 37 walks across 18 starts. He was selected for the National League All-Star team. He was also considered one of the most coveted names on the August 3 trade market — a pitcher several contenders had reportedly circled as a centerpiece acquisition.

That is no longer a possibility.

The extension removes Burns from trade consideration and signals something more fundamental: the Reds are not a team in sell mode anymore. With Burns locked through 2033, Cincinnati has a young ace under contract for the better part of a decade. Whatever the front office builds around him, it has time to do it.

A Franchise-Altering Commitment

The scale of this deal is worth pausing on. Seven years and $105 million for a pitcher who has not yet reached his fourth year of service time is not a cautious, data-hedged transaction. It is a statement of belief — that Burns is the kind of pitcher a franchise can organize itself around, and that 23 is not too young to make that bet.

At $15 million per year, the Reds are paying well below what Burns would command on the open market if he reaches free agency while pitching like this. That structure benefits Cincinnati; it gives them cost certainty through Burns' prime years.

What it costs is the flexibility that comes with waiting. The Reds are betting that a 2.54 ERA in 2026 is a signal, not a sample. Based on what Burns has shown so far, that looks like a reasonable bet.

What This Means for the Trade Deadline

The August 3 deadline arrives in 16 days, and Burns had been one of the most-discussed names in trade speculation. Contenders looking for rotation help will need to look elsewhere.

The ripple effect on the broader market is real. Fewer elite arms are available now. Teams that had Burns as their primary target will recalibrate. And the Reds — who entered the summer with one foot out the door — now have a clear reason to stay in the race rather than accelerate a rebuild.

What's Next

Burns returns to the Cincinnati rotation with his future settled, and the Reds return to their schedule with a clear declaration about their direction. The August 3 deadline is two weeks away. With Burns off the market, the remaining trade landscape reshuffles around whoever is actually available — and Cincinnati, for the first time in a while, is building toward something instead of moving away from it.
