Aaron Judge Has 12 Home Runs and a 1.726 First-Inning OPS — The Career 400 Chase Is Real

By Jake Torres | April 30, 2026

Aaron Judge is 34 years old and hitting like he is 24.

The New York Yankees captain enters May with 12 home runs — tied for the MLB lead — and a career total above 380. The 400-home-run milestone, the threshold that has historically separated very good power hitters from all-time conversations, is now squarely in play during the 2026 season. At his current pace, Judge projects to reach 400 career home runs before September.

But the most fascinating detail in Judge's April is not the total. It is the distribution: five of his first nine home runs came in the first inning.

The First-Inning Phenomenon

In first-inning at-bats this season, Aaron Judge is hitting .450 with a 1.726 OPS. He is punishing pitchers before they have had a chance to establish any rhythm — before they know what their best stuff looks like, before the game has settled. Judge has turned the leadoff spot into a hunting ground, and pitchers have not found a reliable answer.

On April 28, he added another chapter — a 424-foot home run off a Texas Rangers pitcher — in a game that extended the Yankees' lead in the AL East (19-10). It was the kind of authoritative swing that has come to define his 2026 season: big ball, early in the game, when the at-bat matters most.

Since 2022, OPS+ leaders in all of baseball: Aaron Judge at 206, Yordan Alvarez at 174, Shohei Ohtani at 173. Judge's sustained excellence over multiple years puts his recent production in historical context. He is not just having a good April. He is one of the best hitters of this generation, and this April is consistent with that assessment.

The 400-HR Chase

The 400-home-run milestone is meaningful in modern baseball. In the steroid-era-adjusted conversation, reaching 400 cleanly puts a hitter in a category of performers who belong in the all-time record books. Judge, at 380+, is close. A 60-HR season — the pace he is on — would get him to 440+ for his career by year's end and would make him one of the elite power hitters across any era.

Yankees fans who remember watching Mickey Mantle and Roger Maris put up monster numbers at a young age are seeing something similar with Judge — except Judge is doing it at 34, which is the part no one fully expected.

What's Next

The Yankees (19-10) enter May as the AL East leaders, and Judge is the reason. The 400-career-HR milestone is the story to track over the next two to three months. If Judge stays healthy and keeps his current pace, he could reach the milestone at home in the Bronx — one of the great box score moments of the decade. Watch for it.
