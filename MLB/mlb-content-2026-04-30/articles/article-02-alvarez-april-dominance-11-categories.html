Yordan Alvarez Leads the AL in 11 Offensive Categories — One of the Most Dominant Aprils in Modern Baseball

By Jake Torres | April 30, 2026

The numbers Yordan Alvarez has put up in April 2026 require a slow, deliberate read to fully appreciate.

Through 26 games, Alvarez leads the American League in hits, home runs (11), RBIs (26), batting average (.347), on-base percentage (.466), slugging percentage (.779), OPS, OPS+, rOBA, total bases, and intentional walks. That is 11 categories. No player has dominated the statistical landscape in both counting and rate categories simultaneously like this through any comparable stretch in recent memory.

He is on pace for 68 home runs over 162 games. The Astros are 11-18.

Where He Ranks Historically

The most revealing data point may not be the raw numbers — it is the OPS+ comparison. Since 2022, the leaders in OPS+ across all of baseball are Aaron Judge at 206, Alvarez at 174, and Shohei Ohtani at 173. Alvarez's overall peak, sustained over multiple years, puts him in the conversation with the two most discussed offensive players of the modern era.

Carlos Correa, his Astros teammate, made the Bonds comparison earlier this season — the idea that Alvarez is so feared that pitchers would rather give him an intentional walk than deal with the damage he can cause. The number of intentional walks Alvarez has drawn in April validates the comparison. Teams are not pitching to him when they can avoid it. When they must, he makes them pay.

The pace of 68 home runs draws inevitable comparisons to historic single-season mark holders. At 11 home runs through April 26, Alvarez is tracking alongside names that belong in the record books.

The Astros Problem

The painful context around Alvarez's April is the team around him. Houston is 11-18 — the worst record in the AL West and well below expectations for a franchise that has won multiple World Series in the recent past. The supporting cast has not matched Alvarez's production, and it has cost the Astros games that his individual brilliance could not single-handedly recover.

History is full of great players carrying struggling teams, but the math eventually catches up. If the Astros cannot provide Alvarez with meaningful support, his historic April numbers become a fascinating footnote on a season that never fulfilled its potential.

What's Next

Alvarez enters May as the most statistically dominant hitter in baseball, with a body of work that already warrants serious AL MVP consideration. The questions are team-level: can Houston recover from its 11-18 start to make the season meaningful around him? If the Astros can get to .500 or above over the next six weeks, Alvarez's April becomes the foundation of a playoff push story. If they cannot, he may be the best player on the worst team in his division.
