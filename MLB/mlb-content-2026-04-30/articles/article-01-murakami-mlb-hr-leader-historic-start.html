Murakami Has 12 Home Runs in 29 Games — And His Start Is One of the Most Historic in Modern Baseball

By Marcus Cole | April 30, 2026

When Munetaka Murakami joined the Chicago White Sox for the 2026 season, expectations were tempered by the standard caution that accompanies any transition from Nippon Professional Baseball to the majors. NPB produces excellent players, but the adjustment to major league pitching typically takes time.

Murakami has not adjusted. He has dominated.

Through 29 games, the White Sox slugger leads all of major league baseball with 12 home runs. His production has been historic on multiple levels, and as April ends, he has become the most surprising offensive force in the sport — a player no one saw coming, doing something no one has done before.

Breaking Records Before Memorial Day

The most remarkable statistic in Murakami's start is one that requires a bit of digging: his first 12 extra-base hits in the major leagues have all been home runs. Every single one. That is a record that extends back to at least 1900 and surpasses the previous mark of 10 set by Dae-ho Lee in 2016. It means Murakami has not just been hitting home runs — he has been hitting home runs in lieu of everything else. Every extra-base ball he has put in play has left the park.

His 12th home run came on April 28 — a 454-foot blast off Ryan Thompson that, according to Statcast, is the longest home run hit by a White Sox player in the 2026 season. He tied the White Sox franchise record for consecutive games with a home run at five straight. His .965 OPS and 23 RBIs over 29 games place him among the best offensive performances in baseball this month.

He also leads all Japanese-born players in MLB history in home runs through their first 29 career games — surpassing a mark that includes Ichiro Suzuki, Hideki Matsui, and Shohei Ohtani's own sensational debut years.

The White Sox Find a Star

Chicago's season has been difficult by broader measures, but Murakami provides a reason to pay attention every night. He has become one of the essential daily watch items in baseball, a player whose at-bats carry the kind of anticipation that used to accompany visiting teams' cleanup hitters at Wrigley or Guaranteed Rate.

Whether his pace is sustainable is a question May and June will answer. But no one doubted Mike Trout early in his career either, and Murakami's bat speed, launch angle, and consistency suggest this is not a hot streak built on lucky contact. He is squaring the ball up with authority, and pitchers have not figured out a reliable strategy to neutralize him.

What's Next

Murakami enters May as the MLB home run leader. The next questions are: can he hold the lead, and how do pitchers adjust? Historically, when a player puts together a start of this caliber, the approach changes — more pitches away, more breaking balls early in counts. Murakami's ability to handle those adjustments will define whether April is prologue or peak. Either way, the South Side has something worth watching.
