23 HRs and the 60-Club Conversation: Schwarber's Historic Pace and the Perfect Derby Setup

By Ryan Calloway

June 12, 2026

Kyle Schwarber leads Major League Baseball with 23 home runs. Yordan Alvarez is right behind at 22. Aaron Judge, who was the pre-season favorite to lead the HR race, is on the injured list. Munetaka Murakami, who had his own historic pace in May, is also on the IL.

The home run race in 2026 belongs to two men. And the Home Run Derby is in Philadelphia this summer. Schwarber's home park.

The universe is setting this up too well.

The Numbers That Make This Remarkable

Schwarber hit his 20th home run on May 15 — among the earliest dates in MLB history for any player to reach 20. For comparison: Aaron Judge, who hit 62 home runs in 2022, reached No. 20 on June 3. Barry Bonds, who hit 73 in 2001, reached No. 20 on May 19.

Schwarber's current pace projects to approximately 60-plus home runs for the season. That territory belongs to a very exclusive group. Only three players in MLB history have hit 60 or more home runs in a single season: Barry Bonds (73 in 2001), Mark McGwire (70 in 1998, 65 in 1999), and Sammy Sosa (66 in 1998, 64 in 1999, 63 in 2001).

Schwarber hitting 60 would make him just the fourth player in baseball history — and the first in a generation — to join that club.

The Alvarez Factor

Yordan Alvarez is not a passive bystander in this race. At 22 home runs, the Houston Astros' slugger trails Schwarber by just one and has the raw power to overtake the lead on any given night. Alvarez appeared in the second 2026 MVP poll alongside Shohei Ohtani, and his presence ensures that Schwarber cannot afford a prolonged cold streak.

This is exactly the kind of two-man home run race that drives casual fans to tune in mid-summer. With Judge on the shelf and the AL East race somewhat settled, the Schwarber-Alvarez chase is the most compelling individual storyline in baseball right now.

Derby in Philadelphia

The 2026 Home Run Derby will take place at Citizens Bank Park — Schwarber's home field. If he's still near this pace in July, the narrative writes itself: the MLB home run leader, competing in his home park, with a capacity crowd behind him, chasing history.

The last time the Derby was held with the home-city star as the de facto favorite, it generated some of the event's biggest ratings in years. This setup has the same energy — and Schwarber has already shown this season that the home run swing is working at a historic level.

What's Next

Schwarber and the Phillies continue their 2026 schedule through the summer. Each home run either tightens or extends the Schwarber-Alvarez race and either strengthens or weakens the case for a historic final total. The 60-home-run conversation is not yet the dominant narrative — the season is still in June — but it is already a legitimate one. By July, it may be the only MLB conversation that matters.
