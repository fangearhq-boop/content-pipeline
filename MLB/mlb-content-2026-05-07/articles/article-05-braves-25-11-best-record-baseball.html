Braves 25-11 — Baseball's Best Team Has Depth That Will Last All October

By Jake Torres May 7, 2026

One Loss Changes Nothing

The Atlanta Braves lost to Washington 11-4 on Tuesday night. The Nationals played well; the Braves did not have their best night. That sentence describes a baseball game. Atlanta woke up Wednesday morning at 25-11, with the best record in baseball and an NL East lead of more than eight games. The loss to Washington does not belong in a different category of information than an average game on the schedule — because the Braves are not going to be defined by one result in early May.

What they will be defined by is the combination of individual talent and roster construction that has produced baseball's best record through the first seven weeks. The Braves entered the season with a championship-caliber core, and that core has performed at exactly the level that their projections demanded. This is not an overachieving team running on good fortune — it is a deep, well-managed lineup that is doing what it was built to do.

Four MVP-Caliber Seasons, One Lineup

The reason the Braves are the standard against which every other NL team measures itself in 2026 comes down to lineup depth. Matt Olson is having the kind of first-base season that puts him in the MVP conversation. Drake Baldwin at catcher has been the breakout contributor that the organization needed. Ozzie Albies at second base is producing at his peak. Michael Harris II in center field is giving the Braves a complete two-way player at one of the most demanding positions on the field.

Four players on MVP-caliber trajectories from different spots in the same lineup. That is an extraordinary accumulation of offensive production. It is also the reason Atlanta's lead in the NL East is as large as it is — you do not build an eight-game gap in the division on pitching alone. You build it because opposing teams cannot get through a lineup that generates quality at-bats from the first batter to the last.

The Pitching Matches

A team that can only hit is a playoff team. A team that can hit and pitch is a World Series contender. The Braves' rotation and bullpen both rank in the top five in the NL by ERA, which means Atlanta is not trading quality innings for offensive runs — they are doing both. When your lineup scores five runs a game and your pitching staff gives up two and a half, the math that produces your record becomes self-explanatory.

The Rest of the NL Is Playing for Second

Through the first 36 games of the 2026 season, no team in the NL has demonstrated the combination of talent, depth, and consistency that Atlanta has. The Cubs are the NL Central's best story. The Dodgers are always in the conversation. But neither has the eight-game division lead, the MVP-level production from four positions, and the pitching depth that Atlanta has assembled. When the MLB Power Rankings were updated entering May, the Braves were No. 1. Nothing about their Tuesday loss changes that.
