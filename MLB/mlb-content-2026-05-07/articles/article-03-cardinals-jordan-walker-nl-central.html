Jordan Walker's Breakout Is Powering the Cardinals' NL Central Chase

By Ryan Calloway May 7, 2026

From Negative WAR to MLB's Best Exit Velocity

Coming into 2026, Jordan Walker had compiled 527 at-bats across parts of the 2024 and 2025 seasons and produced 11 home runs and a negative bWAR. He was a top prospect who had not yet unlocked his tools, a player whose physical gifts were visible but whose performance had not matched them. There were legitimate questions about whether St. Louis was going to see a breakout or a continued struggle with the game's nuances at the major league level.

The 2026 version of Jordan Walker has answered those questions. In 34 games this season, Walker is batting .308 with 10 home runs, 27 RBIs, and a 97.1 mph average exit velocity that leads all of MLB. His hard-hit rate of 70% ranks second in baseball. His OPS has climbed from .584 in 2025 to .962 this year. These are not hot-streak numbers — they are the numbers of a player who made an adjustment and is now playing the game at a different level.

The Mechanics Behind the Numbers

The clearest expression of Walker's breakout is the exit velocity. Leading MLB at 97.1 mph means that when Walker makes contact, he is consistently making harder contact than any hitter in the sport. That kind of number is typically associated with the elite power profiles — Judge, Álvarez, Acuña. Walker has earned his place on that list in 2026. His fly ball rate has doubled from where it was in prior seasons, meaning the hard contact is now going in the air rather than on the ground, which converts hard hits into home runs rather than groundouts.

The result is a player who has become the Cardinals' most dangerous offensive threat — and one of the most interesting stories in the National League.

The Cardinals in the NL Central Race

St. Louis enters this week in second place in the NL Central, 2.5 games behind the Cubs. Chicago has been running hot — 14 consecutive Wrigley wins, an 8-game overall streak — and they are the clear division leaders. But the Cardinals are not a passive second. They had won four consecutive games heading into May 6 and are playing well enough to stay in the conversation.

The NL Central race between these two programs is going to be one of the defining league storylines through June and July. The Cubs have momentum and a home-field edge that is currently unmatched in the league. But the Cardinals have Walker, and Walker has the kind of individual production that keeps a team in games and in the standings regardless of who they play.

All-Star Case Building

If Walker maintains even a partial version of his current production through June 30, he will be a legitimate All-Star candidate. The voting and selection process can reward narrative, and Walker's narrative — top prospect to actual star — has exactly the arc that voters respond to. More importantly, his numbers would justify the selection regardless of narrative: .308, 10+ home runs, 97.1 mph exit velocity, elite hard-hit rate. A player posting those numbers over a full half-season is an All-Star by any standard.
