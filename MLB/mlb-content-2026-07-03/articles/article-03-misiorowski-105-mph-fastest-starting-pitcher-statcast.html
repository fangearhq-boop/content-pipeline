9-3. 1.45 ERA. 146 Strikeouts. And 105.5 MPH.

By Ryan Calloway July 3, 2026

What Happened on June 26

Jacob Misiorowski was facing the Chicago Cubs on June 26 when he threw a four-seam fastball that registered 105.5 miles per hour on the Statcast radar. It is the fastest pitch ever recorded by a starting pitcher in the Statcast era. The previous record was also his — 104.5 mph, thrown earlier this season on June 12 against the Phillies.

The pitch tied Ben Joyce for the third-fastest reading ever recorded by any pitcher, starter or reliever, since pitch-tracking technology was introduced. Misiorowski went 6 innings, allowed 1 earned run, struck out 8, and the Brewers won 6-2. When asked about the 105.5 mph reading afterward, Misiorowski said: "I slipped."

The Numbers Inside the Numbers

That single pitch does not exist in isolation. Jacob Misiorowski has thrown 42 of the 50 fastest starting-pitcher pitches ever recorded in the Statcast era. All 42 came in 2026. He is 24 years old, in his first full season as a major league starter, and he is rewriting what the velocity ceiling for a starting pitcher looks like.

His season line: 9-3, 1.45 ERA, 0.768 WHIP, and 146 strikeouts, which leads all of Major League Baseball. That ERA, through 16 starts, is one of the lowest recorded through that many starts in roughly half a century. The strikeout total puts him on pace to challenge single-season records. The velocity numbers are in a category of their own.

What It Means for the Brewers

The Milwaukee Brewers are 53-31, the best record in the National League. They lead the NL Central by a comfortable margin and are well-positioned for a deep October run. A significant part of their success is structural — good defense, a balanced lineup, a deep bullpen. But Misiorowski is the engine.

Teams that have frontline starters with elite velocity and elite command at his age are built for October. Misiorowski's fastball generates swings-and-misses at a rate that most relievers cannot sustain, let alone a 24-year-old who pitches every five days. When he takes the mound in October, opposing hitters will be seeing 105 mph in a playoff atmosphere for the first time. That is not a scenario that hitters can adequately prepare for.

The Historical Shelf He Is On

Velocity records in baseball carry a specific weight. The 100 mph barrier defined a generation of pitchers — Nolan Ryan, Goose Gossage, Randy Johnson in his prime. The 105 mph barrier, crossed by only a handful of pitchers ever, puts Misiorowski in a conversation that is almost impossible to have without running out of comparisons.

Ben Joyce threw 105.5 mph. Misiorowski, a starter, matched that number. The difference is that Joyce threw it as a reliever in short bursts. Misiorowski's reading came six innings into a game on a day when he faced 22 batters. The stamina required to maintain that kind of velocity through the middle of a complete outing is the detail that makes his 2026 season historically unusual.

What's Next

Misiorowski heads into the All-Star break with the NL's best ERA and MLB's best strikeout total. The Brewers enter the second half with the NL's best record. The 105.5 mph reading on June 26 was not a peak performance under ideal conditions — it was a Tuesday in the middle of a winning start. If the velocity holds and the command stays sharp, the Brewers' second half has the kind of top-of-the-rotation anchor that turns a good team into a dangerous one.
