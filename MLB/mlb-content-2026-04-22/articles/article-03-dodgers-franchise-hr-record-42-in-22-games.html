Dodgers Set Franchise Home Run Record — 42 in First 22 Games, Power Coming from Every Corner of the Lineup

By Ryan Calloway | April 22, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

The Los Angeles Dodgers hit five home runs in a 12-3 win over the Colorado Rockies on April 20, and when the final home run cleared the fence, the franchise record was in the books. With 42 home runs through the first 22 games of the season, Los Angeles has hit more home runs in any comparable opening stretch than at any point in franchise history.

The record is impressive not just for the total — 42 is a remarkable number — but for how it was constructed. Twelve different Dodgers have hit at least one home run this season. This is not a team riding one or two sluggers. It is a team with power distributed throughout the lineup, from the top of the order to the bottom.

The Contributors

Max Muncy leads the team with 8 home runs and has been one of the most productive left-handed bats in the National League through the first three weeks. Dalton Rushing, still establishing himself in his first full major league season, has already reached 7 home runs and has been a revelation for a lineup that expected him to contribute, but perhaps not at this pace.

Shohei Ohtani and Andy Pages each have 5 home runs, adding their firepower to a lineup that had plenty of it already. The depth of contributors is what makes this record particularly significant — when any individual slumps, someone else fills the gap.

Betts' Absence Makes It More Remarkable

One of the more remarkable footnotes to this franchise record is that Mookie Betts has missed significant playing time with an oblique injury. Betts, when healthy, is one of the most productive hitters in the National League. His absence for stretches of this run means the 42 home runs were hit without one of the team's most consistent power contributors in the lineup at full strength.

When Betts returns to full health and integrates back into a lineup already producing record numbers, the offensive implications are significant. The 2026 Dodgers may not have hit their ceiling yet.

Part of a Broader Dominance

The home run record is the most vivid individual data point, but it represents a broader pattern of offensive excellence for Los Angeles. The Dodgers lead Major League Baseball in batting average (.287), on-base percentage (.364), and slugging percentage (.499). Their pitching staff leads in opponents' batting average (.193) and team WHIP (1.05).

A team that dominates on both sides of the ball at the same time is historically rare. The Dodgers are doing it in a fashion that, through 22 games, looks entirely sustainable given their roster depth.

What's Next

The home run record will continue to rise as the season progresses. The more meaningful question heading into late April and May is whether the Dodgers can sustain this level of production against stiffer competition, particularly as divisional opponents adjust and elite pitchers face the lineup multiple times. Watch for whether Muncy can maintain his pace and whether Betts re-enters the lineup at full production — if he does, the Dodgers' offensive ceiling in 2026 could be historically significant.
