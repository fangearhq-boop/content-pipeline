Ohtani Is Doing the Impossible Again — 52-Game On-Base Streak, 0.50 ERA, and the Best Team in Baseball

By Jake Torres | April 22, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

At some point the superlatives stop being adequate. Shohei Ohtani's 2026 season through 22 games requires a different vocabulary — one that accounts for the simultaneous execution of two careers that would each, individually, qualify as excellent by any major league standard.

Ohtani's on-base streak has now reached 52 consecutive games, the third longest in Dodgers franchise history. At the plate, he carries a .402 on-base percentage and a .908 OPS with 5 home runs. On the mound through three starts, his ERA is 0.50 with a 0.72 WHIP and an 18:6 strikeout-to-walk ratio over 18 innings.

Both of those things are happening. One person. Same season.

What the Numbers Mean

An ERA of 0.50 through three starts is an almost absurd number. Even great pitchers — the ones who go on to Cy Young Award seasons — typically carry ERAs in the low-to-mid 2.00 range after their first month of work. Ohtani's current figure is not a fluke product of weak opponents; his 18 strikeouts in 18 innings against major league lineups are evidence of genuine dominance.

Meanwhile, the batting statistics reflect a hitter who is operating at MVP-caliber level. A .402 OBP and .908 OPS are numbers that typically appear in final-season statistical summaries, not through the first month of a campaign.

The Dodgers as Beneficiaries

The Dodgers have assembled a roster that would be elite with any of its pieces performing as expected. Ohtani is performing at a level that exceeds expectations. The result is a team that leads Major League Baseball in virtually every measurable category: batting average (.287), on-base percentage (.364), slugging percentage (.499), opponents' batting average (.193), and team WHIP (1.05).

Los Angeles is 16-6, the best record in baseball. Twelve different players have hit home runs this season. The franchise just set a record for home runs in the first 22 games of a season (42). And the pitcher responsible for holding opponents to fewer than one run per nine innings is also the same player responsible for much of that offensive production.

Historical Context

There is no clean historical comparison for a two-way player performing at this level. Earlier two-way players in baseball history were typically limited in how many innings they pitched or how prominent a role they filled in the lineup. Ohtani is the ace-caliber starter and the cleanup-level hitter simultaneously, without apparent diminishing returns on either side of his performance.

The 52-game on-base streak is the most concrete evidence of sustained individual excellence. Going back more than a full season without being retired in three plate appearances requires consistency that almost no professional hitter manages for this long. Third in Dodgers history is an elite tier — particularly given the legendary hitters who have played for that franchise over more than a century.

What's Next

Ohtani's next pitching start will draw attention both for its competitive value and for what it says about how his two-way workload holds up over a full season. The question the entire sport is asking: can he maintain both at this level through September? If the first month is any indication, the answer is yes — and the Dodgers may be building toward one of the most dominant single-season performances in the sport's modern era. Watch his next scheduled start for further evidence either way.
