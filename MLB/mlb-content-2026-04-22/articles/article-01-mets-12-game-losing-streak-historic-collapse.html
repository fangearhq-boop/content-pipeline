Mets' 12-Game Losing Streak Is Historic — First Team to Follow a Winning Season with an April Collapse This Long

By Marcus Cole | April 22, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

When Devin Williams walked off the mound Tuesday night having allowed two runs without recording a single out, the New York Mets had lost their 12th consecutive game. The final score was 5-3 in favor of the Minnesota Twins — a game the Mets had led 3-0 entering the ninth inning. By any measure, it was the worst kind of loss: a lead in hand, a reliable closer (theoretically) on the mound, and nothing to show for it.

The Mets are now 7-16 on the season, and their situation has crossed from "bad start" to "historically concerning" territory. According to research compiled around this losing streak, the 2026 Mets have become the first franchise in MLB history to post a winning record one year and then lose 12 consecutive games in April the following year.

The Numbers Are Brutal

Over the 12-game skid, the Mets have scored just 22 runs. That comes out to fewer than two runs per game — a figure that would be considered an outlier even for a lineup built entirely around defense. This is a team that was projected to be a legitimate playoff contender entering the season, with FanGraphs assigning playoff odds of 79.5% before Opening Day.

Those odds now stand at 41.3%. The math is not impossible, but the hill has gotten steep very fast. The offense hasn't shown signs of turning around, and the bullpen has been the single biggest contributor to the collapse, with Williams becoming the most visible symbol of a relief corps that has imploded at the worst possible moments.

A Franchise Record Nobody Wanted

This is the seventh time in Mets history that the franchise has suffered a losing streak of 12 or more games. The last time it happened was in 2002. The 2026 version arrives with particular bitterness because the front office came into the year having made genuine investments in the roster. The expectations were high. The results have been historic in all the wrong ways.

Historically, the prognosis for teams that lose 12 straight is severe. Of the 138 other World Series-era teams to suffer a losing streak this long, none reached the postseason in that same year. The Mets would be the first. That is possible, but it requires a near-complete reversal of everything that has gone wrong.

Devin Williams and the Bullpen

Williams entered Tuesday's game needing to close out a three-run lead. He exited without recording an out. The sequence encapsulates the 2026 Mets experience in a single inning: a lead that felt safe, a reliever who has been trusted, and an outcome that defied logic.

The manager will make adjustments. The roster has talent. But there is a psychological dimension to losing streaks of this magnitude that pure talent alone does not cure. Teams that arrest these collapses typically do so through a combination of results and genuinely improved play — not just opponent weakness or luck.

What's Next

The Mets face the question every 7-16 team faces at this stage of the season: is there still enough runway to recover, or has the hole become too deep? With roughly 130 games remaining, the arithmetic allows for a turnaround — but requires the team to play at a pace significantly above its current level for an extended stretch. Watch for managerial and roster decisions in the coming days that signal whether the organization sees a fix or is beginning to reassess the year. For Mets fans, the next two weeks may define the entire direction of the 2026 season.
