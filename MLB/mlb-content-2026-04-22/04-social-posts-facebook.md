## STORY 1: Mets 12-Game Losing Streak

#### Long-Form Post — 8:00 AM ET

Last night, Devin Williams came in with a 3-0 lead and left without recording a single out. The Mets lost to the Twins 5-3. And with that, the losing streak reached 12.

This is not just a rough stretch. This is historically bad.

The New York Mets are 7-16, having scored only 22 runs across those 12 losses. They've now suffered the franchise's seventh losing streak of 12 or more games — the first since 2002.

But the truly staggering number is this: the Mets had a winning record last season. They were a postseason team. And now they are the first franchise in MLB history to do that — win the year before — and then lose 12 consecutive games in April.

For context on how grim the prognosis looks: of the 138 other World Series-era teams that have suffered 12-game losing streaks, none of them went on to reach the postseason that year. FanGraphs, which had New York at 79.5% playoff odds before Opening Day, has now dropped those odds to 41.3%.

The bullpen has been the bleeding edge of this collapse. Williams, once one of the most reliable closers in baseball, has now been burned in the worst possible moments.

Is there a way back? Possibly. But the road from 7-16 with a 12-game losing streak requires something close to a miracle.

## STORY 2: Ohtani Two-Way Dominance

#### Long-Form Post — 10:30 AM ET

There is no reasonable comparison for what Shohei Ohtani is doing in 2026.

On the mound, he is posting a 0.50 ERA through three starts with an 18:6 strikeout-to-walk ratio over 18 innings and a 0.72 WHIP.

At the plate, he is batting with a .402 on-base percentage and a .908 OPS, having contributed 5 home runs in 22 games, all while extending his on-base streak to 52 consecutive games — the third longest in Dodgers franchise history.

Both of those things are happening. At the same time. From the same person.

The Dodgers are 16-6, the best record in baseball. As a team they rank first in batting average (.287), on-base percentage (.364), slugging (.499), opponents' batting average (.193), and WHIP (1.05). The numbers cascade from Ohtani outward in every direction.

There have been transcendent individual seasons in baseball history. There have been great two-way players in history. But no one has done what Ohtani is doing — not at this level, not at this sustained pace, not as both the offense's anchor and one of the rotation's best starters at the same time.

What we're watching is unprecedented. ⚾

## STORY 3: Dodgers Franchise HR Record

#### Long-Form Post — 6:00 PM ET

The Los Angeles Dodgers just set a franchise record — and they did it with their best hitter barely playing.

After hitting five home runs in a 12-3 rout of Colorado on April 20, the Dodgers moved their season total to 42 home runs through 22 games. That is the most home runs any Dodgers team has hit in the first 22 games of a season — ever.

Twelve different players have gone deep. Max Muncy leads the way with 8, Dalton Rushing has 7, and Shohei Ohtani and Andy Pages have each hit 5.

This is not a one-man show. The Dodgers hit home runs from everywhere in the lineup, from their leadoff spot to the cleanup role, from veterans to young players still in their first full big league seasons.

The fact that Mookie Betts has missed significant time with an oblique injury makes this even more remarkable. This team keeps hitting home runs regardless of who is in or out of the lineup.

The 2026 Dodgers are not just the best team in baseball. They might be one of the most complete offensive teams the franchise has ever put on the field. ⚾

## STORY 4: Jordan Walker Cardinals Breakout

#### Long-Form Post — 12:30 PM ET

The St. Louis Cardinals have been asking a very important question for the past two years: Can Jordan Walker be the player they believed he'd be when they drafted him?

Three weeks into the 2026 season, the answer is yes.

Walker leads the National League with 8 home runs and is slashing .315/.375/.685 for a team that has been searching for offensive identity through its rebuild. His .960-plus OPS is a top-tier mark for any hitter at any age.

And it's not just power. Earlier this month, Walker put together a single game in which he collected a grand slam, a stolen base, three or more hits, and made an outfield assist. According to baseball historians, he became the first Cardinals player since Ray Lankford in 1997 to accomplish all of those things in one game.

For a Cardinals franchise that has dealt with high expectations and injuries around Walker since he arrived, this April feels different. Not just promising — actually different. He's not just showing flashes of potential. He's producing at a consistent, dominant level.

St. Louis needs this version of Jordan Walker to last. Right now, it shows no signs of stopping. ⚾

## STORY 5: Aaron Judge Career Milestones

#### Long-Form Post — 3:00 PM ET

Aaron Judge leads all of Major League Baseball with 9 home runs through 22 games, and the milestones are coming faster than anyone can count them.

This month, Judge hit his 90th career first-inning home run. That puts him third in Yankees franchise history — behind Babe Ruth (126) and Mickey Mantle (103). He also passed Mantle for second on the Yankees' all-time list of multi-home run games, now at 47. Only Ruth has more.

Judge's career total stands at 377 home runs. He needs 23 more to reach 400 — a milestone that at his current pace is achievable this season. Analysts project 500 career home runs could arrive as early as 2028 if he stays healthy and productive.

He turns 34 on Saturday, April 26. For context, Ruth hit 46 home runs at age 34. Judge's 2026 pace through three weeks puts him on track for over 55 home runs.

These individual milestones are fascinating. But the larger point is this: Judge is becoming one of the most accomplished home run hitters in Yankees — and American League — history. Every game he plays, the list of people he has passed grows longer. ⚾

## STORY 6: Reds Lead NL Central

#### Long-Form Post — 8:00 PM ET

Nobody picked the Cincinnati Reds to be leading the National League Central on April 22. Almost no one.

But here they are, at 15-8, posting their best start since 2006, sitting alone in first place in a division that looked wide open at the beginning of the year.

The engine driving the surge is Chase Burns, the 2024 second-overall draft pick who is becoming one of the most exciting young pitchers in baseball. Through four starts, Burns owns a 2.42 ERA with 22 strikeouts in 22.1 innings. His four-seam fastball averages 98.5 mph, and his slider has generated a 56.3% whiff rate. Last night, the Reds pounded the Rays 11-2 with Burns on the mound.

Young, hard-throwing, developing an elite secondary pitch — Burns is exactly what a contending team needs in its rotation. And the Reds have built a roster around him that can actually score runs and play defense.

The NL Central conversation is just getting started. Pay attention to Cincinnati. ⚾
