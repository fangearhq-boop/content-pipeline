AL East Battle: Yankees Lead Rays by 1.5 Games, and Ben Rice Is Carrying the Lineup

By Marcus Cole

June 22, 2026

The American League East doesn't have a runaway leader. It has a race — one of the tightest in baseball — and one of the best individual stories of the summer buried inside it.

The New York Yankees enter Monday at 43-27, ahead of the Tampa Bay Rays (41-28) by one and a half games. No one in the division has broken away. Every series matters. The standings are decided by games, not trends, and both franchises know it.

What makes the Yankees' position in this race remarkable is what it costs them to maintain it. Aaron Judge, the franchise cornerstone and reigning AL MVP, has been out since approximately May 31 with a rib stress fracture. His expected return is mid-August — after the trade deadline, after the All-Star break, deep into the second half of the regular season. The Yankees have been playing without their best player for nearly a month, and they are still leading their division.

The reason they can is named Ben Rice.

Ben Rice: The Rookie Holding the Lineup Together

Rice was called up when the Yankees needed lineup stabilization in Judge's absence. The rookie first baseman has done more than stabilize — he has become the offensive engine of a team that was expected to struggle without its franchise player.

His numbers: .300 batting average. .393 on-base percentage. .638 slugging percentage. 19 home runs. That OPS — over 1.000 — is elite by any standard. Those are numbers that would make a player a legitimate All-Star conversation in most seasons. In the context of filling in for Aaron Judge, they are extraordinary.

Rice leads the Yankees in most offensive categories. He has done it without the benefit of pitchers working around him — without a player of Judge's caliber hitting behind him in the lineup — which makes the production more impressive, not less. Opposing pitchers can challenge Rice. He has responded by hitting for power and average at the same time, a combination that is genuinely difficult to sustain over a full season, let alone a long stretch at the most critical position in the Yankees order.

The Yankees Without Judge

Context is useful here. Losing Aaron Judge is not a routine injury absence. Judge is one of the two or three best players in the American League in any given season. When he went down in late May, the expectation among analysts was that New York would tread water at best — maintaining a division lead without their best player is genuinely difficult in a competitive division.

Instead, the Yankees have gone 43-27. They have widened their lead over the Rays at times and held it when Tampa Bay pushed back. The roster depth around Rice has contributed: pitching has held up, and Spencer Jones was also recalled to add depth to the outfield mix. But Rice's bat is the primary reason the lineup has remained functional.

Judge is expected to return in mid-August. When he does, the Yankees will get back their franchise player for the final stretch of the regular season and whatever playoff run follows. That means Rice's production now is a bridge — but what a bridge it has been. The Yankees are leading their division, and they are doing it without their best player.

Tampa Bay: The Persistent Threat

The Rays have been competitive all year and should not be treated as an afterthought in this race. At 41-28, they are one series win away from reclaiming first place. Tampa Bay has built a franchise model around consistency and organizational depth — they do not fade simply because a division rival is having a good month.

The Rays' challenge is the same one they face in every competitive window: sustaining the pressure against a franchise with significantly greater payroll capacity. If the deadline goes the way most observers expect — the Yankees making at least one significant addition — Tampa Bay will need to execute in a division where the gap in resources is real even when the gap in the standings is not.

For now, the gap in the standings is one and a half games. That is nothing. That is a sweep. The Rays won't go away quietly.

What's Next

The Yankees and Rays are playing this week — Tampa Bay opens a series in Kansas City today, while the Yankees host the Royals. The division race will tighten or extend based on how both clubs perform over the next two to three weeks.

The trade deadline adds a second layer to watch. New York is expected to be among the most active buyers — particularly for starting pitching, where Tarik Skubal's availability makes the Yankees one of the most frequently mentioned acquiring teams. A significant deadline addition on top of Judge's eventual return would make the Yankees a formidable postseason outfit.

For now, the story is Ben Rice. He is 19 home runs into one of the best rookie debut seasons in the American League, carrying a contender through the absence of its franchise player, and doing it quietly, without the spotlight his performance deserves. When Judge comes back, Rice will step aside. Until then, the Yankees' season runs through him.
