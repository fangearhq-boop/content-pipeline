Kyle Schwarber Is at 28 Home Runs and on Pace for One of the Greatest Offensive Seasons in Baseball History

By Jake Torres

June 22, 2026

Kyle Schwarber has 28 home runs. It is June 22. He is on pace to finish the season with more than 60.

That sentence requires context to fully appreciate. The 60-home run threshold is one of the most exclusive marks in all of American professional sports. It has been reached fewer times across all of baseball history than you can count on one hand — by Babe Ruth, Roger Maris, Mark McGwire, Sammy Sosa, and Barry Bonds. The players who did it are discussed in the same breath as the sport's all-time greats. Schwarber is on pace to join them.

After hitting home runs Nos. 26, 27, and 28 on Saturday in the Phillies' 15-3 demolition of the Mets, Schwarber enters Monday leading Major League Baseball in home runs by four — Yordan Alvarez and Byron Buxton are each at 24. The gap is not small. It is the gap between a great season and a historically rare one.

What Happened Saturday

Schwarber's three-homer night against New York was remarkable even setting aside the season-long context. He launched his 26th home run 456 feet. He hit his 26th and 27th in the same inning — the third — making him one of the rare players in baseball history to go deep twice in the same frame. His 28th, a two-run shot, barely cleared fair territory inside the right-field foul pole.

One of those home runs landed in the cheap seats. Another barely stayed fair. That is the nature of a three-homer game: it requires results across a range of trajectories, counts, and pitching situations. Schwarber had all three in one night.

There is also a historical footnote attached to Saturday's performance. Schwarber's three-homer game came on the same night Bryce Harper completed the first cycle of his career in a Phillies victory. That combination — one teammate hitting three home runs, another hitting for the cycle in the same game — has happened only once before in Major League Baseball history. The first time was Lou Gehrig and Tony Lazzeri, New York Yankees, June 1932.

That is the company Schwarber and Harper are keeping.

The Historical Scale

The pace projection tells a specific story. At 28 home runs through the date of June 22, Schwarber is tracking for a total that would rank among the third-highest single-season home run totals in Major League Baseball history — a tie with Sosa's best season.

To understand what 60-plus means: Ruth hit 60 in 1927 and the record stood for 34 years. Maris hit 61 in 1961. The mark was not broken again until McGwire's famous 1998 chase with Sosa — a season in which both players exceeded 60. Bonds hit 73 in 2001, a record that still stands. Those are the names on the list. The list is very short.

Schwarber is not guaranteed to reach 60. The second half of any season brings adjustments, fatigue, and the increasing likelihood that opposing pitchers will work around a dangerous hitter. But the fact that 60-plus is a mathematically realistic outcome of his current pace — not a fantasy projection but a pace-based calculation — speaks to how extraordinary his first half has been.

The Derby Factor

The subplot that every Philadelphia fan is tracking: the Home Run Derby.

The 2026 Home Run Derby is scheduled for July 14 at Citizens Bank Park — Schwarber's home stadium. The event falls in the middle of the All-Star break, one week before the second half of the regular season begins. The MLB home run leader, competing in the Derby at his own ballpark, in front of his home crowd, in the middle of the most prolific home run pace of the modern era, is a scenario that has captured the attention of the sport.

Schwarber has not committed to entering. He has also not ruled it out. The Derby creates its own pressures — the swing adjustments required for the competition format can linger into the second half — and some sluggers choose to sit it out for that reason. But the case for entering, given the setting and the timing, is obvious. A decision either way will generate significant attention.

What's Next

The Phillies return to Citizens Bank Park for a home stand beginning this week. Schwarber will continue to be pitched carefully — he already leads the NL in home runs by four — which means the second-half dynamic of walks and pitch avoidance is something his performance has to account for.

The next marker is 30 home runs, which would put him on track for one of the five or six greatest home run seasons in MLB history. At his current pace, he reaches that number before the All-Star break. After that, the historical comparisons stop being pace projections and start being real. He will be measured against the actual numbers of the players who reached 60.

The summer has barely started. Kyle Schwarber is already doing something that almost no one ever has.
