All-Star Voting Closes Thursday: Ohtani Leads, Three Days Left to Pick the Starters

By Jake Torres

June 22, 2026

Phase 1 of All-Star voting closes Thursday, June 25 at noon ET. Three days remain to decide who makes the ballot for the 2026 MLB All-Star Game — and in a handful of positions, the races are worth paying attention to.

The game itself is July 14 at Citizens Bank Park in Philadelphia. It is the first time in decades that the Midsummer Classic has come to Citizens Bank Park — and the timing creates a local dynamic that has made Philadelphia's fan base among the most motivated in baseball to turn out votes for their own players.

The Leaderboard

Shohei Ohtani leads all players in All-Star voting with 1.165 million votes. That margin is commanding — no player is close. Ohtani's two-way profile, his Dodgers platform, and his status as the most recognizable player in the sport ensure that he leads fan voting in most years, and 2026 is no exception. He will start the All-Star Game as the National League DH, barring something unprecedented.

In the American League, Yordan Alvarez of the Houston Astros leads overall voting. The Astros slugger — entering Monday with 24 home runs — has been one of the best hitters in the AL all year. His lead in AL voting reflects consistent production across a full first half.

Bobby Witt Jr. leads All-Star voting among AL shortstops despite having been held out of Sunday's game with a Grade 1 MCL sprain. His 4.5 fWAR is the best mark among AL position players. Voters noticed. Witt's lead among shortstops is a reflection of production, not just reputation — he has earned every vote he has received, and his lead is holding even through an injury absence.

The Philadelphia Factor

The host city dynamic is real. Citizens Bank Park hosting the All-Star Game means Phillies fans are voting with purpose — the prospect of watching their team's players start the Midsummer Classic at their home ballpark is a powerful motivator.

Kyle Schwarber and Bryce Harper are the primary beneficiaries. Schwarber leads MLB in home runs at 28 — a total he pushed to that mark with three home runs Saturday night. His case for an All-Star spot requires no hometown favoritism; he is producing at a historically elite level. But the home park context adds urgency to the Philadelphia vote: Schwarber competing in the Home Run Derby at Citizens Bank Park on July 14 is a scenario the fan base is actively campaigning for.

Harper completed the first cycle of his career Saturday — the same game Schwarber hit three home runs — and his first-half production adds to a compelling overall case. Two Phillies stars having landmark performances in the same game, in the final days of All-Star voting, with the game coming to their home park: the timing is unusual, and the momentum is real.

How the Voting Works

Phase 1 of All-Star voting, which closes Thursday at noon ET, determines which players make the ballot for Phase 2. The top vote-getters at each position advance to a runoff. Phase 2 voting — where fans vote on finalists — opens Friday, June 26.

Starters are selected through fan voting. Reserves are selected through a combination of player votes and commissioner discretion. The final rosters are announced in early July, giving players and fans a clear picture of who will be in Philadelphia on July 14.

Players on the injured list are eligible for the All-Star Game — with the stipulation that they must be able to participate. Witt's situation creates an interesting edge case: his vote lead is secure, but his participation depends on his knee responding to treatment in the next three weeks.

What's Next

Thursday at noon ET is the deadline. After that, Phase 1 closes and the finalist ballot for Phase 2 is set. Fans who want to influence the starting lineup — particularly in close races at outfield spots, second base, or catcher — have until Thursday to make their votes count.

The All-Star Game itself is three weeks away. Citizens Bank Park will host the Midsummer Classic, the Home Run Derby, and all the surrounding festivities beginning July 13. For Philadelphia, it is one of the biggest baseball weeks in the city's history. For the sport, it is the midpoint of a summer that has already produced a remarkable number of compelling storylines.

Three days left. Vote now.
