Bobby Witt Jr. Evaluation Today: IL or Return for the Tampa Bay Series?

By Marcus Cole

June 22, 2026

The Kansas City Royals find out today whether Bobby Witt Jr. will go on the injured list or return for their series opener against the Tampa Bay Rays — and the outcome carries consequences far beyond one game.

Witt left his June 19 start against the Cardinals in the fourth inning after sliding on defense and felt discomfort in his right knee. An MRI confirmed a Grade 1 MCL sprain. Manager Matt Quatraro described the diagnosis as a "best case scenario," but that optimism didn't translate into Witt appearing Sunday — Kansas City held him out of the second game against St. Louis as well. The Royals said he would be reevaluated before Monday's series opener against Tampa Bay.

That reevaluation is today.

The Stakes for Witt

Context matters here. Bobby Witt Jr. isn't a role player navigating a midseason injury. He is the best player in the American League right now.

Witt leads all AL position players with 4.5 fWAR entering Monday. He is the leader in All-Star voting among AL shortstops. His case for the American League MVP award — still five months from voting — is already one of the strongest in baseball. Missing significant time at this stage of the season would damage all three of those things.

There is also the All-Star Game to consider. The 2026 Midsummer Classic is scheduled for July 14 at Citizens Bank Park in Philadelphia. A Grade 1 MCL sprain — the mildest classification, involving minor fiber damage without ligament tearing — typically carries a recovery window of one to three weeks. If Witt goes on the 10-day IL and is activated at the earliest opportunity, he could return in late June or early July with time to spare before the All-Star break. If the Royals keep him active and manage his workload, he could be back this week.

What a Grade 1 Sprain Means

Not all MCL sprains are created equal. Grade 1 is the lightest possible diagnosis. It indicates minor fiber damage without a structural tear. The ligament remains intact. Most Grade 1 sprains resolve with rest, physical therapy, and a gradual return to activity — no surgery, no structural repair required.

Quatraro's "best case scenario" comment reflects that classification. The Royals knew before Sunday's game that Witt's knee had not suffered a serious structural injury. The open question was — and still is — how quickly his body responds to treatment and whether he can tolerate the physical demands of playing shortstop.

Playing shortstop on a healing MCL is a different proposition than playing first base or designated hitter. The position requires lateral cuts, explosive stops, and forceful throws from a wide range of arm angles. Even with a Grade 1 diagnosis, the Royals have to weigh the risk of aggravating the injury against the value of having Witt in the lineup.

The IL Option

If Kansas City places Witt on the 10-day injured list, it buys the organization certainty. A structured recovery timeline with a defined return window is easier to manage than day-to-day status, which can drag on and create ambiguity about when a player is truly ready.

The 10-day IL also doesn't necessarily mean missing the All-Star Game. If Witt were placed on the IL on Monday, June 22, the earliest he could return is July 2 — nearly two weeks before the All-Star break. The player selection for the All-Star Game is determined by a combination of fan voting (Phase 1 closes June 25), player votes, and commissioner selection. Witt's 4.5 fWAR and AL shortstop voting lead give him a strong case regardless of the exact days he misses.

Royals Context

Kansas City's situation adds a layer of urgency. The Royals are not a team that can afford to lose their best player without consequence. The franchise has been rebuilding around Witt's development into a superstar, and having him miss meaningful games during a season where he is performing at a historic level is not a palatable outcome.

Against Tampa Bay, the Royals face a competitive AL East opponent entering Monday just 1.5 games behind the Yankees. Every game matters. The impulse to rush Witt back must be weighed against the reality that aggravating a knee injury could cost far more games — and far more of the season — than a deliberate 10-day rest.

What's Next

The Royals' announcement today — whether Witt is activated, placed on the IL, or continues as day-to-day — sets the course for the next two to three weeks of one of baseball's most compelling individual storylines. A return against Tampa Bay would be the optimistic outcome, suggesting the Grade 1 diagnosis responded well to a few days of rest. An IL stint would mean watching for an early July return date and monitoring how Witt's knee holds up when he resumes full activity.

Either way, the evaluation today is the answer everyone is waiting for. Witt's MVP candidacy, his All-Star slot, and the Royals' immediate competitive outlook all hinge on what the Kansas City medical staff finds — and what the organization decides to do with it.
