Munetaka Murakami Homers in His First Three MLB Games — Only the 4th Player in History

The White Sox's new Japanese slugger is making history on debut — and he needs just one more game to tie Trevor Story's all-time record.

Three Games, Three Home Runs

Munetaka Murakami opened his Major League Baseball career by doing something only three other players in history have done: homering in each of his first three MLB games. The Chicago White Sox first baseman went deep in Game 1, Game 2, and Game 3 of the team's season-opening series against the Milwaukee Brewers, becoming just the fourth player to accomplish the feat, according to Sportradar.

In his debut on March 26, Murakami hit a solo home run off Jake Woodford in the ninth inning. On March 28, he went deep again against Chad Patrick in the fourth. On March 29, he sent a 3-2 pitch from Brandon Sproat 375 feet to right-center in the second inning. All three games were losses as the White Sox were swept.

Elite Historical Company

The three players who previously homered in their first three MLB games were Trevor Story of the Colorado Rockies (2016), Kyle Lewis of the Seattle Mariners (2019), and Chase DeLauter of the Cleveland Guardians — who accomplished the feat earlier this same week, from March 26 to 28.

That two players accomplished the feat in the same week is itself unprecedented. Story holds the all-time record with home runs in his first four career games. Murakami needs just one more to tie him.

From NPB Royalty to the South Side

Murakami, 26, is no ordinary prospect. He spent eight seasons with the Yakult Swallows of Japan's Central League, hitting 246 career home runs. His 2022 season was one for the NPB record books: he hit 56 home runs, breaking the single-season mark previously held by Sadaharu Oh — one of the most iconic figures in the history of the sport. Murakami signed a two-year, $34 million contract with the White Sox last December.

The power was expected. What has impressed observers equally is his plate discipline. Through his first three MLB games, Murakami drew four walks and posted a .538 on-base percentage. Milwaukee manager Pat Murphy summed it up best: "The park doesn't seem big enough to hold him, you know what I mean? He's impressive."

White Sox Fans Have Their Slugger

Chicago is in the middle of a rebuild, and the 2026 season won't likely end in a playoff run. But Murakami gives the organization a genuine offensive centerpiece and a compelling storyline for what could be a difficult year. He is the kind of player who fills seats and generates excitement even when the standings don't cooperate.

Murakami himself was characteristically humble after Sunday's game: "I'm truly grateful and happy," he said through interpreter Kenzo Yagi. "There's still a long way to go and lots of ways to improve."

What's Next

Murakami will have the opportunity to match Trevor Story's record of home runs in his first four MLB games in Chicago's next contest. Whether he ties it or not, his debut series already belongs in the record books. As the White Sox continue their season, all eyes will remain on whether the NPB legend can sustain this level of production across 162 MLB games — and whether Story's record will need to be updated for the first time in a decade.
