Schwarber at 22, Judge at 20: The June Check-In on Baseball's Most Watched HR Race

By Ryan Calloway June 4, 2026

Where Things Stand

Through early June 2026, Kyle Schwarber leads the majors with 22 home runs. Aaron Judge is right behind at 20. The Phillies left fielder hit No. 20 on May 15 — the earliest date in MLB history for any player to reach that mark — and the pace hasn't broken. The question is where it goes from here.

Schwarber's 22 HRs project to roughly 66 over a full 162-game schedule. That would put him in the same conversation as Sammy Sosa's 66 in 1998, which remains the third-highest single-season total in baseball history. The 60-HR club has four members: Barry Bonds (73), Mark McGwire (70 and 65), Sosa (66, 65, and 63), and Babe Ruth (60). Nobody has joined that list since 2001.

The Two-Man Race

Judge's 20 HRs make this a genuine two-person race now, which changes the dynamic. When Schwarber was alone at the top in May, it was easy to dismiss the pace as an aberration. But Judge reaching 20 as well — first in the majors by game count — suggests there may be something different about home run conditions in 2026.

Or it might just be two generational hitters simultaneously having great years. The Schwarber-Judge comparison is interesting: Schwarber has historically hit HRs at an extreme pull-side, elevated approach. Judge hits the ball harder and farther but doesn't hit as many. Having both at or near a 60-HR pace simultaneously through early June last happened in 2001 when Bonds and Sosa were both over 40 at the same point in the season.

The Second Half Is Where It Gets Hard

Schwarber's season HR/fly ball rate is running well above his career norms, which is the most common reason pace projections fade. Hot rates don't always stay hot. The back half of the schedule will bring tougher pitching, pitcher adjustments, and the inevitable cold streaks that every hitter goes through.

But here's the counterpoint: Schwarber had 22 HRs in early June last year too, and finished with 43. He knows how to hit home runs. And 60 is a long way from 22 even if the pace says otherwise. The number 66 only becomes real if he's still at pace in August.

What's Next

The next real check-in on Schwarber's chase comes around the All-Star break, when his pace at the midpoint will either validate or complicate the projection. Judge will be right there as a comparison point. If both players arrive at the break with 30-plus HRs, the second half of 2026 becomes one of the more compelling offensive storylines in recent memory.
