Schwarber Hit 30 Home Runs First. The Derby Is at His Park. He Hasn't Committed.

By Ryan Calloway July 1, 2026

The Setup

Kyle Schwarber hit his 30th home run of the 2026 season on June 28. He did it first — before anyone else in Major League Baseball reached that number. He leads the league. He plays his home games at Citizens Bank Park in Philadelphia. On July 13, the Home Run Derby will be held at Citizens Bank Park.

He has not committed to participating.

What 30 Home Runs Means at This Point in the Season

Reaching 30 home runs before July is a mark that puts a hitter on pace for a legitimate power season. The league's home run leaders at the end of previous seasons have typically arrived at 30 somewhere in the summer months. Getting there before any other player in baseball, before the first of July, puts Schwarber in elite territory — both in terms of the current standings and in terms of what the rest of the season might look like if he stays healthy and productive.

He is the Philadelphia Phillies' designated hitter, and the Phillies are a team built around offensive production at multiple positions. Schwarber's value is the power. It has always been the power. Thirty home runs before July is the power at its most concentrated.

The Derby at Citizens Bank Park

The Home Run Derby returns to Citizens Bank Park on July 13. Philadelphia's ballpark is known as a hitter-friendly environment, and the left field dimensions suit a left-handed pull hitter in a way that makes the prospect of Schwarber competing there in front of a home crowd an obvious narrative for All-Star Week.

The Derby this year will air on Netflix — the first time the event has been carried on a streaming platform. That represents a significant audience and a different kind of exposure for whoever participates. A player competing in his home stadium, in front of his home fans, on a national streaming platform, while leading the league in home runs, would be one of the cleaner All-Star storylines the sport has had in recent years.

Schwarber has not said yes. Whether that reflects a strategic decision, a concern about disrupting his swing heading into the second half, a logistical consideration, or genuine uncertainty is not publicly known. Players sometimes decline the Derby over concerns about overuse or mechanical disruption. Whether those concerns apply to Schwarber's current situation is something only his camp knows.

Why This Decision Matters

A "no" from Schwarber does not end the Derby or diminish the event. There are other power hitters around the league, and the field will be filled regardless of his decision. But a "no" from the player who leads the major leagues in home runs, in the week when that player's home stadium hosts the event, would be the subtext of every Derby broadcast. His absence would be the story, in a way it would not be for any other competitor who declines.

A "yes" from Schwarber is the obvious conclusion to an obvious story. It would give Philadelphia fans the centerpiece moment that a home Derby is supposed to provide. It would give Schwarber a platform he has earned through the first three months of the season. It would make the Derby easier to explain to a casual viewer: the guy who hits the most home runs is hitting home runs at his home stadium.

What's Next

The Derby field will be finalized in the coming days. Schwarber's decision, whenever it comes, will either add the league's home run leader to the event or create the most notable absence in the Derby's recent history. The All-Star Game itself is July 15 at Citizens Bank Park. Phase 2 All-Star voting closes July 2 at noon Eastern. The second half of the season begins after the break, and Schwarber will carry whatever home run total he's accumulated into a final two-and-a-half months that will determine whether he is a 50-home-run player or something less. The Derby is one decision. The season is still the larger context.
