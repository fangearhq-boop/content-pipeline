Jacob Misiorowski Is Leading the League in Strikeouts and Making a Cy Young Case in His First Full Season

By Jake Torres July 1, 2026

The Numbers

Jacob Misiorowski, Milwaukee Brewers right-hander: 9-3. 1.45 ERA. 0.768 WHIP. 146 strikeouts — which leads all of Major League Baseball. On June 26, he threw a pitch measured at 105.5 miles per hour. The Brewers are 52-31, the best record in the National League.

Those are the numbers. Here is the question they raise: is this a Cy Young season?

Why the Question Is Serious

The historical precedent for a first-year starter winning the Cy Young Award is thin. The award has an established bias toward veterans — pitchers with a track record of sustained excellence who have spent multiple seasons earning the credibility that comes with a strong body of work. Voters have traditionally been reluctant to hand the award to a pitcher in his first full campaign, even when the numbers are competitive.

That reluctance is understandable in principle. A small sample of dominant performances looks different than three or four years of consistent excellence. A pitcher can get hot over a few months. The Cy Young Award is supposed to recognize the best pitcher in the league, and "best" implies something more durable than a single sustained run.

But the argument for precedent giving way to performance becomes considerably more compelling when the candidate is not just competitive — when he is leading the league in strikeouts, carrying a sub-1.50 ERA at the midpoint of the season, posting a WHIP under 0.800, and pitching for the team with the best record in his league. That is not a hot streak. That is a dominant first half of a major league season by any standard the award has historically applied.

The 105.5 mph Pitch

On June 26, Misiorowski threw a pitch clocked at 105.5 miles per hour. Elite velocity is the foundation of his game. At the major league level, that kind of reading is rare — a reminder that the strikeout total and the ERA are not products of deception or sequencing alone. Misiorowski is throwing the ball harder than almost anyone in professional baseball, and he is doing it deep into starts, in meaningful situations, against major league lineups.

Velocity in isolation does not win games. The 1.45 ERA is the confirmation that the velocity translates. What Misiorowski is doing is not a function of one tool; it is the full combination working together.

The Brewers Context

Milwaukee is 52-31, which gives them the best record in the National League. The Brewers are a team that has consistently outperformed expectations in recent years by developing pitching, playing defense, and manufacturing runs without a payroll that competes with the league's largest markets. This season, they are doing it with the best pitcher in the National League at the top of their rotation.

A Cy Young argument built on team success is not required — the award goes to the best pitcher, regardless of wins or losses in team games. But the Brewers' NL-best record adds a layer to the case that carries weight with some voters: Misiorowski is not just performing well in isolation, he is performing well in a way that is visibly contributing to team success at the highest level in his league.

What's Next

The second half of the season will either deepen the case or introduce doubt. A Cy Young campaign requires sustained performance through the trade deadline month and into September. The voter base for the award will be watching whether the ERA holds, whether the strikeout pace continues, and whether the Brewers' position in the NL standings remains strong enough to keep Misiorowski in marquee matchups through the final weeks. If the answer to those questions is yes, the conversation about whether a first-year pitcher can win the award will need to end somewhere specific — and that somewhere will point to Misiorowski's name on the trophy.
