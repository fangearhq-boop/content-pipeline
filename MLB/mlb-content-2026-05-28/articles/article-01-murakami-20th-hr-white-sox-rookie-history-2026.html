Murakami Hits 20th HR — The Most by Any MLB Rookie Since 1901

By Marcus Cole

May 28, 2026

The White Sox rookie crushed a 432-foot shot against Minnesota on Wednesday night, becoming the only rookie to reach 20 home runs this early in the season in 125 years. The AL home run race just found its co-leader.

Munetaka Murakami stepped to the plate in the seventh inning of a blowout on Wednesday night and kept writing history.

With the Chicago White Sox leading the Minnesota Twins 13-1, Murakami drove a 93.9 mph fastball 432 feet over the left-center field wall for his 20th home run of the season. Statcast said the ball would have cleared the fence in all 30 MLB stadiums. That is not a coincidence. That is what elite power looks like.

The White Sox won 15-2. Murakami went 2-for-4 with a walk, three runs scored and the home run. But the number that matters is 20 — and what it means.

What 20 Home Runs Before June Means

No MLB rookie has reached 20 home runs this early in a season since 1901. That is 125 years. Whatever barrier that sentence represents — across all the eras of baseball, all the players who came through, all the conditions of the modern game — Murakami has now cleared it.

He is also only the third player in White Sox franchise history to reach 20 home runs before the end of May. The franchise dates to 1901. Three players in 125 years. Murakami is 26 years old and this is his first MLB season.

He arrived from Japan's NPB, where he was already known as one of the most dangerous power hitters in professional baseball. His MLB adjustment period — the one every international player faces, the one that humbles first-round picks and former MVP candidates — appears to have been abbreviated. Or perhaps it has not happened yet. Either way, 20 home runs before June suggests a player whose power does not require a gradual acclimation to American pitching.

The AL Home Run Race

Murakami's 20th home run on Wednesday tied him with Houston Astros slugger Yordan Alvarez for the American League lead in home runs. Alvarez hit his 20th the same night, off Jacob deGrom in Arlington. Both players arrived at the same number on the same evening, which has made the AL home run race — already compelling — suddenly impossible to ignore.

Kyle Schwarber of the Philadelphia Phillies is also at 20 home runs, having reached the milestone at the earliest date in MLB history. Three players at 20 home runs before the end of May is a convergence worth tracking. The difference is that Murakami is doing it as a first-year player in MLB; Schwarber and Alvarez are accomplished veterans. The context around Murakami's number is categorically different.

What Comes Next

White Sox fans have not had a great deal to celebrate in recent seasons. A 26-year-old Japanese rookie leading the American League in home runs while setting franchise and MLB rookie milestones is the kind of story that changes the temperature of a fanbase. Whether Murakami sustains this pace — whether he can avoid the adjustments opposing pitchers will make as the league catches up to him — remains to be seen.

What is already true: he has done something that no rookie has done in 125 years. And on a 15-2 Wednesday night against the Twins, he did it with his third home run in three straight games.

What's Next

The White Sox will watch as Murakami's milestone resonates through the league. Teams that have not yet faced him will game-plan with his 20-home-run profile in front of them. The pitchers who have already faced him will make their own adjustments. The question now is whether Murakami has more adjustment to absorb — or whether he has been making everyone else adjust to him all along.
