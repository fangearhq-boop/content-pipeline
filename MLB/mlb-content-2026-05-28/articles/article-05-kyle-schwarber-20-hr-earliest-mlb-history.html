Kyle Schwarber Made MLB History With His 20th Home Run — And Nobody Is Surprised

By Jake Torres

May 28, 2026

Schwarber reached 20 home runs before any player in MLB history had reached that mark, surpassing Luis Gonzalez's 2001 record. He's on pace for 66, re-signed for five years, and doing this alongside a starter with the best ERA in the National League.

Luis Gonzalez was 33 years old in 2001 and having the best power season of his career. He reached 20 home runs on May 17 of that year — which, until recently, was the earliest any player had ever hit the mark in a single season. That record stood for 25 years.

Kyle Schwarber passed it this week. He reached 20 home runs through his team's first 49 games — the earliest date that milestone has ever been reached in Major League Baseball history. Twenty home runs before May 17 is a data point that required a new sentence in the record book. Schwarber wrote it.

The Pace and What It Means

Twenty home runs through 49 team games puts Schwarber on pace for 66 home runs over a full 162-game season. The single-season home run record is 73, set by Barry Bonds in 2001. A 66-home-run season would place Schwarber in the historical tier of the sport's most remarkable power years.

Whether that pace holds through the summer heat and the pitcher adjustments and the inevitable rough stretches is the question. In 2025, Schwarber hit 56 home runs. He knows the pace does not typically survive intact over six months. But through 49 games, it has not just held — it has accelerated. He is hitting home runs faster now than he was at the comparable point in any previous season of his career.

His slash line: .237/.365/.645 with 35 RBI. The batting average is what it is. The on-base percentage and slugging percentage together make the point: Schwarber gets on base at a respectable rate and he hits the ball very hard when he connects. Those two qualities, combined with the league-leading power, produce one of the most productive at-bats in baseball.

The Historical Company

Schwarber is now one of 18 players in MLB history to reach 20 home runs through a team's first 49 games. The list is short, and the names on it are not strangers to the record books. The most famous entries belong to Mark McGwire in 1998 and Barry Bonds in 2001, who each hit 25 home runs through 49 games. Schwarber at 20 is behind those peaks but ahead of everyone else.

The most recent member of the list before Schwarber was Josh Hamilton in 2012, who also hit 20 home runs through 49 games. Hamilton finished that season with 43. What Schwarber does with the remaining 113 games will be watched with a similar intensity.

Career Context and a Long-Term Commitment

Schwarber hit his 350th career home run on April 30 against the San Francisco Giants. He hit his 200th as a Phillie on May 10 against the Colorado Rockies. The milestones are stacking up in ways that make his Hall of Fame case more interesting with each passing week.

In December 2025, he re-signed with the Phillies on a five-year, $150 million contract. The franchise saw what Schwarber's 56-home-run season in 2025 represented and locked him in for half a decade. As of May 2026, the return on that investment has arrived early and exceeded expectations.

The Phillies have Cristopher Sánchez extending a 44⅔-inning scoreless streak and the left fielder who just set the MLB record for earliest 20-home-run season. The combination of elite pitching and elite power in the same rotation-and-lineup is exactly what championship-caliber teams require. Both pieces are delivering simultaneously.

What's Next

Schwarber's next target — if he is tracking any — is 25 home runs, which would put him in the specific company of McGwire and Bonds. More practically, he needs to sustain production through June and July before the real milestones come into view.

At 20 home runs through May, the Phillies have the league's most historic home run pace from any player since the steroid era. Nobody who watched Schwarber last season is surprised. The record, however, is official. And official is what counts.
