Ohtani's Batting Resurgence: Five Home Runs in Nine Games, .996 OPS — The Two-Way Experiment Is Working

By Marcus Cole | April 13, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

The slow start was the story for about a week. Shohei Ohtani was getting on base — his OBP was around .455 — but through his first six games, all three of his hits were singles. For the most anticipated offensive player in the National League, it seemed like an uncomfortable stretch.

Then he homered. And kept homering. Five home runs over his last nine games. His OPS through 73 plate appearances in 2026 stands at .996. The slow start is over. Shohei Ohtani is locked in.

The Batting Breakthrough

There was no dramatic mechanical adjustment or coaching intervention that explained the turnaround — it is simply Ohtani finding his timing against major league pitching, which even the best hitters need to do when seasons begin. The talent was never in question. His 73-plate-appearance OPS of .996 is elite by any measure and puts him on pace for another monster offensive year.

What makes Ohtani's situation unique is that every extra-base hit comes with the additional context of him also being a starting pitcher. He is leadoff hitter for the Dodgers and a once-a-week starter in the rotation. The combination has not slowed him down at the plate. If anything, the pitching days give his legs rest that most hitters don't get.

Two-Way Production in 2026

Ohtani's pitching debut on March 31 against Cleveland was everything the sport expected. Six scoreless innings, one hit allowed, three walks, six strikeouts, 87 pitches. The Dodgers won 4-1. Ohtani described feeling "more loose and easy" pitching than he had in previous seasons — a product of a full offseason focused on two-way preparation rather than rehabilitation.

The Dodgers plan to use him on a 6-7 day rest schedule targeting approximately 25 starts this season. That workload is carefully managed, but the results so far suggest Ohtani is capable of handling it without his batting production suffering. Through the first three weeks, both sides of his game are functioning at a high level.

Why It Matters for the Dodgers

Los Angeles is among the best teams in baseball, and Ohtani's bat is central to why. As the leadoff hitter, he sets the table for a lineup that has the depth to compete for a World Series repeat. Andy Pages has been another bright spot early, hitting over .400 with multiple extra-base hits. But Ohtani is the engine.

The NL West has competition, and the Dodgers will face significant tests as the schedule tightens. How Ohtani manages his workload across both roles — especially in the second half of the season — is the most fascinating subplot in the sport. For now, the early answer is clear: it is working.

What's Next

Ohtani's next pitching start will be another reference point for how he manages his two-way schedule. On the offensive side, the resurgence from a slow start to a .996 OPS through 73 plate appearances is exactly the kind of pattern Dodgers fans have come to expect: a brief adjustment period, then sustained excellence. The rest of the NL West has been warned.
