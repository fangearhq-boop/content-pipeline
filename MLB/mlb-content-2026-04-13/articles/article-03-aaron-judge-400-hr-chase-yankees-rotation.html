Aaron Judge Is 29 Home Runs From 400 — And the Yankees' Rotation Is Historic Without Cole or Rodón

By Ryan Calloway | April 13, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

The New York Yankees entered 2026 with two of the best starting pitchers in the American League on their roster. Gerrit Cole and Carlos Rodón combined for four All-Star selections in their time together in the Bronx. They are both on the injured list to start the season. Their rotation, missing both of them, is statistically the best in baseball.

That fact alone tells you something about the Yankees in April 2026. But so does what Aaron Judge is doing at the plate.

Judge and the 400 HR Chase

Judge recently hit his 371st career home run, passing Hall of Famer Gil Hodges for sole possession of 83rd place on the all-time home run list. He needs 29 more to reach 400 career home runs. Given his recent track record — 62 in 2022 (the American League single-season record), 58 in 2024, 53 in 2025 — reaching that number in a healthy season is a near certainty.

Judge has been the most consistent power hitter in the American League for the better part of five years. His plate discipline, his exit velocity, and his ability to hit for both contact and power have made him the most feared bat in the lineup. At 34, he shows no signs of decline that would threaten his pace.

The 400 HR club is selective. Only 57 players in baseball history have reached the mark. When Judge arrives, he will join a list that includes Babe Ruth, Willie Mays, Hank Aaron, and many other icons of the sport. It is a matter of when, not if.

The Rotation Nobody Saw Coming

Without Cole and Rodón, the Yankees' rotation could have been a liability. Instead, it has been the best in baseball. Their starters are a combined 5-0 with an MLB-best ERA of 1.81. Through the first nine games of the season, the Yankees allowed just eight runs total — tying the 2002 San Francisco Giants and 1993 Atlanta Braves for the fewest runs allowed by any team through nine games in modern baseball history.

The emerging story has been Cam Schlittler, who has been dominant in his first two starts: 15 strikeouts, zero walks, and no runs allowed across two outings. Schlittler's command has been exceptional, and his ability to fill the void left by the two aces has been one of the most pleasant surprises of the early season.

The underlying concern — when does the rotation depth get tested? — will become louder as the season progresses. Cole and Rodón are expected to return, but the Yankees have been performing at an elite level without them. That depth will matter in August and September.

What's Next

Judge's home run pace — if he plays a full season — projects to another 50-plus HR year, which would put 400 within reach before September. The rotation question is whether the depth holds as the schedule intensifies. For now, the Yankees look like the class of the American League, built around a generational slugger chasing one of baseball's significant career milestones and a pitching staff that is defying expectations at every turn.
