Red Sox Five-Run 9th Inning Rally: Signs of Life After Baseball's Worst Start in 126 Years

By Jake Torres | April 13, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

In a season where the Boston Red Sox have tied the franchise's worst start in 126 years of baseball history, a five-run ninth-inning rally on Saturday felt like something approaching redemption — even if it was just one win in a month full of losses.

The Red Sox scored five runs in the final inning to secure the victory, a kind of dramatic comeback that had been conspicuously absent from Boston's repertoire to start the year. For a team starved for momentum, it mattered. Not because it changed the standings, but because it confirmed that this lineup can still score in bunches when it needs to.

What Went Wrong

Boston opened the year with high expectations. The offseason was active: Ranger Suárez arrived to front the rotation, Sonny Gray added veteran depth, Willson Contreras brought experience behind the plate, and Caleb Durbin added positional versatility. The roster looked improved. The projections pointed toward a .500-or-better team.

Then the season started. The Red Sox won Opening Day, then lost five in a row. They won a game on April 3, then lost three more. Through their first ten games, they were 2-8 — tied for the worst nine-game stretch in 126 years of franchise history. Five of those losses came by two runs or fewer. Games that should have been won were left on the field, lost in clutch moments and bullpen collapses.

Manager Alex Cora was direct about it: "Unacceptable." Hard to argue.

The Talent Is Still There

What makes the Red Sox situation particularly frustrating is that the pieces are not bad. Suárez and Gray are competent starting pitchers. Contreras is a proven major league catcher. The offense has players who can hit. The five-run ninth inning on Saturday was not a fluke — this lineup is capable of putting up crooked numbers.

The problem has been execution in tight moments. Late-inning opportunities squandered. Leads not held. A bullpen that has allowed runs in situations where the game demanded a stop. Those are fixable problems, theoretically. But they require the kind of collective concentration that is difficult to sustain when a team is losing and confidence is fragile.

Is a Turnaround Possible?

The season is 162 games long. Teams have turned around brutal April starts before. The 2011 Boston Red Sox started 2-10 and finished 90-72. The 2021 San Francisco Giants surprised everyone who wrote them off early. Bad starts can be recovered.

But the Red Sox need more moments like Saturday. The five-run 9th is the kind of win that breeds belief — that says: we have the talent, we just need to put it together. Whether Boston can build on it will determine whether this start is remembered as a rough patch or the beginning of a disastrous season.

What's Next

The Red Sox need wins, full stop. Suárez and Gray need to go deep into games and limit the bullpen exposure that has cost Boston several contests. The offense needs to convert on the scoring chances it creates. If the team can find a three or four-game winning streak in the next ten days, the narrative shifts. If not, Cora's roster decisions will come under increasing scrutiny and the trade deadline conversation will start much earlier than anyone in Boston wants.
