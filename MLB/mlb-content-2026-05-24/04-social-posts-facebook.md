### STORY 1: Schwarber's 60-HR Club Chase

**Long-Form Post**
⚾ KYLE SCHWARBER IS ON A 60-HOME-RUN PACE ⚾

Let's just say that out loud.

Kyle Schwarber hit his 20th home run of the 2026 season on May 15. That's the earliest date in MLB history that any player has reached 20 home runs in a season.

Through his first 45 games, he is on pace for approximately 66 home runs.

To put that in context: the 60-home-run club has exactly three members in the history of baseball. Barry Bonds hit 73 in 2001. Mark McGwire hit 70 in 1998. Sammy Sosa hit 66, 65, and 63 in the same three-year stretch.

No player has been on this pace since Bonds in 2001.

Schwarber returned this week after missing a few games with a GI illness and immediately homered. He looks completely locked in. His exit velocity, his barrel rate, and his contact quality all suggest this is not a fluke.

Will he sustain it? Nobody hits 66 home runs in a season easily. The odds-on outcome is regression toward something more normal — but "more normal" for Schwarber right now still means a 45-55 HR season, which would be one of the best in modern MLB history.

For now, in late May, Kyle Schwarber is on a pace that hasn't been seen since the steroid era. And he is doing it clean.

⚾ Keep watching. This is something. #Phillies

### STORY 2: White Sox Turnaround — Murakami

**Long-Form Post**
⚾ THE MOST SURPRISING TEAM IN BASEBALL RIGHT NOW IS THE CHICAGO WHITE SOX ⚾

No, really.

The White Sox started May at 9-15. Many people (reasonably) thought this was going to be another 100-loss season for a franchise that has been rebuilding for years.

They are now 26-24.

That is a 17-9 stretch over roughly six weeks. And the person most responsible for it is Munetaka Murakami.

If you haven't been paying attention to Murakami, here's what you need to know: He is a former NPB home run champion from Japan who signed with the White Sox this offseason as a big name but a massive question mark. How would his power translate? Could he handle the best pitching in the world?

The answer has been remarkable: 17 home runs, a .941 OPS, and the 2nd-highest home run total in all of MLB behind only Kyle Schwarber.

He already holds the record for the most home runs through his first 21 MLB games by a Japanese-born player — seven, breaking Shohei Ohtani's mark of five.

This is a legitimate AL Rookie of the Year race. Murakami has earned the conversation.

And the White Sox have earned a look. At 26-24, they are a game below .500 — playing competitive baseball in a competitive AL Central. Nobody had them here.

⚾ The White Sox are real right now. And Murakami is why. #WhiteSox

### STORY 5: Ramírez 300-300 Club

**Long-Form Post**
⚾ JOSÉ RAMÍREZ IS NINE HOME RUNS FROM ONE OF THE RAREST LISTS IN BASEBALL ⚾

Last month, José Ramírez stole his 300th career base — becoming only the second player in Cleveland franchise history to reach that milestone, joining Hall of Famer Kenny Lofton.

He has 291 career home runs.

Nine more puts him in the 300-300 club.

Here's why that matters: the 300-300 club — players who have hit at least 300 home runs AND stolen at least 300 bases in their MLB career — is one of the most exclusive lists in the history of the sport. Fewer than ten players have ever done it.

Think about what the 300-300 combination requires. You need power and you need speed. Sustained power and sustained speed. Over a career of 15+ years, without significant injury derailment. It's not just a matter of being good; it's a matter of being a particular kind of exceptional, consistently, across a long window of time.

Ramírez is 33 years old. He's still leading the AL in stolen bases in 2026. He's still hitting for power. He is a better version of himself at 33 than most players are at 28.

Nine home runs. The 300-300 club. It's closer than it's ever been for one of the most underappreciated great players in the game.

⚾ Pay attention to what Ramírez is doing. #ForTheLand #Guardians
