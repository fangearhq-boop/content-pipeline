White Sox at 26-24: Murakami's Takeover and Baseball's Most Surprising Turnaround

By Marcus Cole

May 24, 2026

Chicago was 9-15 in early May and looking like a fourth straight 100-loss season. Then Munetaka Murakami took over. The White Sox are now 26-24 and the most unexpectedly competitive team in the American League.

When the Chicago White Sox signed Munetaka Murakami this offseason, the move came with enormous excitement and equally enormous uncertainty. Murakami was a proven run producer in Japan's NPB, a home run champion who had posted some of the best offensive numbers in Japanese baseball history. But the question of how his power and approach would translate to Major League Baseball — the different pitch spin, the different strike zone enforcement, the best pitching staffs in the world — was entirely open.

The answer, through two months of the 2026 season, is emphatic. Murakami has 17 home runs and a .941 OPS. He is second in all of MLB in home runs, trailing only Kyle Schwarber. He has already set the record for most home runs through the first 21 MLB games by a Japanese-born player. And he has been the central reason the White Sox went from 9-15 in early May to 26-24 today.

The Turnaround in Numbers

Context for how dramatic this shift has been: the White Sox opened May at 9-15 with a trajectory that most projections systems read as another 100-loss campaign. The franchise has been rebuilding for years. Their roster carried real talent in Murakami and some of the younger players they've developed, but 100 losses was not considered an unrealistic outcome.

They proceeded to go approximately 17-9 over the following six weeks. That is a stretch of baseball good enough to win any division in the American League over that sample. The White Sox are not just treading water — they are playing winning baseball in May, and Murakami is driving it.

A .941 OPS is elite by any standard. That marks him as one of the best offensive players in the AL over the course of the 2026 season. His 36 RBIs and consistent clutch production in key situations suggest this is not a product of empty-count swings.

The Rookie of the Year Conversation

Murakami has earned a real AL Rookie of the Year conversation. His numbers across home runs, OPS, and RBIs are top-tier by any standard. The counterargument will center on whether there are other AL rookies with similar value across more dimensions. But from a sheer offensive standpoint, Murakami has delivered one of the best rookie offensive starts in recent memory.

His NPB pedigree provided the baseline expectation — he had been one of Japan's best players. What was not guaranteed was whether the power would carry over against MLB breaking balls and pitchers who attack the zone differently. Two months in, the answer appears to be yes.

What's Next

The White Sox continue their series against the San Francisco Giants on Sunday in the second game of a West Coast series. At 26-24, they are in legitimate contact with the AL Central standings and have turned their season into something worth watching. The bigger question for the White Sox is whether their supporting cast can sustain the winning pace alongside Murakami, or whether regression will pull the team back. For now, they are baseball's most compelling underdog story, and Murakami is its most interesting individual performer.
