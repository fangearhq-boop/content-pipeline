Can Kyle Schwarber Join the 60-Home-Run Club? The Historic Pace Nobody Is Talking About

By Ryan Calloway

May 24, 2026

Kyle Schwarber reached 20 home runs on May 15 — the earliest date in MLB history. He's on pace for 66 home runs. The 60-HR club has three members. Could Schwarber be the fourth?

We are not yet through Memorial Day weekend, and Kyle Schwarber already has 20 home runs.

Not 20 home runs in a good month. Twenty home runs through his first 45 games of the season. He hit No. 20 on May 15 — the earliest date in Major League Baseball history that any player has reached that milestone.

Through the same number of games, Schwarber is on pace for approximately 66 home runs by season's end. That number is not trivial. That number, if it holds, would put him alongside the three members of the most exclusive home run club in sports history.

The 60-Home-Run Club

The 60-home-run club has three members. Barry Bonds hit 73 in 2001. Mark McGwire hit 70 in 1998. Sammy Sosa hit 66 in 1998, 65 in 1999, and 63 in 2001 — contributing to four of the seven total seasons in baseball history where a player hit at least 60 home runs.

No player has been on a 60-home-run pace this deep into a season since Bonds in 2001. That is not an exaggeration. Since Bonds' extraordinary 2001 campaign, no player has sustained this kind of home run rate through May with a full schedule ahead of them.

Schwarber's 66-home-run pace would tie Sosa's 1998 mark for the third-highest single-season total in baseball history. Reaching it would require sustaining a rate of roughly one home run every 2.3 at-bats for the rest of the year — an extraordinary demand, but also one that Schwarber's early results suggest he can meet.

What Makes This Season Different

Schwarber has always been a home run hitter. He hit 46 in 2021, 38 in 2022, and 37 in 2023 — consistent, elite production that established him as one of the game's most dangerous power hitters. But nothing about his previous career suggested a 60-plus pace was possible.

What is different in 2026 is swing efficiency. His barrel rate and exit velocity numbers are tracking above his career bests, and his contact quality on pitches up in the zone — historically a vulnerability — has improved significantly. He is not just getting hot; he appears to be operating at a level that is categorically different from his previous high marks.

He missed a few games earlier this month with a GI illness. He came back and immediately homered. The illness did not appear to affect him. His trajectory continued.

The Regression Question

Every conversation about a historic home run pace has to grapple with regression. Nobody hits 66 home runs easily. The players who have done it represent the greatest power seasons in the history of the game, and they didn't sustain their pace by accident. They were historically exceptional.

The most likely outcome for Schwarber is that his pace slows. A rate of 45 to 55 home runs over a full season would still represent one of the best power performances of the modern MLB era. It would not be the 60-HR club. But it would be remarkable on its own terms.

The more interesting question is whether Schwarber's early numbers suggest enough structural improvement to hold something close to his current pace. That is the conversation baseball should be having.

What's Next

Schwarber and the Phillies continue their series against the Cleveland Guardians on Sunday, giving Schwarber another opportunity to add to his total. The season stretches through the end of September, leaving roughly 117 games remaining. At his current pace, Schwarber would finish with 66 home runs. Whether he gets there depends on health, consistency, and the baseball calendar. For now, in late May 2026, the pace is real and the historical comparison is earned.
