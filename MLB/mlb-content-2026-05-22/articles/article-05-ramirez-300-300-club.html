Ramírez Is Nine Home Runs From One of Baseball's Most Exclusive Clubs

By Jake Torres | May 22, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

José Ramírez passed Earl Averill this week to take sole possession of second place on Cleveland's all-time list of home runs hit at home, with 138. It is the kind of milestone that appears in game notes and scrolls past in broadcasts, the sort of number that registers as significant for those keeping careful track and passes unnoticed by everyone else.

The bigger number is 300. Ramírez is nine home runs away, and he already has the other half of the equation: 300 career stolen bases, reached on May 2.

The 300-300 Club

The 300-300 club — players with 300 or more career home runs and 300 or more career stolen bases — has eight members in the history of Major League Baseball. Willie Mays. Barry Bonds. Alex Rodriguez. Carlos Beltran. Andre Dawson. Deion Sanders. Steve Finley. Bobby Bonds.

That list is not arranged casually. Mays and Barry Bonds are among the greatest players the game has produced. Rodriguez and Beltran were perennial All-Stars. Dawson is in the Hall of Fame. The combination of sustained home run power and sustained base-stealing ability at the levels required to reach 300 of each is one of the rarest statistical achievements in baseball history.

Ramírez needs nine home runs to join them.

The Averill Milestone in Context

Earl Averill played center field for the Cleveland Indians from 1929 to 1939 and was inducted into the Baseball Hall of Fame in 1975. His 226 career home runs include 138 hit at home in Cleveland — a mark that stood for decades as a testament to his production in an era of baseball that has largely faded from public memory.

Ramírez passing Averill is a franchise moment. Only Jim Thome — with 184 home runs at home in Cleveland — remains ahead of him on the all-time list. Thome's record is not out of reach in the remaining arc of Ramírez's career, though it is not the conversation of this week.

This week's conversation is nine home runs and what they mean.

The Underappreciated Career

Ramírez has spent his entire career in Cleveland. He has been one of the most consistently excellent position players in baseball over the past decade — a five-tool player who hits for average, hits for power, steals bases, plays elite defense at third base, and produces in postseason situations. He has finished in the top five of AL MVP voting multiple times.

The national conversation around him has never fully matched his production. Cleveland is not New York or Los Angeles. Third basemen are not shortstops or center fielders in the calculus of media attention. Ramírez has accumulated his numbers without the volume of coverage that players with similar statistics in larger markets would command.

Nine home runs from the 300-300 club is the kind of threshold that demands attention regardless of market size.

What's Next

Ramírez has averaged roughly 25-30 home runs per season over his productive years. At that pace, he will reach 300 during the 2026 season — likely sometime before the All-Star break if his production continues at its current rate.

When he hits home run No. 300, the celebration will be for one of the most quietly assembled elite careers in the history of Cleveland baseball. Willie Mays. Barry Bonds. Alex Rodriguez. Carlos Beltran. André Dawson. Deion Sanders. Steve Finley. Bobby Bonds.

And then: José Ramírez.

Nine home runs. Start paying attention.
