Nick Kurtz's 20-Game Walk Streak Is Over — Two Short of the All-Time Record

By Ryan Calloway | May 22, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

It ended in a 14-6 loss to the Cleveland Guardians. Nick Kurtz did not walk. His streak of 20 consecutive games with at least one base on balls was over, and with it went the closest challenge to an 80-year-old record that most baseball fans have never heard of.

Two games. That was the gap between Kurtz and Roy Cullenbine's all-time record of 22 consecutive games with a walk, set in Detroit in 1947.

What the Streak Looked Like

Kurtz's run began quietly, as these things often do. An Oakland Athletics first baseman drawing walks is not the kind of statistic that drives clicks in the early weeks of a season. But as the games accumulated — ten, then fifteen, then past the mark of twenty — the streak demanded attention.

At 20 games, Kurtz tied Barry Bonds (2002-03) for the second-longest consecutive-game walk streak in MLB history. That is not a comparison anyone makes lightly. Bonds' stretch in 2002-03 is part of the most disciplined plate approach the modern game has produced. Kurtz drew walks at a 55.3% strike rate — against pitchers throwing him hittable pitches, he was still getting on base.

He led the major leagues in walks during the streak. He hit .270 with five home runs over the same period. The walks were not coming because pitchers were avoiding him. They were coming because Kurtz was making excellent decisions.

Roy Cullenbine and the Record That Stood

Roy Cullenbine played for nine different teams in a 10-year career that ended in 1947. He is not in the Hall of Fame. He is not widely remembered. But his record of 22 consecutive games with at least one walk has stood for 79 years, and it stood again this week.

Cullenbine's 1947 season with the Detroit Tigers is the source of the record. He drew 137 walks that year — an extraordinary total in any era — and finished second in the American League MVP voting. His plate discipline was exceptional. The streak was the purest expression of it.

Kurtz came within two games of ending Cullenbine's place in the record books. He did not. Roy Cullenbine still holds the record.

A Small-Market Story Worth Knowing

The Oakland Athletics have been rebuilding for years. Kurtz emerged from that process as one of the most patient and disciplined young hitters in the game. The walk streak brought a small-market first baseman into a national conversation about one of baseball's forgotten statistical categories.

That conversation matters beyond Oakland. Plate discipline is the foundation of offensive baseball — the ability to identify a ball, lay off it, and force a pitcher to throw you something you can hit. Kurtz demonstrated that skill at a historical level for 20 straight games. The fact that he fell short of the record does not diminish what he did.

What's Next

Kurtz returns to the lineup Friday without a streak to protect. His underlying plate approach has not changed. He will continue to draw walks, continue to reach base, and continue to be one of the most disciplined hitters in the American League.

The record is Roy Cullenbine's for now. It has stood since 1947. It almost fell in 2026, on a rebuilding team in Oakland, because a first baseman spent 20 games making some of the best at-bat decisions of any hitter in the sport.

That deserves to be remembered even without the record next to his name.
