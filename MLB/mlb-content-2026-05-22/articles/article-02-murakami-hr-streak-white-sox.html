Murakami Homers in 5 Straight Games — Now 3 Away From the MLB All-Time Record

By Jake Torres | May 22, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

Nobody expected the Chicago White Sox to be generating the most compelling individual storyline of the 2026 MLB season. Yet here we are, with a Japanese-born rookie who has just homered in five consecutive games, tied the White Sox franchise record, and put himself three games away from one of baseball's most famous records.

Munetaka Murakami is not supposed to be doing this. The White Sox were not supposed to be in any national baseball conversation in 2026. Baseball, as usual, did not care about the script.

The Franchise Record Is Tied

Murakami's home run in his fifth consecutive game tied the White Sox franchise record for consecutive games with a home run. The names who held that mark before him form a who's who of Chicago power hitters: Frank Thomas (twice), A.J. Pierzynski, Paul Konerko, Carlos Lee, Ron Kittle, and Greg Luzinski.

That is not ordinary company. Murakami is a rookie. He is also now in that company.

He came to Chicago having already set the record for most home runs by a Japanese-born player in his first 21 MLB games — seven, surpassing Shohei Ohtani's five. That mark established him as a legitimate power hitter. The five-game streak elevated the conversation entirely.

Three Games From Immortality

The all-time MLB record for consecutive games with a home run is eight. It has been matched three times in the history of the sport, each occasion by a player whose name belongs in any conversation about the game's greatest hitters.

Dale Long set the record first, in May 1956 with the Pittsburgh Pirates. Don Mattingly tied it in July 1987 with the Yankees. Ken Griffey Jr. matched it in July 1993 with Seattle. Three players. One number. Eight straight games with a home run.

Murakami needs three more games to join that list.

The math is straightforward. The achievement would not be. Griffey was at the peak of his powers in 1993. Mattingly was a perennial All-Star. Long was a first baseman on a good Pirates team. Murakami is a 24-year-old in his first MLB season, on a White Sox team that few outside Chicago were watching before this streak began.

The Broader Context

Murakami's power numbers in Japan were well-documented before his MLB debut. He won back-to-back Central League MVP awards with the Tokyo Yakult Swallows and led the NPB in home runs multiple times. The question scouts asked was always whether those numbers would translate to American pitching.

The answer, through the first weeks of the 2026 season, has been emphatic. Murakami has hit for power against a range of pitchers and pitch types. The home run in game five of the streak came against an established major league arm. This is not a hot streak built on a favorable schedule.

What's Next

Murakami's next three games will draw attention the White Sox have rarely seen. The question is simple: can he homer in game six, seven, and eight? Each game that passes without a home run ends the chase. Each home run extends it.

If he reaches eight consecutive games with a home run, Munetaka Murakami will have one of the most improbable entries in the record books in baseball history — a White Sox rookie, in his first MLB season, alongside Griffey, Mattingly, and Long.

Three games. One record. Watch the White Sox.
