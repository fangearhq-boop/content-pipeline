Schwarber's Historic May Pace Puts the 60-HR Club Conversation on the Table

By Marcus Cole | May 22, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

On May 15, Kyle Schwarber hit his 20th home run of the 2026 season. That single swing made him the earliest player in the history of Major League Baseball to reach 20 home runs. Earlier than Barry Bonds in his 73-home run season. Earlier than Mark McGwire. Earlier than Sammy Sosa.

Through 49 games, Schwarber is on pace for 66 home runs. The 60-HR club has exactly three members, and none of them reached the mark after 2001. The current conversation has to start now.

The Numbers Behind the Pace

Pacing a season from 49 games requires caution — players slow down, pitchers adjust, and the summer grind tests every hitter. But Schwarber's pace is not just fast. It is unprecedented in its timing.

The previous record for earliest 20th home run belonged to Luis Gonzalez, who reached the mark on May 17, 2001 — during the season he hit 57 home runs. Schwarber beat that by two days. His slash line entering Friday stands at .237/.365/.645 with 35 RBI. The on-base percentage and slugging percentage reflect a hitter who is both disciplined and dangerous.

At the current rate, he needs 40 more home runs in the final 113 games. That is a pace of roughly 0.35 home runs per game for the remainder of the season. It is not guaranteed. It is also not impossible for a hitter doing what Schwarber has done in the first seven weeks.

The 60-HR Club

The club has three members. Barry Bonds hit 73 home runs in 2001 — the single-season record. Mark McGwire hit 70 in 1998 and 65 in 1999. Sammy Sosa hit 66 in 1998, 64 in 1999, and 63 in 2001. No player has reached 60 home runs in the 25 seasons since.

An entire generation of baseball fans has grown up without ever watching a player seriously threaten 60 home runs in a season. The last time a player entered September with a realistic chance at 60 was two decades ago. Schwarber, through 49 games, is the first player in that span whose pace has made the conversation legitimate rather than premature.

The comparison to the Bonds-McGwire-Sosa era will come with its own complications. That era carries the weight of the performance-enhancing drug investigations and the questions that followed. Schwarber's pace exists in a different context, and the conversation about what it means to be in this company is one that baseball writers and fans will have regardless of how the season ends.

The Phillies Equation

Schwarber's home run pace exists alongside a Phillies team with legitimate World Series aspirations. Philadelphia entered the season as one of the stronger National League contenders, and Schwarber's production is the engine of an offense that creates problems for opposing pitchers throughout the lineup.

For the Phillies, a Schwarber chasing history is also a Schwarber making their opponents' jobs harder. Every walk they give him is a baserunner. Every mistake pitch is a potential two-run home run. His individual pursuit and the team's October goals are aligned.

What's Next

If Schwarber is still on this pace entering June, the 60-HR conversation will no longer be a curiosity — it will be the central storyline in the National League. The pace needs to hold for another month before it demands that kind of weight.

For now, the math is real. The milestone is real. Kyle Schwarber hit his 20th home run earlier than any player in the history of Major League Baseball, and the number 60 is no longer off the table.

Watch the Phillies every night. Schwarber is the reason.
