### STORY 2: Murakami's 5-Game HR Streak — 3 Away from the MLB Record

**Long-Form Post**
⚾ THREE MORE. THREE. ⚾

Munetaka Murakami has just done something no White Sox player has done since 2012: homer in five consecutive games. Five in a row. He just tied the Chicago White Sox franchise record.

A.J. Pierzynski. Paul Konerko. Carlos Lee. Frank Thomas (twice). Ron Kittle. Greg Luzinski. Those are the names who held it before him. Now Murakami is in that company.

But here's the thing — he's not stopping there. The all-time MLB record is 8 consecutive games with a home run, held by three of the most recognizable names in baseball history: Ken Griffey Jr. (1993), Don Mattingly (1987), and Dale Long (1956).

Murakami needs three more.

The White Sox were not supposed to be in the national baseball conversation this year. Yet here they are, with a Japanese-born rookie who hit more home runs in his first 21 MLB games than any player from Japan who came before him — including Shohei Ohtani.

Three games. One historic record. The least-expected storyline of the 2026 MLB season.

### STORY 3: Nick Kurtz's Walk Streak Ends at 20 — Two Short of the All-Time Record

**Long-Form Post**
⚾ IT'S OVER. AND IT WAS HISTORIC. ⚾

Nick Kurtz walked in 20 consecutive games.

Then Oakland lost to the Cleveland Guardians 14-6. And Kurtz didn't draw a walk.

The streak is over at 20 consecutive games — tied with Barry Bonds (2002-03) for the second-longest in MLB history, two games short of Roy Cullenbine's record of 22, set in Detroit in 1947.

Two games. That's the gap. If Kurtz had drawn one walk in each of those two games, his name would be etched next to Cullenbine's in the record books.

But here's what shouldn't get lost: what Kurtz did was remarkable. He led the major leagues in walks. Pitchers were throwing him strikes — his strike rate was 55.3% — and he was still walking. That kind of plate discipline doesn't come from patience alone. It comes from a hitter who understands what he's doing at the plate at a level that most major leaguers never achieve.

Roy Cullenbine's record has stood for 79 years. It almost fell this week. Nobody saw it coming.

### STORY 4: Schwarber's 20-HR May — The 60-HR Club Conversation Has to Start Now

**Long-Form Post**
⚾ ARE WE REALLY DOING THIS? ⚾

Kyle Schwarber hit his 20th home run on May 15. Not a big deal on its own — except that it's the earliest any player in the history of Major League Baseball has reached 20 home runs.

Earlier than Barry Bonds in his 73-home run season. Earlier than Mark McGwire. Earlier than Sammy Sosa. Earlier than all of them.

Through his first 49 games of 2026, Schwarber is on pace for 66 home runs.

Let's put that in context. The 60-HR club has three members: Bonds (73), McGwire (70), and Sosa (66). No player has joined the club since 2001 — 25 years ago. The current generation of fans has essentially never watched a player seriously threaten 60 home runs in a clean season.

Schwarber is doing it.

The Phillies outfielder isn't just a power hitter running hot. He set a milestone on the calendar that no one in the history of baseball has cleared before. The numbers are not just impressive. They are unprecedented in when they arrived.

Is the pace sustainable? Maybe not. But the conversation is legitimate. The math is real. And Schwarber is the reason you should be watching the Phillies every night.

### STORY 5: Ramírez Passes Averill, Closes in on 300-300 Club

**Long-Form Post**
⚾ PAYING ATTENTION TO JOSÉ RAMÍREZ YET? ⚾

Quietly, patiently, one game at a time, José Ramírez keeps adding to one of the most extraordinary statistical resumes in the history of Cleveland baseball.

This week: Ramírez passed Earl Averill for sole possession of second place on Cleveland's all-time home run list at home, with 138 career home runs at Progressive Field. Jim Thome, the all-time leader, has 184. Ramírez is in the conversation.

But the bigger number is 300. Ramírez is 9 home runs away from 300 career home runs. He already has 300 career stolen bases — reaching that milestone on May 2.

When he hits No. 300, he will join one of the most exclusive clubs in the history of the sport. The 300-300 club — players with both 300 career home runs and 300 career stolen bases — has 8 members: Willie Mays, Barry Bonds, Alex Rodriguez, Carlos Beltran, Andre Dawson, Deion Sanders, Steve Finley, and Bobby Bonds.

Nine home runs from belonging to that group.

Nine.
