Justin Verlander's Final All-Star — Three Cy Youngs, 21 Seasons, One Last Night on the Stage

By Ryan Calloway | Ballpark Banter | July 14, 2026

Justin Verlander will not throw a pitch tonight at Citizens Bank Park. He hasn't thrown a pitch in a game since March 30. At 43 years old, with hip and hamstring injuries that have kept him on the 60-day injured list since early April, there is no mound appearance coming.

But there will be a moment.

Commissioner Rob Manfred named Verlander as the American League's "Legend Pick" for the 2026 All-Star Game — his 10th All-Star selection overall. He will be honored on the field at Citizens Bank Park during tonight's ceremony. Shortly after the announcement, Verlander confirmed that 2026 is his final season.

In his own words: "The opportunity to attend once again is something I'll cherish, and it will be an incredibly special moment for me and my family."

The Career

The numbers have been in the books for months; only the retirement made them official. Justin Verlander: 266 wins, 159 losses, 3.33 ERA, 3,554 strikeouts, 21 seasons. Three Cy Young Awards — 2011 with the Detroit Tigers, 2019 with the Houston Astros, 2022 with the Astros. The 2011 award came with the AL Most Valuable Player trophy as well, won unanimously. Two World Series rings (Houston, 2017 and 2022).

He started his career in Detroit in 2005. He's ending it in Detroit in 2026. The geography is as clean as the career arc.

The Era He Defined

There is a generation of baseball fans for whom "dominant starting pitcher" and "Justin Verlander" are essentially the same phrase. He was the defining AL power arm from his Cy Young season in 2011 through his resurgence with Houston in his late 30s — winning his third Cy Young Award at age 39 in 2022. The Tommy John surgery recovery that cost him two seasons made the 2022 Cy Young one of the more impressive achievements in modern pitching history.

For players of his era, the comparison was always Verlander or Max Scherzer. Two different right-handers, two different archetypes, two Hall of Fame careers running in parallel for the same decade. Verlander was the one with the fastball that stayed in the upper 90s until his 40s, the one who made opponents look genuinely uncomfortable even late in counts, even late in seasons, even late in careers.

Tonight in Philadelphia

There are better ways to go out. A last start. A last strikeout. The camera finding you in the dugout for one more moment. Verlander doesn't get that version of retirement — not this year, not with what his body has required in 2026.

What he gets is tonight. A crowd of 40,000 people at Citizens Bank Park acknowledging what he was. His family watching. His name announced on the field for the last time at an All-Star Game. A moment that, while smaller than a championship celebration, is its own kind of honor.

He said it will be special. It will be.

What's Next

Verlander will remain with the Tigers for the remainder of the 2026 season, whether or not he returns from the injured list. His Hall of Fame eligibility begins in 2031, five years after retirement. There is no reasonable baseball argument against his induction. The question for tonight is only this: did you watch it happen?
