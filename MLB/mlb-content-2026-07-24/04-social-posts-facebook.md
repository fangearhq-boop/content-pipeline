### STORY 1: Skubal Speaks — Trade Deadline

#### Long-Form Post — 8:00 AM ET

Tarik Skubal made it official on Thursday.

"My preference is to finish the season with the Tigers and win the World Series," the back-to-back AL Cy Young winner said publicly — adding that he understands his next start could be his last in a Detroit uniform.

That is the most direct public statement Skubal has made about his future. It does not mean the Tigers keep him. But it means every executive on the other side of these trade calls now knows exactly what the pitcher they're trying to acquire does not want.

ESPN's Jeff Passan and Kiley McDaniel have the trade odds at 60%. The Dodgers have emerged as the top suitor, according to multiple reports. Los Angeles can offer what Detroit is asking for: a controllable starter to slot into their rotation plus quality prospects. The Dodgers have both.

Detroit is 6.5 games back in the AL Central and 4.5 out of the Wild Card. Those numbers are not disqualifying for a team that believes it has the pitching to make a late run — and the Tigers believe that. Which is why there is a real version of this where Skubal stays and the Tigers use the next 10 days to add rather than subtract.

There is also a real version where the Tigers decide the gap is too large, the offer from the Dodgers is too good, and Skubal pitches his last game in Detroit before the week is out.

The deadline is August 3. Ten days.

### STORY 2: Red Sox — From Fire Sale to Buyers

#### Long-Form Post — 10:30 AM ET

On June 24, the Boston Red Sox were 37-48. They were two games under .500 in late June, and the trade deadline conversation around them centered on who Boston would be selling and what prospects they could accumulate.

Thirty-seven days later, they're 52-49, on the outer edge of the AL Wild Card race, and the conversation has completely reversed. The Red Sox are buyers.

Reports indicate Boston has interest in Zach Neto, the shortstop from the Los Angeles Angels, who are in full sell mode heading into August 3. The Red Sox built their 15-game win streak with a team ERA of 1.98 and a bullpen that went 7-0 with a 1.15 ERA during the run — and then extended it to 20-3 in their last 23 games.

The question now is whether the Red Sox front office believes this version of the team — the one that has played at a .741 winning percentage over the past three weeks — is the real one. If it is, adding Neto at short transforms this roster from a streaking surprise into a genuine playoff threat.

If the streak was an outlier and the 37-48 team was the real one, adding a shortstop just means they lose with better defense.

The next few weeks will tell us which team Boston actually is. But the fact that they're having this conversation at all, after June looked like it did, is the part of this story that keeps getting undersold.

### STORY 3: Ohtani's Pitching Return — The Process

#### Long-Form Post — 12:30 PM ET

Shohei Ohtani threw 30 pitches at Citizens Bank Park in Philadelphia on Wednesday afternoon. Pitching coach Mark Prior said it looked like a normal between-start side session.

That is good news. It is also not close to enough.

Ohtani received an Orthovisc injection — a lubricating agent — in his left knee during the All-Star break. The left knee is the landing knee for a right-handed pitcher, which means every pitching delivery puts force directly through the joint that has been causing him problems. The injection was designed to reduce inflammation and restore the cushioning the knee needs to absorb that force.

Wednesday's bullpen session was the first test of whether the injection has done its job.

The answer is that it seems to have, but the Dodgers are not rushing. Manager Dave Roberts said it will be "at least a week to 10 days" before the team even entertains a conversation about a return to the rotation. Roberts also noted that he "can't foresee (Ohtani) going out there and throwing 100 pitches on his first outing" — meaning even when he returns, it will be a build-up start, not a full game.

The next bullpen session is expected Saturday at Citi Field in New York, where the Dodgers open a series against the Mets. If that goes well, the conversation about a return to the rotation can start. If it does not, the window pushes back further.

Before the knee issue, Ohtani was 8-2 with a 1.79 ERA, 95 strikeouts, and 85.2 innings pitched. His Cy Young case is compelling even in the absence. What it looks like when he comes back is the question every pitcher and hitter in the NL is watching.

### STORY 4: White Sox 54-47 — The Rebuild Math

#### Long-Form Post — 3:00 PM ET

The numbers on the 2026 Chicago White Sox are genuinely hard to believe if you were watching baseball two years ago.

2024: 41-121. The most losses in modern MLB history.
2025: 60-102. Still losing 100 games, but the losses came down.
2026 (as of July 24): 54-47. First place in the AL Central.

That is a 30-win improvement in a single off-season — a rate of turnaround that matches only seven franchises in baseball history. The White Sox did it without making splashy free-agent signings or trading away the future for rental players. They developed.

Colson Montgomery has 20 home runs. Edgar Quero — 22 years old, called up from Triple-A Charlotte after Kyle Teel went on the IL with a high ankle sprain — is the new catcher. The rotation has stabilized. The bullpen is holding leads.

The Cleveland Guardians are one game back at 54-49. Minnesota just beat Cleveland 3-1 on Thursday, which helps Chicago. The race is real.

The question that matters now is buyer or seller. The White Sox are in first place in late July. The answer is almost certainly buyer — or at minimum, hold. The deadline in 10 days will tell us whether the front office believes this version of the team can play into October. Based on the math of the last two years, they have earned the right to be believed.

### STORY 5: July 23 Recap — Braves 6, Padres 5

#### Long-Form Post — 6:00 PM ET

Chris Sale struck out 11 batters Thursday at Petco Park in San Diego.

That matched his season-high. The Braves won 6-5. Atlanta hit four home runs in the game. Sale did not need any of them to put up his stat line, but he got them anyway.

Sale is 37 years old. He has been to the World Series. He has survived major injuries and multiple surgeries. He has pitched for the White Sox, the Red Sox, and now the Braves. Thursday's game was a reminder that when Sale is dealing — when the slider is working and the fastball is sitting where he needs it — he is still one of the more difficult left-handers to handle in the National League.

Eleven strikeouts. Atlanta wins. The Braves stay in the NL playoff race.

Elsewhere Thursday: The Detroit Tigers beat the Kansas City Royals 4-3 on a Dillon Dingler tiebreaking double in the eighth inning, one inning after James Outman crushed a three-run home run in the seventh. The Blue Jays beat the Rays 3-1 in Toronto. And the Minnesota Twins defeated the Cleveland Guardians 3-1 — a result that helps the Chicago White Sox hold their AL Central lead.

Thursday was a good night of baseball. Friday starts in a few hours.
