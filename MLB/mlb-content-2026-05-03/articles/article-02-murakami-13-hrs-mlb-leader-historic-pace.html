Murakami's 13th HR Extends MLB Lead — A Historic Start Nobody Saw Coming

By Jake Torres | May 3, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

Munetaka Murakami keeps finding the seats. The Chicago White Sox slugger hit his 13th home run of the season on Saturday against the San Diego Padres, going 1-for-3 with two walks and three RBI in the process. He leads all of Major League Baseball in home runs, and the pace at which he is doing this is genuinely unprecedented.

Thirteen home runs through roughly 30 games. A pace that, if sustained, would land somewhere near 65 for the full season — territory no player has visited since the steroid era. The all-time single-season record is 73, set by Barry Bonds in 2001. We are in the first week of May.

The Stat That Defies Explanation

Here is the number that keeps circulating in baseball circles: Murakami's first 12 extra-base hits in the major leagues were all home runs. Not a single double. Not a triple. Twelve extra-base hits, all cleared the fence. According to historical records, that is the longest streak of its kind since at least 1900, surpassing the previous mark of 10 set by Dae-ho Lee in 2016.

It speaks to the way Murakami hits the baseball. He does not spray line drives into the gaps — he elevates and obliterates. His swing is short and violent, and when he makes contact, the sound is different. White Sox fans have become very familiar with that sound.

A New Kind of White Sox Record

Murakami has already set the White Sox franchise record for home runs before the calendar reaches May. He previously tied the franchise's record for consecutive games with a home run at five — matching Frank Thomas, Paul Konerko, and Ron Kittle — and simultaneously tied the MLB rookie record for the same feat. The White Sox have not always given their fans much to cheer about, but Murakami has given them something to genuinely wonder at every night.

The Japanese slugger — who dominated NPB in Japan before coming to MLB — appears to have made the transition without missing a beat. If anything, the adjustment period that typically hampers Japanese hitters has not materialized. The question from here is not whether Murakami belongs — it is how high his ceiling actually is.

What's Next

Murakami and the White Sox continue their 2026 season with the home run race getting more attention with each passing game. The next meaningful threshold is 20 home runs, which could arrive by the end of May at his current pace. Beyond that lies uncharted territory — a 60-HR season would rank among the most historic in the modern era. The rest of baseball is watching, and so is everyone else.
