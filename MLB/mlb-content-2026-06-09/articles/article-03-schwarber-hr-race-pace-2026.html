Schwarber Paces the HR Race Alone: On Pace for 65+ with Judge and Murakami on the IL

By Jake Torres June 9, 2026

The Leader, Alone

Kyle Schwarber leads MLB in home runs. He has been leading for weeks. But as of this week, the competition has effectively stepped aside. Aaron Judge is on the injured list with a rib stress fracture. Roki Murakami is sidelined with a hamstring injury. The two players who had been chasing Schwarber in the home run race are now watching from the clubhouse.

Schwarber, meanwhile, keeps swinging. He is on pace for somewhere between 65 and 70 home runs — and his early-season trajectory was already historic before either competitor went down.

The Historical Baseline

The milestone that put Schwarber's season in perspective came on May 15. That was the date he hit his 20th home run of the season — the earliest any player in MLB history has reached that number. Barry Bonds, who holds the all-time single-season record with 73 home runs in 2001, reached his 20th on May 19. Mark McGwire, who held the record before Bonds, reached 20 later. Aaron Judge, who hit 62 in 2022, got there on June 3.

Schwarber beat all of them by days or weeks. That is not pace-setting. That is record-setting territory, and the calendar still has more than four months of games ahead.

The 60-HR Club

The only players in MLB history to hit 60 or more home runs in a single season are Barry Bonds (73 in 2001), Mark McGwire (70 in 1998, 65 in 1999), and Sammy Sosa (66 in 1998, 64 in 2001, 63 in 1999). No player has reached 60 since Sosa in 2001. That is a 25-year drought. Schwarber's current pace would end it.

The caveat is obvious and worth stating: paces change. A hot stretch inflates projections. A slump or an injury deflates them. Schwarber does not need to maintain this exact trajectory to have a historically significant season — any number above 55 would rank among the greatest home run seasons ever recorded. But the trajectory is what it is, and right now it points somewhere very high.

The Race Returns in July

When Judge returns — estimated around late July — and if Murakami comes back around the same time, the home run race resumes. By then, Schwarber may have built a lead that's difficult to overcome. Or the gap may be manageable and the final months of the regular season become the defining stretch of one of baseball's most compelling storylines.

Either way, June belongs to Schwarber. He is the sole leader. He is on a historic pace. And he is doing it without anyone in his rearview mirror.

What's Next

His next major milestones: 25 HRs (potential by late June), 30 HRs (potentially before the All-Star break), and 40 HRs by August would all put him in rarefied territory. The pace stays visible — and the competition will be back.
