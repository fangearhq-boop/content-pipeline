The Braves Are Baseball's Best Team: 45-21, +109 Run Differential, and a Case Built to Last

By Marcus Cole June 9, 2026

The Record Speaks Clearly

Through 66 games, the Atlanta Braves are 45-21. That is the best record in Major League Baseball — better than the Rays in the AL East, better than the Yankees, better than any team in the National League by a margin that has grown week by week into something substantial. The NL East lead alone tells most of the story: Atlanta sits 9.5 games ahead of the Philadelphia Phillies. That gap is not a hot streak. It is a product of consistent, disciplined baseball played at an elite level over more than a third of the season.

The run differential confirms what the wins suggest. At +109 through 66 games, the Braves are not simply winning close games and beating projections. They are outscoring opponents by an average of more than 1.6 runs per game — a margin that reflects depth, not variance.

What's Driving It

Chris Sale has anchored the rotation with the consistency of a veteran who has figured something out. The left-hander, entering what may be the final chapter of his career, has given Atlanta exactly what a staff needs from its ace: length, strikeouts, and an ability to set the tone at the top of the order. The rotation behind him has followed his lead.

The road record deserves its own mention. Atlanta is 23-10 away from Truist Park. That number matters because road performance is among the most reliable indicators of a team's true quality — it strips away the comforts of home, puts teams in front of hostile crowds, and tests depth in ways a home schedule does not. The Braves have passed that test emphatically.

The NL in Context

Below Atlanta, the National League is fighting for five playoff spots with genuine urgency. The Giants, Cubs, Phillies, and Dodgers are all in the conversation, and every game in June carries weight for each of them. That race will produce compelling baseball through the summer. But the race for the top spot in the NL — the best record, the home field advantage, the No. 1 seed — is not particularly close. Atlanta has built a margin that would require a dramatic collapse to surrender.

The trade deadline approaches in August. The Braves, from a position of strength, will not be desperate buyers. If anything, the question is whether they can add depth without disrupting a roster that is functioning at a very high level. That is a different problem than most contenders face — and it is a good problem to have.
