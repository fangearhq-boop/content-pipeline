Zack Wheeler Turned Down His Own All-Star Game: 'I Didn't Want to Be a Fifth Choice'

By Ryan Calloway | Ballpark Banter | July 12, 2026

Zack Wheeler was offered a spot on the NL All-Star roster this week. He said no.

When injuries and withdrawals thinned the NL pitching staff ahead of Tuesday's game at Citizens Bank Park, Wheeler received a call offering him a replacement slot. His answer was direct. "I didn't want to be a fifth choice," he told reporters on Friday. "I didn't want to be a first choice on the replacement list. I wanted to be on the team."

The Numbers That Should Have Gotten Him There

Wheeler's case for the All-Star roster is not a close call. Through 14 starts this season, he owns a 2.28 ERA with 98 strikeouts in 87 innings — a performance line that puts him among the best starting pitchers in the National League. In his final start before the break, he struck out 14 batters against the Cincinnati Reds, a dominant outing that also ended up being the one that cost him the All-Star nod.

The scheduling rule that governs All-Star eligibility disqualifies pitchers who start on the Sunday before the game. Wheeler pitched Sunday. Under the rule, he was ineligible for the roster — not because of his performance, but because of when he was scheduled to throw.

"Just because I pitch on a certain day, I can't pitch an All-Star Game, or even be there, or get the recognition for it," Wheeler said. "It pisses me off. I feel like I've earned it."

The Home Park That Makes It Worse

The 2026 MLB All-Star Game is being played at Citizens Bank Park in Philadelphia. It is Wheeler's home park. His teammates are in the field. The city is hosting. And the Phillies' best pitcher — by ERA, by strikeout rate, by any reasonable measure — will not be part of it, not even on the bench as part of the ceremony.

That specific geography is what separates this from a routine snub story. A pitcher getting left off the All-Star roster happens every year. A pitcher with a 2.28 ERA watching the game from his home park, as a non-participant, because of a scheduling rule — that's something different.

The Reaction

Wheeler's agent, B.B. Abbott, did not hold back. In a statement, Abbott described MLB's handling of Wheeler's situation as "tone deaf," a pointed criticism of an organization that found itself asking one of its most deserving players to accept a consolation spot.

Former Phillies shortstop Jimmy Rollins, competing in the MLBx 3-on-3 event this weekend in Philadelphia, was equally direct. "I 100 percent believe he was snubbed," Rollins said. "Zack Wheeler has been doing it for a while."

Wheeler's decision to decline the replacement spot, rather than accept a lesser form of recognition, tracks with the broader reaction from people who know him. He did not want to be at the game as an afterthought. He wanted to be there because he belonged there from the start.

What's Next

Wheeler and the Phillies return to regular-season play when the second half opens on Thursday, July 17. The All-Star Game takes place Tuesday, July 14, at Citizens Bank Park, beginning at 7:30 PM ET on FOX. Wheeler will not be there. The scheduling rule that made him ineligible is not new, but the combination of his performance, his home park, and his decision to turn down the replacement spot has brought it into focus in a way that may prompt a conversation about whether the rule serves the game well. For now, the question is what he does in the second half with a 2.28 ERA and something to prove.
