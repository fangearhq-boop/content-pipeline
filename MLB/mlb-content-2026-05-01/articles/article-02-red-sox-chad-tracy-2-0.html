Red Sox Go 2-0 Under Chad Tracy — Can a Managerial Change Actually Save Boston's Season?

By Jake Torres | May 1, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

Two games. Two wins. That is the early evidence of the Chad Tracy era in Boston.

When the Red Sox fired Alex Cora on April 25 — after a 10-17 start that had the fanbase furious and the front office running out of patience — it was the franchise's first in-season managerial dismissal since Jimy Williams was let go in August 2001. Players reportedly cried. Cora, who led the Red Sox to the 2018 World Series championship, had built genuine relationships throughout the organization. Letting him go was painful.

But 10-17 is 10-17. The AL East does not offer participation trophies, and with the Yankees at 19-10, time was already running short for a team that entered 2026 with playoff expectations.

Tracy's Fast Start

Chad Tracy was managing the Triple-A Worcester Red Sox when the call came. He had spent five years in Worcester, compiling a 324-296 record and developing many of the prospects now on Boston's major league roster. His organizational knowledge runs deep — he knows the players, the systems, and the culture that Cora built.

His first two games at the helm went better than anyone could have scripted: a 5-3 win over Baltimore, followed by a 5-0 shutout of the defending American League champion Toronto Blue Jays. Two wins, two complete efforts, zero losses. The locker room tone reportedly shifted immediately, with Roman Anthony — one of Boston's brightest young stars — publicly declaring that the Red Sox "can still win."

The early optimism is real. But so is the caution. Two games is a sample size of exactly nothing in a sport played over 162 of them. The structural issues that put Boston at 10-17 under Cora did not disappear the moment Tracy walked through the clubhouse door.

The Bigger Picture

For Boston to make the postseason from where they sit, they will need to climb over teams that are already playing excellent baseball. The AL East is unforgiving. The Yankees are leading the division, and the wild card picture is crowded with teams that haven't stumbled the way the Red Sox did in April.

Tracy's task is not just to win games — it is to reestablish a culture of urgency and accountability that a 10-17 start had eroded. The talent is there: Anthony, a loaded bullpen (when healthy), and enough bats to compete with anyone when things click. Cora's firing was not about the roster failing — it was about the environment around that roster.

If Tracy can reset the culture as effectively as his first two wins suggest, the Red Sox are a dangerous team with several months of baseball remaining. If the losses resume? Boston's 2026 season could be a very long summer indeed.

What's Next

The Red Sox continue their schedule entering May with the AL East standings still very much in play. Chad Tracy's early record will be tracked game by game as the season unfolds. The first real test of whether the managerial change has produced lasting change comes over the next 10-15 games — when the novelty fades and the pressure of the standings becomes real. Boston's 2026 fate will be decided in May and June.
