Aaron Judge Is Chasing 400 Career Home Runs — and He Could Get There Before Summer Ends

By Jake Torres | May 1, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

Aaron Judge has hit the ball very hard in a lot of places over his career. He has hit it into the seats in right field at Yankee Stadium. He has hit it over the monuments in center. He hit 62 of them in 2022 — the most in American League history. And now, entering May 2026, he is approximately 20 home runs away from joining the most exclusive club in baseball: the 400-HR club.

Judge entered 2026 needing approximately 32 home runs to reach the milestone. He has 12 through April, putting him on pace for over 60 on the season. At that rate, milestone No. 400 could arrive sometime in August or September — before the postseason push, before the leaves change, before most milestones of this magnitude typically get reached.

The First-Inning Phenomenon

One of the stranger and more fascinating aspects of Judge's 2026 start is the distribution of his home runs by inning. Of his first 9 home runs this season, five came in the first inning — a remarkable clustering in the game's opening frame. In first-inning at-bats, Judge is hitting .450 with a 1.726 OPS. Pitchers who try to get ahead of Judge early in the game are losing that battle decisively.

The implication is one that opposing pitching coaches are likely already grappling with: do you pitch carefully to Judge in the first inning and risk falling behind in counts? Or do you try to challenge him and accept that he might put his team ahead before the game has truly started? Neither option is good, which is exactly the kind of problem a generational hitter presents.

The 400-HR Club

The 400-home run club is not a collection of names — it is a testament to sustained excellence over many seasons. Reaching 400 requires not just power but durability, health, and the ability to perform at an elite level across a decade or more of professional play. Judge, who turns 34 this season, has been one of the most consistently elite offensive players in the game since his 2017 emergence.

He has never hit fewer than 32 home runs in a full season (the 2020 shortened year aside, when he had only 101 at-bats). His 53-HR season in 2025 and 58-HR season in 2024 demonstrate that the power has not diminished. If anything, the approach has sharpened.

The Yankees (19-10, leading the AL East) have been built around Judge's offensive force for the past several years. His presence in the lineup creates run-scoring opportunities for everyone around him and forces opposing managers into uncomfortable tactical decisions throughout games. When Judge reaches 400, it will be a milestone celebrated not just as a counting stat but as confirmation of something the baseball world has known for years: he is one of the great power hitters of his generation.

What's Next

Judge and the Yankees continue into May with the AL East lead and the 400-HR chase running simultaneously. The next meaningful milestone marker comes somewhere around HR No. 390 — a point at which the record could realistically arrive in a single good month. If his April pace holds, that moment arrives well before the All-Star break. The home run count will be tracked obsessively from here forward. Every first-inning at-bat carries the possibility of history.
