The NL Central Has Five Teams Above .500 Entering May — This Has Almost Never Happened

By Marcus Cole | May 1, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

Baseball's most historically balanced division is making history again. As May begins, all five teams in the National League Central — the Reds, Cubs, Cardinals, Pirates, and Brewers — remain above the .500 mark. This is the second-latest date in baseball history that all five teams in a single division have simultaneously been above .500, trailing only May 29, 2004.

The math is both fascinating and cruel. The NL Central sends three teams to the playoffs. Three out of five. Which means that at least two teams currently playing winning baseball will be watching October from home.

The Historic Context

In the Divisional Era — which began in 1969 — no five-team division has ever finished a complete season with every team above .500. The closest approximations have come and gone as the summer heat erodes someone's record, the injuries pile up, the pitching falters. It has simply never held all the way through.

What the NL Central is doing entering May is historically significant precisely because this is when things usually begin to separate. Teams with flaws get exposed over 162 games. Depth matters. Starting pitching matters. Roster construction becomes the difference between a 88-win team that makes the playoffs and a 90-win team that doesn't. In the NL Central this year, nobody has shown the kind of sustained weakness that normally breaks a division into clear tiers.

The Reds have led the way, bolstered by the emergence of rookie Sal Stewart — who had been mashing with eight home runs and 21 RBI earlier in the season, tied for the NL lead at that point. Elly De La Cruz has continued to be the most electric player in the division. But the Cubs, Cardinals, Pirates, and Brewers have all played competitive baseball as well, which is why the standings look this compressed.

The Playoff Picture Problem

The NL Wild Card picture adds another layer of complexity. Two wild card spots in the National League are available for teams outside their division leaders. With five NL Central teams potentially in contention — and the NL East and NL West sending their own contenders — the arithmetic becomes genuinely complicated.

What this division is creating is a scenario where the internal series matter more than anywhere else in baseball. Every Cubs-Reds game, every Cardinals-Pirates matchup, every Brewers-Cubs contest carries postseason significance. A sweep in June could be the difference between October and a golf course.

Fans of all five teams should be paying close attention. The NL Central in 2026 might be producing something that baseball has never seen before: a full division where everyone matters all year long.

What's Next

The NL Central division race continues through May with every series in the division carrying outsized importance. The key names to track: Sal Stewart's rookie development, Elly De La Cruz's continued brilliance, and how each team handles the inevitable stretches of adversity that a long season delivers. The race that has never been won in this form — a five-team division all above .500 at season's end — begins in earnest now.
