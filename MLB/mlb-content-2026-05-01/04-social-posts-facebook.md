### STORY 1:

#### Long-Form Post -- 8:00 AM ET

The best player most baseball fans underrate just had a historic April.

José Ramírez won the AL Player of the Month Award for April 2026, and the numbers are staggering: in 21 games, he hit .342 with a .722 slugging percentage, 7 home runs, 28 RBI, 7 doubles, a triple, 12 runs scored, and 9 walks. That's a pace of roughly 54 HR and 215 RBI over a full season.

But Ramírez's April wasn't just about the stats. On April 6, he played in his 1,620th game as a Cleveland Guardian — breaking Terry Turner's franchise record for games played, a mark that had stood for over a century. It's a record most people have never heard of, but it speaks to the durability and consistency that defines Ramírez's career.

Now the bigger chase: a third consecutive 30-30 season. Ramírez has already pulled off two consecutive 30-30 campaigns. A third would make him the ninth player in major league history to record at least two consecutive 30-30 seasons. He turns 35 this month, and he's somehow getting better.

José Ramírez doesn't get the headlines of Aaron Judge or Shohei Ohtani. But right now, entering May, he's putting up numbers that rival both of them. The most underrated superstar in baseball had himself quite an April.

### STORY 2:

#### Long-Form Post -- 12:30 PM ET

Two games in. Two wins. Is the Red Sox turnaround actually happening?

When Alex Cora was fired April 25 with Boston sitting at 10-17, it felt like damage control more than a real fix. Chad Tracy — the Worcester Triple-A manager for five years (324-296) — was handed a sinking ship and tasked with keeping it afloat.

His first two games as interim manager went like this:

Game 1: Red Sox 5, Baltimore 3. Win.
Game 2: Red Sox 5, defending AL champion Toronto 0. Shutout. Win.

That's 2-0. It won't fix what ailed this team overnight — the offense that wasn't showing up, the bullpen inconsistency, the lack of urgency that plagued Cora's last weeks in the dugout. But early results matter for momentum, and the Red Sox got two wins when they desperately needed them.

Roman Anthony, the team's top young talent, said publicly after the Cora firing that the Red Sox "can still win." Players were reportedly in tears when Cora was let go — a sign that the relationship was real, even if the results weren't coming.

Tracy has the organizational knowledge and the respect of the players. The AL East isn't won by any means. But Boston is back on the right side of the ledger — for now.

### STORY 3:

#### Long-Form Post -- 6:00 PM ET

Here's everything you need to know about Munetaka Murakami's plate approach: he walks, strikes out, or hits a monster home run.

That's it. That's the whole thing.

The Chicago White Sox's rookie first baseman leads MLB in home runs — an extraordinary feat for any player, let alone a first-year man in a new country adjusting to a new league and a new culture. But the HOW of Murakami's dominance is what makes it historic.

His first 12 MLB extra-base hits were ALL home runs. Every single one. That's a record dating back to at least 1900, surpassing Dae-ho Lee's mark of 10 from 2016. On top of that, he's drawn 21 walks — ranking in the 98th percentile — with a .394 on-base percentage and a .992 OPS. He's on pace for approximately 63-67 home runs over a full season.

The baseball analytics community has a term for this kind of hitter: "three true outcomes." Murakami is practically the Platonic ideal of that archetype. You walk him, you watch him swing through a slider in the dirt, or you watch the ball disappear over the fence in a place most people didn't know was possible.

The White Sox have gone through some lean years building this roster. Murakami is the kind of signing that changes everything — if it keeps going. And right now, with the most home runs of anyone in baseball, it is very much keeping going.

### STORY 4:

#### Long-Form Post -- 10:30 AM ET

Welcome to May — where the most fascinating division in baseball continues to defy history.

Every single team in the NL Central is above .500 entering May 2026. The Reds lead the way, with the Cubs, Cardinals, Pirates, and Brewers all sitting with winning records. This is the second-latest date in baseball history that all five teams in a single division have simultaneously been above .500 — trailing only May 29, 2004.

If the NL Central somehow finishes with all five teams at winning records? It would be the first time in the Divisional Era (since 1969) that a full division ever finished above .500. That has never happened.

Here's the brutal math: only three NL Central teams make the playoffs. Two winning teams are going home early. Someone who could easily be a Wild Card contender in another division is going to miss October entirely.

The Reds have been the early leaders, with rookie Sal Stewart providing a breakout bat in the middle of their lineup. But this race isn't close to being over. Every series within the division matters more than in any other part of baseball right now.

You think you've seen parity? The NL Central is rewriting what that word means.

### STORY 5:

#### Long-Form Post -- 3:00 PM ET

Aaron Judge is coming for the 400-home run club.

Judge entered 2026 needing approximately 32 home runs to join that exclusive fraternity — players like Barry Bonds, Hank Aaron, Babe Ruth, and Willie Mays. He has hit 12 home runs through April, putting him on pace for 60+ on the season. At that pace, he could reach the milestone sometime before September.

What makes Judge's chase even more impressive is WHERE he's hitting the ball. Five of his first 9 home runs this year came in the first inning. In first-inning at-bats, Judge is hitting .450 with a 1.726 OPS — numbers that suggest pitchers are trying to get ahead of him and failing spectacularly. He's essentially a cleanup hitter leading off his own half-inning.

The Yankees (19-10) are leading the AL East behind Judge's bat. He has never hit fewer than 32 home runs in a full season (he had just 101 at-bats in 2020's shortened year). His 2022 season — 62 HRs, the AL record — remains the standard by which all of his years are measured.

The 400-HR club isn't broken open by season totals. It's broken in stages: one at-bat, one first-inning blast, one day at a time. Judge is closing in.

### STORY 6:

#### Long-Form Post -- 8:00 PM ET

Shohei Ohtani just finished April looking like the best baseball player who has ever lived.

That's not hyperbole. Consider:

On the mound: a sub-0.40 ERA as a starting pitcher. He has 7 pitch types — one of just 16 pitchers in all of MLB to deploy that many. Hitters are essentially guessing every single at-bat.

At the plate: a .996 OPS, 5 home runs, and an on-base streak that now sits at 48 consecutive games — the third-longest in Dodgers franchise history.

The Dodgers are 20-9, tied for the best record in baseball.

Ohtani's 48-game on-base streak is a testament to something beyond power. It speaks to discipline, approach, and the kind of consistency that only the elite maintain over a full month — let alone 48 games. In all of baseball history, there is no comparable two-way player. The record books don't have a column for what he's doing.

April is over. May is here. And Shohei Ohtani is still doing things that have never been done before.
