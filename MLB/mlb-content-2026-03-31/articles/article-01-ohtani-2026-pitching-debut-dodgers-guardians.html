Sasaki, Ohtani, Yamamoto: The Night Baseball History Gets Made Three Times in a Row

By Ballpark Banter Staff | March 31, 2026

Tonight at 7:10 PM Pacific time, Shohei Ohtani walks to the mound at Uniqlo Field. No pitch count restrictions. No innings limit. Just the two-way star that the city of Los Angeles paid the largest contract in professional sports history to watch — pitching in a regular season game for the first time in 2026.

It is the most anticipated individual pitching start of the year. It is also, according to the Elias Sports Bureau, the middle act in the first three-game sequence in MLB history to feature consecutive starts by Japanese-born pitchers. Roki Sasaki took the mound Monday. Yoshinobu Yamamoto pitches Wednesday. Tonight belongs to Ohtani.

The Road to This Moment

Ohtani's path back to full pitching health has been documented, celebrated, and agonized over by baseball fans for nearly three years. His second Tommy John surgery in 2023 meant he couldn't pitch at all in 2024 — a year he spent entirely as a designated hitter, winning his first World Series championship with Los Angeles.

In 2025, he returned to the mound with restrictions — building up over 14 starts, totaling 47 innings pitched with a 2.87 ERA. He was brilliant but careful, and the Dodgers were careful with him. He also started four playoff games that October, including Game 7 of the World Series.

This year is different. Manager Dave Roberts has made clear that 2026 is the full two-way season. No training wheels.

"Honestly, with Shohei, you've got to be willing to adapt," Roberts told reporters ahead of tonight's game. "Because if he's really efficient, then you're still trying to win the game. And if it makes sense, I'm not going to just pull the plug just because of a certain number."

Making History With Every Lineup Card

Beyond Ohtani's personal story, tonight's game carries a piece of collective history. The Dodgers had Sasaki start Monday's game against Cleveland. Ohtani pitches tonight. Yamamoto goes Wednesday. Three consecutive starts by Japanese-born pitchers — something no MLB team has ever done before in the sport's 150-plus year history.

The significance isn't merely cultural. Sasaki, Ohtani, and Yamamoto are three genuinely elite starters. The Dodgers have constructed a rotation featuring three pitchers who were dominant in Nippon Professional Baseball before making the move to North America. That they can line up three of them in consecutive games against the same opponent — the defending American League Central contenders from Cleveland — is a testament to the transformation of baseball into a truly global sport.

The Context: Guardians Already Won Game 1

Ohtani will not be pitching in a vacuum. The Cleveland Guardians came to Uniqlo Field on Monday and won Game 1 of the series 4-2, handing the Dodgers their first loss of the 2026 season. Parker Messick threw six scoreless innings. Cleveland scored three times in the seventh inning and the Dodgers, who had swept the Diamondbacks in three consecutive comeback wins to open the season, found themselves on the wrong end of a score for the first time.

There will be additional motivation for Ohtani and the Dodgers tonight beyond the historical significance.

Opposing Ohtani will be right-hander Tanner Bibee, who allowed three runs across five innings in his first start of the season — all on solo home runs — and struck out seven.

What's Next

Whether tonight becomes a landmark performance or a workmanlike outing, it marks the official beginning of what could be Ohtani's most significant pitching season. Without restrictions, with a healthy arm and a full spring of preparation, the question shifts from "how many innings will he throw?" to "how dominant can he be?"

Cy Young conversations have already started. The season is six days old. Yoshinobu Yamamoto pitches Wednesday. And Dodger fans — along with baseball fans across the country — have their eyes on the mound tonight.
