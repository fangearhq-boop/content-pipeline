Comeback Magic, Then a Reality Check: The Dodgers' Three-Peat Bid Is Already Being Tested

By Ballpark Banter Staff | March 31, 2026

The Los Angeles Dodgers opened the 2026 season by doing something that hadn't been done in over 25 years: they won their first three games of the year by coming from behind in each game, becoming just the seventh team since 1901 to open a season with three consecutive multi-run comeback victories.

It felt like a statement. The defending two-time World Series champions — the franchise that plays at Uniqlo Field (formerly Dodger Stadium, renamed after a sponsorship deal on March 16), that signed Shohei Ohtani to the largest contract in professional sports history, that has made the postseason every year since 2013 — started 2026 the way teams start seasons when they intend to make history.

Then the Cleveland Guardians came to town.

Monday's Loss: A Reminder That No Season Is a Coronation

On Monday night, Cleveland's Parker Messick threw six scoreless innings at Uniqlo Field. The Guardians scored three times in the seventh inning to turn a close game into a comfortable 4-2 win. The Dodgers had their first loss of 2026.

It was a reminder that even the best teams lose, and that no team — not even one built like the Dodgers, with Ohtani, Freddie Freeman, Mookie Betts, and a rotation featuring three Japanese-born starters — wins them all.

Roki Sasaki started Monday for the Dodgers and took the loss. Tonight, Shohei Ohtani starts. Wednesday, Yoshinobu Yamamoto goes. The three-game Japanese starter sequence — which the Elias Sports Bureau confirmed is unprecedented in MLB history — continues, and the Dodgers will look to bounce back with their best available arm.

The Three-Peat in Historical Context

Only four franchises in the history of Major League Baseball have won three consecutive World Series championships. The most recent was the New York Yankees, who won in 1998, 1999, and 2000. The Oakland A's won three from 1972 through 1974. The Yankees also won three in a row from 1949 through 1951. And the Yankees won four straight from 1936 through 1939.

The Dodgers have won back-to-back titles. They are chasing what no National League team has ever done in the modern era: three consecutive championships.

The task is enormous. Teams that win the World Series face extra attention, exhausted rosters, and opponents motivated to knock them off. The Dodgers are not immune to those forces. Monday's loss demonstrated that plainly.

The Road Ahead

The season is six days old. The Dodgers are 3-1. That is not a concerning record for a team with World Series aspirations. What matters is that the roster is healthy, the rotation is deep, and the offense — when Ohtani is in the lineup as both a hitter and a pitcher — is the most dangerous in baseball.

Tonight's game is Ohtani's first pitching start of the season and the middle act of the historically unprecedented three-game Japanese starter sequence. Whether the Dodgers win or lose, the moment has its own significance.

The three-peat bid is alive. It was always going to require surviving some adversity. The Guardians just delivered the first test.

What's Next

Ohtani pitches tonight. Yamamoto goes Wednesday. Then the Dodgers move on to face the next opponent on a long, winding schedule that culminates in October. The early losses are absorbed. The late-season losses are what hurt.

Three-peat or not, the 2026 Los Angeles Dodgers are built to compete. The most interesting story in baseball is just getting started.
