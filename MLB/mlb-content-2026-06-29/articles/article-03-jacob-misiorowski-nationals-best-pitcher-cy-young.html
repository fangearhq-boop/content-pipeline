1.45 ERA. 138 Strikeouts. Why Isn't Anyone Talking About Jacob Misiorowski?

By Ryan Calloway June 29, 2026

The Numbers First

Jacob Misiorowski is 24 years old. He pitches for the Washington Nationals. And through late June 2026, he leads all of Major League Baseball in ERA (1.45), WHIP (0.75), and strikeouts (138). He has also thrown a shutout, which is increasingly rare in the modern game and signals complete command over at least one opponent for an entire nine innings.

Those numbers are not close calls. A 1.45 ERA is not a pace that has been common in recent baseball history — it represents a level of run prevention that only a handful of pitchers achieve even for a month, let alone a sustained first half of the season. A 0.75 WHIP means Misiorowski is allowing fewer than one baserunner per inning. A WHIP under 1.00 for a full season would be historically exceptional. He is on pace.

The Platform Problem

The reason Misiorowski is not dominating national baseball coverage is straightforward: he pitches for Washington. The Nationals are not in playoff contention. Their games appear on regional television and the streaming package. Their wins and losses do not affect any October race in any division. The sport's narrative engine runs on stakes, and right now the stakes that surround Misiorowski's starts are limited to the personal and statistical.

This has happened before. Some of the best pitching performances in recent decades unfolded on losing teams in smaller markets, and the pitchers were only fully appreciated when Cy Young voters caught up to the numbers at the end of the season. The voters who track statistics rather than highlight reels will find Misiorowski easily. The casual fan who watches playoff teams will not encounter him until the award is announced in November.

Why He's the Real Thing

Being the best pitcher in a non-contending market does not diminish the achievement. The hitters Misiorowski faces are the same hitters that Dodgers starters face, that Braves starters face, that every team in baseball has to navigate. The batters do not take it easy on the Nationals because the Nationals are out of it. They go up there trying to hit.

Leading the league in ERA, WHIP, and strikeouts simultaneously is a three-dimensional achievement that makes it nearly impossible to dismiss the season as the product of favorable conditions. Run prevention could theoretically be explained by excellent defense or extreme batted ball luck. Strikeout dominance reflects direct pitcher skill. Leading both at once says something real about what Misiorowski is doing on the mound.

The NL Cy Young betting markets have priced him at -140 — the clear frontrunner. Betting markets are not infallible, but they aggregate a lot of information and do not often price a pitcher as the frontrunner for the sport's top pitching award without statistical justification. The market is telling you what the highlight shows are not: Misiorowski is the best pitcher in the National League.

The Second-Half Question

The question that always follows a dominant first half from a young starting pitcher is whether the numbers can hold. Regression is real, and pitchers who run extreme ERA and WHIP totals in the first half often see those numbers drift toward normal range as opposing teams make adjustments, as fatigue accumulates, and as the sample size grows. Misiorowski is 24. He does not have a track record of sustaining this level of performance over a full season.

But the strikeout rate is the most encouraging indicator for second-half sustainability. Pitchers who miss bats at high rates tend to hold their ERA better than contact-management pitchers who rely on weak contact and excellent defense. Misiorowski's 138 strikeouts suggest he is earning his outs, not catching them.

What's Next

Misiorowski will make the All-Star team as one of the NL's pitching selections — his numbers make exclusion impossible. The Cy Young race will run through the second half, and if his numbers remain close to their current pace, November's award will go to a 24-year-old Nationals starter who spent the entire season being underrated. Keep watching. The numbers will eventually force the conversation, whether or not the platform catches up to them first.
