All-Star Phase 2 Is Live. You Have Until July 2 to Vote.

By Marcus Cole June 29, 2026

The Window Opens

Phase 2 of MLB All-Star voting opened today at noon ET. You have until July 2 at noon ET to vote at MLB.com/vote. After that, the ballots close and the full roster — including pitchers and reserves selected by the Commissioner's Office — gets announced on July 4 on FOX. The game itself is July 14.

Phase 2 is a reset. The votes from Phase 1 do not carry over. Every position starts fresh, with the ballot limited to the top two Phase 1 finishers at each spot — and the top six outfielders per league. Two players have already punched their tickets and won't appear on the Phase 2 ballot: they're going regardless of what happens in the next 72 hours.

The Locked-In Starters

Shohei Ohtani won the NL DH vote in Phase 1 with 3,341,257 votes. He is the automatic NL DH starter. This was not a surprise — Ohtani's combination of star power and legitimate MVP-caliber production makes him the default winner at whatever position he occupies. His NL MVP odds sit at -1600, which is about as locked-in as betting markets allow.

The more interesting Phase 1 result was Ernie Clement, who won the AL 2B vote with 3,232,932 votes and is now an automatic starter. Clement plays second base for a mid-market team without the New York or Los Angeles megaphone behind it. His Phase 1 victory is a testament to a fanbase that organized, voted, and delivered. In the All-Star era of social media campaigns and coordinated voting pushes, Clement's win is the model: identify the moment, activate the base, and follow through.

What Phase 2 Decides

Every other starting position on both rosters is up for grabs in the next 72 hours. The most closely contested races will play out across multiple positions, and in some cases the margin will be narrow enough that organized fan campaigns make the difference. That is not a flaw in the system — it is the system, and the teams with the most engaged fanbases have always understood how to use it.

The NL starting spots outside of Ohtani's DH spot will include multiple players from the Dodgers, Braves, and Brewers — the three strongest NL records heading into July. In the AL, the race for spots at first base, outfield, and the pitching staff will generate the most debate. The July 4 announcement will produce the usual mix of obvious selections, legitimate debates about who was left off, and one or two results that generate enough controversy to carry into the game week itself.

Why the Next 72 Hours Matter

MLB All-Star voting has always had a campaigning element, but Phase 2's concentrated timeline makes the intensity sharper. Unlike the weeks-long Phase 1 window, Phase 2 requires real-time engagement. Teams, players, and fanbases that show up consistently over three days get results. Those that rely on Phase 1 momentum without showing up for Phase 2 watch their candidates miss out.

For individual players, an All-Star selection is not just a résumé line. It affects awards voting at the end of the season, it affects perception, and it affects the biographical summary of a career. Players who go to the All-Star Game tend to be remembered as All-Stars. The ones who narrowly miss are forgotten by October.

What's Next

Voting closes July 2 at noon ET. The complete starting lineups and pitching selections are announced July 4 on FOX. Player and manager selections fill out the rosters in the days that follow. The All-Star Game itself is July 14. Between now and then, Phase 2 is the only thing that matters — and it lasts exactly 72 hours from the time the ballot opened today.
