### STORY 2: Aaron Judge Goes on the IL

**Long-Form Post**
The Yankees' injury situation just became significantly more serious.

Aaron Judge has been placed on the injured list with a stress fracture in his rib. He will miss significant time — no specific return date has been announced, and rib stress fractures are not the kind of injury that resolves on a two-week timeline.

This is the third Yankees regular to hit the IL in the past month. Austin Wells (catcher) went down June 6 with cervical headaches and neck stiffness. Giancarlo Stanton has been dealing with his own IL stint. Judge's placement means the Yankees are now playing without three core contributors simultaneously — and this comes one day after getting swept in four games by the Red Sox.

For the AL MVP race, the math changes immediately. Judge had been considered a strong candidate. With him out, Yordan Alvarez (+105 betting odds) and Bobby Witt Jr. — who just returned from his own MCL injury on June 28 — are the frontrunners. The AL MVP conversation for the second half of the season looks entirely different today than it did Saturday.

The Yankees will need to make decisions at the August 3 trade deadline about what their second half actually looks like. With a depleted roster, the calculus of being a buyer becomes more complicated. Watch for New York to target upgrades in specific spots — and watch for the AL East standings to become a conversation again in July.

### STORY 3: Jacob Misiorowski's Historic First Half

**Long-Form Post**
You should know who Jacob Misiorowski is. The fact that many people don't is entirely a function of where he pitches.

The 24-year-old Washington Nationals starter leads Major League Baseball in ERA (1.45), WHIP (0.75), and strikeouts (138) entering the final week of June. He's also responsible for the only shutout on that list of leading statistics. The NL Cy Young betting markets have him at -140 — the clear frontrunner heading into the second half.

The problem is that Misiorowski pitches for Washington. The Nationals are not in contention. They are not competing for a playoff spot. The games he pitches matter for his individual legacy but not for October baseball. And in a sport where the narrative engine runs on October stakes, a dominant pitcher on a non-contending team gets a fraction of the coverage he would receive if he were doing these things in Atlanta or Los Angeles or New York.

That does not make the numbers less real. A 1.45 ERA is elite by any historical standard. A 0.75 WHIP means fewer than one baserunner per inning. 138 strikeouts in late June is a pace that, maintained over a full season, would produce one of the best strikeout totals in recent history.

Misiorowski will make the All-Star team. He should win the Cy Young if these numbers hold through September. He deserves the coverage that his performance has earned — even if the team behind him doesn't.

### STORY 4: Red Sox Sweep Yankees — 4-Game Series

**Long-Form Post**
The Boston Red Sox just sent a message to the AL East.

Jarren Duran singled home the winning run in the 10th inning on Sunday, capping a 5-4 Red Sox win and a four-game sweep of the New York Yankees. Boston took all four games of the series — and they did it against a Yankees team that entered with multiple regulars already on the injured list, and will leave with Aaron Judge now joining that list.

A four-game sweep of the Yankees is not a small thing. Even a diminished Yankees team has enough starting pitching and depth that you don't just run through them without competing. Boston earned this. Duran's walk-off was the exclamation point on a week that has the Red Sox very much in the AL East conversation.

The AL East standings entering July will show a race that is tighter than it seemed a week ago. The Rays have been strong all season. The Yankees are now managing roster disruptions that could take weeks to resolve. And a Red Sox offense led by Duran is showing that Boston's second-half push is real.

The question from here: is Boston a buyer at the August 3 deadline, or does the organization see this as a window-opening season rather than a window-now season? The answer to that question will be determined by where they are in the standings when the deadline arrives. After sweeping the Yankees, they're a lot closer to the race than anyone expected.

### STORY 5: Trade Deadline — Arraez at 90%

**Long-Form Post**
The trade deadline market is taking shape, and the names go beyond Tarik Skubal.

Luis Arraez now sits at 90 percent trade probability — the highest of any player on the market. Skubal remains at 85 percent with the Tigers holding out for a CC Sabathia-level package. Arraez's profile is different from Skubal's but equally compelling for contenders: he is the best contact hitter in baseball, and his defense at second base has improved to the point where his value is no longer solely offensive.

For teams looking to add without surrendering premium starting pitching prospects, Arraez is the most appealing name on the board. A lineup that adds Arraez's .330-plus average and on-base skills immediately becomes more patient, harder to retire, and more consistent in high-leverage situations.

The market beyond those two names is also worth watching. Oakland is under a 5-day window to find a trade partner for Brett Harris before he would head to outright waivers. Philadelphia is actively shopping for outfield help. The teams with genuine contending rosters are already having conversations, and with 23 of 30 MLB teams either leading their division or within four games of a playoff spot, the buyer pool is wider than most deadlines in recent memory.

August 3 is 35 days away. The real conversations have already started.
