Broken Jaw, Seven Fractures, Still Managing — Craig Albernaz Is a Different Kind of Manager

By Ryan Calloway April 15, 2026

How It Happened

During Monday's Baltimore Orioles game against the Arizona Diamondbacks at Camden Yards, Jeremiah Jackson hit a line drive off the bat at 70.6 miles per hour. The ball traveled toward the Orioles dugout and struck Craig Albernaz — Baltimore's first-year manager — directly in the right cheek. He was standing on the top step of the dugout, in the space before the protective netting begins. He went down, was evaluated by the training staff, and returned to the dugout to finish managing the game. Baltimore beat Arizona 9-7.

The next day, medical evaluation confirmed the damage: a broken jaw and seven fractures in the right cheek. No surgery was required, and his jaw did not need to be wired shut. He is on a baby-food and liquid diet. He returned to manage that day's game, too. Stood in the same spot.

The Part That Gets You

Baseball has a long tradition of managers who project toughness onto their teams. The ones who take the mound to argue a call until ejection, who step in front of a confrontation in the dugout, who show their players that authority comes with accountability. Albernaz added something different to that tradition Monday: he walked back to the same step where he was hurt and stood there.

That is not bravado for the cameras. That is a manager who understands that where he stands matters to the players watching him. A manager who retreats after an injury sends one message. A manager who plants his feet in the same spot sends a completely different one. The Orioles' clubhouse saw both choices and knows which one Albernaz made.

The Medical Reality

Seven facial fractures is not a minor injury. The jaw involves muscles connected to chewing, speaking, and breathing mechanics. A break without surgical wiring means the bone can heal with controlled movement, but it's painful and limiting. Liquid and baby-food diets are the protocol for a reason. Albernaz is managing a big league team while on a nutrition plan designed for recovery from facial surgery.

He could have stepped back and appointed a bench coach to handle games while he recovered. Nobody would have questioned that decision. He made the opposite choice, and his players will remember it when the summer gets long.

What's Next

Albernaz is expected to continue managing the Orioles through his recovery, with no timetable given for any roster changes or temporary arrangements. Baltimore is in the early stages of what could be a competitive AL East season, and the manager who came back the next day after a broken jaw is the one leading them into it. Watch for the Orioles' clubhouse culture to reflect that energy as the season unfolds.
