Jackie Robinson Day 2026 — The 79th Anniversary and Why No. 42 Still Matters

By Marcus Cole April 15, 2026

Seventy-Nine Years Ago Today

On April 15, 1947, Jackie Robinson stepped onto Ebbets Field in Brooklyn wearing a Dodgers uniform and changed baseball. He was 28 years old, a four-sport athlete from Pasadena who had already served in the Army and earned a law degree from UCLA. The hostility he faced that first season from opponents, fans in certain cities, and occasionally even his own teammates tested something most people would not have been able to withstand. Robinson played through it and won Rookie of the Year.

In the 11 seasons that followed, he won a batting title, led the National League in stolen bases twice, made six All-Star teams, and was named MVP in 1949. He did all of it under conditions that had no precedent in professional sports. In 1997, MLB retired his number across the entire league. In 2004, April 15 became Jackie Robinson Day, and every player in the sport began wearing No. 42 to honor him. Today is the 79th year of that legacy.

Tonight's Game Carries Special Weight

The most fitting Jackie Robinson Day matchup in 2026: the Los Angeles Dodgers host the New York Mets at Dodger Stadium exclusively on ESPN at 10 PM ET. The Dodgers are the franchise that signed Robinson, the organization whose willingness to break baseball's color barrier under Branch Rickey is now inseparable from the history of the sport. Playing on April 15 at Dodger Stadium means something different than it does at any other park in the country.

Joe Buck calls the game, with Ron Darling and Orel Hershiser providing analysis. ESPN is also launching 1080p HDR broadcast capability for this game, marking the first MLB broadcast in that format. The Dodgers have been 17-4 on April 15 since the holiday became a league-wide event in 2004. That number tends to get written off as coincidence. But the Dodgers historically show up for this day.

What No. 42 Means in 2026

The players wearing Robinson's number today span 30 teams, every position, and dozens of countries. Robinson's barrier-breaking happened in an America that formally segregated its institutions. The major leagues were not an exception. He made them one. The uniform number shared across the sport today is baseball's way of acknowledging that a single person's courage changed what the sport is.

This year's celebration also includes a game at the Jackie Robinson Training Complex in Vero Beach, Florida, where Robinson trained with the Dodgers organization. The Minor League game connects directly to the origins of the story, at the grounds where it took shape.
