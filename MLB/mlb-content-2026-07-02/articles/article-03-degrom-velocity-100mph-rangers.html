The Velocity Is Back: deGrom Hits 100 MPH Again and the Rangers Have a Second-Half Weapon

By Ryan Calloway July 2, 2026

What Happened in Cleveland

Jacob deGrom started Tuesday in Progressive Field against the Cleveland Guardians and threw five pitches above 100 miles per hour. His top reading was 100.5 mph — the fastest pitch he had thrown since April 23, 2023. That date matters because of what came after it: a string of arm-related injuries, stints on the injured list, and a long stretch during which the velocity that made deGrom one of the most intimidating starters of his era was present only in glimpses or not at all.

On July 1, it was back in the way it was before. Five pitches above 100 in a single start is not an anomaly from an adrenaline spike. It is a sustained effort at that velocity threshold over the course of an outing that lasted seven innings.

The Full Start

deGrom allowed a two-run home run to Cleveland's Kyle Manzardo in the first inning, but then shut the door. From that point on, he retired 18 of the next 19 batters. Final line: 7 innings, 2 earned runs, 4 hits, 0 walks, 9 strikeouts. The Rangers won 4-2.

His 2026 season to this point: 7-5, 3.48 ERA, 0.99 WHIP, 115 strikeouts. In June he went 4-0. In his last seven starts, he has pitched at least six innings in each and allowed two earned runs or fewer in five of them. The Rangers have won four of his last five starts. He earned his 100th career win on June 1. What was unclear entering July was whether the underlying velocity indicated a return to his pre-injury form or simply a pitcher working through a functional version of good. The July 1 numbers answered that question.

What the Velocity Means for the Rangers

Texas does not need to make a trade to improve its starting rotation. This is a significant point heading into the August 3 deadline, because every contending team in baseball has a rotation question somewhere. Teams that answer that question at the deadline give up prospects or payroll to do it. The Rangers are watching deGrom throw 100 mph in the seventh inning of a mid-week start in Cleveland and getting the same answer for free.

A pitcher with deGrom's track record, throwing at his previous velocity level, and building momentum into the second half of a playoff-relevant season is not a trade deadline target — he is the reason a team does not need one. Texas's path to a postseason run in 2026 runs directly through how much of this version of deGrom shows up between now and October.

What's Next

The question now is whether the July 1 velocity holds in his next start and the ones after it. One game at 100+ mph is a data point. Three or four in sequence is a trend that changes how opposing managers and hitters prepare for a team's rotation. If the velocity is back as a baseline rather than a peak, the Rangers are a more dangerous team in the second half than they were in the first. That conversation starts in his next scheduled start.
