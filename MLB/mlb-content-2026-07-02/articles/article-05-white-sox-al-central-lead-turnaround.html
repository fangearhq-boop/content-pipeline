From 41-121 to AL Central Leaders: The White Sox Turnaround Nobody Predicted

By Jake Torres July 2, 2026

The Premise Requires a Second Look

The Chicago White Sox are leading the American League Central. If you missed that sentence, read it again. The White Sox — the team that went 41-121 in the 2024 season, one of the worst records in modern major league history — are approximately 41-37 through the first three months of 2026 and hold first place in the AL Central over the Cleveland Guardians by approximately one game.

There is no precedent in recent memory for a turnaround at this velocity. The 2024 White Sox were not merely bad in a normal way. They were historically bad, tracking toward a loss total that prompted serious conversation about whether the organization was capable of competing in any reasonable timeframe. Two years later, they are in first place in July.

Murakami and the Power That Nobody Expected

Munetaka Murakami is part of the explanation. The international signing from Japan hit seven home runs in his first 21 MLB games — more than any Japanese-born player in major league history through that same span, surpassing the five home runs Shohei Ohtani managed through his first 21 games. The seven-home-run pace through 21 games is historically unusual by any standard, not just the international player comparison.

Murakami brings a profile that was familiar to Japanese professional baseball fans — a left-handed slugger with elite plate discipline and plus raw power — but translating that to MLB pitching at the scale of 21 games was not guaranteed. The early returns suggest the power plays at the major league level. Whether the strikeout rate and pitch recognition hold as pitchers study him more thoroughly through the second half remains the key question for his 2026 arc.

The Division Race

The Cleveland Guardians are approximately 41-39, one game behind the White Sox in a race that has been competitive in both directions throughout the season. The two teams recently split a key series — Chicago won the first two games, including a walk-off 6-5 victory, before Cleveland took the finale in extra innings 4-3. Neither team has been able to pull away cleanly.

The Tigers' presence in the division adds an interesting wrinkle. If Detroit continues to pursue a trade of Tarik Skubal — their best pitcher and a potential free agent at the deadline — it likely signals that the Tigers are not buying in as buyers. A non-buyer in the AL Central means the White Sox and Guardians face a reduced intra-division threat from their biggest rival. The standings may tighten or loosen by week, but the two-team race framing is accurate for now.

What's Next

The second half of the MLB season begins after the All-Star break on July 17. The White Sox will carry their AL Central lead into the break and resume with 80-some games remaining to hold it. For a franchise that spent much of the past five years rebuilding under circumstances that tested fan patience to its limit, first place in July is not the destination — it is a checkpoint on a race that is far from over. It is also a story that casual baseball fans have largely not discovered yet. The second half, and what the trade deadline brings, will determine whether they need to.
