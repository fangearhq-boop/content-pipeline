Cubs 23, Padres 3: Swanson's 8 RBI From the 9-Hole and Suzuki's 100th Homer Define a Historic Night

By Marcus Cole July 2, 2026

The Box Score That Felt Like a Mistake

The final from Wrigley Field on July 1 was 23-3 in favor of the Cubs — a margin so lopsided it prompted a double-take on any phone screen displaying it. The Cubs (49-38) beat the San Diego Padres (43-42) by 20 runs, extending their win streak to five games and completing a series sweep in a fashion that bore no resemblance to a close competitive baseball series.

Inside the scoreline were two moments worth separating from the general mayhem. Dansby Swanson hit three home runs, including a grand slam, and drove in eight runs while batting ninth. Seiya Suzuki hit his 100th career major league home run. Each was a distinct story. Together, they turned a lopsided game into a night Wrigley will reference for a while.

Swanson's 8 RBI From the Ninth Spot

What Dansby Swanson did on July 1 occupies a very specific place in baseball history. Eight RBIs from the ninth spot in a lineup is the second most ever recorded by any player batting in that position in major league history. The ninth slot in a batting order is, by design, where a manager places the weakest offensive option in a lineup. It is a spot built around getting the pitcher out of the way — or, in a designated-hitter era, cycling back to the top of a lineup without much damage. Eight RBIs from that position, in a single game, is not supposed to happen.

Swanson had also homered in the game prior to July 1, making him the first Cubs player to hit multiple home runs in back-to-back games since Patrick Wisdom did it in August 2021. The three-homer game included a grand slam and produced an eight-RBI night that would have been the headline of a normal game. It was one of several headlines on July 1.

Suzuki's 100th Career Homer

Seiya Suzuki hit his 100th career MLB home run on July 1, becoming the fourth Japanese-born player in major league history to reach that milestone. He joins Shohei Ohtani, Hideki Matsui, and Ichiro Suzuki in that group — four players representing an era when Japanese baseball exports began producing power numbers alongside the strikeout totals and contact rates that first defined the pipeline.

The milestone has both cultural weight and baseball weight. Suzuki's career arc with the Cubs has included stretches of high production and stretches of durability questions, but the 100-homer mark confirms a level of sustained offensive output over a meaningful MLB sample that not every international arrival reaches.

The Rest of the Lineup

The Cubs did not stop at Swanson and Suzuki. Michael Conforto, Pete Crow-Armstrong, and Michael Busch also homered, giving Chicago eight home runs in the game — tying the franchise record for a single contest. The 20-run winning margin was the largest in a Cubs home game since 1977. Twenty-three runs at Wrigley Field in one afternoon is not the kind of thing that happens at random. The Cubs entered July showing every sign of a team that has figured something out offensively that their opponents have not.

What's Next

At 49-38, the Cubs sit in a favorable position heading into the second half. The win streak is five games. The sweep of San Diego — a team with postseason aspirations of its own — is the kind of series result that changes perception of where a team stands. With All-Star rosters being announced July 6 and the trade deadline arriving August 3, the Cubs' offensive production tonight is a reminder to any team considering a move that Chicago belongs in the conversation about second-half threats in the National League.
