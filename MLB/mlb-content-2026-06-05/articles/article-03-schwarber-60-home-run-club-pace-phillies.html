Schwarber Is On Pace for 66 Home Runs — and the 60-HR Club Has Only Three Members

By Ryan Calloway June 5, 2026

The Numbers Demand Attention

Kyle Schwarber has 22 home runs. He reached 20 on May 15, the earliest date in Major League Baseball history that any player has hit that milestone. Through 56 games, he is on pace for approximately 66 home runs over a full season. Those are not projections from an optimistic fan — those are the math of sustained excellence.

The reason those numbers matter beyond their face value is the context they sit inside. The 60-home run club has three members. Barry Bonds holds the all-time record with 73 home runs in 2001. Mark McGwire hit 70 in 1998 and 65 in 1999. Sammy Sosa hit 66 in 1998, 64 in 2001, and 63 in 1999. That is the complete list. Seven player-seasons across three players. Every other home run season in history falls below that threshold.

Schwarber is on a pace, in June, that would put him inside that club.

What Makes This Season Different

Schwarber is not a new power source. He has hit 50-plus home runs before and is one of the most consistent left-handed sluggers of his generation. But 2026 has taken his game to a different level. The pace he set through May — including the record-earliest 20th home run — demonstrates something beyond the ordinary hot streak.

His approach at the plate has been disciplined alongside the power. The combination of volume and efficiency is what separates a legitimate 60-HR candidate from someone riding a hot first two months. Schwarber has shown both.

What It Would Take

Sustaining a 60-HR pace through September requires near-perfect health and consistent production across the second half of the season. Pitchers adjust. Teams shift their approach against high-profile home run threats. The physical and mental grind of pursuing a record that only three players have ever reached is itself a challenge unlike anything a player faces in a normal season.

Bonds, McGwire, and Sosa all entered their record seasons already established as elite power hitters. Schwarber arrives at this conversation from the same starting point. Whether he can sustain it is what the rest of 2026 will determine. But right now, in early June, the conversation is not premature. It is earned.

What's Next

Every Schwarber at-bat for the rest of the 2026 season will carry a different weight than it has before. The Phillies need him at the center of their lineup as they push toward the NL East standings and eventually October. What Schwarber is doing individually is the kind of story that transcends any single team — it is a record-book conversation that baseball fans will be tracking through September. Watch him swing. Every time.
