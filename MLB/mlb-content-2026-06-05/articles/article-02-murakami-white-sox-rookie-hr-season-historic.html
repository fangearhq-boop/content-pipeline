Murakami's Historic Rookie HR Season Makes the White Sox Turnaround Story Real

By Jake Torres June 5, 2026

From 121 Losses to AL Rookie of the Month

The Chicago White Sox lost 121 games in 2024. That number was historic in the worst possible sense — one of the worst seasons a modern Major League Baseball franchise has ever produced. If you had told a White Sox fan last October that they would have one of the most exciting young sluggers in the AL in twelve months, they might have looked at you skeptically.

Munetaka Murakami has made believers out of all of them. The rookie won the American League Rookie of the Month award for May after hitting eight home runs in 26 games and posting a .556 slugging percentage. He tied the White Sox franchise record with home runs in five consecutive games. Through the first two months of the season, he has been among the AL leaders in home runs and is forcing daily conversations about what the White Sox turnaround could actually look like.

What the Numbers Show

Murakami's May line — .244/.382/.556 with eight home runs and 18 RBI — represents consistent power production from a player still adjusting to Major League pitching. His .382 on-base percentage demonstrates patience at the plate alongside the power, a combination that the most dangerous hitters in baseball maintain regardless of experience level.

The White Sox franchise record he tied — five consecutive games with a home run — is a detail that illustrates how focused his stretches of dominance have been. These are not solo shots scattered across forgettable at-bats. Murakami has been locked in for stretches of the season that have made him must-watch baseball.

The AL ROY Conversation

The AL Rookie of the Year race runs all season, and no award is decided in June. But Murakami has done more than put his name in the conversation — he has become the conversation. For any player to emerge as a viable AL ROY candidate from a team that was arguably the worst in baseball a year ago is a testament to the individual talent involved and the steps Chicago has taken to build around it.

The White Sox are not yet a playoff team. The rebuild is ongoing. But Murakami gives their fans a reason to pay attention every single night, and that matters when a franchise is in the process of rebuilding its identity.

What's Next

The AL Rookie of the Year race heads into June with Murakami as the frontrunner. The White Sox will continue building around their new centerpiece, and the question that has replaced "can we win games?" is now "how far can Murakami carry this team?" It is a much better question to be asking. The turnaround from 121 losses is not complete — but it is visible, and it has a face. Watch Murakami at the plate every night. The story is just getting started.
