Ben Brown's Quiet Takeover: Cubs Blank Braves 2-0 as Starter Earns His Rotation Spot

By Jake Torres | May 15, 2026

The Chicago Cubs spent the first weeks of 2026 using Ben Brown as a reliever. Twelve appearances out of the bullpen, modest usage, a pitcher being managed carefully as the organization figured out where he fit. Then the Cubs moved Brown into the rotation, and two starts later, they may have found one of the most important pieces of their season.

On Thursday night at Truist Park in Atlanta, Brown worked four innings against one of the National League's best lineups — one hit, zero runs, seven strikeouts in 65 pitches. Combined with his first start, he has now thrown eight innings as a starter this season without allowing an earned run. The Cubs won 2-0, snapped a four-game losing streak, and denied Atlanta a sweep.

What Brown Showed Thursday

Braves hitters rank among the more dangerous in the National League. They lead the major leagues in limiting runs allowed and are a complete team in every dimension. When Brown was effective Thursday it was not against a lineup of organizational filler — he was pitching against a team that will compete for the NL pennant.

The seven strikeouts in four innings represent an exceptional strikeout rate, achieved efficiently enough that Brown threw just 65 pitches before the Cubs shifted to their bullpen. That efficiency — dominant results with limited pitch counts — is the hallmark of a pitcher who can contribute multiple quality starts per week without overtaxing a staff.

Hoby Milner earned the win with two scoreless innings. Phil Maton and Jacob Webb each contributed clean frames. Daniel Palencia struck out two in the ninth for his third save. Brown's performance set the tone, and the bullpen delivered the frame.

Ian Happ and the Chop House Homer

Brown was the story, but Ian Happ provided the defining image of Thursday night. His solo home run leading off the eighth inning off Reynaldo López traveled 424 feet to right field and bounced off the side of the Chop House restaurant that sits beyond the right-field seats at Truist Park. A memorable home run in a memorable location.

The Cubs' first run arrived the harder way — through an infield error. With Ian Happ on first base, Seiya Suzuki hit a grounder to shortstop Ha-Seong Kim. Kim's throw home went over the head of second baseman Ozzie Albies while attempting to turn a double play, leaving runners on the corners. Matt Shaw's fielder's choice scored Happ. It was the kind of opportunistic run that good teams manufacture and bad defenses surrender.

What This Means for the Cubs

Chicago entered Thursday in the middle of a four-game losing streak that had eroded some of the goodwill accumulated from two separate 10-game winning streaks — the first since the 1955 Dodgers to do so in their first 40 decisions. A loss Thursday would have meant a sweep at the hands of the NL's best team and a slide that would have been difficult to absorb in the standings.

Instead, the Cubs avoided the sweep and move to 28-16. The Braves are approximately 30-14 — still the NL's best record and two games ahead of Chicago. But the series finale result matters: it tells the Braves that Chicago is not a team they can simply sweep off their schedule when they need to make a run. Brown's emergence makes that more true by the start.

Chicago continues the road trip Friday night against the White Sox in the Crosstown Classic. A team that just discovered a reliable front-of-rotation starter entering its rivalry series carries a different kind of confidence.

What's Next

Ben Brown's next start will be watched carefully. Eight consecutive scoreless innings as a starter — against major league competition — earns a real rotation slot and real expectations. The Cubs move to 28-16 and open Rivalry Weekend on Friday against the White Sox in the Crosstown Classic. If Brown continues the trend in his third start, the Cubs' rotation picture will look fundamentally different from what anyone assumed in March. And Atlanta, having split the series 2-1, will need to answer a question they didn't expect: the Cubs just showed they belong in the same conversation.
