Schwarber Hits 18th, Extends Streak to 6 — Don Mattingly's All-Time MLB Record in His Sights

By Marcus Cole | May 15, 2026

Kyle Schwarber is two games from breaking his manager's all-time record. Let that sentence sit for a moment.

On Thursday night in Boston, Schwarber launched a 417-foot, two-run home run off Tyler Samaniego in the eighth inning, giving the Philadelphia Phillies a 3-1 victory over the Red Sox. It was his 18th home run of the season — the most in all of Major League Baseball — and his sixth consecutive game with a homer. Seven home runs over that six-game stretch. A pace that makes even the most hardened baseball observers stop and do the math.

The math, right now, points toward something genuinely historic.

The Record and the Man Who Holds It

The all-time MLB record for consecutive games with a home run is 8, set by Don Mattingly of the New York Yankees in 1987. The same feat was later matched by Ken Griffey Jr. in 1993 and Barry Bonds in 2004. No player in baseball history has ever homered in more than 8 consecutive games.

Don Mattingly is currently the interim manager of the Philadelphia Phillies.

This is the kind of circumstance that baseball writers invent for fiction — and this one is real. Schwarber works for the man he is two games away from surpassing in the record books. Phillies fans have been buzzing about it all week, and after Thursday's 417-foot shot cleared the visiting bullpen in right field at Fenway Park, that conversation moved from interesting to urgent.

The Game: Luzardo, Schwarber, and a 3-1 Statement

Thursday's victory in Boston was constructed the way playoff teams win: a dominant starting pitching performance followed by a decisive late-inning blow. Jesus Luzardo worked six shutout innings for Philadelphia, allowing the Phillies to sit on a 0-0 tie through the middle of the game. Trea Turner led off the eighth with a single and Schwarber followed with his 18th home run to put the game away.

The home run shot traveled an estimated 417 feet — a towering drive that left no doubt. Samaniego took the loss, falling to 0-1. The win pushed the Phillies to their fifth consecutive series victory and extended their record under Mattingly to 12-4.

That number — 12-4 under Mattingly — is worth dwelling on. Before Mattingly took over as interim manager on April 28, the Phillies were 9-19. The transformation of this roster from a franchise in crisis to a Wild Card contender in a matter of weeks has been extraordinary, and Schwarber's home run surge has been the engine of it.

The Streak in Context

To homer in 6 consecutive games requires a combination of talent, timing, and fortune that is almost impossible to manufacture. Pitchers adjust. Bad weather causes games to be relocated. Slumps interrupt even the best hot streaks. The fact that Schwarber has maintained this pace across a full week of games speaks to the unusual quality of his current contact and swing mechanics.

He is hitting .336 during the streak and has collected seven home runs off a combination of fastballs and off-speed pitches from both left- and right-handed arms. This is not a hot streak built on favorable matchups — it is Schwarber at or near his peak, doing damage indiscriminately.

The Phillies play Friday night. If Schwarber goes deep, the record ties. Saturday after that, it falls. The entire baseball world will be watching, and the man who set the record will be standing in the dugout with a decision to make about the lineup card.

What's Next

Schwarber enters Friday needing one more home run to tie the all-time MLB record of 8 consecutive games — one away tonight, one more Saturday to break it outright. The Phillies (22-22) continue their road schedule with 12-4 now under Mattingly since April 28. Whether or not the streak reaches 8 or beyond, Schwarber's 18 home runs through mid-May already represent a pace that puts legitimate 50-HR territory in conversation. The story is just beginning to find its next chapter.
