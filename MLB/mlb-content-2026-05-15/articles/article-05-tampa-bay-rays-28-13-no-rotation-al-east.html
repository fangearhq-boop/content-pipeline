28-13 With No Rotation: How Tampa Bay Is Leading the AL East Anyway

By Jake Torres | May 15, 2026

Sometime in the last week of April, the Tampa Bay Rays lost their third starting pitcher of the 2026 season. Their ace, Drew Pepiot, had already undergone hip surgery and been ruled out for the year. Shane Matz was on the injured list with an elbow problem. Brent Boyle joined the IL not long after. Three starting pitchers — gone.

The Rays responded by winning games. More precisely, they responded by going 16-2 in their best recent stretch, and their overall record entered the second week of May at 28-13 — the best record in the American League East and one of the best in all of baseball.

How does a team without a conventional starting rotation lead its division in May? In Tampa Bay, the answer is the same as it always is: organizational depth, creative strategy, and a refusal to panic.

The Opener Revolution, Continued

The Rays were among the earliest teams to institutionalize the "opener" strategy — using a short reliever to begin the game and face the opponent's lineup once through, before turning to longer arms in the bullpen to finish the game. Other teams viewed this as a gimmick or a desperation measure when starters weren't available. Tampa Bay viewed it as a structural advantage.

In 2026, with three starters unavailable, the Rays are not using openers as a fallback — they have built their entire pitching schedule around it. The system works because Tampa Bay has spent years developing pitchers who thrive in flexible, high-leverage roles without being given traditional starter responsibilities. The organizational pipeline delivers arms who can execute two to three innings at maximum effort, and the Rays cycle through them without depleting any single arm's recovery time.

The Players Delivering Results

No opener strategy works without the position players delivering in close games. Taylor Walls provided one of the most memorable moments of Tampa Bay's recent run when his walk-off RBI single in the 10th inning gave the Rays a 7-6 victory over the Toronto Blue Jays. Walk-off wins compress the standings. They tell the rest of the division that losing to Tampa Bay is not an option even when the game goes to extra innings.

The Rays' position player group has been collectively efficient — not dominated by any single superstar but contributing enough run production through the middle of the lineup to win games where the pitching holds opponents to four or five runs. That is the kind of baseball Tampa Bay has always required, and this roster is delivering it.

The Organizational Model That Makes It Possible

What separates Tampa Bay from teams that panic when their rotation collapses is the depth model that general manager Peter Bendix and manager Kevin Cash have built over years. The Rays develop pitchers differently — they track spin rates and deception profiles at every level of the minor leagues, and they graduate arms who can contribute in high-leverage situations without a traditional development path through a five-man rotation.

When Pepiot went down, there was no organizational emergency, because the next seven arms were ready to be cycled in rotation. When Matz and Boyle followed, the Rays extended their bullpen-by-committee approach rather than reaching for a veteran stopgap on the trade market. The confidence required to make that call in mid-April — with a first-place record to protect — reflects an organization that genuinely believes in its infrastructure.

What's Next

Tampa Bay opens Rivalry Weekend tonight at home against the Miami Marlins (7:10 PM ET) — a game where the Rays are significant favorites and can extend their AL East lead. With Matz and Boyle expected to return at some point in the next month and the opener system continuing to produce wins in the meantime, the Rays' 28-13 record may represent a floor rather than a ceiling for 2026. The rest of the AL East, including the Yankees, cannot afford to dismiss a team that just went 16-2 without three starting pitchers. Tampa Bay is built for exactly this kind of adversity. They've been here before, and they just keep winning.
