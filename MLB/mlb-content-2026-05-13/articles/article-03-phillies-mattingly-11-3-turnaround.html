Mattingly's Phillies Are 11-3 Since Thomson Firing — One of the Best Managerial Turnarounds in Years

By Ryan Calloway | May 13, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

On April 28, 2026, the Philadelphia Phillies were 9-19, held the worst record in baseball, and had just fired Rob Thomson — a manager who had led them to back-to-back National League East titles and four consecutive playoff appearances.

Two weeks later, the Phillies are 11-3 under interim manager Don Mattingly. They've won three straight. They've won 11 of 14. Kyle Schwarber is hitting home runs at a rate that would have seemed absurd in April. Philadelphia is 20-22 overall and very much in the conversation for NL playoff positioning.

This is one of the better managerial turnarounds in recent baseball memory.

Why Thomson Was Let Go

Rob Thomson's tenure in Philadelphia was legitimately successful. He managed 355 wins in four-plus seasons, took the team to its first World Series since 2009 in 2022, and consistently put the Phillies in position to compete. When the 2026 season opened with the team going 9-19, president of baseball operations Dave Dombrowski made a difficult decision: the talent level didn't match the record, and the calculus demanded a change.

Don Mattingly, who had been serving as bench coach, was the choice to take over on an interim basis. At 65, Mattingly brings five years managing the Dodgers and seven managing the Marlins to a role that has a family dimension — his son Preston Mattingly is the Phillies' general manager. That dynamic raised eyebrows when it was first established, but Tuesday night's 2-1 win over Boston quiets most skeptics.

What Changed

The roster didn't change. The players are the same. What appears to have shifted is execution and energy — the often-intangible factors that player performance can mask. Schwarber's recent home run tear (17 HRs, ML-best) has been the most visible symptom of an offense that's finally playing to its capability, but the pitching has also stabilized under the new regime.

The Phillies haven't made any dramatic roster changes since the Thomson firing. The 9-19 record appears to have been a performance anomaly from a team that, at its best, belongs in the NL's upper echelon. Mattingly has simply gotten them to play closer to that level, one game at a time.

What's Next

Philadelphia is still four games under .500. Reaching true contender status will require a continued winning pace through May and June. But the trajectory matters more than the snapshot right now. A team that seemed to be drifting toward an early exit from the playoff race has righted itself in a two-week window.

The Phillies continue their series against the Red Sox on Wednesday. If Schwarber goes deep again and they win a fourth straight game, the turnaround story will become impossible to ignore. For a franchise that was written off in late April, that's a remarkable place to be in May.
