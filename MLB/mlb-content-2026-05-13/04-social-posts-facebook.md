#### Long-Form Post -- 8:00 AM ET

Kyle Schwarber is the new face of the MLB home run race — and it happened fast.

The Judge-Murakami race captivated early in the season. But the home run race just got a third competitor — and Schwarber might be peaking at the right time.

Don Mattingly, serving as bench coach, was named interim manager. The 65-year-old has a long managerial resume — five years with the Dodgers, seven with the Marlins — and now, in a twist that would make any baseball family proud, is managing a team whose GM is his own son, Preston Mattingly.

What happened next was remarkable. The Phillies are 11-3 under Mattingly. That means in 14 games under new leadership, the team has played like a postseason contender. The 9-19 roster that looked broken hasn't been dramatically changed — the talent has simply started performing. And no one more so than Kyle Schwarber, whose 17-HR, franchise-record-tying run has powered the offense into gear.

Philadelphia is now 20-22. Still under .500. But the trajectory is unmistakable. Mattingly has them believing.

Rob Thomson built something real in Philadelphia over four-plus years. But the Phillies couldn't afford to wait out the slow start. Now, two weeks later, it looks like the right call.

### STORY 5: Elly De La Cruz Mid-May Check-In

#### Long-Form Post -- 6:00 PM ET

The 24-year-old Reds shortstop entered Wednesday hitting .288 with 10 home runs, 29 runs scored, 29 RBI, and 9 stolen bases. He leads all shortstops in Baseball-Reference WAR. And the advanced metrics tell an even better story: a 93.9 mph average exit velocity, 50.9% hard-hit rate, 15.5% barrel rate, and a .383 wOBA.

These numbers — power and speed at that scale, from a shortstop — are rare. De La Cruz became the first shortstop since 1900 to hit 5 or more home runs and steal 5 or more bases in a team's first 16 games of a season. That kind of combination simply doesn't exist at the position.

The narrative around the 2026 season has been dominated by the Judge-Murakami HR race, Schwarber's recent surge, and the Braves' dominant pitching. De La Cruz tends to work in a different lane — the Cincinnati Reds aren't in the national conversation the way the Braves or Yankees are — but the numbers demand attention.

Halfway through May, it's worth asking: is Elly De La Cruz the best shortstop in baseball right now? By WAR, the answer is yes. By every measurable power-speed metric, the answer is also yes.

The Reds might not make the playoffs. But De La Cruz is making an MVP-caliber argument whether anyone's watching or not.
