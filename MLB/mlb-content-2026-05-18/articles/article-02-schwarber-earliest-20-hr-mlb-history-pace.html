Schwarber Already Broke a 25-Year Record. Now His Pace Points Toward Bonds Territory.

By Jake Torres | May 18, 2026

Whatever happens for the rest of Kyle Schwarber's 2026 season, this part is already in the books: On May 15, he hit his 20th home run, making him the first player in Major League Baseball history to reach that milestone that early on the calendar. The previous record belonged to Luis Gonzalez, who hit his 20th on May 17, 2001, with the Arizona Diamondbacks. Schwarber broke it by two days.

A record has been set. That matters regardless of what comes next.

But what comes next is where the conversation gets genuinely interesting. At his current pace, Schwarber is on track for approximately 67 home runs in the 2026 season. Barry Bonds holds the all-time single-season record with 73, set in that same 2001 season when Gonzalez was the comparison point. If those two numbers sound close to each other, that's because they are. The math, as of May 18, is pointing somewhere that has not been discussed in a generation.

The 7-Game Streak and What It Means

The seven-game consecutive home run streak that drove most of the recent conversation ended before it could reach eight — the threshold that would have tied Dale Long (1956), Don Mattingly (1987), and Ken Griffey Jr. (1993) for the all-time MLB record. The streak is over. The pace story is not.

Schwarber still leads all of baseball with 20-plus home runs entering Monday. His closest competitor, Aaron Judge, has 16. The four-home-run gap represents a significant lead this early in the season. The 20-HR-by-May-15 record is his. The question now is whether the rest of the year will be about chasing something even bigger.

One layer that makes this story richer: Don Mattingly, who held the consecutive-game HR record Schwarber was pursuing, is now the Phillies' interim manager. Mattingly took over a team that was 9-19 and has gone 14-4 since, improving Philadelphia's record to 23-23. The manager and the slugger are building something in Philadelphia, and both of their names are in the record books now.

The Pace and the Projection

20 home runs in roughly 44 games extrapolates to approximately 67 over 162. That is a projection, not a prediction. Schwarber is not going to hit a home run in every 2.2 games for the rest of the year. Players slow down. Pitchers adjust. Injuries happen.

But the projection is not the point. The point is that no player since Bonds has had a May this historically productive in terms of home run pace. Even acknowledging the normal regression to come, Schwarber is in a place that has not been discussed in the sport since 2001. If he finishes the year with 45 home runs, the record he already set is still there. If he finishes with 55 or 60, the conversation becomes something else entirely.

What's Next

The Phillies open a series at home against the Cincinnati Reds on Monday night, with Schwarber in the middle of their lineup. The record is set. The pace is documented. The question for the rest of the 2026 season is whether Schwarber can stay healthy, stay productive, and give baseball fans a September that feels worth paying close attention to. The answer, right now, appears to be yes.
