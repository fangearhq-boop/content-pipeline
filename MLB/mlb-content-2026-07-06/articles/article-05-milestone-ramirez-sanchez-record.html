History in Progress: Ramírez Chases the 300/300 Club, Sanchez Breaks a 115-Year Record

By Jake Torres

July 6, 2026

Two milestones are happening right now, in real time, and they are not getting enough attention. One involves a third baseman nine home runs away from one of the most exclusive designations in baseball history. The other involves a starting pitcher who just broke a franchise record that had stood for 115 years.

Ramírez: Nine Home Runs From Immortality

José Ramírez hit his 300th career stolen base earlier this month. The Cleveland Guardians third baseman now has 291 career home runs. Do the math: he needs nine more to reach the 300 HR / 300 SB club — one of the most exclusive benchmarks in baseball.

Only four players in the history of the sport have ever done it: Barry Bonds, Alex Rodriguez, Willie Mays, and André Dawson. That is not a list that gets new entries often. Ramírez is 31 years old, still producing at an elite level, and actively pursuing it this season. He is not fading toward the milestone. He is approaching it at full speed.

There is more. Ramírez also became the first player in Cleveland Guardians franchise history to hit a home run against all 29 other MLB teams. That is a quieter record, but it says something about durability, longevity, and consistency that is hard to quantify any other way. He has done damage everywhere, against everyone, across his entire career.

The best argument for Ramírez's place in the conversation about the greatest third basemen of his generation is not one statistic. It is the accumulation: All-Star selections, the stolen base totals, the home run pace, and now the approaching threshold that only four players have ever cleared. Nine home runs. Watch this space.

Sanchez: A Name Next to Grover Cleveland Alexander

Cristopher Sanchez extended his consecutive scoreless innings streak to 41 and two-thirds this week. That number broke a Philadelphia Phillies franchise record that had stood since 1911 — held by Grover Cleveland Alexander, one of the greatest pitchers in baseball history.

Alexander pitched at the turn of the 20th century and is one of just a handful of pitchers who can credibly be placed in the conversation with the all-time greats. His record stood for 115 years. Sanchez broke it in 2026.

Sanchez is having the kind of season that earns a pitcher his first All-Star selection — which is exactly what happened. His ERA sits at 2.00 on the year over 117 innings pitched. He is the kind of starting pitcher that makes a rotation look like something opponents dread preparing for. His streak, and the history attached to it, will stay in the record books long after this season ends.

What's Next

Ramírez carries his chase into the All-Star break and the second half. Nine home runs is not a long road at his production level — this could close quickly. Sanchez takes his streak and his first All-Star appearance to Philadelphia next week, pitching in his home park in the Midsummer Classic. Both stories are worth following for the rest of the season. Baseball's long calendar makes milestones feel distant until they aren't. These two are very close.
