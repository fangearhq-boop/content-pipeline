The 2026 MLB All-Snub Team: Brice Turang, Murakami, and Sonny Gray Deserved Better

By Jake Torres

July 6, 2026

Every year, the All-Star Game produces a roster — and a counter-roster. The players who should have made it. The names that analytics say belong in Philadelphia and got left home anyway.

The 2026 version is a good one. Here are the cases that are hardest to explain.

Brice Turang, Brewers, 2B — The Statistical Snub

There is no clean way to justify leaving Brice Turang off the NL roster. The Brewers second baseman ranks No. 7 in the National League in bWAR among all position players. He is, by the most comprehensive measure of player value, one of the seven most valuable offensive players in the league. That is All-Star territory. That is not a close call.

Turang is not a household name in the same way some of the players who made the roster are. He plays in Milwaukee. He doesn't have massive social media numbers or a marquee contract. But he plays every day, he produces, and his WAR doesn't care about market size. He is the most egregious omission of 2026, and multiple analytics outlets agree.

Munetaka Murakami, White Sox, 3B/DH — Twenty Home Runs in 57 Games

Munetaka Murakami has 20 home runs in 57 games. That is historic pace for a player in his first season in Major League Baseball. He set a new record for most home runs by a Japanese-born player in his first 21 MLB games — seven, surpassing Shohei Ohtani's five. He is playing for the first-place Chicago White Sox, who went 41-121 in 2024 and are now 47-42 and one of baseball's best stories.

He was not selected for the American League All-Star team. Multiple sources immediately called him the top AL snub. His absence from the roster while playing in the middle of the year's biggest turnaround story will be debated for the rest of the season.

Sonny Gray, Red Sox, SP — Nine Wins and a 2.69 ERA

Sonny Gray is 9-1 with a 2.69 ERA in 15 starts. If someone handed you that line and said "guess which league this pitcher made the All-Star team in," the answer wouldn't be "neither." Gray is pitching as well as almost anyone in the American League this season. The Red Sox have been a second-half contender behind his work. He was not selected for the AL pitching staff.

This one is harder to explain than the Turang snub because wins and ERA are the kinds of numbers that casual fans notice. Gray is 9-1. He is among the top three AL starters by ERA. And he is not going to Philadelphia.

Zack Wheeler, Phillies, SP — Left Off in His Own Park

Wheeler pitches for the Philadelphia Phillies. The All-Star Game is at Citizens Bank Park. He is not in the NL pitching staff. His teammate Cristopher Sanchez, who earned his spot, made the roster. Wheeler, who has been producing solid numbers for years and has All-Star history of his own, watches this one from home.

The Phillies get five All-Stars, including a Commissioner's Legend Pick. Wheeler, the veteran ace who has anchored their rotation, is not one of them.

Willson Contreras, Cardinals, C

The most commonly cited positional snub on the NL side is Cardinals catcher Willson Contreras. Multiple analytics outlets flagged his omission as among the most glaring positional gaps on the NL roster. The Cardinals have had an inconsistent season, but Contreras's individual numbers haven't reflected the team's struggles.

What's Next

The All-Star Final Vote is the one remaining way for a snubbed player to reach Philadelphia. One player from each league will be added via the Final Vote — a fan-driven online election. Turang and Gray are the two most compelling cases to watch from their respective leagues. The Final Vote winner is announced before the Derby on July 13.
