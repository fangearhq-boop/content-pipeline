### STORY 2: The 2026 All-Star Snubs

#### Facebook Long-Form Post

Every All-Star Game has them. Here are the best players not going to Philadelphia.

**Brice Turang, Brewers, 2B:** The statistical case is airtight. Turang ranks No. 7 in NL bWAR among all position players and No. 8 in fWAR. He's been one of the most consistently valuable players in the NL this season — and he's not going to the All-Star Game. Every metric analysis of the 2026 rosters calls him the most egregious omission.

**Munetaka Murakami, White Sox, 3B/DH:** The man has 20 home runs in 57 games. That is historic production from a player doing it for the first time in the American League. He set the record for most home runs by a Japanese-born player in his first 21 MLB games (7, surpassing Shohei Ohtani's 5). He was not selected for the AL roster, despite playing for the first-place White Sox. Make that make sense.

**Sonny Gray, Red Sox, SP:** Gray is 9-1 with a 2.69 ERA in 15 starts. There is no rational case for leaving him off the AL pitching staff. And yet here we are. Gray will have to watch from home while several pitchers with worse numbers make the trip to Citizens Bank Park.

**Zack Wheeler, Phillies, SP:** Wheeler pitches for the Phillies — the team hosting the All-Star Game. He is not going as an All-Star. Meanwhile, his teammate Cristopher Sanchez (who earned it) made the NL roster. The irony is thick.

**Willson Contreras, Cardinals, C:** Multiple analytics outlets call him a positional snub. His numbers warrant consideration. He's not going.

The All-Star Game is one of baseball's best traditions, and the debates it produces each year are part of what makes it great. These five players gave their fans plenty to argue about this year.

### STORY 3: Home Run Derby — Field Taking Shape for Netflix July 13

#### Facebook Long-Form Post

The 2026 Home Run Derby is one week away, and the field is starting to come into focus.

The one confirmed participant: Junior Caminero of the Tampa Bay Rays. The 22-year-old third baseman was the 2025 runner-up and is back for more — hitting 28 home runs on the season and coming off a stretch where he hit seven in a single week. He announced his participation on Instagram. He is ready.

The player everyone wants to see: Kyle Schwarber. The Phillies first baseman leads MLB with 30 home runs and has more motivation than anyone — Citizens Bank Park, where he plays, is hosting the event. Schwarber said he would participate on two conditions: if he is healthy and if he makes the All-Star Game. He just made the All-Star Game. His back has been an issue, but the remaining barrier is health, and that picture could clear any day.

The notable absence: Aaron Judge. The Yankees outfielder was diagnosed with a stress fracture in his right first rib in June and is on the IL. He will not be at the Derby, and his All-Star return is uncertain. It is a real blow to the event's marquee factor — Judge is one of baseball's most compelling power hitters.

Also of interest: Ben Rice of the Yankees told reporters in June he wants to participate and has 22 home runs on the season to back it up. Yordan Alvarez opted out.

The format is new in 2026: instead of a timed round, hitters get 20 swings in Round 1 and 15 each in Rounds 2 and 3. Strategy matters in a way it didn't with the clock.

The Derby streams live on Netflix on Monday, July 13, at 8 p.m. ET from Citizens Bank Park.

Other teams circling: the Brewers, Cubs, Phillies, Padres, Rays, and Red Sox have all had varying levels of interest reported. Every one of them would benefit from adding a pitcher of Skubal's caliber.

The Tigers have covered themselves somewhat — they went 38-49 with Skubal, which means the record isn't an argument for contention. It's an argument for getting value now while the market is at its peak.

August 3. Mark the date. The Skubal era in Detroit is likely coming to an end in 28 days.

### STORY 5: Milestone Watch — Ramírez and Sanchez

#### Facebook Long-Form Post

Two milestones heading into All-Star Weekend deserve more attention than they're getting.

**José Ramírez is nine home runs away from baseball immortality.**

The Cleveland Guardians third baseman hit his 300th career stolen base earlier this month. He now has exactly 9 home runs to reach the 300 HR / 300 SB club — one of the most exclusive designations in baseball history. Only four players have ever done it: Barry Bonds, Alex Rodriguez, Willie Mays, and André Dawson. Ramírez is 31 years old and still producing at an elite level. He also became the first Guardians player in franchise history to hit a home run against all 29 other MLB teams. He is not a casual baseball statistic. He is a living argument that the best third baseman of his generation should have been in many more All-Star conversations.

**Cristopher Sanchez rewrote Phillies history this week.**

The Philadelphia starter extended his consecutive scoreless innings streak to 41⅔, breaking a franchise record that had stood for 115 years. The record was previously held by Grover Cleveland Alexander — a name that belongs in the same conversation as the greatest pitchers who ever threw a baseball. Sanchez broke it. In 2026. His first All-Star selection is the reward, and it is well earned.

Both stories are the kind that make baseball's long season worth following. They are happening right now, for anyone paying attention.
