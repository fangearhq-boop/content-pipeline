The Phillies Just Made MLB History — Three Consecutive Go-Ahead 9th-Inning Home Runs

By Marcus Cole | Ballpark Banter | June 27, 2026

Some records happen in categories nobody was tracking. The Phillies just created one.

Philadelphia became the first team in MLB history to hit a go-ahead home run in the ninth inning of three consecutive games. Not home runs that added to an existing lead. Not home runs that tied the game and sent it to extra innings. Home runs that took the lead, in the ninth, three games in a row.

That streak belongs entirely to them, and there is nothing else in the record books to compare it to.

What the Streak Looks Like Up Close

The ninth inning is a specific place in a baseball game. Managers have already made most of their moves. The best reliever is usually already on the mound. The opposing team's lineup has had a full look at the starting pitcher and has been managed through the middle innings to get to this point. Scoring in the ninth — on a lead-changing home run — requires everything to go exactly right at exactly the wrong time for the pitcher on the mound.

The Phillies did it three times in three games. That is not luck. That is a team that believes it can score in the ninth regardless of the situation, and that belief is justified by the roster.

Kyle Schwarber anchors the offense. He leads all of baseball with 29 home runs through the games of June 26 and has been the most consistent threat in the Phillies lineup for the past three weeks. When a team needs a home run in the ninth inning, Schwarber is exactly the player that offense is built around.

The Derby Question

The Home Run Derby is July 13 at Citizens Bank Park. The crowd for that event is going to be among the loudest in recent Derby history. Citizens Bank Park is a building that responds to home runs the way few other stadiums in the sport do, and an entire Derby night in front of that crowd is exactly the kind of setting that makes the event must-watch television.

Schwarber, who leads the majors in home runs and will be playing in his home park in front of his home crowd, has not committed to entering the Derby. The question of whether he participates will be one of the louder storylines of the next two weeks.

His argument for entering: 29 home runs, the best setting imaginable, and a three-game streak that has made his name the first one mentioned when anyone discusses the Phillies' offense. His argument against: the risk of a mechanical change mid-season, the fatigue factor, the possibility that the extra swings interfere with his production in the second half.

The history the Phillies just made does not require Schwarber to enter the Derby. But it makes the question harder to avoid.

What's Next

The Phillies enter this stretch of the season with the ninth-inning identity of a team that is always one swing away from changing a game. That reputation is built over time by exactly the kind of production they have demonstrated over these three games. Whether the streak continues is secondary to what it represents: a lineup constructed around the idea that the game is not over until it is over, and that the ninth inning is as good a time to win it as any other.
