Brewers Skid Hits Six Games — How the NL Central Race Changed in Two Weeks

By Marcus Cole | April 17, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

The Milwaukee Brewers entered the 2026 season with a roster built around depth and pitching — the kind of design that survives the inevitable early-season injuries. Two weeks in, the injuries have been neither small nor isolated. The result: a six-game losing streak, the longest Milwaukee has suffered since 2003, and a National League Central race that no longer looks the way anyone expected.

The injury report tells the story. Christian Yelich, Milwaukee's offensive anchor, went down with an adductor strain and is not expected back until mid-to-late May. Jackson Chourio, last year's breakout star whose age-21 season generated genuine excitement for the franchise's future, broke his hand and is targeting an early May return. Andrew Vaughn broke his hand as well, extending a run of remarkably bad luck concentrated in one team's roster. Starting pitcher Quinn Priester is sidelined with thoracic outlet syndrome, and Jared Koenig is dealing with an elbow issue.

Five key players out simultaneously, at different positions, from different injury events. The Brewers did not do anything wrong to earn this. They simply ran into the kind of April that every MLB team fears and few experience at this concentration.

The Division Pivots

While Milwaukee has been losing, the Pittsburgh Pirates and Cincinnati Reds have been winning. Both teams entered this week at 11-7, tied at the top of the NL Central — a result that would have surprised most preseason projections, which had the Brewers as favorites and the Pirates and Reds as mid-tier contenders at best.

Pittsburgh has benefited from unexpectedly strong pitching early, and Cincinnati's offense has run hot. Neither team has faced the same injury adversity as Milwaukee, and both have taken advantage of the Brewers' stumble by stringing together consistent wins during the same stretch.

The NL Central is now genuinely open. Three weeks ago, it was Milwaukee's division to lose. Today, it is a three-team race, and the team that was supposed to be running away from the field is the one with the most uncertainty around its roster.

The Comeback Timeline

The Brewers' recovery depends primarily on Chourio and Vaughn returning in early May as projected. Yelich's adductor timetable is longer — the mid-to-late May target means Milwaukee could be playing meaningful games without its best hitter for another four to six weeks. If Chourio and Vaughn come back on schedule and the current roster can minimize the damage in the interim, the Brewers should have the talent to compete in the second half of April and into May.

The risk is if the losses pile up so heavily before the roster recovers that the gap becomes insurmountable. The Pirates and Reds are not going to simply wait around for Milwaukee to get healthy. They are playing their own seasons, and every game Milwaukee drops now is a game they need to make up later.

What's Next

Watch Milwaukee's schedule over the next two weeks before Chourio's projected return. If the Brewers can stabilize at or around .500 baseball while depleted, they maintain realistic title contention when the roster returns to full strength. If the skid extends to 10 or more games, the psychological and standings damage could be difficult to reverse. Early May is the target date for the first real test of this team's resilience.
