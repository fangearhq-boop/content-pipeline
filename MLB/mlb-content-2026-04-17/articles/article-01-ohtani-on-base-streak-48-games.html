Ohtani's On-Base Streak Reaches 48 Games — Chasing Dodgers Franchise History

By Marcus Cole | April 17, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

Shohei Ohtani extended his on-base streak to 48 consecutive games as of April 14 — placing him third in Dodgers franchise history and continuing the most remarkable two-way performance in modern baseball. The streak, which dates back to August 23, 2025, has already toppled Ichiro Suzuki's Japanese-born MLB record (44 games, set in 2009) and passed former Dodger Ron Cey (47) in the franchise record books.

The Dodgers franchise leaders who remain ahead of Ohtani: Willie Keeler (50), Shawn Green (53), and Duke Snider (58). The all-time MLB record belongs to Ted Williams, who reached base in 84 consecutive games during the 1949 season.

For those unfamiliar with on-base streak definitions: it counts every game in which the player reaches base at least once — by hit, walk, hit-by-pitch, or error. It does not require a hit, but it requires meaningful production every single time a player dresses in uniform. Across 48 games at the major league level against the best competition on earth, Ohtani has not once gone without getting on base.

The Pitching Side

If the on-base streak is extraordinary, add this: Ohtani has not allowed a single earned run across his first two pitching starts of the 2026 season. Twelve innings pitched, 0.00 ERA. His most recent start came on April 16 — Jackie Robinson Day — when he struck out 10 Mets batters in six innings as the Dodgers completed a sweep.

There is no reasonable historical comparison for an individual player sustaining this level of contribution in both directions simultaneously at the major league level. The two-way career that Ohtani built in Japan, refined with the Angels, and is now executing at full performance with the Dodgers is something the sport had not seen before — and is still struggling to account for statistically.

On any given day when the Dodgers play, Ohtani might be the most important offensive player on the field, the most dominant starting pitcher, or both. On April 16, he was the dominant pitcher. In the 47 games before that, he was the unbeatable hitter.

The Record Trajectory

Getting from 48 to 50 would mean passing Willie Keeler to become the second-longest on-base streak in Dodgers history. Getting to 54 or beyond would make Ohtani the all-time franchise leader. Those milestones are within view — mathematically, at least — assuming health and continued elite production.

The more interesting question is how this compares to Ted Williams at 84 games. The gap between 48 and 84 is enormous. Williams' streak was the product of a season in which he hit .343 and walked 162 times. It is not a record likely to fall soon. But in a season where Ohtani is simultaneously pitching at a 0.00 ERA, nobody in baseball can be ruled out of doing the unreasonable.

What's Next

Every Dodgers game from here forward carries the weight of the streak. Ohtani's next pitching start will force the question again of whether he appears in the batting lineup. Watch how manager Dave Roberts manages the two-way workload as the streak extends further into franchise and national record territory.
