John Sterling Dies — Aaron Judge's Homer and Michael Kay's Tribute Honored a Yankees Legend

By Jake Torres | May 5, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

John Sterling passed away on Monday, May 5. Baseball lost one of its most distinctive voices — a man whose signature home run calls became part of the fabric of a franchise, a city, and four decades of baseball history.

And then, almost as if scripted, Aaron Judge walked to the plate in the bottom of the first inning at Yankee Stadium, with a runner on base and the Orioles on the mound, and hit a two-run home run — his 14th of the season. Yankees broadcaster Michael Kay paused, and honored his longtime partner with Sterling's legendary phrase: "THEEEE YANKEES WIN!"

It was the most perfect, painful, and beautiful moment baseball has produced this season.

A Voice Like No Other

John Sterling worked Yankees games for more than 35 years. His home run calls were unique to each player — a signature phrase crafted for every Yankee who sent a ball into the bleachers. Fans didn't just hear home runs; they heard Sterling's delight in them, his love of the game, and his genuine connection to the players he covered.

He had a polarizing broadcast style — effusive, theatrical, occasionally over-the-top — and that was precisely the point. Sterling made Yankees baseball feel larger than life on the radio. For millions of fans who listened in the car, on the porch, or through a small speaker in a kitchen, Sterling's voice was summer. It was baseball. It was New York.

The Yankees' Night

New York didn't just win Monday. They dominated. The Yankees rolled the Orioles 12-1, completing a four-game series sweep in which they outscored Baltimore by 29 total runs. Judge finished 2-for-4 with four RBI — the home run, a tribute. The stadium, for however briefly, carried the weight of something larger than a box score.

The Yankees are 24-11, leading the AL East. The game mattered. But the moment — Judge's homer, Kay's words, Sterling's memory — mattered more.

What's Next

In the days ahead, the Yankees and baseball at large will find more formal ways to honor Sterling — tributes, ceremonies, dedications. But nothing will match Monday night: a great player hitting a home run in the first inning on the day that a great broadcaster died, and the voice in the booth doing exactly what Sterling would have done.

Rest in peace, John Sterling. The call you made for four decades echoes still.
