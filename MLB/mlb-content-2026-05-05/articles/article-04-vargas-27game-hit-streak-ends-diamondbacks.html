Ildemaro Vargas's 27-Game Hitting Streak Ends — A Season-Defining Run Comes to a Close

By Marcus Cole | May 5, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

Ildemaro Vargas went 0-for-4 against the Chicago Cubs at Wrigley Field on May 2, and just like that, the most unlikely hitting streak of the 2026 baseball season was over.

The Arizona Diamondbacks utility player's 27-game hitting streak — which had grown into one of the most compelling stories of the young season — was baseball's longest since Trea Turner's 27-game run that bridged the end of the 2021 season and the start of 2022. It was also the longest hitting streak ever recorded by a Venezuela-born player in Major League Baseball history.

What the Streak Was

Context matters here. Vargas entered 2026 as a utility player — the kind of versatile depth piece that every roster needs and almost nobody outside the organization's fan base could identify in a lineup card. He plays multiple positions. He fills in where needed. He was not projected to lead anyone in anything.

And then he hit in 27 consecutive games.

Over the course of the streak, Vargas won the National League Player of the Month award for March and April, slashing .378/.398/.689 with six doubles, two triples, and six home runs. On May 1 — his final game of the streak — he went 4-for-4 with a walk and raised his batting average to .404. The Diamondbacks franchise record book got a new name in it: second-longest hitting streak in team history, behind only Luis Gonzalez's 30-game run.

How It Ended

The Cubs held Vargas to 0-for-4 on May 2 at Wrigley Field, and that was it. No drama, no extra innings collapse — just a quiet afternoon where the hits didn't come. Streaks end. Even the best ones end simply, without ceremony, in the middle of a road trip.

That is the nature of hitting streaks. The attention builds in inverse proportion to how comfortable the player becomes with it. Vargas handled the run with the same quiet professionalism he approaches every other aspect of his game. He did not change his approach in pursuit of the streak. He played to win and happened to keep hitting.

What's Next

Vargas now plays the rest of the season as a player who proved he can be one of the best hitters in baseball. His .400-plus average from the streak will normalize, as all averages do over a full season. But the NL Player of Month award, the Venezuela-born record, and the second-longest streak in Diamondbacks history are permanent entries in the record book.

For the Diamondbacks, the question now is whether Vargas — having established himself as something more than a depth piece — continues to hit in a lineup that needs consistent production. He's earned the right to find out.
