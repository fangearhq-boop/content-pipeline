Tarik Skubal Needs Elbow Surgery — Tigers Ace Out 2-3 Months as Free Agency Year Begins

By Ryan Calloway | May 5, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

The Detroit Tigers received the news they feared on Monday: Tarik Skubal, the reigning back-to-back American League Cy Young Award winner, needs arthroscopic surgery to remove loose bodies from his left elbow. The pitcher is expected to miss 2-3 months, with a realistic return to the mound possible before the end of August.

Skubal's arm locked up during a bullpen session on Sunday, forcing the Tigers to scratch him from his scheduled start against the Boston Red Sox on Monday. Tests revealed the loose bodies — small fragments of cartilage and bone that can cause sudden locking and inflammation — and surgery was quickly determined to be the necessary course of treatment.

What It Means for Detroit in 2026

The Tigers entered 2026 with Skubal as their unquestioned rotation anchor and their most compelling reason for optimism. He has been one of the two or three best pitchers in baseball over the past two seasons, and the prospect of a full year from him was a meaningful part of Detroit's competitive hopes.

A May-through-July absence strips the Tigers of their best pitcher during the heart of the schedule — the months when playoff positioning is established. The rotation will need to absorb his starts through internal options and, potentially, the trade or waiver market. Detroit is not a team with an obvious internal replacement for what Skubal provides.

The surgery itself is described as minimally invasive. Loose body removal is a common procedure with a generally reliable recovery trajectory. If Skubal returns before September, the Tigers could still make a meaningful push in the AL Central standings. That is the optimistic scenario.

The Free Agency Question

The implications extend well beyond 2026. Skubal was widely projected to be the most coveted free-agent pitcher of the upcoming offseason — a candidate to receive the largest guaranteed contract ever given to a starting pitcher. Teams had already begun internal evaluations of what a Skubal deal might look like.

An elbow surgery in the contract year changes those calculations. It does not necessarily eliminate Skubal's elite status — the procedure is not Tommy John surgery, and the recovery timeline suggests he could pitch a full workload in 2027 — but it introduces uncertainty that front offices will price into their risk assessments. The largest contract in pitching history may still come, but with a different lens applied.

What's Next

Skubal is expected to undergo the procedure in the coming days. The Tigers will provide updates on his recovery timeline as they become available. A return to the mound before the end of August remains the working goal, according to an orthopedic surgeon familiar with his case.

The remainder of Detroit's season — and Skubal's future — hinges on how the surgery goes and how his elbow responds. For now, baseball waits and hopes that one of the sport's best pitchers comes back strong.
