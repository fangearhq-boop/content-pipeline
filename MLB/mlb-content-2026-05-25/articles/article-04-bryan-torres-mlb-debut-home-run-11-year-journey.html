Bryan Torres Hits Homer in MLB Debut After 11 Years in the Minor Leagues

By Marcus Cole

May 25, 2026

At 28 years old, after 11 years of minor league baseball, Bryan Torres finally got his shot with the Cardinals on Friday — and made it a night no one who watched will forget.

Baseball keeps records of almost everything. Box scores going back to the 19th century, batting averages from 1871, pitching lines from games played before most of the current players' grandparents were born. So when Baseball Reference says "first since at least 1901," it means something.

Bryan Torres became the first Cardinals player to collect two hits, a home run, and a walk in his Major League Baseball debut since at least 1901. He did it on Friday evening at Busch Stadium, against the Cincinnati Reds, in his first game after 11 years in the minor leagues.

He was 28 years old when it finally happened.

The Wait

Torres first appeared in professional baseball's minor leagues in 2015. He spent the years that followed moving through the system — Double-A, Triple-A, back to Double-A, back up — never quite getting the call. Good enough to keep going. Not quite given the chance to see what he could do at the top level.

Eleven years is a long time to wait for anything. In baseball's minor leagues, it is a near-lifetime. Players come up at 20 and retire at 35 in the span that Torres spent waiting for his first big-league at-bat. He kept playing. He kept working. And on Friday, St. Louis finally said it was his time.

The Debut

Torres went 3-for-5 with a walk in his first MLB game — an 8-1 Cardinals blowout over Cincinnati in Game 1 of a doubleheader. The hits and the walk were impressive enough. The home run made it historic.

In the ninth inning, Torres hit a 2-run home run 377 feet to right-center field. It was his first career MLB hit that left the yard — and it capped a performance that stamped his name into the record books. No Cardinals player had done what he did in a debut in over 125 years of franchise history.

Torres also became the first Puerto Rican-born player to homer in his MLB debut since Isan Díaz did it in 2019 — part of a long, proud tradition of Puerto Rican players in professional baseball that Torres is now part of.

What It Means

A debut performance is one game. It does not guarantee a long career, a roster spot, or a second chance at this level. Torres knows that better than anyone — he spent 11 years learning how thin the margin is between Triple-A and the big leagues.

But what Friday's game proved, beyond the statistics, is that Torres came prepared. He had the tools to produce at this level. His at-bats were quality. His homer was authoritative. And when the Cardinals gave him the chance after all those years, he performed like someone who had been ready for a long time and was simply waiting to be asked.

What's Next

Torres is in St. Louis's lineup going forward — the Cardinals will decide over the coming weeks whether his debut performance is sustainable at the MLB level. Monday's Memorial Day game continues the homestand against Cincinnati. For Torres, the question is no longer whether he can get here; he has answered that. Now the question is how long he can stay.
