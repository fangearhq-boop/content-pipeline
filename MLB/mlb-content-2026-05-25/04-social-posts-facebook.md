### STORY 2: Corbin Carroll One Triple From D-Backs Franchise Record

**Long-Form Post**
Ask a casual baseball fan what the most exciting play in the sport is and they'll say a home run. Ask a purist. They'll say a triple.

Corbin Carroll has been rewriting the history of the three-bagger all season — and now he's one triple away from franchise immortality.

On Sunday, Carroll went 4-for-4 with two triples against Colorado, pushing his career total to 51. Stephen Drew holds the Arizona Diamondbacks franchise record at 52. One triple. That's it.

What makes this chase even more remarkable is the pace. Carroll has played 537 career games to reach 51 triples. Drew needed 773 games to get to 52. Carroll is getting there in fewer at-bats, at a younger age (26), with a legitimate shot at blowing past the record entirely.

He also leads MLB in 2026 with 8 triples, and trails only Mike Trout and Starling Marte among active players in career total triples.

The home run era has squeezed out the triple. Carroll doesn't care.

Watch for the record to fall this week.

### STORY 3: Hernandez Walk-Off Grand Slam in 0-0 Game

**Long-Form Post**
This is one for the baseball history books.

The Marlins needed it, too. They had dropped three of four to Atlanta earlier in the week. Bouncing back with a sweep — capped by one of the rarest endings in baseball — was exactly the right answer.

### STORY 4: Bryan Torres — 11 Years in the Minors, Then a Debut HR

**Long-Form Post**
Eleven years.

That's how long Bryan Torres spent in the minor leagues before the St. Louis Cardinals finally called him up. From his first minor league appearance in 2015 to his MLB debut on Friday — 11 years of bus rides, Double-A hotels, and the daily hope that today might be the day someone noticed.

He was 28 years old when he got the call.

In his first MLB at-bat, Torres recorded a hit. Then another hit. Then a walk. Then, in the 9th inning of a Cardinals blowout, he drove a 2-run homer 377 feet to right-center field.

That performance — two hits, a walk, and a home run — was the first time any Cardinals player had done that in a debut since at least 1901. Torres also became the first Puerto Rican-born player to homer in his MLB debut since Isan Díaz in 2019.

Baseball is a sport where some people come up at 22 and some people come up at 28. Bryan Torres spent 11 years proving he belonged. On Friday, he proved it in the best way possible.

That's the kind of story that makes baseball worth watching.
