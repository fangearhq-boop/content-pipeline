Five Rookies, Five Historic Moments — The 2026 MLB Rookie Class Is Already Something Special

By Ryan Calloway | April 3, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

Six games into the 2026 season, the rookie class has already delivered more historically significant moments than most classes produce in a full year.

It started on Opening Day and has not slowed down. From home run records to walk-off hits to a debut batting lineup slot not seen since the Eisenhower administration, the first-year players of 2026 are making their presence felt in the most meaningful ways possible: by doing things that have never been done before.

DeLauter and Murakami — Third and Fourth Players Ever

Chase DeLauter of the Cleveland Guardians and Munetaka Murakami of the Chicago White Sox each homered in each of their first three major league games. They are the third and fourth players in MLB history to accomplish that feat — and the first two to do it in the same season.

DeLauter announced himself with two home runs on Opening Day against Seattle, then added another in game two and another in game three. Through six games, he is 6-for-17 with four home runs, an .897 slugging percentage, and the American League Rookie of the Week award for the season's opening frame. Murakami, who played for the Yakult Swallows in Japan's NPB before joining the White Sox, matched DeLauter's first three-game run with three home runs of his own.

The White Sox were not expected to compete in 2026. But Murakami's early production gives their fans a genuine reason to watch every game.

McGonigle, Wetherholt, and Stewart

The list continues. Detroit Tigers second baseman Kevin McGonigle had a four-hit MLB debut — only the second Tiger in franchise history to do so, and just the 21st player in all of baseball since 1900. Through six games, McGonigle is hitting .364 with a .985 OPS and is already drawing early American League Rookie of the Year conversation.

JJ Wetherholt, the Cardinals shortstop, delivered a walk-off 2-run single in the 10th inning in one of his first two major league games. He became just the second Cardinal in the divisional era — dating to 1969 — to record a walk-off hit that early in his career.

And Sal Stewart of the Cincinnati Reds earned a cleanup spot on Opening Day — the first Reds rookie to bat cleanup on Opening Day since 1958. He won the National League Rookie of the Week for the season's first frame, making him and DeLauter only the second pair of rookies in MLB history to sweep the first weekly awards of a new season.

What's Next

Konnor Griffin, baseball's No. 1 overall prospect, was called up by Pittsburgh on Thursday — adding another name to a class that is already historically significant through one week.

Classes like this don't come around often. The players who emerge from it — whoever sustains their early production over a full season — will define how baseball talks about the generation that entered the sport in 2026. The first week suggests the conversation is going to be memorable.
