MLB Draft Combine 2026: Cholowsky vs. Emerson and the Prospects Who Moved Their Stock

By Marcus Cole | Ballpark Banter | June 26, 2026

The 2026 MLB Draft Combine concluded Friday at Chase Field in Phoenix, wrapping up four days of workouts, velocity readings, and exit velocity data that will inform draft boards from now through July 11 in Philadelphia.

What the Combine confirmed: the top of this class is excellent. What it revealed: several prospects below the top two changed the conversation about where they belong on those boards.

The No. 1 Decision Is Still a Debate

Roch Cholowsky entered the Combine as the consensus No. 1 overall prospect, and nothing that happened at Chase Field changed that. The UCLA shortstop slashed .338/.463/.707 with 21 home runs in his junior spring — a combination of contact rate, on-base ability, and power that doesn't show up in prospect profiles very often. His tools confirmed what the numbers already said.

Grady Emerson is the counter-argument. The 6-foot-2 left-handed shortstop from Fort Worth Christian High School in Texas represents the higher-ceiling, higher-variance alternative. High school shortstops who can hit from the left side with elite athleticism are the kind of prospect that organizations who bet on projection tend to prefer over polished college products.

Current mock draft estimates put the Chicago White Sox — who hold the No. 1 overall pick — at 55 to 60 percent probability of taking Cholowsky, with 40 to 45 percent for Emerson. The gap is narrow enough that the decision is genuinely open. Either pick would be defensible. The White Sox front office knows which direction they're leaning; the rest of the sport is waiting to find out.

The Prospects Who Made a Statement

Jack Beck arrived at Chase Field as a high-variance prospect from Columbia Central High School in Tennessee. He left as something different. Beck hit the longest ball of the entire Combine at 465.6 feet and registered 20 batted balls at 100 miles per hour or harder, with a peak exit velocity of 113 mph. The combination of distance and consistent hard contact separated him from hitters who post one impressive number.

Brady Snow put his name on the radar in a different way. The right-handed pitcher from American Heritage High School in Plantation, Florida posted the fastest velocity of any high school pitcher at the Combine at 97.7 mph — averaging 96 mph — while spinning his slider at 2,990 rpm at 83 mph. A high school arm throwing 97.7 with a high-spin breaking ball has first-round written on him regardless of where in the round he lands.

Collin Bland, a first baseman from Houston High School in Germantown, Tennessee, registered multiple exit velocities at 112.5 mph or higher. First basemen with that kind of raw power profile get drafted on the strength of the bat. Bland gave evaluators enough to project.

Logan Reddemann, the UCLA right-hander, sat 92 to 94 mph with carry — a velocity band that translates reliably from college to professional hitters, unlike some arms that live in that range without movement to back it up.

What the Combine Actually Does

The Combine is not a draft. It doesn't move picks and it doesn't guarantee outcomes. What it does is give organizations one more data point — a standardized environment where everyone throws on the same mound, everyone hits off the same machine, and the readings are comparable across the entire class.

The 2025 Combine saw 82.8 percent of attendees eventually drafted. This year's 335-prospect field — 195 college, 140 high school — will produce a similar rate. The players who performed well this week have done what they can do to present themselves. The draft boards are being finalized now.

July 11 in Philadelphia begins the process of turning all of this information into selections.

What's Next

The MLB Draft opens July 11 in Philadelphia with Day 2 on July 12. The White Sox will make the first selection and set the tone for where the class's top shortstops land. Mock draft updates will continue through the first week of July as organizations finalize their boards. Beck, Snow, and the other Combine standouts will see their valuations adjust in the next round of rankings. The sport's next generation takes one more step toward professional baseball in two weeks.
