Drake Baldwin's Historic Start: First Brave Since Hank Aaron to Score in 7 Straight Season Openers

By Jake Torres | April 11, 2026

Hank Aaron's name doesn't come up in baseball conversations lightly. He is one of the greatest players in the history of the sport — a Hall of Famer who spent most of his career setting records that defined what excellence looked like across multiple generations.

And yet, Drake Baldwin's name appeared next to Aaron's this week.

The Atlanta Braves rookie became the first Brave to score a run in each of the team's first seven games of the season since Hank Aaron accomplished the feat to open the 1957 campaign. That's 69 years between one Brave and another reaching that benchmark — and the player who ended the gap is a first-year player who had never appeared in a major league game before this spring.

The Numbers Behind the History

Baldwin's historical note is impressive, but his raw production would stand out even without the Aaron comparison. Through 13 games in 2026, the Braves' rookie catcher and utility player is hitting .321 with a .390 on-base percentage and a .623 slugging percentage. Five home runs. Fifteen runs batted in — the most of any player in Major League Baseball.

The RBI lead is particularly significant because it reflects situational performance. Getting 15 RBIs in 13 games requires not just hitting well, but hitting when it matters — with runners on base, in crucial moments, in games where those runs make a difference on the scoreboard. Baldwin has done all of that consistently since Opening Day.

His plate discipline is also notable for a first-year player. A .390 on-base percentage suggests he's not just swinging at everything — he's working counts and getting on base in multiple ways. That kind of approach is typically developed over years in the minor leagues and often takes a year or two to translate to the majors. Baldwin appears to have skipped the adjustment period.

The Braves' Lineup Is Deep Enough to Enable This

Credit should also go to the Atlanta lineup around Baldwin. When Ronald Acuña Jr., Matt Olson, and Michael Harris II are all hitting, opposing pitchers don't have the luxury of pitching extra carefully to a rookie. They're already focused on three different dangerous hitters before Baldwin even steps to the plate.

This is how lineup depth works in practice: the stars at the top create situations where younger players can take advantage of pitchers who are stretched thin. Baldwin has benefited from that environment while also proving he belongs there.

On Friday night, all three of Atlanta's big names — Acuña, Olson, and Harris — hit home runs. Baldwin's RBI total won't be a one-week story; it reflects a lineup that is genuinely producing top to bottom.

The Question Mark Ahead

Sustaining a .321 batting average and five-home-run pace over 162 games is not realistic for most hitters, let alone rookies. The league will adjust to Baldwin. Pitchers will probe his weaknesses, attack his tendencies, and eventually find a way to limit the damage.

That's when the real test of Baldwin's talent will arrive. Whether he can adapt when the league makes its second-and-third-time-around adjustments is the difference between a player who has a great April and a player who has a great career.

Two weeks in, the early returns are outstanding. The Hank Aaron comparison will fade if Baldwin starts to struggle — but the production he's shown so far is real, and the historical footnote is already written.

What's Next

Baldwin and the Braves continue their series against Cleveland on Saturday and Sunday. If he adds to his RBI total this weekend, the historical connections will keep building — and the national spotlight that rookie sensations earn when they sustain their early production will follow.

First-year players don't usually invite Hank Aaron comparisons. Drake Baldwin did it in his first two weeks. Watch to see if he earns more of them before the season is over.
