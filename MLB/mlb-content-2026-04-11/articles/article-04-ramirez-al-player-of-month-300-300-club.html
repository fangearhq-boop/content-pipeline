Jose Ramirez Named AL Player of the Month — And He's Chasing History

By Marcus Cole | April 11, 2026

Jose Ramirez has won awards before. He's been an All-Star, finished in MVP voting, and earned the respect of people inside baseball who know exactly how good he is. But in the broader sports conversation, he's often described as "underrated" — a word that feels inadequate for a player of his caliber.

April 2026 makes the case again. Ramirez was named the American League Player of the Month after one of the best stretches of his career: a .342 batting average, .722 slugging percentage, seven home runs, and 28 RBIs across 21 games. Those numbers would be remarkable for a full month at any point in anyone's career. For Ramirez, they came in a season where something much larger is quietly building.

The 300/300 Club

The 300/300 club — reaching both 300 career home runs and 300 career stolen bases — is one of the rarest achievements in the history of professional baseball. Only eight players have joined it: Bobby Bonds, Barry Bonds, Steve Finley, Carlos Beltran, Reggie Sanders, Jeff Bagwell, Larry Walker, and Alex Rodriguez.

Ramirez entered the 2026 season with 285 career home runs and 287 career stolen bases. With seven home runs in April, he's now approaching 292 career HRs. He needs 13 stolen bases to reach 300 in that category as well. Both milestones are reachable this season.

If he gets there, he becomes the ninth member of one of baseball's most exclusive fraternities — and arguably the most overlooked of the bunch, given that he'll have done it playing in Cleveland throughout his entire career rather than in a major media market.

Why Ramirez Is Still Overlooked

It shouldn't still be true in 2026, but Jose Ramirez remains one of the sport's most underappreciated superstars. Part of it is market size — Cleveland doesn't generate the national media coverage of New York, Los Angeles, or Boston. Part of it is position — third basemen don't always get the same highlight-reel treatment as sluggers or strikeout pitchers. And part of it is the nature of Ramirez himself: he plays consistently excellent baseball without the surrounding drama that draws national attention.

A .342 batting average with 28 RBIs in one month should be the top of every baseball highlight package. His April numbers were as good as any player in the American League. His April award is deserved — and overdue for him to receive the national conversation that goes with it.

What It Means for Cleveland

The Guardians are a team built around Ramirez's production. When he's at his best — hitting for average, slugging for power, taking walks, and contributing on the bases — Cleveland's ceiling rises significantly. Their October contention depends in large part on Ramirez continuing to produce at this level into the summer and fall.

A 28-RBI April is not something that happens every year. It reflects both individual excellence and opportunistic hitting — runners on base, two-out hits, the ability to deliver in moments that matter. Ramirez has always excelled in those moments. April 2026 was further confirmation.

What's Next

Ramirez and the Guardians continue their road series against the Braves before returning home to Progressive Field. Every home run in May and June is one step closer to 300. Every stolen base brings the 300/300 milestone within reach.

Baseball history is made one game at a time. Ramirez has been making it quietly for years. In 2026, the spotlight is starting to catch up to what the numbers have always said about him.
