### STORY 4: Jose Ramirez Named AL Player of the Month

**Long-Form Post**
*Posting Window: 5:30 PM ET*

If you're still sleeping on Jose Ramirez, April 2026 should be your wake-up call.

The Cleveland Guardians' third baseman was named the American League Player of the Month for April after one of the best single-month stretches of his career: a .342 batting average, .722 slugging percentage, seven home runs, and 28 RBIs in 21 games. Those are MVP-level numbers for a full month.

But the bigger story is where Ramirez sits historically. He entered the 2026 season with 285 career home runs and 287 career stolen bases. With seven April home runs, he's closing in on 292 career HRs. He needs just 13 stolen bases to reach 300 for his career.

The 300/300 club — 300 home runs and 300 stolen bases — is one of the rarest achievements in baseball history. Only eight players have done it: Bobby Bonds, Barry Bonds, Steve Finley, Carlos Beltran, Reggie Sanders, Jeff Bagwell, Larry Walker, and Alex Rodriguez. Ramirez is chasing history with every game he plays.

He's already one of the best players in franchise history. A healthy 2026 season could put him in conversation with some of the greatest switch-hitters to ever play the game.

### STORY 5: Drake Baldwin's Historic Start

**Long-Form Post**
*Posting Window: 3:30 PM ET*

Not many players get compared to Hank Aaron in their second week of the season. Drake Baldwin now has that distinction.

The Atlanta Braves rookie scored a run in each of the team's first seven games of the 2026 season — and he became the first Brave to accomplish that since Hank Aaron did it to open the 1957 season. That's not a stat you see very often.

In addition to the Aaron comparison, Baldwin's production has been elite by any measure: a .321 batting average, five home runs, and 15 RBIs through just 13 games — good enough to lead all of Major League Baseball in RBIs.

What makes Baldwin's start so impressive is that rookie players almost always need an adjustment period. The jump from Triple-A to the majors comes with a learning curve. So far, Baldwin hasn't shown any signs of that curve. He's hitting for average, hitting for power, and driving in runs at a rate that most veteran hitters don't sustain.

The Braves needed someone to step up and be a catalyst alongside Acuña, Olson, and Harris. They found one. Whether Baldwin can keep this pace for 162 games is the question — but two weeks in, he's making a case that no one expected.

### STORY 6: Red Sox Historically Bad Start

**Long-Form Post**
*Posting Window: 6:30 PM ET*

When your manager calls your team's performance "unacceptable," you know things are bad. When your record ties a 126-year franchise low, you know things are historically bad.

That's where the Boston Red Sox find themselves two weeks into the 2026 season.

Boston went out this offseason and made moves — adding Ranger Suárez, Sonny Gray, Willson Contreras, and Caleb Durbin, among others. The expectation was a bounce-back season after winning 89 games in 2025. Instead, they're sitting at 2-8, the worst record in Major League Baseball, with a pattern of losses that is difficult to fix quickly.

Five losses have come by two runs or fewer. They are 0-3 in one-run games. The close losses pile up and the run differential reflects a team that competes, but doesn't convert. That's often a sign of bullpen and clutch-hitting problems — neither of which has an easy overnight fix.

Alex Cora has been around long enough to know when to sound the alarm. He's sounded it.

The Red Sox season isn't over. Baseball is long, and teams have recovered from worse starts. But every day that ticks off the calendar at 2-8 makes the math harder.
