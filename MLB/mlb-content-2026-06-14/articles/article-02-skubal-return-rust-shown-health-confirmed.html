Skubal Returns: Rust Shown, Health Confirmed, Deadline Watch Continues

By Marcus Cole

June 14, 2026

Tarik Skubal made his return Saturday in Cleveland. He was not sharp. He was also healthy — and for a pitcher who had a loose body removed from his left elbow just 38 days earlier, that is the only thing that actually matters right now.

The two-time AL Cy Young Award winner lasted 4⅔ innings against the Guardians, allowing three runs (two earned) on five hits with four strikeouts and 80 pitches thrown. Cleveland won 3-1. Skubal took the loss. And Daniel Schneemann's two-run homer in the third inning was the decisive blow.

Contextualizing the Performance

What Skubal's line does not capture is what it took to produce it. He had not thrown in a major league game since April 29, when he left a start against Atlanta with elbow discomfort. Surgery followed on May 6 — a minimally invasive procedure to remove a loose body from his left elbow. Skubal returned on a timeline that was aggressively fast by any medical standard.

Rust is not a physical problem — it is a timing problem. The feel for a specific pitch, the crispness of a fastball's spin, the comfort of trusting a slider at 3-2. Those things come back with repetition, not with rest. Skubal's first start back was exactly that: a start. Not a statement, not a showcase, not a proof of concept. A start.

Skubal declined to make it anything more in his postgame remarks: "I don't want to play into that narrative of trying to be back." He was right not to. The elbow held up. The velocity was there. The command will follow.

The Trade Deadline Picture

Skubal is a free agent after the 2026 season. The Detroit Tigers, currently 7-2 in June but still well outside playoff contention, will face a legitimate decision between now and the August 3 non-waiver trade deadline: sell their two-time Cy Young winner to a contender, or hold him for a stretch run that the standings may not support.

Every team in World Series contention has Skubal circled on their deadline board. One rusty first start does not change that. What Saturday's outing did confirm — and this matters enormously — is that the health concern is behind him. A pitcher who has logged 80 pitches in his first start back from elbow surgery with no reported discomfort is a pitcher programs can build a plan around.

What's Next

Skubal's next scheduled start will tell a more complete story. With the elbow health question answered, evaluators will now look at stuff, command, and stamina across multiple outings. A second strong showing would dramatically accelerate deadline speculation. The market for a healthy, two-time Cy Young winner entering the prime of his career is the most competitive in baseball. Saturday was start one. The real picture develops from here.
