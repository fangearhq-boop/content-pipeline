## STORY 2: Skubal Return Analysis

#### Long-Form Post — 10:30 AM ET

Tarik Skubal is back. And last night was a reminder that coming back from elbow surgery — even quickly, even cleanly — is not the same as coming back as yourself.

In his first start since April 29, Skubal lasted 4⅔ innings and allowed three runs (two earned), including a two-run homer to Daniel Schneemann. He threw 80 pitches, struck out four, and took the loss in Cleveland's 3-1 win.

He was rusty. He also showed why health was the only real question.

The stuff was there in moments. The command was not always there. But a pitcher coming off a procedure 38 days earlier is not expected to be sharp in his first outing. The point was to confirm the elbow is healthy. That box appears checked.

Skubal was characteristically direct postgame: "I don't want to play into that narrative of trying to be back."

Translation: let the results speak. There will be more starts. The two-time AL Cy Young Award winner is a free agent after this season, and every contender watching the August 3 deadline has him circled. One uneven return start does not change the picture.

## STORY 3: Schwarber-Alvarez Tied at 24 HR

#### Long-Form Post — 12:30 PM ET

This home run race just got interesting.

Yordan Alvarez hit two home runs Friday night — a grand slam and a solo shot — against the Royals in the Astros' 10-8 win. That output moved him into a tie with Kyle Schwarber at 24 home runs, the most in baseball. Byron Buxton sits third at 21.

The timing is perfect. The Home Run Derby is set for July 13 at Citizens Bank Park in Philadelphia. That's Schwarber's home park — the place where he played the best years of his career and where Phillies fans are expecting a show.

If both Schwarber and Alvarez participate — which seems all but certain at this pace — it will be one of the most anticipated Derby matchups in recent memory. Alvarez has the power, the on-base skills, and the quiet menace of a hitter who never seems surprised by any pitch. Schwarber has the home crowd and the history of being one of the best pure power hitters in the game.

Only five players in MLB history have ever hit 60 home runs in a season: Bonds, McGwire, Sosa, Maris, and Mantle. At this pace, both Schwarber and Alvarez are legitimate threats to crash that list.

## STORY 4: Braves and Acuña IL

#### Long-Form Post — 3:00 PM ET

The best team in baseball just lost its best player.

Ronald Acuña Jr., the Atlanta Braves' superstar outfielder, has been placed on the injured list with a left hamstring strain. The injury occurred while he was running to first base, and the Braves have dropped three consecutive games since.

Atlanta entered the week at 45-23, the best record in baseball with an eight-game NL East lead — a stunning turnaround for a team that went 76-86 last season. Chris Sale has been the anchor, leading the majors with 92 strikeouts and eight wins in his first year with Atlanta. Matt Olson has provided the offense with 20 home runs and 51 RBIs. Michael Harris II is hitting .304 with 13 home runs.

But Acuña is the axis around which the Braves' offense rotates. His presence changes how opposing pitchers approach an entire lineup. Without him, opposing staffs can pitch more carefully to the middle of the order.

The bigger question is how this changes Atlanta's approach to the August 3 trade deadline. A month ago, the Braves were buyers looking to add a final piece to a strong team. Now they have a health question around their franchise player and a three-game slide to navigate.

MLB All-Star voting is officially open, and this year's game comes with some genuinely interesting storylines to follow.

Phase 1 runs through June 25, with the starting lineups finalized by July 2. The game itself is July 14 at Citizens Bank Park in Philadelphia.

The most compelling individual story to watch: Ben Rice. The Yankees first baseman has quietly put together one of the best offensive starts by a player his age in Yankees history. Through 57 games, Rice is slashing .300/.393/.638 with 17 home runs and a 181 wRC+ — a number that indicates he has been about 81 percent better than a league-average hitter. He was never supposed to be this story. With Aaron Judge out to a rib stress fracture and needing 4-6 weeks to heal, Rice stepped into the cleanup spot and performed at an All-Star level. He is likely to be named a first-time All-Star and could earn the starting spot at first base.

Judge himself may receive enough fan votes to technically win a starting spot — but with his return not expected until mid-August, he would almost certainly withdraw and be an injury scratch. That clears the path for Rice.
