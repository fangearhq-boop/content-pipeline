Schwarber Is Back and Still at 20 HRs — His Pace Is Historic

By Jake Torres

May 23, 2026

Kyle Schwarber missed three games with a GI illness and returned Friday to hit his 20th home run of the season, keeping his MLB-leading total intact. He hit No. 20 on May 15 — the earliest any player has ever reached that mark in major league history.

Kyle Schwarber was sick. He missed three games. He came back Friday and hit a home run.

That is, in summary form, what happened. The longer version is that Schwarber's 20th home run of the season — hit Friday night — is part of one of the most remarkable individual offensive stretches in recent major league history. He is the MLB leader in home runs. He hit No. 20 on May 15, which is the earliest date in baseball history that any player has reached that mark. He missed three games after that milestone due to a gastrointestinal illness. He returned and immediately picked up where he left off.

There is something almost absurdly on-brand about that sequence for Schwarber.

The Historical Context

Reaching 20 home runs by May 15 has never been done before in major league history. The previous earliest date was somewhere in late May. Schwarber obliterated that record by roughly two weeks.

For context: the pace required to hit 20 home runs by May 15 projects to roughly 65-70 home runs over a full 162-game season. No player has ever hit 70+ home runs in a season — Barry Bonds holds the record at 73, set in 2001. Schwarber is not on pace to break that record; his rate will almost certainly decline as the season progresses. But the burst he has produced in the first six weeks of the season is genuinely unprecedented.

He leads the majors with 20 home runs. The player in second place has fewer. The gap is real.

The Team Around Him

The Philadelphia Phillies are 25-25. That record is a stark contrast to the individual numbers Schwarber is posting. The team has been inconsistent — winning streaks followed by losing streaks, injury interruptions, rotation questions — and has not been able to build a dominant record around their most productive hitter.

Schwarber is doing everything within his power. He is hitting the ball out of the park at a historic rate. He returned from illness and immediately produced. But Philadelphia needs more from the rest of the roster if this season is going to result in anything significant.

For now, though, the story is the home runs. And the home run story in 2026 belongs entirely to Kyle Schwarber.

What Comes Next

Schwarber is healthy and back in the lineup. He is 20 home runs into his season before Memorial Day. The question at the beginning of summer is how long he can sustain this pace — and whether the Phillies can start winning enough games to make his historic production matter in October.

Those are questions for later. Right now, a man came back from being sick, stepped to the plate, and hit a home run. That's the kind of player Kyle Schwarber is. The records and milestones follow naturally.
