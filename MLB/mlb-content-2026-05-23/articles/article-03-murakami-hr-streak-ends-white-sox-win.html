Murakami's 5-Game HR Streak Ends — But He Delivers a Bases-Clearing Double in a 9-Run Inning

By Ryan Calloway

May 23, 2026

Masataka Murakami's streak of homering in five consecutive games — which tied a White Sox franchise record — came to an end Friday against the Giants. He still drove in multiple runs with a bases-clearing double in a 9-run 4th inning as Chicago won.

The streak was going to end sometime. Friday was that day. Masataka Murakami did not hit a home run against the San Francisco Giants, bringing his run of consecutive games with a home run to a stop at five.

That streak had tied a White Sox franchise record. It was remarkable while it lasted — a sustained stretch of power production that put Murakami at the center of one of the better individual stories in the American League this spring.

The White Sox won anyway. And Murakami was in the middle of it.

The 9-Run 4th

Chicago erupted for 9 runs in the 4th inning — a massive single-inning outburst that turned what might have been a competitive game into a blowout. Murakami was a central figure in that inning, driving a bases-clearing double that did significant damage and knocked the game open.

The home run streak may have ended, but the production did not. That is the consistent theme with Murakami this spring: he finds a way to contribute even when his signature statistical line — the home run — isn't appearing in the box score.

Why Murakami Matters

Masataka Murakami came to the White Sox with an extensive track record in Japan's NPB — a proven power hitter with excellent plate discipline. The transition from NPB to MLB is one baseball observers have watched with great interest, and Murakami has delivered convincingly. The five-game home run streak was a franchise record-tying performance for a player still establishing himself in his first full American season.

The White Sox were projected by many analysts as a candidate to finish last or near-last in the American League Central. They are hovering around .500. That is a genuine surprise, and Murakami's emergence as a consistent offensive contributor is one of the central reasons for Chicago's competitive standing.

The Bigger Picture in Chicago

The White Sox have not been a playoff team recently. The rebuild has been long, and Murakami's arrival represents one of the more compelling pieces of their next competitive window. His performance in the first two months of the 2026 season — including a record-tying home run streak — has given White Sox fans something to follow, debate, and celebrate.

The streak is over. The wins keep coming. On the South Side, that's more than enough for now.
