The Yankees are getting a true ace at 25. That is very good news for a franchise that needed one.

### STORY 4: White Sox Turnaround — From 121 Losses to Wild Card Contenders

#### FB Long-Form — 3:00 PM ET

In 2024, the Chicago White Sox went 41-121.

That wasn't just a bad season. It was the most losses by any team in a single season in the history of modern Major League Baseball. The 121 losses set a record. It was, in the clinical language of sports, a historic failure.

Two years later, the Chicago White Sox are 28-27 and holding an AL Wild Card position.

That is not normal. Franchise rebuilds take longer. There is no template for a 121-loss team becoming a Wild Card contender in two seasons — not because teams never improve quickly, but because teams rarely fall that far and have enough structure to climb back this fast.

The engine of the turnaround is Munetaka Murakami, who has hit 20 home runs in his first 55 MLB games — the most by any rookie in baseball history since 1901 — and is tied with Yordan Alvarez for the AL HR lead. But as a recent MLB.com feature noted, it's not just Murakami. The White Sox have multiple contributors, roster changes that quietly shifted the talent level, and an organizational direction that is working.

The 28-27 record doesn't put them in first place. It puts them in a Wild Card spot, which in May means: keep going, stay healthy, don't implode. For a franchise that was historically bad in 2024, that is an extraordinary ask that is somehow being answered.

Baseball is full of things that are supposed to be impossible. This is one of them.

### STORY 5: José Ramírez and the 300/300 Club

#### FB Long-Form — 6:00 PM ET

On May 2, José Ramírez stole his 300th career base. It was a stolen third base in the fifth inning, and it passed largely without ceremony — because Ramírez does everything without much ceremony.

But here's what that number means in context.

Ramírez now needs just nine more home runs to reach 300 career home runs. That would put him in the 300/300 club — 300 career home runs and 300 career stolen bases — one of the most exclusive statistical groupings in baseball history. The players who have done it include Willie Mays, Barry Bonds, and Alex Rodriguez. The list is short because the combination of power and speed required to reach those numbers in both categories is almost impossibly rare.

Ramírez has been building this career quietly over the past decade with the Cleveland Guardians. He is not the type of player who generates massive national attention in any given week — he just produces, game after game, season after season, at a level that accumulates into something historic. He already has one of the better careers of any third baseman in the modern era. If he reaches 300/300, the conversation about his Hall of Fame case becomes much simpler: you just point to the list he's on.

Nine home runs. At the pace Ramírez has been hitting in 2026, that is not a long wait. This is a milestone watch worth your attention — because when it happens, it will be worth celebrating.
