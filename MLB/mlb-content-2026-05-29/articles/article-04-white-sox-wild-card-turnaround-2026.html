From 121 Losses to Wild Card: The White Sox Are 28-27 in 2026 and Baseball's Biggest Story

By Marcus Cole

May 29, 2026

The same franchise that set a modern MLB record with 121 losses in 2024 is now holding an AL Wild Card position in 2026. There's no formula for what the White Sox are doing — except that it's working.

Numbers tell stories. The story these two numbers tell is extraordinary: 121 and 27.

The Chicago White Sox lost 121 games in 2024, going 41-121 in one of the most historically bad seasons in American League history. That record — the most losses by any team in a single modern MLB season — became the emblem of an organization that had fallen so far that climbing back seemed like a multi-year, multi-rebuilding-cycle project.

Two years later, the White Sox are 28-27 in 2026 and holding an American League Wild Card position. The 121 and the 27 coexist in the same franchise timeline, less than two calendar years apart.

Munetaka Murakami as the Catalyst

The clearest explanation for the White Sox's rapid ascent is Munetaka Murakami, who arrived from Japan's Yakult Swallows with the nickname "Japanese Babe Ruth" and has done nothing to distance himself from the comparison. Through his first 55 MLB games, Murakami has hit 20 home runs — tied with Yordan Alvarez for the American League lead — and posted a .936 OPS. The 20 home runs through 55 games are the most by any MLB rookie since 1901.

Murakami was covered here yesterday in the context of his 20th HR milestone, but the White Sox's Wild Card position puts his impact in a different frame. He is not just a compelling individual story — he is the offensive anchor of a team that is legitimately in postseason position. Those are different things.

Beyond Murakami

The White Sox's front office and MLB.com have both noted that this turnaround is not a one-player story. The franchise made organizational changes in the offseason, added depth in areas where the 2024 roster was vulnerable, and shifted the tone of the clubhouse. The 28-27 record reflects multiple contributors — the kind of baseline competence across the 25-man roster that produces a winning record when the lineup also contains a generational power hitter.

A Wild Card position in May is not a playoff berth. The White Sox need to sustain this through a 162-game season that still has more than 100 games remaining. Teams that are 28-27 at this stage of the season finish .500 and miss the playoffs with some regularity. But for a franchise that was historically bad in 2024, reaching this record is not a rounding error — it is evidence that the direction has genuinely changed.

What This Means for the AL Wild Card Race

The White Sox are currently in second place in the AL Central behind the Minnesota Twins, which puts them in Wild Card territory rather than division-lead territory. The AL Wild Card field is competitive, and holding that position through summer requires consistency the White Sox haven't shown over a full season in years.

But the existence of the conversation is itself significant. A year ago — certainly two years ago — "Chicago White Sox Wild Card contender" would have been a punchline. It is not a punchline now. It is a reality that baseball fans in Chicago, and anyone who follows the game closely, needs to take seriously.

What's Next

The question for the White Sox heading into summer is straightforward: can they maintain? Murakami's durability over a full MLB season is unknown — this is his first year in the league. The pitching staff needs to hold up. The supporting cast needs to produce.

If they can stay near .500 through July and remain in Wild Card position heading into the trade deadline, the White Sox become a deadline buyer — a phrase no one expected to type about this franchise in 2026. Baseball's most stunning turnaround is a work in progress. But the progress is real.
