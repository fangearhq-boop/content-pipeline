Shohei Ohtani: 6 Hitless Innings and a Leadoff HR in the Same Game — First Pitcher Since 2015

By Marcus Cole

May 29, 2026

The last Dodger to homer and pitch six hitless innings in the same game was Don Drysdale — in 1959. Sixty-seven years later, Shohei Ohtani did it Thursday against Colorado.

There is a specific category of baseball achievement that does not have a name because the sport has not needed to name it frequently enough. It goes something like this: pitcher, same game, both hits the first home run of the night for his team and throws six hitless innings.

On Thursday at Dodger Stadium, Shohei Ohtani checked both boxes against the Colorado Rockies. The Dodgers won 4-1. Ohtani's ERA is 0.82 through nine 2026 starts.

The Home Run First

Ohtani led off the first inning against Rockies starter Tomoyuki Sugano with a home run on a 1-1 pitch — a 424-foot shot to dead center field that gave the Dodgers an immediate 1-0 lead. It was Ohtani's fourth home run of the 2026 season as a designated hitter.

He had already given his team the lead. Then he went to the mound and worked to preserve it.

Six Hitless Innings

Ohtani threw 99 pitches over six innings. He struck out seven Rockies batters. He allowed no hits. He allowed one earned run — on a Willi Castro groundout in the fourth inning that ended his 19⅓-inning scoreless streak, raising his ERA from below 0.82 to 0.82. One groundout, one run, and still, no one got a hit off him in six innings.

The Dodgers' relief pitchers extended the hitless innings into the eighth before Colorado finally broke it up. But the effort Ohtani turned in — six hitless innings while also being the player who had swung the bat and hit the first run of the game — belongs in a separate discussion from any other starting pitching performance of 2026.

The Historical Context

No pitcher has both hit a home run and thrown six hitless innings in the same game since 2015. That is not a typo — a gap of over a decade separates Thursday's game from the last time anyone accomplished this combination.

The last Dodger to do it before Ohtani was Don Drysdale. That was 1959. Drysdale was one of the most dominant pitchers of his era and a Hall of Famer. Between Drysdale in 1959 and Ohtani in 2026, every other pitcher who put on a Dodger uniform — Sandy Koufax, Fernando Valenzuela, Orel Hershiser, Clayton Kershaw — played without achieving this particular combination in a single game.

Ohtani has now done it once. Drysdale did it once. That is the complete list of Dodgers.

The 2026 Season in Numbers

Through nine starts, Ohtani owns a 0.82 ERA — the best mark of his MLB career. He has pitched 55 innings. His strikeout rate is elite. The Dodgers are winning the games he pitches at a high rate. As a hitter, he is batting .269 with a .400 on-base percentage and four home runs. He is doing both of these things simultaneously, in the same season, for the same team.

The ESPN 2026 awards watch column has him as the frontrunner for NL MVP. The Cy Young race is a different question — the NL has multiple strong pitchers — but the MVP case is built on total value, and no player in either league is producing value on both sides of the ball the way Ohtani is.

What's Next

Ohtani's next scheduled start will carry exactly this level of attention regardless of what the opponent is or what the standings look like. He has placed himself in a position where every time he takes the mound, the audience is watching to see what the next unexpected thing will be.

On Thursday, the unexpected thing was: six hitless innings plus a leadoff home run. The next time, it will be something else. That is what makes him the most compulsively watchable player in baseball — the sense that the ceiling has not yet been located.
