Cam Schlittler Is 7-2 With a 1.50 ERA and Leading the AL Cy Young Race in 2026

By Jake Torres

May 29, 2026

The 25-year-old Yankees right-hander leads the American League in ERA, WHIP, batting average against, fWAR, and FIP. He does it by throwing a fastball nearly 91% of the time.

Conventional baseball wisdom holds that a pitcher who relies too heavily on any single pitch will eventually be exploited. Hitters will time it, adjust, and find the gap. In the modern game, where every team's analytics department is building tailored approach charts for every opposing starting pitcher, going to the same pitch over and over should be a recipe for getting hit hard.

Cam Schlittler has been throwing a fastball 90.8% of the time in 2026. He has a 1.50 ERA and leads the AL in nearly every pitching category that matters. He is the frontrunner for the American League Cy Young Award.

Conventional wisdom, in this case, is wrong.

The Numbers

Through 12 starts, Schlittler is 7-2 with a 1.50 ERA, 81 strikeouts in 72 innings, a 0.86 WHIP, a .183 batting average against, a 2.7 fWAR, and a 1.78 FIP. He leads the American League in all five of those advanced metrics. He received the most first-place votes among AL pitchers in the second MLB.com Cy Young poll of the season.

The ERA of 1.50 is not a product of a particularly favorable schedule or a handful of dominant outings mixed with mediocre ones. It is consistent. The WHIP of 0.86 reflects a pitcher who is not walking people. The FIP of 1.78 indicates the performance is real, not the result of defensive support. By the numbers, Schlittler is the best pitcher in the American League right now.

The Three-Fastball Approach

What makes Schlittler's case intellectually interesting beyond the raw numbers is the mechanism behind them. He throws what has been described as a three-fastball mix — variations within the fastball family (likely a four-seamer, a cutter, and a sinker or riding fastball) — at high velocity, 90.8% of the time. The remaining 9.2% is the only time hitters see something different.

The reason this works, despite the predictability, is that the three fastball types have meaningfully different movement profiles and velocity differences that hitters still cannot consistently square up even when they know roughly what's coming. The pitch is not one thing — it's three things wearing the same name, each behaving slightly differently out of the same arm slot. That is a different problem for a hitter than facing a pitcher who mixes in a curveball and a slider.

At 25, Schlittler is still early in his development. The approach that is working now may or may not work as hitters accumulate more data on him. That is the question hanging over any young pitcher who finds a formula that succeeds: can it last?

The AL Cy Young Race

Tarik Skubal, who won the AL Cy Young in 2025, is not in a position to challenge Schlittler right now. The race for the 2026 award is Schlittler's to lose in the American League. His numbers lead every relevant category by a meaningful margin, and the second Cy Young poll validated that standing with first-place votes.

The Yankees, who needed a true ace for their rotation, have found one in Schlittler at age 25. If his ERA holds and his health stays strong through the second half, the award case builds without requiring any additional argument. The first half has been essentially perfect.

What's Next

Schlittler's next start will be monitored closely — not because his performance has been inconsistent, but because each game is another data point in a Cy Young case that is being constructed in real time. The All-Star break will be the first major checkpoint: if Schlittler arrives there with an ERA still around 1.50 and a record above .700, the award conversation will be impossible to ignore.

For now, the Yankees have something they have been trying to find for years: a starter who goes out every fifth day and makes winning feel likely. Schlittler is delivering that, one fastball at a time.
