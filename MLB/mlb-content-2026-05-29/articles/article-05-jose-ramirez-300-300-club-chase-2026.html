José Ramírez Is Nine Home Runs From the 300/300 Club — One of Baseball's Most Exclusive Groups

By Jake Torres

May 29, 2026

Three hundred career stolen bases. Nine home runs from 300 career home runs. The quiet Cleveland Guardian is approaching one of the rarest milestones in the sport's history.

On May 2, José Ramírez stole third base in the fifth inning of a Cleveland Guardians game. The steal was his 300th career stolen base, a milestone that was noted and then mostly set aside in the flow of a regular-season game that continued on either side of it.

That milestone deserved more attention than it received, because of what it represents in the broader context of Ramírez's career.

He needs nine more home runs to reach 300 career home runs. At that point, José Ramírez becomes one of the very few players in the history of Major League Baseball to record both 300 career home runs and 300 career stolen bases. The 300/300 club is one of the sport's most exclusive statistical groupings. The names on that list are not random — they are among the most accomplished all-around offensive players the game has ever produced.

The 300/300 Club

The combination of 300 career home runs and 300 career stolen bases requires two qualities that rarely coexist: elite power and elite speed, sustained over the course of a long career. Players tend to be one or the other. Very few are both at a high enough level, for long enough, to reach three-hundred in both categories.

Willie Mays is the most famous member of the club — his career encompassed both power (660 home runs) and speed (338 stolen bases) to a degree that makes every other player's case look smaller by comparison. Barry Bonds reached the 300/300 plateau before continuing to home runs totals that became complicated by other circumstances. Alex Rodriguez, whose career in New York produced four consecutive 30-HR seasons with consistent baserunning production, crossed the threshold later in his career.

The list beyond those three is extremely short. Joining it is a genuine historical achievement.

Ramírez's Case

Ramírez has spent his career with the Cleveland Guardians — formerly the Indians — and has been one of the more consistent offensive performers in the game for nearly a decade. He is not a player who generates national headlines in any given week. He shows up, plays well, and accumulates the kind of career numbers that reveal themselves fully only when you step back and look at the total picture.

At third base — a position that historically favors power hitters over speedsters — Ramírez has maintained a stolen base production that most third basemen cannot match. His career has been built on the unusual combination that makes the 300/300 pursuit possible: a player who can do both, for a long time, without losing either quality as the years accumulate.

His 2026 pace suggests the nine-home-run gap will close in the coming weeks. The question is not whether it will happen, but when — and whether it will receive the attention it deserves when it does.

The Hall of Fame Dimension

Ramírez is already regarded as a strong Hall of Fame candidate based on the totality of his career production. Reaching the 300/300 club would add a specific, compelling argument to his case — the kind of numerical evidence that provides a clear answer to voters who want a landmark achievement to point to.

The players already in the 300/300 club are, with varying degrees of complication, among the most celebrated offensive players in baseball history. Being in that company by that specific measure is not a trivial thing. It is a statement about the kind of player Ramírez has been for a very long time.

What's Next

Nine home runs, at the pace of a player who has been a reliable 25-to-30 home run hitter over the course of his career, is a matter of weeks — not months, barring injury. The countdown exists. The milestone is approaching. When Ramírez hits home run No. 300, the reaction should match the historical weight of what it represents.

The 300/300 club is one of baseball's most exclusive groups. Ramírez is nine swings from knocking on the door.
