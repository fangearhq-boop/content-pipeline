Ohtani Hit No. 300. Schwarber Leads MLB. Crow-Armstrong Did It Again. The First Half Power Numbers Were Historic.

By Jake Torres

The second half of 2026 begins today. Before the calendar turns, it is worth pausing on three power performances from the first half that genuinely deserve a moment at the break.

Shohei Ohtani: 300 Career Home Runs

Shohei Ohtani hit his 300th career home run just before the All-Star break. The number itself is significant. The context makes it historic in a way that has no parallel in the sport.

Ohtani spent his first several MLB seasons pitching and hitting at an elite level simultaneously — a combination that no player in the modern game has replicated for more than a handful of starts. He accumulated those 300 home runs while also posting strikeout rates and ERA numbers that would have made him a valuable pitcher regardless of his bat. The milestone is not just 300 home runs; it is 300 home runs from a player who was also an ace-caliber starting pitcher for years.

There is no historical comparison that fits cleanly. There is no other player who has done what Ohtani has done. That makes every counting-stat milestone he reaches a different kind of achievement.

Kyle Schwarber: 32 Home Runs, MLB Lead

Kyle Schwarber leads all of Major League Baseball with 32 home runs through the first half. That pace, sustained into the second half, puts him in position for one of the more memorable power seasons in recent memory.

The historical company he is keeping: only Babe Ruth, with 235 home runs in his first five seasons with the New York Yankees, and Mark McGwire, with 220 home runs in his first five seasons with the St. Louis Cardinals, have hit more home runs in that same five-season window with one team. Schwarber's 32 at the break ranks him third on that all-time list.

Schwarber has always been a power hitter built on a particular skill set — high exit velocity, a willingness to accept strikeouts in exchange for damage when he makes contact, and an approach that generates home runs at a rate few hitters ever sustain. This year, that approach is producing at the highest level of his career.

Pete Crow-Armstrong: 20 HR / 20 SB Before Game 95

Pete Crow-Armstrong of the Chicago Cubs hit his 20th and 21st home runs before his team's 95th game this season, while also posting more than 20 stolen bases. He is one of just four players in Major League Baseball history to reach 20 home runs and 20 stolen bases before game 95 in multiple seasons.

The more remarkable part: he did it in back-to-back years, in 2025 and 2026. Speed-and-power combinations at this level are rare in any single season. Doing it twice consecutively, at his age and stage of career development, places Crow-Armstrong in genuine historical company.

The Cubs have a player who can change a game with his legs or his bat, and who is producing at a pace that has precedent in only a handful of careers across the entire history of the sport.

The Second Half Has to Answer

The first half of 2026 belonged to hitters. Ohtani's 300th. Schwarber's 32. Crow-Armstrong's back-to-back 20/20 before game 95. These are not round numbers manufactured for the occasion — they are genuine historical achievements that arrived on their own schedule.

The second half starts tonight. The question is whether it can produce anything to match what the first half delivered.
