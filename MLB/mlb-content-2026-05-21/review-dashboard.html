In the entire history of Major League Baseball. Willie Mays. Barry Bonds. Alex Rodriguez. Carlos Beltran. Andre Dawson. Deion Sanders. Steve Finley. Bobby Bonds. That's the list. It hasn't changed in years. And it is about to get one name longer. José Ramírez reached 300 career stolen bases on May 2, when he swiped third base against the Oakland Athletics — his 13th steal of the 2026 season and the 300th of his career. Only Kenny Lofton, in Cleveland franchise history, had ever reached 300 steals before him. Now he just needs home runs. About eight of them, based on where his career total stands. When he gets there, he joins a club that hasn't had a new member in years — one that includes some of the most versatile, complete players the sport has ever produced. Ramírez has already made history in 2026 in other ways. He set the Guardians' all-time games played record (1,620, passing a mark held by Terry Turner since approximately 1918). He became the first player in Cleveland franchise history to hit a home run against all 29 other MLB teams. The résumé is extraordinary. The 300-300 milestone is the crown jewel waiting at the finish line. ### Long-Form Post ⚾ THE 60-HOME RUN CLUB: THREE MEMBERS. COULD SCHWARBER BE FOUR? ⚾ There is a threshold in baseball history that is more exclusive than 3,000 hits, 500 home runs, or 300 wins. It has been reached exactly six times, by exactly three players. Sixty home runs in a single season. Barry Bonds did it with 73 in 2001. Mark McGwire did it with 70 in 1998. Sammy Sosa did it three times — 66, 64, and 63 across the late 1990s and early 2000s. No other player in major league history has ever hit 60 in a single year. Kyle Schwarber is on pace to join that list. Schwarber hit his 20th home run on May 15 — the earliest any player in MLB history has reached that mark. The previous record was set by Luis Gonzalez on May 17, 2001, the same year Bonds set the all-time record. Schwarber arrived four days ahead of Gonzalez. His current pace projects to somewhere between 66 and 70 home runs. The 60-HR threshold is the threshold. Not because of the round number — because it's where the all-time greats are. Bonds. McGwire. Sosa. If Schwarber finishes this season anywhere near that neighborhood, 2026 becomes one of the most significant offensive seasons in baseball history. Can he maintain this pace? Almost certainly not — season-long projections from early-season rates are notoriously aggressive. But the foundation is real, the power is real, and the trajectory has not slowed. ### Long-Form Post ⚾ NICK KURTZ IS THE MOST PATIENT HITTER IN BASEBALL — AND PITCHERS CAN'T STOP HIM ⚾ Here's what makes Nick Kurtz's 2026 season so strange and so fascinating. Opposing pitchers know he walks. They've decided to throw him strikes. He's seeing a 55.3% strike rate — teams are actively not walking him on purpose. And he's leading Major League Baseball in walks anyway. The Oakland Athletics first baseman walked in 20 consecutive games earlier this season, tying Barry Bonds for second-most consecutive games with a walk in MLB history. The all-time record belongs to Roy Cullenbine, who walked in 22 straight games with the Detroit Tigers in 1947. Kurtz got within two of the most exclusive streaks in baseball. His walk total through late May: 33. The most by any hitter before May in the majors since Bryce Harper walked 38 times in that same span in 2018. During his consecutive game walk streak: Kurtz was batting .270 with five home runs and 29 walks. He averaged 1.20 walks per game while pitchers threw him strike after strike. The plate discipline is not passive — it's active. Kurtz is reading pitches, identifying locations, and turning potential strikes into balls through knowledge of the strike zone. For an Athletics team rebuilding and developing young talent, Kurtz is providing an example of what elite plate discipline looks like at the major league level. Teams will keep trying to get him out by throwing strikes. He'll keep walking anyway. [Is Nick Kurtz on your radar yet? ### Long-Form Post ⚾ THE BEST PLAYER IN BASEBALL IS HAVING A STRANGE MAY ⚾ There are two Shohei Ohtanis this month, and they're pointing in opposite directions. Ohtani the pitcher: approximately 34 strikeouts, 4 earned runs, and 30 innings pitched through his starts in 2026. That ERA. Those strikeout numbers. That command profile. It's what every team in baseball would want from a front-of-the-rotation starter. Ohtani the hitter: batting around .111 in May with no home runs in that stretch. Through the full season, he's at .240 with 7 home runs — solid but not the elite offensive threat his 2024 contract promised. The two-way paradox is genuinely strange. When Ohtani is cold as a hitter, his impact on the team comes almost entirely from his pitching days. The Dodgers are good enough to absorb a Ohtani hitting slump without losing ground — they have enough talent around him — but they signed the most expensive contract in sports history on the bet that he would be elite in both directions simultaneously. May is not the whole story. Players — even the best ones — have cold stretches. Ohtani in April was hitting .240 with 7 home runs, meaning May has been a reset. The bat will almost certainly come back. The more interesting question is whether this May is a preview of what happens when the hitting and pitching aren't both peaking at the same time. For now, his arm is carrying him. He has carried teams with his bat before. Baseball is long.
