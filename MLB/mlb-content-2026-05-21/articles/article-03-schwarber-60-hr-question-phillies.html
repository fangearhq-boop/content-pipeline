Kyle Schwarber Is on Pace for 66 Home Runs — Could He Join the 60-HR Club?

By Jake Torres

May 21, 2026

Schwarber hit his 20th home run on May 15 — the earliest date in MLB history. He's projecting to 66+ HRs for the season. The 60-HR club has exactly three members. That could change.

The 60-home run threshold is not just a round number. It is a boundary that separates the great from the all-time, because only three men in major league history have ever crossed it: Barry Bonds (73, in 2001), Mark McGwire (70, in 1998), and Sammy Sosa, who did it three separate times — 66 in 1998, 64 in 2001, and 63 in 1999.

No one else has reached 60. Not Ruth. Not Maris. Not Mantle. Not Aaron. Six times, by three players, in the last three decades.

Kyle Schwarber is on pace to join them.

The Historic May 15 Milestone

Schwarber hit his 20th home run of the 2026 season on May 15 — the earliest calendar date in MLB history that any player has reached that total. The previous record belonged to Luis Gonzalez, who hit his 20th on May 17, 2001, the same year Bonds set the all-time mark with 73.

Schwarber arrived four days ahead of Gonzalez.

That comparison matters because it contextualizes the pace. Gonzalez was putting up historic numbers in one of the most notable offensive seasons of his career. Schwarber has exceeded even that benchmark in 2026. He is the eighth player in MLB history to reach 20 home runs in his team's first 45 games.

His current trajectory projects to somewhere between 66 and 70 home runs. That projection assumes his pace holds — which is an assumption, not a guarantee. But the foundation is real. The power is real. The swing decisions are producing home runs at an extraordinary rate.

What 60 Would Mean

Finishing a season with 60 home runs does not just mean producing an impressive statistic. It means entering a club that baseball has not admitted a new member to since the early 2000s — a club associated with some of the most dramatic individual seasons the sport has seen.

Bonds' 73-HR season in 2001 remains the all-time record. McGwire's 70 in 1998 was the season-long record chase that revived national interest in baseball. Sosa's three trips over 60 made him one of the era's most celebrated power hitters.

Schwarber would be doing it in 2026, without the asterisks of the late 1990s' steroid era context. He would be doing it as himself — a left-handed pull hitter who has always been defined by his ability to drive the ball, now taking that ability to a level that baseball has rarely seen.

No projection from May guarantees a 60-HR finish. Pitchers adjust. Injuries happen. Baseball is 162 games long. But the starting point matters, and Schwarber's starting point has never been seen before in May.

What's Next

The question for Schwarber is how his pace holds up as the season progresses and pitchers make adjustments. His next milestones: 25 home runs before June, 30 by the end of June, and staying on pace through the All-Star break. If he is within range of 60 entering September, baseball will be paying very close attention to every plate appearance.
