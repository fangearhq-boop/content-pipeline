Nick Kurtz Is Walking in 20 Straight Games — Tied With Barry Bonds — and Pitchers Can't Stop It

By Ryan Calloway

May 21, 2026

The Oakland Athletics first baseman leads MLB with 33 walks while opponents throw him strikes. He matched Barry Bonds' consecutive-game walk streak. The most patient hitter in baseball right now isn't a name most fans expected.

Here is what makes Nick Kurtz's walk totals so strange: pitchers are not afraid of him the way they were afraid of Barry Bonds.

When Bonds was accumulating walks at historic rates in the early 2000s, opposing managers were intentionally walking him to avoid giving up home runs to the most feared hitter in baseball. The walks were a product of fear — rational, calculated avoidance.

Kurtz's walks are different. Opponents are throwing him strikes. He is seeing a 55.3% strike rate — pitchers actively trying to get him out by attacking the zone. And Kurtz is leading Major League Baseball in walks anyway.

The 20-Game Streak

Earlier this season, Kurtz walked in 20 consecutive games — the second-longest consecutive walk streak in MLB history, tied with Barry Bonds. The all-time record belongs to Roy Cullenbine, who walked in 22 straight games for the Detroit Tigers in 1947. Kurtz fell two short of the record before his streak ended on May 2.

During that 20-game stretch, Kurtz batted .270 (24-for-89) with five home runs, four doubles, and 15 RBI — while also drawing 29 walks. He was averaging 1.20 walks per game. That is not a power hitter being pitched around. That is a hitter with genuine plate discipline recognizing balls from strikes at an elite level, even against pitchers who are consistently throwing strikes.

Through late May, Kurtz leads the majors with 33 walks — the most by any hitter before May since Bryce Harper drew 38 in that same period in 2018. Harper was one of the most feared hitters in the National League at the time. Kurtz is a first baseman for an Oakland team in the middle of a rebuild.

What Elite Plate Discipline Actually Looks Like

Walking in baseball is often discussed as a passive skill — simply not swinging at pitches outside the zone. The reality is more active. Elite plate discipline requires recognizing pitch type, location, and spin in fractions of a second, then committing to a swing or a take based on that recognition. Kurtz is doing that faster and more accurately than nearly anyone in the majors.

His on-base streak of 25 consecutive games — one of the longer active streaks in baseball — is the broader evidence of that skill. He is not just walking. He is reaching base, putting himself in position to score, and contributing to an Athletics offense that is trying to establish itself as a young team builds toward contention.

Pitchers have not found an answer. The 55.3% strike rate says they are trying the direct approach. Kurtz is not relenting.

What's Next

The question for Kurtz is whether this walk rate — extraordinary even by historical standards — is sustainable over a full season. Walk rates can regress as pitchers build books on hitters and as counts and game situations change through 162 games. But through late May, Kurtz has been one of the most fascinating offensive stories in baseball, doing something that only a small number of hitters in the sport's history have come close to matching.
