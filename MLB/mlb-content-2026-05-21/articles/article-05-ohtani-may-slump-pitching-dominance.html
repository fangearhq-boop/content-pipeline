Shohei Ohtani's May: Cold at the Plate, Dominant on the Mound

By Marcus Cole

May 21, 2026

The best player in baseball is hitting around .111 in May with no home runs in that stretch. His ERA and strikeout numbers from the mound are extraordinary. The two-way story of 2026 is more complicated than expected.

Shohei Ohtani is the most unique player in baseball. He is the only active player who both pitches and hits at a genuine major league level. He signed the largest contract in sports history before the 2024 season. The Dodgers' bet on him was a bet on two-way dominance sustaining itself over a decade.

May 2026 has offered a complicated answer to what that looks like when the two halves are not both peaking simultaneously.

Ohtani the pitcher in 2026: approximately 34 strikeouts, 4 earned runs, 30 innings across his starts. Elite numbers in any company. His ability to miss bats, to get weak contact, to work through lineups on the days he takes the mound has been among the best in the game this season.

Ohtani the hitter in May: batting around .111 with no home runs in that stretch. Through the full season, he sits at .240 with 7 home runs — a productive player, but not the transcendent offensive force his contract anticipates.

The Two-Way Paradox

Baseball has not had a player like Ohtani before, which means it does not have established frameworks for understanding what his cold stretches at the plate mean in context. A conventional DH with a .111 May would be concerning. A pitcher with his May ERA would be a rotation anchor.

Ohtani is both players at the same time, which creates an unusual accounting challenge. When he pitches well and hits poorly, what does that mean for the Dodgers? The answer, most nights, is that the Dodgers have enough talent surrounding him that a cold stretch from their highest-paid player does not derail their season.

But the premise of Ohtani's contract — the premise of two-way baseball at the highest level — is that both things will happen, most of the time, simultaneously. May has offered a month-long test case of what happens when only one of those two things is working.

Context and History

Every player has cold months. A .111 May is within the range of what baseball statistics consider normal variance — the variance is simply amplified by who Ohtani is and what is expected of him. There is no reason to believe based on one difficult month that his hitting will not return to the levels he produced earlier in 2026.

The more interesting data point is his pitching. A pitcher throwing in a DH role — handling the physical demands of two fundamentally different athletic skills — sustaining elite mound performance while hitting numbers fluctuate is exactly the kind of resilience that made Ohtani's long-term value case so compelling.

When Ohtani is right at the plate and right on the mound simultaneously, baseball has not produced an answer for it. May is not that version of Ohtani. But May is not the whole story.

What's Next

The question for June and beyond is whether Ohtani's bat returns to the levels his early-2026 production suggested. He had 7 home runs through roughly 39 games, meaning April was productive. A full return to that pace makes the two-way combination work the way it's supposed to. If the May slump extends, the conversation about what the Dodgers are getting versus what they paid for will grow louder. For now, his arm is carrying his end of the deal more than adequately.
