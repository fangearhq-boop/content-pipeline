José Ramírez Is Eight Home Runs From the Most Exclusive Club in Baseball History

By Marcus Cole

May 21, 2026

The 300-300 club — 300 home runs and 300 stolen bases in a career — has eight members all-time. Ramírez already has his 300 steals. He needs eight more home runs to join Mays, Bonds, and A-Rod.

There are measurements in baseball that define greatness — batting titles, ERA titles, strikeout records. And then there are the thresholds so rare that reaching them puts a player in a category entirely their own.

The 300-300 club is one of those thresholds. Three hundred career home runs and three hundred career stolen bases, combined in a single player's résumé, has happened exactly eight times in the history of Major League Baseball. Willie Mays. Barry Bonds. Alex Rodriguez. Carlos Beltran. Andre Dawson. Deion Sanders. Steve Finley. Bobby Bonds.

José Ramírez is about to become the ninth.

The Stolen Base Milestone Is Already Done

On May 2, Ramírez swiped third base against the Oakland Athletics — his 13th stolen base of the 2026 season and the 300th of his major league career. It was a moment that made history in the Cleveland franchise: Ramírez became only the second player in franchise history to reach 300 career stolen bases, joining Hall of Famer Kenny Lofton.

That achievement would have been the centerpiece of a lesser player's legacy. For Ramírez, it was a checkpoint on the way to something larger. He already had the speed. He already had the stealing ability. He just needed the home runs to complete the equation.

He is approximately eight away.

What Makes the 300-300 Club So Rare

Power and speed do not usually coexist at this scale. The physics of building a body capable of generating 300 career home runs tend to work against the athleticism required for 300 career stolen bases. Players who hit for power generally do so at the cost of speed; players who steal bases in volume generally do not have the raw power to drive the ball out of the park in triple-digit quantities.

The eight members of the 300-300 club found a way around that. They were not just good at both things — they were historically good at both things, simultaneously, for long enough to accumulate numbers that require sustained excellence across a decade or more of play.

Ramírez fits that description. He has spent the 2026 season alone adding to his legacy in multiple ways: setting the Guardians' all-time games played record (1,620 games, April 6), becoming the first player in Cleveland franchise history to hit a home run against all 29 other MLB teams (completed April 11), and reaching his 300th career steal. The home run milestone is the final piece.

What's Next

Ramírez is approximately eight home runs away from entering the 300-300 club. At his 2026 pace, that milestone could arrive before the end of June. When it does, he joins a list of eight players whose names will remain in baseball's record books indefinitely. The club has not gained a new member in years. Ramírez is the one who will change that.
