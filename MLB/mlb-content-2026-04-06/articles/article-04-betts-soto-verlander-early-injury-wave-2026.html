Betts, Soto, Verlander: Three Stars on the IL Before the Season's First Week Is Over

By Marcus Cole | April 6, 2026

The 2026 season is one week old and the injury news is already significant. Mookie Betts has an oblique strain. Juan Soto is dealing with a calf injury. Justin Verlander's hip is acting up. Three marquee names. Three different teams. One painful week.

One week of baseball. It should not be enough time for the injury report to become a dominant storyline. And yet here we are.

Three significant names are now on or trending toward the injured list: Mookie Betts, Juan Soto, and Justin Verlander. Three different teams. Three different injury types. One shared outcome: contenders managing the early-season calendar without centerpiece players.

Mookie Betts — Dodgers, Oblique Strain

The most significant of the three, in terms of team implications, is Mookie Betts. The Los Angeles Dodgers placed their leadoff hitter on the 10-day injured list with a right oblique strain after he exited a game against the Washington Nationals in the first inning complaining of lower back pain. An MRI confirmed the injury.

Manager Dave Roberts offered a cautiously optimistic timeline — four to six weeks, likely on the shorter end. But oblique strains in baseball have a stubborn habit of lingering, and any timeline with the word "weeks" in it is meaningful for a team chasing a third consecutive World Series title. The Dodgers have the depth to survive a short Betts absence. A longer one would be a more serious problem.

Juan Soto — Mets, Calf Strain

Juan Soto exited a road game against the San Francisco Giants in the first inning with calf tightness. An MRI revealed a right calf strain, though Soto himself characterized it as "minor" and the Mets are treating it as day-to-day. A trip to the injured list has not been ruled out.

For a Mets organization that spent aggressively on Soto this offseason to make him their offensive cornerstone, even a two-week absence is an unwelcome development. The New York offense has questions beyond Soto, and managing the lineup without him — even briefly — puts pressure on players who were not brought in for that responsibility.

Justin Verlander — Tigers, Hip Inflammation

Justin Verlander returned to Detroit in the offseason after re-signing with the Tigers. His homecoming started on an encouraging note in spring training. It started the regular season less encouragingly: Verlander went on the 15-day injured list with left hip inflammation, retroactive to April 1, after allowing five runs on six hits in three and two-thirds innings in his first start.

Manager AJ Hinch called the move "precautionary" and expressed optimism about a return around April 16. For a pitcher Verlander's age, however, hip issues require careful management. Detroit fans, who welcomed him back with considerable excitement, are hoping the optimism is well-founded.

What's Next

All three players will be monitored closely as April progresses. The Dodgers, Mets, and Tigers each have enough roster depth to manage short IL stints. The concern is that any injury lingering deep into April or beyond changes the calculus for the full season. For now, the best-case scenario for all three is a return by mid-to-late April — a manageable window in a 162-game season.
