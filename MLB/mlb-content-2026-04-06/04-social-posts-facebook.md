### STORY 1: Joey Wiemer — Live-Ball Era Record

#### Long-Form Post -- 8:00 AM

Here is a fact that deserves a full minute of appreciation: Joey Wiemer was released by the San Francisco Giants before this season began. He cleared waivers, signed with the Washington Nationals, and entered opening week as little more than a depth piece.

Then he went 8-for-8 with two walks in his first 10 plate appearances.

That streak — reaching base safely in each of his first 10 PAs of the season — ties Carlos Delgado's 2002 record for the longest out-free start to a season in the live-ball era (since 1920). Wiemer's exit velocity averaged above 100 mph during the streak. His hard-hit rate was 83.3%. He was not just squeaking on base — he was hitting the ball hard every time.

His first out came in his 11th plate appearance, a groundout to Phillies starter Taijuan Walker. By then, the record had already been set.

Baseball has a wonderful habit of producing stories like this. A player is deemed not good enough by one organization. He goes somewhere else. And he proceeds to etch his name in the record books in the first week of the year. Keep an eye on Joey Wiemer.

### STORY 2: Konnor Griffin's Debut

#### Long-Form Post -- 10:30 AM

PNC Park was sold out. Thirty-eight thousand nine hundred and eighty-six people packed into Pittsburgh on April 3 for the Pirates' home opener against the Baltimore Orioles. Most of them were there to see one specific thing: Konnor Griffin's first major league at-bat.

They were not disappointed.

Griffin, 19 years old, stepped to the plate in his first career appearance and lined an RBI double off the wall. He went 2-for-3 with a walk, scored a run, and drove in another. The Pirates won 5-4. ESPN called him a "difference-maker." It was the most anticipated debut of opening week.

The historical comparisons are worthy of the moment. Griffin became the first teenager to play shortstop in the major leagues since Alex Rodriguez made his debut as an 18-year-old for the Seattle Mariners in 1994. He is also the first Pittsburgh Pirate under 20 years old to reach base twice in a debut since Bobby Del Greco did it in 1952.

He is a teenager. He plays the hardest position on the field. And he looked like he belonged.

### STORY 3: Chase DeLauter — Historic Start

#### Long-Form Post -- 12:30 PM

Trevor Story's 2016 first week with the Colorado Rockies is one of the most memorable starts in recent MLB history. He hit four home runs in his first three games. It was the kind of thing you do not forget.

Chase DeLauter just matched it.

The Cleveland Guardians outfielder entered Monday's games with five home runs in his first eight MLB games. He hit four in his first three, becoming the first player to match Story's 2016 pace in the years since. Through eight games, he is batting .346 with eight RBIs and an OPS above 1.290 — fourth in all of baseball.

The April 4 home opener told the story well: in a game the Guardians needed after a slow road trip, DeLauter delivered a two-run homer in the seventh inning to help Cleveland beat the Cubs 4-1. He has been the engine of their offense from the first day.

It is early. But DeLauter has earned every superlative being thrown at him.

### STORY 4: Betts, Soto, Verlander — Early Injury Wave

#### Long-Form Post -- 3:00 PM

One week of baseball. Three marquee names already headed to the injured list. The 2026 season is not wasting any time.

Start with the biggest blow: Mookie Betts is on the 10-day IL with a right oblique strain. He exited a game against the Nationals in the first inning with lower back pain, and an MRI confirmed the injury. Manager Dave Roberts said he expects Betts back in four to six weeks, likely on the shorter end of that range. For the Dodgers — defending World Series champions pursuing a three-peat — losing their leadoff hitter and defensive centerpiece for even a month is a significant setback.

Then there is Juan Soto. The Mets signed Soto to be their cornerstone, and he is already managing a right calf strain after exiting a road game against the Giants in the first inning. Soto called it a "minor strain" and the team is treating it as day-to-day. An IL stint has not been ruled out. The Mets, already a team of questions at the top of their rotation, cannot afford extended time without their best hitter.

Finally, Justin Verlander. The veteran right-hander returned to Detroit in February and is now on the 15-day IL with left hip inflammation, retroactive to April 1. He is eligible to return around April 16. In his first start back with the Tigers, he allowed five runs in three and two-thirds innings before the hip acted up. Calling this a "precautionary" move is somewhat optimistic — for a pitcher Verlander's age, hip inflammation needs to be taken seriously.

Three stars. One week. Buckle up.

### STORY 5: Aaron Judge — HR No. 371, Climbing the All-Time List

#### Long-Form Post -- 10:00 PM

Aaron Judge hit his third home run of the 2026 season on Opening Night at Yankee Stadium — and it was a historic one.

The blast, a slider from Miami Marlins right-hander Eury Pérez launched into the left-field seats at Yankee Stadium, was the 371st home run of Judge's career. It moved him past Hall of Famer Gil Hodges for sole possession of 83rd on baseball's all-time list.

It was part of the Yankees' dominant 8-2 home opener win over the Marlins, a game that featured one other highlight worth mentioning: Cody Bellinger made what could be the best catch of the 2026 season, sprinting 62 feet, having the ball pop off the heel of his glove, and then securing it with a blind 180-degree sweep. If that play does not end up on the highlight reel in October, something went wrong.

Judge enters Monday's games with the Yankees at 6-1 and looking every bit like the team some predict will be the American League's best this year. The all-time HR list has no shortage of legends ahead of him, but he is climbing. And at 33, he may be entering his peak again.

### STORY 6: Munetaka Murakami — History on the South Side

#### Long-Form Post -- 6:00 PM

When Munetaka Murakami signed with the Chicago White Sox this past offseason, most fans outside of Japan were just learning his name. He had been the biggest star in Nippon Professional Baseball for years — a power hitter in the mold of the sluggers baseball fans love most. But NPB-to-MLB transitions are not guaranteed successes, and the White Sox had not exactly been a magnet for marquee international talent recently.

Murakami has answered every question in the first week.

Through eight games, the White Sox third baseman has four home runs and seven RBIs. He homered in each of his first three MLB games — all on the road in Milwaukee — and then hit his fourth homer of the season on April 4 at Rate Field against the Blue Jays (a two-run blast in a 3-0 White Sox win). Four home runs in eight games is the most by any Japanese-born player in their first eight MLB games.

He is 26 years old. The transition to American pitching appears to be no transition at all.

### STORY 7: Week 1 Standings Wrap

#### Long-Form Post -- 8:00 AM (secondary)

One week of baseball tells us a lot — and almost nothing — at the same time. But the 2026 standings after the first week are at least worth examining.

In the American League, the Yankees have lived up to their billing with a 6-1 record. They went into the week without Judge on the IL (he just hit his third homer), their rotation looked sharp, and the Bellinger signing already looks like it paid off in week one alone. In the AL West, the Houston Astros are off to a 6-3 start, showing early that their transition from the Verlander era has been seamless. Cleveland leads the AL Central at roughly 5-3.

The NL picture is still forming, but the Milwaukee Brewers — who traded away Freddy Peralta in the offseason and were picked for regression by many analysts — are off to a surprising start at 7-2. The Dodgers, nursing the Betts IL situation, are still expected NL favorites.

At the bottom: the Boston Red Sox and Oakland Athletics are both around 2-6, and the White Sox are mid-pack despite Murakami's heroics.

Disclaimer: it is April. These standings will look very different by Memorial Day.
