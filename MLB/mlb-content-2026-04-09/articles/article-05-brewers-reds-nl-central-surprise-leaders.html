Brewers and Reds Are the NL Central's Surprise Co-Leaders — and the Numbers Say They're for Real

By Jake Torres | Ballpark Banter | April 9, 2026

PECOTA projected the Milwaukee Brewers to finish 81-81 in 2026. The Cincinnati Reds were not expected to compete for a division title. Two weeks into the season, both teams are 8-4, tied for first place in the NL Central, and producing results that demand a reconsideration of what the division was supposed to look like.

The NL Central was widely dismissed in preseason analysis — a division without a clear favorite, without the star power of the NL West, without the historical gravitas of the NL East. Two weeks into the season, it has produced two of the more compelling early-season stories in baseball.

Milwaukee's Run Differential Is Not Normal

The Brewers are 8-4, which is good. Their run differential is +30, which is second-best in all of baseball. A team that PECOTA projected to go .500 over a full season has outscored its opponents by 30 runs through 12 games. That is not a mirage. That is a team playing baseball at a level that preseason projections did not account for.

Brandon Misiorowski set the tone on Opening Day. The 22-year-old right-hander struck out 11 batters in his first start, the most by any Milwaukee pitcher on Opening Day in franchise history. He also became the youngest pitcher to record 10 or more strikeouts on Opening Day since Felix Hernandez in 2007. That debut established expectations, and the rest of the rotation has maintained them.

The Brewers' depth up and down the lineup — including Christian Yelich, who collected six hits in the first week, and a bullpen that has been reliable in late innings — has contributed to the kind of run differential that looks less like luck and more like a roster functioning above its projected ceiling.

Cincinnati's Pitching Duo Is the NL's Best-Kept Secret

The Reds have a different kind of argument. Their offense ranks last in the National League in on-base percentage with runners in scoring position — they swept the Texas Rangers despite getting almost nothing from their lineup in high-leverage situations. That is a difficult way to win, and yet they have done it.

The reason is Matt Burns and Rhett Lowder.

Burns is pitching to a 0.82 ERA through his first few starts, with an average fastball velocity of 98.3 mph — third-fastest among all MLB starters this season. His performance against the Rangers produced nine strikeouts and showcased the combination of velocity and movement that makes him one of the more difficult pitchers to center in the early going.

Lowder has been even more statistically striking over a longer sample. His 1.30 ERA through eight career starts is the lowest maintained through that many starts by any Reds pitcher since 1913. That is not a recent franchise benchmark — it is a 113-year franchise benchmark.

Two starters pitching at this level simultaneously is what allows a team to win series despite not hitting in clutch situations. It is not sustainable at this precise rate, but it does not need to be. Even a regression to merely excellent from historically elite still leaves Cincinnati with a formidable top of the rotation.

A Division Race That Was Not Supposed to Be One

The NL Central was supposed to be a placeholder — a division that produced a wild card team by default rather than genuine competition. Instead, two weeks in, it has co-leaders with legitimate statistical cases for why they belong near the top of the standings.

The Brewers have the run differential. The Reds have the pitching staff. Neither team is relying on luck to explain their results. The preseason projections missed something real about both rosters, and the next several months will determine whether that miss was modest or significant.

What's Next

Milwaukee's road trip to Cincinnati — whenever the two teams square off directly — will serve as an early measuring stick for what this division race looks like when it becomes a head-to-head competition. In the near term, watch Misiorowski's next start, Burns' next outing, and Lowder's continued ERA maintenance. If the pitching holds and the Brewers keep scoring at their current rate, this division race is real. And it belongs in the conversation about the most surprising stories in baseball through two weeks.
