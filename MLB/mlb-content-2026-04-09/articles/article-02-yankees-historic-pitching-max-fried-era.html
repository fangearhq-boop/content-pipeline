Yankees 8-3 Despite A's Loss — Their Pitching Staff Is Writing History

By Jake Torres | Ballpark Banter | April 9, 2026

The New York Yankees dropped a game Wednesday night. That is worth noting only because nothing else about their start to 2026 looks remotely ordinary.

The Yankees fell to the Oakland Athletics 3-2 on April 8, with Brent Rooker's sacrifice fly in the ninth inning delivering the go-ahead run. It dropped New York to 8-3 and snapped a streak of dominant results. But one loss cannot obscure what this pitching staff has produced over the first nine games of the season.

The Yankees have allowed eight runs through nine games. That total matches the 1943 St. Louis Cardinals and the 2002 San Francisco Giants for the fewest runs allowed through nine games in the modern era of Major League Baseball. Their team ERA stands at 1.16. They have thrown three shutouts. And at the center of all of it is a starter who arrived via free agency and has wasted no time making himself at home in pinstripes.

Max Fried and a Record That Goes Back to 1967

Max Fried has thrown 13.1 consecutive scoreless innings over his first two starts as a Yankee. He is the first Yankees starter to accomplish that feat since Mel Stottlemyre did it in 1967. Fried did not pitch Wednesday against Oakland — Will Warren took the mound — but his presence has defined the tone for a rotation that came into the season without Gerrit Cole and Carlos Rodon, both unavailable due to injury.

The Yankees have not needed them. The rotation has absorbed those absences and produced historically elite results anyway. That is not a compliment offered loosely. Matching run prevention totals from teams in the 1940s — in an era with far more offense — requires a level of sustained pitching performance that is rare in any decade.

Wednesday's Game and What It Means

The loss to Oakland came against a team the Yankees are expected to handle routinely over the course of 162 games. Luis Severino — a former Yankee — started for the Athletics. New York got to him early, plating two runs in the first inning on singles from Trent Grisham and Aaron Judge, followed by a Cody Bellinger hit. The A's bullpen shut the Yankees down from there, and Rooker's sacrifice fly put Oakland ahead for good in the ninth.

It was the kind of loss that will happen over the course of a long season. The Yankees have won eight of their first 11 games and lead the AL East. The bullpen and lineup both contributed to the defeat, which is the kind of thing that happens once in a while to every team, regardless of how historic their rotation looks through the first two weeks.

Judge and the Offense

Aaron Judge has three home runs on the season, including a shot in the home opener on April 5 that was the 371st of his career. He needs 29 more to reach 400. Judge's presence near the top of the lineup has given the pitching staff protection, even if Wednesday was not the offense's finest performance. In a season built around run prevention, every Judge home run functions as a multiplier — a margin that the pitching staff does not need to manufacture on its own.

The bigger story remains the pitchers. Eight runs allowed in nine games is not a pace that will continue indefinitely, and the Yankees know that. But it is a foundation, a statement about who this team is and how it intends to win. When a rotation without Cole and Rodon is matching records from the 1940s, something genuinely unusual is happening in the Bronx.

What's Next

The Yankees continue their schedule with an eye on the return of Cole and Rodon as the season progresses. In the near term, Max Fried's third start will draw close attention — his scoreless streak is a running story, and each outing either extends it or measures where his limits are. New York remains atop the AL East with the second-best record in baseball behind the Dodgers.
