Dodgers Lead MLB at 9-3 — No Betts, No Problem

By Marcus Cole | Ballpark Banter | April 9, 2026

The Los Angeles Dodgers have the best record in baseball. They are doing it without their starting shortstop. And by the time Mookie Betts returns, they may already have lapped the field.

Los Angeles entered Thursday at 9-3, the best mark in all of baseball. Their road record is a pristine 5-0 — unbeaten anywhere outside Dodger Stadium. Betts has been on the 10-day injured list since suffering a right oblique strain while running the bases against the Washington Nationals, a type of injury that typically sidelines hitters for four to six weeks. But the early prognosis is trending sharply in his direction. Betts himself described his recovery as "light years ahead of where I thought I was going to be," and manager Dave Roberts characterized the strain as "certainly more moderate than significant." A return before the end of April is a real possibility.

In the meantime, the Dodgers are not waiting.

Ohtani Is Carrying Everything

Shohei Ohtani has been the story of the early season, and the numbers behind his start are staggering by any standard. Through 12 games as a hitter, Ohtani owns three home runs and seven RBIs, a .420 weighted on-base average, an average exit velocity of 94.6 mph, and a hard-hit percentage of 55.2 percent. His 36-consecutive-game on-base streak — which began during the 2025 season and has carried over — remains alive.

But the conversation around Ohtani in 2026 has always centered on the other side of the ball, and his pitching debut delivered. On March 31, against the Cleveland Guardians, Ohtani took the mound to open the Dodgers' season and produced six scoreless innings: one hit, three walks, six strikeouts, 87 pitches. Los Angeles won 4-1. His next start is expected before the end of April.

This is Ohtani's second full two-way season in the major leagues but his first in the National League, where the absence of a designated hitter spot means he faces lineups configured differently and pitchers he sees less frequently as a hitter. So far, none of that adjustment has shown up in the box score.

When Betts Comes Back

The Dodgers were a 9-3 team before Betts returned, and a conversation worth having is what happens the moment he does. Betts has been one of the most complete players in baseball for nearly a decade — a five-tool shortstop who bats near the top of an already-loaded lineup. When the Dodgers placed him on the injured list, Hyeseong Kim was recalled to fill the roster spot. The team did not miss a beat.

That speaks to the depth of this roster. But depth is not the same as having Betts. A fully healthy Mookie Betts inserted back into the top of this order — with Ohtani, Freddie Freeman, and the rest of the Dodgers lineup surrounding him — pushes Los Angeles into territory that is genuinely difficult to evaluate by historical comparison.

For now, the Dodgers are doing just fine without him. The best record in baseball, an unblemished road performance, and the sport's most compelling two-way player driving the offense and rotation simultaneously. The Betts news is a bonus. The Dodgers were already winning without it.

What's Next

The Dodgers continue their homestand at Dodger Stadium through the week. Ohtani's next pitching appearance is anticipated sometime before the end of April, and Betts' timeline will be monitored closely as he continues to progress ahead of schedule. A healthy return before May 1 would give Los Angeles its full lineup just as the division race begins to take shape in the NL West.
