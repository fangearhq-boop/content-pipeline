Red Sox at 2-8: Tied for the Worst Nine-Game Start in 126 Years

By Ryan Calloway | Ballpark Banter | April 9, 2026

Two weeks ago, the Boston Red Sox were a playoff contender. Today, they have the worst record in Major League Baseball and a nine-game start tied for the lowest point the franchise has reached since 1900.

Boston stands at 2-8, matching the franchise's worst nine-game mark in 126 years. Five of those eight losses came by two runs or fewer. They are 0-3 in one-run games. The bullpen has been shaky on a nightly basis. The offense has gone quiet at the moments it has been needed most. Manager Alex Cora, rarely a man to choose careful language when something needs to be said, called the team's performance "unacceptable" and warned that changes would come if the problems persisted.

There is nothing soft about that assessment. And there is nothing soft about the numbers that produced it.

How It Unraveled

Boston opened the season with a win on Opening Day — a promising start that dissolved quickly. The Red Sox then lost five consecutive games, a stretch that framed the first full week of the season in the worst possible terms. They paused the slide with a victory on April 3 against the San Diego Padres, a result that briefly suggested they might be finding something. Then came three more losses in a row.

The pattern has been consistent enough to be alarming. The Red Sox are losing close games, which means the margin between where they are and where a functioning team would be is not enormous by box score standards. But the aggregate effect has been that Boston cannot finish. Leads disappear in the late innings. Rallies stall with runners on base. The bullpen absorbs blame one night, the offense the next, and occasionally both in the same game.

What Was Expected vs. What Has Happened

Boston entered 2026 as a legitimate AL East contender. The pitching staff was projected as a strength, the lineup had legitimate run-producing depth, and the organization had made moves designed to compete now rather than rebuild for later. None of that has translated to wins.

The pitching, specifically, has been inconsistent at a level that the rotation's projected ceiling did not suggest. A staff built to be a strength has given up leads in ways that compound the damage done by a bullpen that has not provided the kind of reliable late-inning coverage a winning team requires.

Cora's frustration is understandable in that context. He is managing a roster that was assembled to compete and watching it play as if the pieces have not yet been introduced to each other. The 0-3 record in one-run games is particularly telling — those are the games that separate contenders from teams that spend September watching from the outside.

The Historical Marker and What It Means

Tying the franchise's worst nine-game start since 1900 is the kind of record no team wants to share with clubs from more than a century ago. But history also offers some comfort. Teams have recovered from 2-8 starts before. The season is 162 games long, and roughly 150 games remain. A 2-8 team that wins 90 of its remaining games finishes at 92-70, which historically is enough to compete for a postseason spot.

That is the optimistic framing, and it is not wrong. But it assumes a correction is coming, and right now the Red Sox have not shown evidence that the correction has arrived. Until it does, the worst record in baseball belongs to a team that expected to be fighting for a division title.

What's Next

The Red Sox face a stretch of games over the coming week that will begin to define whether this is a rough start or something more systemic. Cora has made clear that roster decisions are on the table if the performance does not improve. The bullpen is likely the first area where changes could come. Boston needs a win streak — not just a single victory — to signal that the season has not already slipped away in April.
