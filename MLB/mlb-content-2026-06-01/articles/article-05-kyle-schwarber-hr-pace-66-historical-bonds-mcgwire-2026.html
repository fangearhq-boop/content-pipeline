Bonds, McGwire, Schwarber: The Historic Company He's Keeping Right Now

By Jake Torres | June 1, 2026

There is a very short list of MLB players who have started a season the way Kyle Schwarber is starting 2026. Through the Philadelphia Phillies' first 49 games, Schwarber has hit 20 home runs — a pace that projects to 66 for the full season and places him in historical territory that only two players have occupied at a faster clip.

Those two players are Barry Bonds in 2001 and Mark McGwire in 1998. Bonds hit 73 home runs that season. McGwire hit 70. Both are in the record books as the two greatest single-season power displays in Major League Baseball history.

What Schwarber is doing right now belongs in the same sentence.

The Historical Context

Schwarber is just the 18th player in MLB history to reach 20 home runs through a team's first 49 games. The last player to accomplish it before him was Josh Hamilton in 2012. The group is elite by definition — you don't reach 20 that quickly without doing something unusual.

What separates Schwarber's current pace from most of that group is his projection. At his current rate, 66 home runs would eclipse Aaron Judge's AL record (62 in 2022) and would rank as the highest home run total by any player since Bonds' 2001 season. The number itself is staggering to consider.

The honest caveat: projecting home run paces from 49 games is not predictive science. Hot starts slow down. Pitchers adjust. Injuries happen. The question is not whether Schwarber will hit 66 — it's whether what he is doing right now represents a historically rare level of power that deserves attention.

The answer to that question is yes.

The Leaderboard Picture

Schwarber stands alone atop the MLB home run leaderboard. Munetaka Murakami of the White Sox had tied him before being placed on the injured list with a hamstring injury earlier this week; Murakami is expected to return in mid-June. Aaron Judge is the next closest active challenger, but the gap is significant.

With Murakami sidelined, Schwarber faces no near-term competition for the home run title. The question of whether anyone catches him will depend on how quickly Murakami returns, how consistent Judge's power output is across the next several weeks, and whether Schwarber himself can maintain the torrid pace.

The Phillies' Context

Philadelphia took the loss in Sunday's series finale against the Dodgers, 9-1. But Schwarber's season is not defined by one game, and the Phillies' performance this weekend does not diminish what their designated hitter is doing in the larger picture.

Schwarber also hit his 350th career home run earlier this season — becoming the ninth active player to reach that milestone. He is having the best power season of his career while simultaneously building toward the top of the all-time leaders list. For a player who was always known for his power, 2026 has elevated him into a different conversation entirely.

What's Next

Every Schwarber at-bat over the next few weeks will carry additional significance. He is the defining offensive story of the early 2026 season, and the question of how long he can sustain this pace will be one of baseball's central narratives through the summer.

Bonds and McGwire are not worried. But they are the only ones who have done what Schwarber is doing right now — and done it better. For the time being, Schwarber is keeping their historical company.
