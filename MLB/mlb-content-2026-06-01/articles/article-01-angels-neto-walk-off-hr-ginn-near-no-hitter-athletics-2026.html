From Near No-Hitter to Walk-Off: Neto's Blast Stuns Ginn and the A's

By Marcus Cole | June 1, 2026

J.T. Ginn should have won. He had thrown 8 no-hit innings, struck out 10 batters, used 105 pitches to build a 1-0 lead, and was three outs from one of the most celebrated performances in baseball. Then Adam Frazier put a ball on the infield grass to lead off the ninth, and everything changed.

Three pitches later, Zach Neto drove a 2-0 sinker 413 feet to center field. Two-run walk-off homer. Angels 2, Athletics 1. In the span of one half-inning, Ginn went from pitching the game of his life to absorbing a heartbreaking loss.

Ginn's Eight Masterful Innings

Through eight innings, Ginn was dominant by any measure. He fanned the side in the seventh. He allowed one walk and hit Neto with a pitch in the sixth — the only two baserunners he put on all game. He was working efficiently with pinpoint command, and his 64 strikes in 105 pitches reflected a pitcher who was not laboring.

The A's had taken a 1-0 lead into the ninth, and it appeared sufficient. No team had touched Ginn, and the Angels' lineup had done nothing to suggest the ninth would be different.

Ginn became just the sixth MLB pitcher since at least 1974 to allow no hits and no runs through eight innings and still take the loss, joining Rich Hill (Dodgers, 2017) as the most recent before him. For a pitcher who delivered such a performance, the outcome was almost unbearably unjust.

The Ninth-Inning Collapse

The exact details of the Angels' ninth-inning rally are straightforward in description but hard to believe in context. Frazier lined a leadoff single to break the no-hitter. With the crowd suddenly alive and the Angels' dugout energized, Neto dug in against a Ginn fastball sequence and turned on a 2-0 sinker.

The ball carried 413 feet to center field. Two runners scored. Game over.

According to baseball historians, this was only the third time since 1961 that a team was no-hit through at least 8 innings and won the game on a walk-off homer. The two previous instances: Don Lock of the Washington Senators in 1963, and Josh Harrison of the Pittsburgh Pirates in 2017 (in extra innings). Neto's blast joins that select group.

What It Means for Both Teams

For the Angels, the win broke a six-game losing streak and provided an emotional jolt to a team trying to stay relevant in the AL West. The comeback also preserved an under-the-radar team record: Los Angeles hasn't been no-hit since September 11, 1999 — the longest active streak in Major League Baseball. Neto's ninth-inning single and home run ensured that streak continues for at least another game.

For the A's and Ginn, the loss is the kind that lingers. Ginn threw a career-high 105 pitches, posted a career-high 10 strikeouts, and was within three outs of a no-hitter. His effort deserved a better result. Baseball has its own logic, and sometimes that logic is brutal.

What's Next

The Angels will look to build on the momentum of a signature win — particularly one that came in such unlikely fashion. Zach Neto's walk-off homer is the moment of the series, and of the early June schedule.

For J.T. Ginn, the challenge will be processing an outcome that sits in a unique category. He did everything right for eight innings. The baseball gods had other ideas in the ninth.
