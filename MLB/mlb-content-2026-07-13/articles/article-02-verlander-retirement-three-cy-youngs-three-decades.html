Three Cy Youngs. Three Decades. Justin Verlander Is Retiring.

By Ryan Calloway | Ballpark Banter | July 13, 2026

Justin Verlander announced this week that he will retire at the end of the 2026 season. Commissioner Rob Manfred honored the announcement by designating Verlander as the AL "Legend Pick" — a special selection giving him his 10th All-Star Game appearance. He is currently on the 60-day injured list with hip and hamstring injuries. He may not pitch again.

"I've realized that time has come."

That was the retirement statement. It said everything that needed to be said.

The Numbers

Verlander's career line: 266 wins, 159 losses, 3.33 ERA, 3,554 strikeouts, 21 seasons. He was a 10-time All-Star, a three-time Cy Young Award winner, a two-time World Series champion, and a 2011 AL MVP — unanimous, with the Pitching Triple Crown in the same year.

Three Cy Youngs in three different decades. He won his first in 2011, when he was 28 and at the height of his initial prime. He won his second in 2019, when he was 36 and had become something different — not a flamethrower, but a craftsman who had reinvented himself in Houston with Statcast and spin rates and pitch design. He won his third in 2022, when he was 39, having returned from Tommy John surgery and joined the New York Mets before coming back to Detroit for his final chapter.

The Tommy John surgery at 35 should have ended his career. It didn't. He came back at 37, picked up where he left off, and won a Cy Young two years later. That story — the second act, the third act, the refusal to go quietly — is as much a part of his legacy as any single season statistic.

Detroit, Then and Now

Verlander was drafted by the Detroit Tigers in the first round of the 2004 MLB Draft. He made his debut in 2005. He spent most of his career there, building a legacy that includes the 2011 AL MVP, a no-hitter, and the years when Detroit was a legitimate World Series contender with a rotation that included Verlander, Max Scherzer, and Doug Fister.

He was traded to the Houston Astros in 2017 and won a World Series that same season, adding another ring in 2022 with the Astros as well. He spent one year with the Mets. And then he came back to Detroit, where he started and where, now, it ends.

He is finishing his career with the organization that gave him his first chance. That is not nothing.

What the Legend Pick Means

The Commissioner's "Legend Pick" is a designation intended to give the game's great retiring players a farewell All-Star platform. Verlander's 10th selection allows him to stand on the field at Citizens Bank Park during Tuesday's festivities, in front of a baseball audience, one more time.

He is on the injured list. He cannot pitch. But he will be there. That matters for a player who gave the game 21 seasons, who reinvented himself more than once, and who went out not on a bad year but on an injury — the kind of ending that feels like a misfire on the last pitch rather than a slow fade.

What's Next

The 2026 MLB All-Star Game is tomorrow, July 14, at Citizens Bank Park in Philadelphia. Verlander will be part of the proceedings as the AL's Legend Pick. His formal retirement takes effect at the end of the 2026 season. Whether he pitches again before then remains uncertain. What is not uncertain is the career he leaves behind: 266 wins, three Cy Youngs, and a story that refuses the simple narrative of a single peak.
