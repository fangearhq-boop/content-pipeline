Cam Schlittler's Numbers Haven't Been Seen Since Walter Johnson in 1913

By Marcus Cole | May 17, 2026

Walter Johnson finished the 1913 season with a 1.14 ERA, 243 strikeouts, and a record that cemented his status as the defining pitcher of his era. He was 25 years old. He pitched for the Washington Senators. He was, by every available metric, the best starting pitcher in baseball.

One hundred and thirteen years later, a 25-year-old pitcher for the New York Yankees is producing numbers through his first nine starts that reference the same era.

Cam Schlittler has a 1.35 ERA, a 5-1 record, 59 strikeouts, 9 walks, one home run allowed, and a 0.81 WHIP through nine starts. He is the first pitcher in over a century — since Walter Johnson in 1913 — to post that combination of metrics through the first nine starts of a season. The comparison is not manufactured. It's what the numbers produce when you compare Schlittler's 2026 to baseball's complete historical record.

The Numbers, in Context

It's worth sitting with each of these statistics individually before combining them into a historical argument.

A 1.35 ERA through nine starts is exceptional in any era of baseball — a rate that would, if sustained, rank among the best single-season ERA marks in the modern game. Through nine starts, it suggests Schlittler is not merely lucky. He's not benefiting from an exceptional strand rate or a schedule of weak opponents. He's been dominant across a meaningful sample.

Fifty-nine strikeouts in nine starts is an elite strikeout rate. Nine walks — barely one per start — is an elite walk rate. The combination of 59 strikeouts and 9 walks produces a strikeout-to-walk ratio that exceeds anything most starters achieve in a full season, let alone through nine games.

One home run allowed through nine starts means Schlittler is not getting hit for extra bases. His pitch mix and command are preventing hitters from squaring up the ball with authority — the hallmark of a pitcher who understands what he's doing every time he takes the mound.

These numbers together? The last person to match them through the same milestone was pitching against batters who had never seen a slider.

The Subway Series Showcase

Schlittler's latest start came Friday in Game 1 of the Subway Series against the Mets at Citi Field. Even in a game where he allowed Juan Soto's 250th career home run — one of the most historic at-bats of the 2026 season — he was dominant. The Yankees won 5-2. Schlittler pitched deep into the game, maintaining his command and executing his pitch plan against a Mets lineup that has several difficult hitters to navigate.

The Soto home run was the exception in a start full of the kind of execution that has defined Schlittler's 2026. Great pitchers give up great hits from great hitters. What matters is whether those moments are isolated or routine. For Schlittler, through nine starts, the great hits against him have been isolated.

What This Means for the Yankees

The New York Yankees did not need Cam Schlittler to become a historic figure in order to be competitive in 2026. They had Aaron Judge's power, Juan Soto departed for the Mets, and a roster built to compete. What they got instead was a 25-year-old starting pitcher whose ERA reference point requires a three-digit historical search.

Schlittler's performance has stabilized the Yankees' rotation and given them a reliable game-by-game anchor that teams win on. When you know every five days you're going to get a pitcher who allows fewer than two runs and rarely walks anyone, you can build a winning team around that certainty.

What's Next

Cam Schlittler's next start will come in roughly five days, at which point he'll carry a resume that baseball historians will be citing for years. Whether he can sustain it through the summer — into the heat of a pennant race, against lineups that have had time to build additional data on his approach — is the question that will define whether 2026 becomes a legendary season or the first chapter of something even larger. For now, the numbers say what they say: Schlittler is having the most historically dominant pitching start in over a century. And Yankee fans should be watching every inning.
