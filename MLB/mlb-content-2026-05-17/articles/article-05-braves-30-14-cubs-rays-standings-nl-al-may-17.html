Braves Lead Baseball at 30-14, Cubs and Rays Own Their Divisions: Weekend Standings Check

By Jake Torres | May 17, 2026

The third week of May has concluded, and the shape of the 2026 baseball season is beginning to clarify. Some division races are wide open, some are already tilting heavily, and a few teams that were supposed to matter are not where anyone expected them to be.

Here's where things stand, division by division, with the weekend's results fully incorporated.

National League East: Braves Lead Baseball at 30-14

The Atlanta Braves have the best record in all of baseball at 30-14. That's not just an NL East lead — it's the most convincing argument that any team in the sport has made for division dominance through the first six weeks of the season.

Atlanta has been built around sustained offensive production and a rotation that keeps games close. The Braves enter Sunday with a comfortable cushion in the NL East, and their overall record suggests they're playing the most complete baseball in the National League right now.

The most compelling subplot in the NL East is Philadelphia's resurgence. The Phillies — 14-4 under interim manager Don Mattingly — reached .500 on Saturday for the first time since mid-April. A roster that seemed to be fading has reversed direction dramatically. Whether that Mattingly-era surge is sustainable determines whether the NL East becomes a two-team race by summer.

National League Central: Cubs Lead at 28-16

Chicago is having a season that Cubs fans have been waiting for. At 28-16, the Cubs lead the NL Central by a comfortable margin and are playing the kind of consistent winning baseball that doesn't require any single player to carry the load. This team distributes its production across the lineup and rotation in a way that's harder to disrupt than a squad built around one or two stars.

The Cubs won the Crosstown Classic against the White Sox earlier in the weekend — a rivalry game that always means more than the standings suggest it should. The wins in rivalry matchups reflect something about a team's health and confidence that regular-season games sometimes obscure. The Cubs looked like the better team in Chicago, and right now, they are.

American League East: Rays at 29-14

The Tampa Bay Rays continue to do what the Rays always seem to do: win games that by salary, market size, and resource comparison they shouldn't necessarily be winning at this frequency. At 29-14, Tampa Bay leads the AL East — a division that includes the Yankees, Red Sox, Blue Jays, and Orioles — and they've done it without the kind of flashy individual performances that typically drive narrative around a division leader.

The Rays' success is structural. They develop pitching. They find undervalued position players. They optimize matchups with a coaching staff that has mastered the analytical side of roster management. It works in April, it works in May, and if history is any guide, it will still be working in July when the division race gets serious.

The Yankees, meanwhile, have Cam Schlittler producing one of the most historically dominant starting pitching performances in over a century. A Schlittler-led rotation with a healthy Judge-era lineup should make the AL East a contested race through the summer months.

What's Next

Sunday closes the weekend with meaningful games across all divisions. The Subway Series finale between the Yankees and Mets at Citi Field is the marquee matchup. As the season moves toward Memorial Day — traditionally the first unofficial checkpoint for separating contenders from pretenders — these division races will sharpen further. The Braves, Cubs, and Rays have established themselves as the teams to beat in their respective divisions. The question for the next four weeks is whether anyone in their divisions can mount a meaningful challenge before the trade deadline changes the calculus entirely.
