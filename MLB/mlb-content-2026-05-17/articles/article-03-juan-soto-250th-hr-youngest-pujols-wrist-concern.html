Soto's 250th HR Joins Baseball's Most Elite History — But Mets Are Watching His Wrist

By Ryan Calloway | May 17, 2026

The ball left Juan Soto's bat at 102.6 miles per hour, climbed at a 28-degree launch angle, and landed 393 feet away in dead center at Citi Field. Career home run No. 250. The Citi Field crowd erupted. The milestone was real, and it was extraordinary.

Three innings earlier, Soto had swung and missed a Cam Schlittler cutter and grimaced. His right wrist. He stayed in the game. He hit the home run. The Yankees won 5-2. And the wrist remained a conversation that no celebration could entirely quiet.

That's where the New York Mets find themselves on May 17, 2026 — celebrating a generational achievement from their franchise player while holding their collective breath about whether he can stay healthy enough to build on it.

Where 250 Home Runs at 27 Places You

Juan Soto is 27 years old. His 250th career home run — hit off Schlittler, the Yankees' dominant starter, in the seventh inning of a Subway Series game at his own ballpark — places him in conversation that most players never enter.

He is the youngest player to reach 250 career home runs since Albert Pujols. He joins an extraordinarily short list of players who have combined 250-plus home runs with 95-plus stolen bases before their 28th birthday — a group that includes Alex Rodriguez, Ken Griffey Jr., Mickey Mantle, and Mike Trout. The names on that list are not comparable to ordinary baseball careers. They are the players history remembers as the best to ever play the game.

Soto is in that conversation at 27. The trajectory of his career, if it continues without interruption, points toward a Hall of Fame induction that would be among the most celebrated in generations.

The Wrist Concern

Baseball history is filled with players whose trajectories bent or broke because of injuries to the structures that generate swing power. Wrist injuries, in particular, are among the most consequential for hitters — the mechanics of a baseball swing load enormous force through the wrists, and damage to those structures can alter a swing for months or permanently.

Juan Soto's grimace on a missed swing three innings before his historic home run was not ignored. He kept playing — the competitive drive that defines his approach to the game is not in question — but he was clearly experiencing discomfort in a body part that matters enormously for a hitter of his profile.

The Mets' medical staff and front office will be monitoring the situation carefully. Whether it's a minor issue that resolves with rest and treatment, or something that requires more intervention, will define the next several weeks of the Mets' season. A healthy Soto makes the Mets a different kind of team. An impaired Soto still plays, but the risk of compounding a wrist injury by playing through it is real and worth watching.

The Subway Series Context

The game itself — Yankees 5, Mets 2 — was a showcase for Cam Schlittler's continued dominance. The Yankees starter was the better pitcher Friday, though Soto's seventh-inning home run provided the Mets' most dramatic moment. New York City's Subway Series always carries outsized emotional stakes, and Soto's 250th home run delivered the kind of moment that fans on both sides will remember — though for very different reasons.

The series continues with Sunday's finale at Citi Field. The Mets need a win to avoid losing the series. What they need most, regardless of the series result, is a healthy Juan Soto heading into the second half of May.

What's Next

The Mets and Yankees play Sunday's Subway Series finale at Citi Field at 1:40 PM ET. Soto's availability and health status will be the storyline to watch in the coming days. For the 250th home run itself, the milestone is in the books — a permanent entry in a career that is, by any historical measure, already one of the most productive in baseball. The question now is whether that career can continue at full strength, with the wrist healthy and the swing as mechanically pure as it's been when Soto is at his best.
