### STORY 1: Kyle Tucker Back Spasms — Dodgers

**Long-Form Post**
DODGERS INJURY WATCH: KYLE TUCKER EXITS MONDAY'S GAME WITH BACK SPASMS

This is the kind of news no Dodgers fan wants to see — even when the team wins.

Kyle Tucker, the $240 million outfielder the Dodgers brought in to push them over the top, left Monday night's game against the Minnesota Twins in the second inning with low back spasms. He was warming up for defense between innings in the first when he felt it.

"Back just lit up," Tucker told SportsNet LA's Kristen Watson after the game.

The team listed him as day-to-day and described the exit as completely precautionary. Manager Dave Roberts said he wasn't overly concerned. The Dodgers won the game anyway, 2-1.

But Tucker wasn't the only Dodger who left early. Catcher Dalton Rushing also exited Monday's game for concussion testing. Two starters out in the same game, and the World Series favorites still took the W.

The reassuring part: Grade 1 MCL sprains, back spasms, day-to-day designations. These are manageable injuries for a roster this deep. The Dodgers don't panic over these things.

The less reassuring part: it's June. The trade deadline is August 3. The All-Star break is July 14. Tucker is a central piece of a World Series run, and "day-to-day" is always one flare-up away from becoming "10-day IL."

We'll be watching this one closely.

### STORY 2: Schwarber at 29 HRs — Phillies

**Long-Form Post**
KYLE SCHWARBER IS ON A HOME RUN RAMPAGE — AND THE HISTORY BOOKS ARE IN SIGHT

Let's take stock of where Kyle Schwarber stands right now: 29 home runs, leads all of MLB, and on pace for 63+ on the season.

To put that in perspective: the 60-home run club has exactly five members in the history of baseball.

Barry Bonds hit 73 in 2001. Mark McGwire hit 70 in 1998 and 65 in 1999. Sammy Sosa hit 66 in 1998, 65 in 2001, and 63 in 1999. Roger Maris hit 61 in 1961. Babe Ruth hit 60 in 1927.

Schwarber is chasing a list that includes the most controversial and most revered power hitters in the sport's history.

He's doing it in Philadelphia, at Citizens Bank Park, for the first time since this lineup first started clicking. He's doing it heading toward a Home Run Derby that will be held at Citizens Bank Park on July 14 — his home ballpark, in front of his fans.

The context that makes this even more remarkable: Schwarber set a career-high with 56 home runs last season. He came into 2026 and got better. He's already at 29 heading into late June.

Yordan Alvarez is No. 2 in the home run race at 25. The gap is growing.

At some point in the next few weeks, we may need to stop framing this as "can he do it?" and start treating 60 HRs as a reasonable expectation. That's where this is headed.

### STORY 5: Bobby Witt Jr. Injury Update

**Long-Form Post**
WHERE IS BOBBY WITT JR.?

Royals fans are asking the question that matters most right now: will their franchise player be on the IL when rosters are sorted out today?

Bobby Witt Jr. — 26 years old, the AL's most exciting young shortstop, and the front-runner in the AL MVP race with 4.5 fWAR — has been dealing with a Grade 1 MCL sprain in his right knee since sliding for a ball in a game against the St. Louis Cardinals last week.

Manager Matt Quatraro called the diagnosis "best case scenario" for the injury type. MCL sprains come in three grades: Grade 1 is the mildest — a ligament stretch without a tear. The medial meniscus is intact. The typical recovery for a Grade 1 is one to three weeks of limited activity.

Witt was held out of Sunday's game against the Cardinals. The Royals said he'd be reevaluated before Monday's series opener against the Tampa Bay Rays. Maikel Garcia has been covering at shortstop in the interim.

Here's the tension: Phase 1 of MLB All-Star voting closes Thursday, June 25. If Witt can't demonstrate he's healthy this week, the conversation around his All-Star appearance (July 14 in Philadelphia) gets complicated. A 10-day IL stint starting today would have him eligible to return July 3.

For now, the Royals are hoping day-to-day stays day-to-day. The AL MVP race — and the All-Star ballot — are watching.
