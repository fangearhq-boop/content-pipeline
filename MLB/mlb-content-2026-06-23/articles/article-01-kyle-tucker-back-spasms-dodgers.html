Kyle Tucker's Back Spasms: Dodgers Lose Star Early, Win Anyway

By Marcus Cole

June 23, 2026

Kyle Tucker left Monday night's game against the Minnesota Twins in the second inning. Low back spasms. He described feeling it while going out for defense between innings — "back just lit up," he told SportsNet LA's Kristen Watson after the game. The team listed him as day-to-day and called the exit "completely precautionary." The Dodgers won anyway, 2-1.

For the Los Angeles Dodgers, a 2-1 win while your $240 million outfielder limps off the field early is about as good an outcome as you can expect. For fans watching the calendar — trade deadline August 3, All-Star break July 14, postseason somewhere in October — it's a reminder that no team is immune to the attrition of a 162-game season, not even the clear World Series favorite.

What Happened

Tucker walked with one out in the second inning and ran gingerly to second on a single to right field by Tommy Edman. He appeared uncomfortable throughout. The Dodgers held him out for the remainder of the game.

Catcher Dalton Rushing also left the same game — separately — for concussion testing. Two starters, one game, two injuries. Manager Dave Roberts addressed the media after the game and said he wasn't overly concerned about either situation, but offered no timeline for Tucker's return.

"Completely precautionary" is the language the team used. Day-to-day is the designation. Those are the best-case framings for this kind of situation, and the Dodgers appear to mean them.

Why It Matters

Tucker is not replaceable. He was brought to Los Angeles on a $240 million deal specifically because the Dodgers wanted another dangerous bat in a lineup that already had Shohei Ohtani, Freddie Freeman, and Mookie Betts. His absence for any extended period changes the calculus of how opposing pitchers attack this lineup.

The timing matters too. The Dodgers are in the middle of a pennant race, approaching the trade deadline, and entering a period of the season where roster management becomes increasingly important. Back injuries have a way of turning day-to-day designations into longer stints if they're not handled properly. The Dodgers' medical staff will be cautious — which is the right approach, even if it means missed games.

The Dodgers' Depth Remains the Story

Here's what the Dodgers' 2-1 win Monday told us: they can absorb short-term injury losses. Their depth — built through years of aggressive roster construction — gives them options that most organizations don't have. When Tucker exits in the second inning, other players step in, the pitching staff executes, and the team wins.

That depth is also why the Dodgers are such dangerous trade deadline buyers. They're not looking to fill gaping holes. They're looking to add to an already strong roster. Skubal or any other starter they add would be the fifth or sixth piece, not the cornerstone.

The injury watch is worth following. But don't read too much into one day-to-day designation for a player with no structural damage reported.

What's Next

Tucker will be reassessed daily. Roberts has said he expects the outfielder to be fine in the near term, and the team doesn't appear to be considering the 10-day IL at this stage. If he plays Tuesday against the Twins, the story ends here. If he misses additional games, the conversation about a potential IL stint begins.

Catcher Dalton Rushing's concussion testing results will also determine his availability in the coming days. The Dodgers play a full schedule through the All-Star break, and they'll want their full roster healthy heading into the second half.

For now: Tucker is day-to-day. The Dodgers won. The injury watch begins.
