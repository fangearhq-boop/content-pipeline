Schwarber at 29: Can He Really Hit 60 Home Runs?

By Jake Torres

June 23, 2026

Kyle Schwarber leads all of Major League Baseball with 29 home runs heading into June 23. His pace projects to 63 on the season — a number that would place him among the most elite power hitters in baseball history. The 60-home run club has five members: Barry Bonds, Mark McGwire, Sammy Sosa, Roger Maris, and Babe Ruth. Schwarber is chasing a list that most people assumed was permanently closed.

He's doing it for the Philadelphia Phillies. At Citizens Bank Park. Where the 2026 Home Run Derby will be held on July 14.

The History He's Chasing

Let's be precise about what "the 60-home run club" means, because the history here matters.

Babe Ruth hit 60 in 1927. Roger Maris hit 61 in 1961 — breaking Ruth's record in a season that sparked national controversy about expanded schedules and the "asterisk" debate. Then the Steroid Era arrived and the record fell multiple times: Sammy Sosa hit 63 in 1999, 65 in 2001, and 66 in 1998. Mark McGwire hit 65 in 1999 and the landmark 70 in 1998. Barry Bonds hit 73 in 2001, a record that still stands.

Schwarber set his own career-high last season with 56 home runs. He's already at 29 in late June. If he maintains this pace, he would become only the eighth season in baseball history — and the first without steroid-era controversy attached to it — to reach 60.

The Recent Run That Made People Pay Attention

This wasn't a slow build. Schwarber rocked a three-home run game against the New York Mets on Saturday, hitting his 26th, 27th, and 28th of the season in a single game. The following day, he hit his 29th. A four-home run weekend pushed him to 29 with the season barely past its midpoint.

Yordan Alvarez is the closest pursuer at 25 home runs. The gap is four, and it's growing at a rate that should concern everyone hoping to see a real race. Schwarber is not slowing down. The pitching quality across the league doesn't seem to matter when a ball is in his swing zone. His launch angle data and exit velocity numbers suggest a hitter in the most locked-in stretch of his career.

The Derby Factor

The 2026 Home Run Derby is July 14 at Citizens Bank Park in Philadelphia. That's Schwarber's ballpark. That's his crowd. If he enters the Derby at his current pace, the combination of storylines — hometown slugger, historical HR pace, defending champion Phillies, Midsummer Classic at CBP — creates one of the great individual sports moments of the summer.

Derby participation sometimes affects a hitter's second-half production. The data on this is mixed — some hitters swing out of their shoes for two days and come back sharp, others don't. For Schwarber, whose swing is already tuned to maximum power output, the risk might be lower than for a typical hitter. But it's a conversation worth having if he's sitting at 35-plus home runs when July arrives.

Can He Do It?

Pace-based projections are inherently uncertain. A hitter can run hot for two months and cool off significantly in the second half. Pitchers adjust. Weather patterns shift. Slumps happen. All of these variables make 63-HR projections in June more optimistic than reliable.

But the answer to "can he do it?" is: yes, there's a real path. He'd need to roughly maintain his current home run rate through September — which is exactly what the pace projection assumes. It's not guaranteed. It's not even likely, in the sense that most "historically elite pace" seasons moderate somewhat. But Schwarber has shown this season that he is capable of sustained power production at a rate that puts 60 in his range of outcomes.

What's Next

The next milestone to watch: home run No. 30, which would make Schwarber the first Phillies player to reach 30 HRs before the All-Star break in recent memory. Watch his Home Run Derby participation announcement — and whether Philadelphia's home fans pack Citizens Bank Park for a Derby that might feature the hottest home run hitter in baseball doing his thing in front of his own crowd.

The 60-HR conversation will intensify after the All-Star break if Schwarber stays on pace. For now, the number to remember is 29, the date to mark is July 14, and the question to keep asking is: how far can he go?
