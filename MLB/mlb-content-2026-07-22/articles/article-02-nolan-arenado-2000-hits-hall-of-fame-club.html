The Six-Player Club. The Five Hall of Famers. And Nolan Arenado.

By Jake Torres | Ballpark Banter | July 22, 2026

On July 20, in the fifth inning of a game in Phoenix against the Oakland Athletics, Nolan Arenado lined a double into the outfield for his 2,000th career hit. He rounded first, pulled into second, and became the sixth player in baseball history to reach a specific combination of numbers that baseball statisticians have long used as a proxy for the sport's most complete careers.

The combination: 2,000 or more hits, 350 or more home runs, and 10 or more Gold Gloves. It is a cross-section of offensive production and defensive excellence sustained across more than a decade. Only six players have ever done all three. The other five — Ken Griffey Jr., Willie Mays, Mike Schmidt, Johnny Bench, and Al Kaline — are all in the National Baseball Hall of Fame in Cooperstown, New York.

Who Is in This Club

The list is worth sitting with. Willie Mays is widely considered the greatest all-around player in baseball history. Johnny Bench is the standard by which every catcher is measured. Ken Griffey Jr. defined a generation. Mike Schmidt is the consensus greatest third baseman in the sport's history. Al Kaline played 22 seasons for the Detroit Tigers and never played a day in the minor leagues.

Nolan Arenado is now on that list. He is the only third baseman in the club. He is the only active player.

At the moment the double landed, Arenado's career line stood at .280/.337/.503. He had driven in 1,228 runs and scored 1,038 more. He had 366 home runs — 16 over the 350-HR threshold — and 424 doubles. He had won 10 Gold Gloves, 5 Silver Sluggers, and appeared in 8 All-Star Games.

The Schmidt Connection

Arenado has spoken often about Mike Schmidt. He considers Schmidt the greatest third baseman who ever played. After reaching the milestone, Arenado noted that Schmidt "was the first to say, 'I love the way this guy plays.'" That kind of direct acknowledgment from the man he measured himself against carries its own weight.

Standing beside Schmidt in this six-player club is not a coincidence. Arenado's game — the ability to hit for both average and power while playing defense as well as anyone who has ever stood at the hot corner — is precisely the profile that lands a player in a club this small.

The Hall of Fame Case

Hall of Fame debates are often circular. Writers and voters disagree about how to weigh career length, peak value, and defensive contributions. Arenado's case has always had a straightforward rebuttal to any skeptic: the defensive record is unprecedented at third base, and the offensive numbers now support it with the kind of volume that no longer requires a qualifier.

The 2,000-hit/350-HR/10-Gold-Glove club is not a made-up threshold designed to flatter any one player. It emerged from the historical record because it is extraordinarily difficult to achieve. Every player who has done it belongs in Cooperstown. The sixth member is still active and still playing at a high level in 2026.

What's Next

Arenado and the Arizona Diamondbacks continue their schedule through the second half of the 2026 season. The milestone conversation will follow him the rest of the year and into the offseason.
