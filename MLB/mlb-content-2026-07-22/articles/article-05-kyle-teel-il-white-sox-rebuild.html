The White Sox Lost 121 Games Two Years Ago. Now They Lead the AL Central. Kyle Teel's Injury Is the First Real Test.

By Jake Torres | Ballpark Banter | July 22, 2026

The Chicago White Sox have placed catcher Kyle Teel on the 10-day injured list with a left high ankle sprain. The organization's projected recovery timeline is 6 to 12 weeks — a window that almost certainly extends through the end of the regular season. Edgar Quero has been called up from the minors to serve as the replacement. Teel, for all practical purposes, is done for 2026.

This matters because of where the White Sox sit. Chicago enters Wednesday 52-46, leading the AL Central division. They are one of the most surprising teams in baseball this year, and Teel was a meaningful part of why.

Where This Team Came From

Two seasons ago, the Chicago White Sox went 121-41. That is the worst record in the history of Major League Baseball. It was not a close race for last place. It was a historic collapse of organizational depth that bottomed out on a number that will follow this franchise for decades.

Last season, the White Sox went 60-102. Still bad. Still rebuilding. Still not competing.

This season, they lead the AL Central with more than a third of the schedule remaining. The turnaround has been one of the most compelling stories in the sport in 2026. It is not the story of a team getting lucky in a weak division — though the AL Central has been historically mediocre. It is the story of a rebuild that has started producing real major-league players.

Colson Montgomery and What the Rebuild Has Produced

The clearest evidence of the rebuild's output is at third base. Colson Montgomery is making history at the plate in his second major-league season. He has hit 21 home runs in 2026, and he is now the first player in MLB history to hit 20 or more home runs within his first 275 at-bats in each of his first two major-league seasons. That is not a platitude about a young player with potential. It is a documented historical first.

Montgomery is the kind of player a six-year rebuild is supposed to produce. He arrived with significant minor-league pedigree and has exceeded it in the major leagues. He represents what Chicago was building toward during the losing years.

What Teel's Absence Changes

Teel was a key offensive contributor to a lineup that had been one of the most productive in the AL Central. His absence removes a bat from a rotation that will now need to compensate through other sources. Edgar Quero, his replacement, is a legitimate prospect — but there is a reason Teel was the starter and Quero was in the minors.

The question is not whether the White Sox can survive Teel's absence. Baseball teams absorb injuries. The question is whether this specific team — a team built through a multi-year rebuild, still proving itself over a full season for the first time — is deep enough to absorb it without losing the division lead.

The AL Central standings in late July will provide the first real answer. This is the test the rebuild has been waiting for.

What's Next

Chicago continues its 2026 schedule as AL Central leaders. Teel will undergo continued evaluation; if the ankle heals faster than projected, a September return is possible but not expected. Ballpark Banter will track the White Sox standings position and Quero's adjustment through the deadline and beyond. This is the most important story in the AL Central right now.
