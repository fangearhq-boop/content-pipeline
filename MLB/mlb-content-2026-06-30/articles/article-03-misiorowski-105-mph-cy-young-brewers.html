Jacob Misiorowski Threw 105.5 MPH — And He Might Win the NL Cy Young Award

By Ryan Calloway June 30, 2026

The Velocity That Nobody Noticed

On June 26, 2026, Jacob Misiorowski threw a pitch against the Chicago Cubs that registered at 105.5 miles per hour. The Milwaukee Brewers won 6-2. The pitch tied for the 3rd-fastest ever recorded in Major League Baseball history, alongside Ben Joyce's mark.

It was a Tuesday afternoon. It happened in Milwaukee. National baseball media was primarily focused on the AL East series playing out in other stadiums. Misiorowski's 105.5 MPH reading got a video on MLB.com and a brief mention from velocity-tracking accounts. The game continued. The Brewers won. And one of the most remarkable single-pitch moments of the 2026 season went mostly unnoticed outside Wisconsin.

That is the story of Jacob Misiorowski's 2026 season in miniature form.

The Numbers Are Not Close

Misiorowski leads Major League Baseball in three categories entering July. His ERA is 1.45 — lower than anyone else in the sport. His WHIP is 0.75 — lower than anyone else in the sport. His strikeout total is 138 — more than anyone else in the sport. His opponents' batting average is .146.

Over his last nine starts, covering 60.1 innings, Misiorowski allowed three earned runs total. Three. In nine starts. Against Major League lineups, facing the adjustment pitchers typically see as hitters accumulate data on their tendencies across a full season.

He is 24 years old. These are not the numbers of a veteran who knows every trick in the craft. These are the numbers of a young pitcher throwing the baseball at historically elite velocity and command simultaneously.

The History-Making Milestone

105.5 MPH is not just a number that shows up in velocity tracking. It is a threshold almost nobody reaches. The list of pitchers who have thrown 105 or above in the modern era is short enough to count on one hand. Misiorowski is now on it, tied for the third spot in the record books.

What makes the velocity significant in context is what it does to opposing hitters. A fastball at 105.5 MPH with the command Misiorowski demonstrates — 0.75 WHIP means his walks are at an extremely low rate — is not something hitters can time with normal adjustments. They cannot sit on the fastball and wait for it, because the off-speed pitches arrive at a meaningful enough gap in velocity to make that strategy untenable.

He and Brewers outfielder Jackson Chourio were named NL Co-Players of the Week for June 8-14 — the first time two Milwaukee Brewers have shared that honor in MLB history. Two history-making moments in a single month, from a pitcher and a team that most national coverage skips past.

The Cy Young Case

The argument for Misiorowski as the NL Cy Young Award winner is built on the numbers, and the numbers are overwhelming. ERA, WHIP, strikeouts — he leads MLB in all three. The argument against him is circumstantial: he plays in Milwaukee, not a media center. His team is not in a pennant race that would bring his name up weekly in national coverage. The sample size question — can he sustain a 1.45 ERA through the second half? — is fair, but it applies to every pitcher on an awards ballot and cannot be used as a disqualifier in June.

The Cy Young is supposed to go to the best pitcher in the National League. By every quantifiable measure available through the end of June, that pitcher is Jacob Misiorowski of the Milwaukee Brewers.

What's Next

The second half begins in three days. Misiorowski's numbers will face their hardest test: the adjustment cycle, the grind of July and August starts, and the accumulation of data that opposing teams spend months gathering. If he continues pitching at anything close to his first-half level, the NL Cy Young conversation will not be able to stay in Milwaukee any longer. The award will find him — or he will have earned it by such a wide margin that ignoring him becomes impossible.
