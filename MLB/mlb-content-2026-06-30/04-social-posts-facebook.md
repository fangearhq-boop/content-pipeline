### STORY 2: Aaron Judge Recovery

**Long-Form Post**
Aaron Judge played six weeks with a stress fracture in his rib before he would admit it was time to stop.

"I fought as long as I could."

That's what he told reporters after being placed on the injured list on June 5 with a right first rib stress fracture. The injury is believed to have occurred on April 26 — while Judge was diving for a fly ball during a Yankees-Astros series — and he kept playing through it for nearly six weeks before the diagnosis caught up with him.

A stress fracture in the rib does not heal by playing through it. But it also does not sideline a player who has the Yankees' history and Judge's mentality without a fight.

The medical reality is straightforward: re-imaging is expected in approximately 4-6 weeks from the June 5 diagnosis, putting the evaluation in early-to-mid July. A return before August is considered unlikely. The earliest the Yankees could realistically have him back in the lineup is sometime in August, which means he will miss somewhere between 8 and 12 weeks of the season.

The broader consequence for New York is significant. The Yankees are already without Giancarlo Stanton and Austin Wells — that's three regulars on the IL simultaneously. Manager Aaron Boone has said there's no firm timetable on Judge's re-imaging, which means even the August target is a best-case estimate rather than a schedule.

For the AL MVP race, Judge's absence opens the floor. Yordan Alvarez has been consistent and productive for Houston. Bobby Witt Jr. returned from his MCL sprain and is chasing the kind of season that wins MVP awards. With the Yankees' most visible offensive presence off the field, the conversation around the AL's best player is genuinely open in a way it wasn't two weeks ago.

The Yankees need him back. The question is whether he comes back early enough to matter.

### STORY 3: Misiorowski's 105.5 MPH and Cy Young Case

**Long-Form Post**
On June 26, Jacob Misiorowski threw a pitch at 105.5 miles per hour against the Chicago Cubs. It is the third-fastest pitch ever recorded in MLB history, tying Ben Joyce's mark. The Brewers won 6-2. It was Tuesday afternoon in Milwaukee. Nobody outside of the Midwest was paying much attention.

That is the thing about Jacob Misiorowski's 2026 season: almost everything he does flies under the national radar, even when it is historically remarkable.

His numbers through June 30 are extraordinary. He leads Major League Baseball in ERA at 1.45. He leads MLB in WHIP at 0.75. He leads MLB in strikeouts with 138. His opponents' batting average is .146. Over his last nine starts — 60.1 innings — he allowed three total earned runs.

He is 24 years old. He pitches for the Milwaukee Brewers. He is not in New York, Los Angeles, or Boston. That matters for national attention, even when it should not matter for an awards conversation.

The NL Cy Young Award is supposed to go to the best pitcher in the National League. The best pitcher in the National League, by every quantifiable measure available right now, is Jacob Misiorowski. His ERA lead, his strikeout lead, his WHIP lead — these are not close races. He is running away from the field.

The case against him is primarily circumstantial: he plays in a smaller market, his team is not a pennant race story, and there is a lingering skepticism about whether these numbers can hold into the second half. But the numbers he has put up in the first half have him in the same tier as the most dominant single-season pitching performances of the last decade.

He and Brewers outfielder Jackson Chourio were named NL Co-Players of the Week for June 8-14 — the first time two Brewers players have shared that honor in MLB history. That's two history-making achievements in one month from a team that national baseball media treats as an afterthought.

Pay attention to Jacob Misiorowski. He is the best pitcher in the National League, and the 105.5 MPH reading from last Thursday is just the most recent proof.

### STORY 4: Skubal Trade Deadline Decision

**Long-Form Post**
The most interesting decision in this year's trade deadline market is not whether Tarik Skubal gets traded. At 85 percent probability per ESPN's reporting, the odds strongly favor a deal. The interesting decision is whether the Detroit Tigers convince themselves the Wild Card math is real enough to keep him.

Here's the situation: Skubal is the two-time defending AL Cy Young Award winner. He just returned from bone chip surgery on his elbow and is already hitting 99.9 miles per hour — the health concern that loomed over his market has been answered. The Tigers are 34-46, 12 games below .500 and 7.5 games out of first place in the AL Central.

But they are only 5 games out of the American League Wild Card.

Five games with 34 days left before the August 3 trade deadline and 34 more days of the regular season after that. Is that close enough to hold the most coveted trade piece in the market and make a run?

The answer probably depends on how many games the Tigers win over the next 20 days. A team that plays .600 ball for three weeks looks very different than a team playing .400. If Detroit goes 15-5 from now through July 20, the conversation shifts. If they go 8-12, it shifts the other way.

The asking price for Skubal is significant. Analysts have compared it to CC Sabathia's mid-season trade to Milwaukee in 2008 — a pair or trio of top-100 prospects, at least one in the top 10, in exchange for a pitcher with multiple Cy Youngs and confirmed health. Teams with that level of prospect depth — the Dodgers, Yankees, Braves, Blue Jays — are already in conversations.

If the Tigers trade Skubal, they are signaling a rebuild under a new timeline and likely extending their prospects at the expense of a 2026 playoff run that was already a long shot. If they keep him, they are betting on a late-season run with a four-game-per-week starting pitcher who has already proven he's one of the three best in the AL.

The deadline is 34 days away. Detroit has 20 days of baseball before that decision becomes urgent. Watch their record between now and then. It tells you everything.

### STORY 5: White Sox at 43-39

**Long-Form Post**
The Chicago White Sox entered this season as one of the least-discussed teams in the American League. They were not built to contend in 2026 by any credible offseason projection. They were not a team that analysts were picking to win their division or challenge for a Wild Card.

At the end of June, the White Sox are 43-39. They are in first place — or within a game of first place — in the AL Central.

The division standings tell a story that is more interesting than any one team within it. Chicago at 43-39. Cleveland at 44-40. The Guardians are a game ahead of the White Sox, but within a margin so small that any week of baseball can flip it. Behind them: Minnesota at 40-45, Detroit at 35-49, Kansas City at 35-50.

This is a division that can be won. And the White Sox are positioned to do it.

The turnaround from preseason expectations is meaningful here. A team that was expected to struggle in the mid-30s in wins is now on pace for a record that could challenge for a playoff spot. Something changed between March and June — whether it's pitching consistency, lineup production, bullpen performance, or a combination — and the numbers say it's real at sample sizes too large to dismiss as variance.

There are real questions about whether Chicago can sustain a winning record through the summer. Second-half collapses for surprise teams are common enough that the skepticism is fair. The pitching staff, the depth of the lineup, and the bullpen will all face harder questions as August arrives.

But right now, the White Sox are one of the best stories in baseball — and one of the most underappreciated ones. When Chicago is in first place at the end of June, that is news worth paying attention to regardless of how you feel about the team.

The second half starts in three days.
