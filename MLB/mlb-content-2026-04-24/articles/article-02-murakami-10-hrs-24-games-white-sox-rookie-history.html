Murakami Hits 10th HR in 24th Career Game — White Sox Rookie Sets Japanese-Born MLB Record

By Jake Torres | April 24, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

Munetaka Murakami came to Chicago as one of the most anticipated Japanese imports in recent baseball history. He has already made history in ways no one fully expected this soon.

Murakami hit his 10th home run of the 2026 season this week, doing so in his 24th career MLB game. That is the most home runs by any Japanese-born player through their first 24 games in the history of Major League Baseball — surpassing Shohei Ohtani, who hit six home runs through his first 24 games as a designated hitter. Murakami has also homered in five consecutive games, tied for the longest home run streak by any rookie in MLB history.

He also recorded a 113 mph home run earlier in the season — a shot that briefly left analysts scrambling for context before concluding the number was real.

Joining Elite Company

Murakami now joins Shohei Ohtani and Seiya Suzuki as the only three Japanese-born players to homer in four consecutive MLB games. That is a list of some of the most decorated international players in modern baseball history, and Murakami reached it before completing two full months in the league.

The context matters. Murakami came over after an exceptional career in Japan's NPB, where he established himself as one of the most dangerous power hitters in the league's history. But translating NPB power to MLB is not automatic — Ohtani's early struggles with MLB pitching are well-documented, and even Suzuki required an adjustment period. Murakami has hit the ground at a dead sprint.

His approach has been notably disciplined for a player with his power profile: he has not expanded the zone excessively, has not gotten overanxious against late-breaking offspeed pitches the way many powerful right-handed hitters do early in MLB careers. Whatever adjustment period Murakami was expected to undergo, it appears to be happening at a compressed pace.

The Montgomery Factor

Murakami's presence in the Chicago lineup is amplified by shortstop Colson Montgomery, who has added six home runs of his own. The 2024 No. 2 overall pick entered 2026 as one of the most anticipated prospects in baseball, and he has rewarded that billing with a power display that gives opposing pitchers a second dangerous option in the Chicago order.

The combination of Murakami and Montgomery has created what Fox News described as a historically rare rookie tandem in Chicago — two players making their marks simultaneously in the same lineup, each capable of changing a game with a single swing. For a White Sox organization that has spent several years in rebuilding mode, this is the payoff starting to arrive.

What's Next

The five-game home run streak is the most immediate milestone to watch. Any extension would break the all-time MLB rookie record outright, and Murakami has shown no sign of the kind of mechanical inconsistency that typically ends such runs. Beyond the streak, the broader question is how opposing pitchers adjust their game plans as they accumulate scouting data on him. Murakami's response to those adjustments — likely coming over the next few weeks — will define whether this start is a historic flash or the foundation of a legitimately elite MLB career.
