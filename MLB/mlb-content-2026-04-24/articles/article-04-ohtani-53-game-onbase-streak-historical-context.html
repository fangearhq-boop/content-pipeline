Ohtani's 53-Game On-Base Streak Ends — Where Does It Rank in MLB History?

By Marcus Cole | April 24, 2026 | Ballpark Banter

Shohei Ohtani went 0-for-4 at the plate on Tuesday night. He also pitched six innings and struck out seven batters. The Dodgers lost 3-0 to the Giants at Oracle Park.

And in that loss, one of the most extraordinary individual streaks in baseball history came to an end.

Ohtani's on-base streak ended at 53 consecutive games. It began on August 24, 2025, crossed an entire offseason, and continued into 2026 before the Giants' pitching staff finally retired him four times in a single night. The streak now belongs to the historical record books — and the record it produced is genuinely unprecedented.

Where It Ranks

The 53-game streak ties Shawn Green's 2000 season for second in Dodgers franchise history. The franchise record is held by Duke Snider, who reached base safely in 58 consecutive games during the 1954 season. Ohtani's streak fell five games short of that mark.

The streak is also the longest on-base streak in MLB history by any Japanese-born player, surpassing Ichiro Suzuki's previous record of 43 consecutive games. Ichiro's record stood as an acknowledgment of his extraordinary contact ability — Ohtani's surpassing it reflects a different kind of dominance, driven by power and plate discipline rather than Ichiro's legendary bat control.

CBS Sports placed the 53-game streak among the historically notable on-base streaks in the broader context of baseball history, noting its rarity and the circumstances that made it unique — particularly the cross-offseason element.

What Makes It Singular

Every element of Ohtani's 53-game streak carries an asterisk that makes it more impressive, not less. The streak ran from late August 2025 through April 21, 2026 — meaning it survived an entire winter without a plate appearance. Most hitting streaks are broken by a single bad night. An on-base streak is harder to end — a walk is enough to extend it — but the same principle applies: opponents eventually figure you out, pitchers attack differently, coaches scheme to get you out.

Ohtani's streak survived all of that. And it did so while he was simultaneously serving as a starting pitcher. On the night the streak ended, Ohtani threw six innings, allowed zero runs, and struck out seven batters — then went 0-for-4 with the bat. He was not fatigued at the plate because of his workload. He simply had a night where the Giants' defense and pitching came out ahead.

No player in baseball history has maintained a 50-plus-game on-base streak while carrying a starting pitcher's workload. Not DiMaggio. Not Bonds. Not Williams. Ohtani is alone in that space.

What's Next

The streak is over, but Ohtani's season is not. The Dodgers (16-7) remain the No. 1 team in MLB's power rankings, and Ohtani's two-way contributions remain central to their identity. Manager Dave Roberts has indicated he is evaluating how to manage Ohtani's pitching workload carefully through the full season. Ohtani's OPS entering the week sits at .854 — below his historical peak but still elite by any reasonable measure. The next on-base streak will start the next time he steps to the plate. Given what we know about him, that may not stay at zero for long.
