Paul Skenes Lasted Two Outs on Opening Day — A Historic Disaster in Pittsburgh

By Ballpark Banter Staff March 28, 2026

The Line That Shocked Baseball

Paul Skenes entered Opening Day as the best pitcher in baseball. He left after two outs. The reigning NL Cy Young Award winner recorded 0.2 innings against the New York Mets on Thursday at Citi Field — giving up four hits, five earned runs, and throwing 37 pitches before manager Don Kelly pulled him from the game. The Mets won 11-7. The final box score line for Skenes reads like a misprint: 0.2 IP, 4 H, 5 ER, 2 BB, 1 K.

It was the shortest Opening Day start ever by a reigning Cy Young Award winner in MLB history — and the first time a starter recorded fewer than three outs on Opening Day since Jose Berrios in 2022. For a pitcher who entered 2026 with a 1.96 career ERA across 200 major league innings, the outing was as stunning as it was historically notable.

What Actually Happened

The full story is more complicated than the line score suggests. The Mets scored five runs before Skenes was pulled, but the defense in front of him made a pair of critical errors that amplified what might have been a rough but manageable first inning. With runners on base, shortstop O'Neil Cruz misread a Brett Baty line drive, allowing a bases-clearing triple to score three runs at once. On the very next play, Cruz lost a Marcus Semien fly ball in the sun — no sunglasses on — and another run scored.

Skenes himself walked two batters and allowed three hits, but the context matters: a bases-clearing triple on a misread ball and a lost fly ball in the sun are the kind of plays that turn a difficult inning into an inescapable one. Manager Don Kelly cited pitch count as the primary reason for the pull, not wanting Skenes to waste bullets approaching the mid-30s before the first inning was complete. Skenes threw 37 pitches and got two outs. The math was unavoidable.

Why This Doesn't Necessarily Change the Skenes Story

One start doesn't define a 162-game season, and one brutal outing — especially one shaped by extraordinary defensive misfortune — doesn't change what Skenes has shown across his young career. His 1.96 ERA, 11.1 K/9, and .238 xwOBA against in 2025 weren't noise. They were signal. The Cy Young Award was deserved, and there is no reason to believe the pitcher who won it has suddenly become something else because of a 37-pitch Opening Day implosion.

The Pittsburgh Pirates, however, have a legitimate problem to examine. The Cruz miscues on Opening Day weren't isolated: a misread fly ball and a lost pop-up in the sun speak to either preparation, defensive positioning, or both. Skenes can only work with what's behind him. If the infield continues to magnify rather than mitigate his bad moments, the Pirates' postseason ceiling — already limited — shrinks further. The question for Pittsburgh isn't whether Skenes is good. It's whether the team around him can give him a real chance.

What's Next

Skenes will make his next start in a few days, and all expectations are that this outing will serve as a footnote rather than a trend line — much like any elite pitcher's occasional bad game. The more important storyline to track is the Pirates' defense: if Cruz continues to struggle with reads in the outfield and infield, it will dog Pittsburgh all year regardless of how Skenes performs. His next start will be the first real test of how quickly — and fully — he can put Thursday behind him.
