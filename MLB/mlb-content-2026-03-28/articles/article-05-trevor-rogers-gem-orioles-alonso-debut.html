Don't Sleep on the Orioles — Trevor Rogers' Gem and Helsley's Debut Close Out the Twins

By Ballpark Banter Staff March 28, 2026

Rogers Makes the Case Nobody Expected

While the sports world was watching Paul Skenes' Opening Day implosion in New York and three rookies making history across three stadiums, Trevor Rogers was quietly taking a bat to the Minnesota Twins at Camden Yards. The Orioles left-hander threw seven innings of shutout ball — three hits, four walks, five strikeouts, and three double plays induced — in one of the most efficient Opening Day performances in Baltimore baseball history. The Orioles won 2-1, and Rogers was in complete control from start to finish.

The walk total elevated his pitch count enough that manager Brandon Hyde came to get him after the seventh, but the numbers tell a deeper story: those four walks did not produce a single run. Every time Rogers got in trouble, he got a double play. Every time a runner reached scoring position, he escaped it. Seven innings, zero runs, three double plays. That's not luck — that's command.

Helsley's Entrance: Six Pitches, Six Times Triple Digits

Ryan Helsley came to Baltimore this offseason as the Orioles' new closer, and he introduced himself to Camden Yards on Opening Day by throwing six consecutive pitches at 100 miles per hour or faster. He struck out the side in the ninth inning — three up, three down, six pitches, six triple digits. The Twins had no answer. It was exactly the kind of debut that shifts a team's calculus at the back of the bullpen: when your closer is throwing 100+ on every pitch, the ninth inning becomes a foregone conclusion.

For an Orioles team that has been building toward genuine AL contention over the past three seasons — two division titles in the last two years — a shutdown closer is the piece that can take a good team and make it a dangerous playoff threat. Helsley looked, on Friday, like that piece.

Pete Alonso Starts a New Chapter

Pete Alonso went 0-for-3 in his first game as an Oriole, which will be filed under "footnotes" by the time the season is over. Alonso signed with Baltimore this offseason after driving in 126 runs in his final season with the New York Mets — a franchise he spent his entire career with before the two sides couldn't agree on a contract extension. The 0-for-3 is one plate appearance in a career defined by production. His first Orioles home run — and the production that will inevitably follow — is coming.

The Baltimore lineup with Alonso in it is a different look than the one the Orioles fielded in previous years. His presence in the middle of the order will draw attention from opposing pitchers and create protection for the hitters around him. The Opening Day result is a single data point. The broader question — whether Alonso can give the Orioles the middle-of-the-order power they've been missing — will be answered over the next six months.

What's Next

The Orioles are 1-0 and facing the Twins again in Game 2, with a fresh challenge for their rotation behind Rogers. The more important development to monitor is Helsley's velocity and consistency as the season progresses: a closer throwing 100+ mph on every pitch in April is a weapon; one maintaining that into September is a nightmare for postseason opponents. If Rogers can replicate his Opening Day performance with any regularity, and if Helsley is everything his debut suggested, Baltimore has the foundation for a legitimate October run.
