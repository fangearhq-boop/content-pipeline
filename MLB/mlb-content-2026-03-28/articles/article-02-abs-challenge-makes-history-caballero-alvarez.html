Baseball's ABS Challenge Era Has Officially Begun — Two Days, Two History Books

By Ballpark Banter Staff March 28, 2026

The First Challenge in MLB History

On Opening Night at Oracle Park, the Automated Ball-Strike challenge system made its regular-season debut — and it didn't take long for history to be made. In the fourth inning of the Yankees' 7-0 win over the San Francisco Giants, shortstop Jose Caballero stepped in to face Logan Webb and challenged the very first pitch he saw: a 90.7 mph sinker that was called a strike. The review confirmed the pitch was in the strike zone. Call upheld. Challenge unsuccessful.

Caballero walked back to the batter's box having just become the first player in major league history to use the Automated Ball-Strike system in a regular-season game. The outcome wasn't in his favor, but the milestone was etched regardless: on March 25, 2026, baseball entered a new era.

The First Successful Challenge — 24 Hours Later

The ABS challenge system didn't have to wait long for its first successful use. In the Mets' 11-7 Opening Day win over the Pittsburgh Pirates the following afternoon, Mets catcher Francisco Alvarez challenged a ball call on a Freddy Peralta pitch. This time, the review went the other way: the pitch was ruled a strike, overturning the original ball call and making it a strikeout of Oneil Cruz.

Two challenges in two days: one miss, one make. The system worked in both directions — upholding the original call when it was correct, overturning it when it wasn't. For a technology that spent years in the minor leagues before arriving in the majors, the first 48 hours offered exactly the balanced debut that proponents of the system would have scripted.

What the ABS Challenge System Actually Does

The Automated Ball-Strike challenge system allows players to challenge ball and strike calls once per at-bat, triggered by the batter, catcher, or manager. When a challenge is initiated, the call is reviewed against a radar-based strike zone that measures exact pitch coordinates. If the technology confirms the original call, the challenge is lost and not returned. If the call is overturned, the challenge is preserved.

The system has been in use in the minor leagues for several seasons, producing a data set that shows human umpires miss a meaningful percentage of borderline pitches — particularly those at the top and bottom of the zone, and on the corners. The major league adoption was approved after years of testing, with the goal of improving accuracy without removing the human element of calling pitches in real time. The first two uses suggest the system will be invoked selectively and at high-stakes moments — Alvarez's successful challenge in a 5-run first inning didn't change the game, but it demonstrated that players understand when and how to deploy it.

What's Next

The ABS challenge will only grow more consequential as teams develop strategies for when to challenge and when to conserve the opportunity. Expect analytics departments to begin tracking individual umpire tendencies and pitch type accuracy rates to optimize challenge decisions — a new layer of in-game strategy that will develop over the coming weeks and months. The first two challenges were historic; the ones that come in the ninth inning of a tie game in September will be remembered even longer.
