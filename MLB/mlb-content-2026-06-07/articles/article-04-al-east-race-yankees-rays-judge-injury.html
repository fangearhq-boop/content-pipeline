Half a Game, No Judge: The AL East Race Is On Without the Yankees' Best Player

By Marcus Cole June 7, 2026

The Numbers

As of June 7, the New York Yankees lead the AL East at 37-25. The Tampa Bay Rays are 36-23, a half-game back. The gap is as thin as a baseball game gets in the standings.

The wrinkle: Aaron Judge, the Yankees' captain and the best hitter in the American League, is on the injured list with a stress fracture in his right rib. He was placed on the 10-day IL on June 4. The team expects to reimage the injury in four to six weeks, which means Judge's return through the All-Star break is essentially off the table — and there is a realistic scenario where his absence extends toward the August 3 trade deadline.

What Judge Means to the Yankees

Judge has been among the most productive hitters in baseball for several seasons running. His presence in the lineup does two things simultaneously: it produces at-bats directly and it elevates every hitter around him by compelling opposing pitchers to throw strikes they might otherwise avoid. Without him, the Yankees' lineup construction changes. The pitching staff faces higher-leverage situations without the same run support. Everything gets harder.

Spencer Jones was recalled from Triple-A to fill the roster spot. He is an interesting prospect, but he is not Aaron Judge. José Caballero and Max Schuemann have been playing right field while the organization evaluates the best path forward. The Yankees have options, but none of them approximate what they lose when Judge is unavailable.

The Rays' Opportunity

Tampa Bay has been playing winning baseball all spring. Their 36-23 record represents genuine organizational quality — not a hot streak waiting to normalize. The Rays have a history of maximizing talent efficiently, and their current roster is doing exactly that.

With Judge gone, the calculus for the entire AL East shifts. The Blue Jays at 30-33 and the Orioles at 30-33 are both 8 games out — mathematically in the mix, but needing everything to break their way. The Red Sox at 26-35 are effectively out of the division race, though they remain within range of a Wild Card spot.

The Rays are the team with the clearest opportunity. They are built for consistent production, they are already in second place, and they now have a chance to take the lead while their primary competition is shorthanded.

Can the Yankees Hold?

The answer depends on depth. The Yankees roster was constructed with enough talent to remain competitive without any one player — but Judge is not any one player. He is a generational talent and the team's identity in many ways.

If the Yankees rotation pitches well and the supporting cast holds its production level, holding a half-game lead for six weeks until the All-Star break is achievable. If there are further injuries or the lineup proves unable to generate enough offense without Judge, the lead will erode.

The fear in the Bronx was that the rib fracture might cost Judge the season. Avoiding that worst case is the silver lining. The realistic case is an absence of six to eight weeks — enough time for the Rays to pull ahead if New York is not careful.

What's Next

Judge will be reimaged in four to six weeks. His medical team will determine the appropriate next steps based on healing. In the meantime, the Yankees play without him through June and into July, with the Rays applying constant pressure. The next ten to fifteen games in this division race will tell us a great deal about whether the Yankees can hold their position without their captain on the field.
