First Since Norman in 1986: Scheffler's Runner-Up Streak and the Path to Redemption at Doral

By Ryan Calloway | Golf Fanrecap

April 29, 2026

Greg Norman was in the middle of his own run of excellence in 1986 when he ended up on the wrong side of two consecutive near-misses at two of the most important events on the golf calendar. Forty years later, Scottie Scheffler finds himself in the same historical bracket. The world's No. 1 player is now the first since Norman in 1986 to finish runner-up at both the Masters and the following week's RBC Heritage.

This is not a failure — it needs to be said clearly. Losing to Rory McIlroy by one shot at Augusta in the final round of the Masters is not a collapse; it is a golf match. Losing to Matt Fitzpatrick in a one-hole playoff at Harbour Town Golf Links is not a mental breakdown; it is a sport with outcomes. But the cumulative weight of these two finishes, across two consecutive weekends of the highest-pressure golf on the calendar, has made Scheffler's winless stretch at the biggest moments the defining narrative of his 2026 season.

He enters this week's Cadillac Championship at Trump National Doral with one win and five top-five finishes in eight starts, which would represent an exceptional season for virtually any other player on tour. For Scheffler, it feels like underachievement — not because of anything he has done wrong, but because of the standard he has set for himself and the expectations that come with being the best player in the world.

A Field Without His Recent Conquerors

This week offers something Scheffler has not had in recent events: a field without the two men who beat him. Rory McIlroy is not playing the Cadillac Championship. Matt Fitzpatrick — who won RBC Heritage in a playoff over Scheffler — is also absent. Neither is Xander Schauffele. That removes significant competition from the equation and creates a cleaner path to the trophy that Scheffler has been chasing.

Norman, for what it's worth, went on to have one of the most productive stretches of his career after his own runner-up period. The frustration of almost winning can fuel some of the most decisive performances in a player's history. There is no reason to believe Scheffler is anything other than the best player in the field this week at Doral — and the Blue Monster, with its demands for precise iron play, distance control, and course management under pressure, is exactly the kind of course where his complete game translates into a winning result.

What's Next

The Cadillac Championship Round 1 begins Thursday, April 30 at Trump National Doral. If Scheffler converts this week and breaks the runner-up streak, the psychological lift heading into the U.S. Open season could be significant. If he finishes second again — particularly against a field that does not contain his two most recent conquerors — the narrative will grow louder. For now, he has his best opportunity in weeks to get back to winning. The Blue Monster is waiting.
