Scheffler's Birthday Grand Slam: Sunday Could Be the Greatest Day in Golf in Decades

By Marcus Cole | Golf Fanrecap | June 18, 2026

Gene Sarazen. Ben Hogan. Gary Player. Jack Nicklaus. Tiger Woods. Rory McIlroy.

Six players in the history of professional golf have completed the career Grand Slam — winning all four major championships at least once. The list is short enough that most dedicated golf fans have it memorized. McIlroy joined it in April at the 2026 Masters, completing a quest that consumed most of his career narrative for nearly a decade.

Now, in June, Scottie Scheffler is standing at the door.

What He's Already Done

Scheffler needs only the U.S. Open to complete the career Grand Slam. He has won every other major: the Masters in 2022 and 2024, the PGA Championship in 2025, and The Open Championship in 2025. His major collection, assembled before his 30th birthday, makes him the best player of his generation by any reasonable measure.

He enters this week at +550 odds and as the consensus favorite. The U.S. Open has been the one gap — a tournament he has contended at but not closed. His runner-up finish at the 2026 Masters (behind McIlroy's historic round) showed his game is where it needs to be heading into this week at Shinnecock Hills.

He has been the No. 1 player in the world by FedEx Cup strokes-gained rankings throughout 2026. He leads the PGA Tour in scoring average. His tee-to-green game, putting consistency, and competitive steadiness are all characteristics the U.S. Open rewards above virtually any other major.

The Birthday Narrative

Here is the detail that makes this week genuinely extraordinary: if Scheffler wins the 2026 U.S. Open, the final round is played on Sunday, June 21.

That's Father's Day.

That's his 30th birthday.

Winning the career Grand Slam on your 30th birthday, on Father's Day, on the hardest major course in golf — that is the kind of story that gets written into the permanent record of the sport. It does not happen by coincidence. It requires winning a golf tournament. But the coincidence, if it occurs, would be unprecedented in its narrative weight.

Scheffler himself has been characteristically understated about all of it. "It's not a motivating factor," he said. "I won't consider it a failure if I don't win here." These are the words of someone who has learned to manage external pressure by reducing its significance internally — the same approach that has allowed him to win four majors before age 30 without appearing to be consumed by the pursuit.

The Historical Context

Completing the career Grand Slam is genuinely rare. Many of the greatest players in history never did it: Arnold Palmer, Lee Trevino, Phil Mickelson, and Greg Norman each won multiple majors without completing the set. Sam Snead won seven majors — and never won the U.S. Open.

McIlroy's completion at the 2026 Masters was celebrated as a career-defining moment because it had taken him 12 years of near-misses and close calls after winning the other three. Scheffler's path has been different — faster, more linear, less emotionally tortured. He approaches the U.S. Open not as a burden he must lift, but as the next tournament on the calendar that he has the best game to win.

That may be exactly the right approach for Shinnecock Hills, where the course punishes anxiety and rewards patience.

What's Next

Round 1 is underway. Four rounds remain between Scheffler and history. The U.S. Open concludes Sunday, June 21 — Father's Day, Scheffler's 30th birthday.

Whether the story ends with a Grand Slam completion or another chapter in an unfinished pursuit, this week will define a significant stretch of golf's historical narrative. That much is already true, whatever happens on Sunday.
