Shinnecock Hills Is the Most Brutal US Open Venue — And Thursday Proves It Again

By Ryan Calloway | Golf Fanrecap | June 18, 2026

No player finished under par.

Not one, in a 156-man field of the best golfers in the world. Brooks Koepka won the 2018 U.S. Open at Shinnecock Hills with a score of +1. His winning total of 281 was one stroke over par. On Saturday, the third round, the scoring average across the entire field was 75.3 strokes — on a par-70 course. The USGA had to water the greens mid-round to keep them from becoming unplayable.

That was eight years ago. The same course. The same Southampton, New York exposure to the Atlantic and Long Island Sound wind. And on Thursday, with gusts expected to reach 35-40 miles per hour through the afternoon, the conditions are setting up for a recurrence.

Why Shinnecock Is Different

Golf courses are not all created equal in terms of their susceptibility to weather. Most PGA Tour venues are designed with some natural wind shelter — tree lines, elevation, strategic buffering. The U.S. Open typically plays harder than regular Tour events, but the degree of that difference varies by venue.

Shinnecock Hills is an outlier. The course was designed in 1895 — the second oldest U.S. Open venue in rotation — and it occupies an exposed stretch of the Southampton coastline where the layout runs in multiple directions across open, heathland-style terrain. There are no trees to speak of on much of the course. The greens are elevated and fast, catching wind from any direction. The rough can be severe depending on USGA setup decisions.

When the wind blows at Shinnecock, it blows everywhere, on every hole, in every direction simultaneously as the layout changes orientations. A 20-mile-per-hour wind that produces a crosswind on one hole becomes a headwind two holes later and a downwind after that. Managing that variability across 18 holes, four times in a week, is the central challenge of this U.S. Open.

The 2018 Context

The 2018 U.S. Open at Shinnecock Hills remains the reference point for modern scoring difficulty at the venue. Koepka's +1 winning total was the highest winning score at a major since Retief Goosen won the 2001 U.S. Open at Southern Hills at +4.

The Saturday third round was the most extreme. The USGA, faced with greens that had baked beyond playability under the summer sun and afternoon wind, made the unusual decision to water the greens mid-round — essentially slowing them down while players were on the course. It was an acknowledgment that the setup had crossed a line from difficult to unfair, and the resulting media criticism of former USGA executive director Mike Davis was significant.

The 2026 version of the USGA is demonstrating greater caution. Moving the first round tee times up by 30 minutes — starting at 6:35 AM rather than 7:05 AM — was specifically designed to get as many players through their rounds as possible before the afternoon wind arrives. The USGA has also indicated it is prepared to adjust the course setup if conditions deteriorate beyond what was planned.

The Morning/Afternoon Disparity

One of the inherent fairness questions at every U.S. Open on an exposed course is the tee time disparity. Players who drew afternoon starting times on Thursday are competing on a course playing meaningfully harder than their morning counterparts. There is no systemic solution to this — wind doesn't care about scheduling — but it creates a scoreboard dynamic that requires context.

If the morning wave includes players who get in under par, they will hold the Round 1 leaderboard positions. Those players face potentially much harder conditions in Round 2 when the draw flips. By the cut, the playing field levels somewhat — but the Round 1 leader's card is heavily influenced by when they tee off.

Understanding this dynamic is essential for following the U.S. Open scoring narrative this week. A player at -3 after Round 1 may have played in calm conditions. A player at +2 may have played brilliantly under 40-mile-per-hour gusts. The stories beneath the leaderboard are often more compelling than the headline numbers.

What's Next

Watch the afternoon wave carefully on Thursday. If the wind delivers as forecast, today's afternoon scoring will tell the story of 2026 at Shinnecock Hills. The leaderboard after Round 1 sets the stage for three more days of one of golf's most demanding annual tests.

The 2018 winner finished at +1. Some prediction models have the 2026 winning score at +2 to +4, accounting for slightly calmer expected conditions on the weekend. That would make this week among the highest winning scores at a major in the modern era. Shinnecock Hills tends to deliver on its reputation.
