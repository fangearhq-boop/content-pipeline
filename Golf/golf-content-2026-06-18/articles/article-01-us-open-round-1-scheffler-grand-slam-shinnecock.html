US Open Round 1: Scheffler's Grand Slam Chase Begins as Shinnecock Hills Wind Arrives

By Ryan Calloway | Golf Fanrecap | June 18, 2026

The 126th United States Open Championship is underway at Shinnecock Hills Golf Club in Southampton, New York, and the course is already delivering exactly the conditions it promised.

Morning tee times began at 6:35 AM ET after the USGA moved the schedule up approximately 30 minutes to help the first wave of players complete their rounds before the afternoon wind fully arrives. Forecasts show sustained winds of 12-24 miles per hour with gusts reaching 35-40 miles per hour through the afternoon — conditions that, on the exposed Shinnecock layout, transform an already demanding course into something close to unplayable for the back half of the field.

The player everyone is watching is Scottie Scheffler, who teed off at 8:14 AM alongside defending champion J.J. Spaun and 2025 U.S. Amateur champion Mason Howell. Scheffler is pursuing the career Grand Slam, needing only a U.S. Open victory to complete a set that only six players in history have finished.

The Scheffler Chase

Scheffler enters the week at +550 odds and as the consensus favorite. He won the Masters in 2022 and 2024, the PGA Championship in 2025, and The Open Championship in 2025. The U.S. Open is the only gap in his major resume.

The stakes this specific week are elevated by calendar coincidence: if Scheffler wins, the final round falls on Sunday, June 21 — which is Father's Day and his 30th birthday. The convergence of those dates has created a narrative framework that even Scheffler himself acknowledged, though he downplayed its significance in pre-tournament media sessions.

"It's not a motivating factor," Scheffler said. "I won't consider it a failure if I don't win here. I just want to play good golf."

That is precisely the mentality that has made him the world's No. 1 player. Whether the narrative matters to him or not, it matters to the sport — and this first round is where that story either begins or stalls.

McIlroy and the Double Major Bid

Rory McIlroy teed off at 7:52 AM alongside Tommy Fleetwood and Ludvig Åberg in one of the most-watched pairings on the morning sheet. McIlroy completed his own career Grand Slam by winning the 2026 Masters in April. He is now in pursuit of something equally rare: winning two majors in the same calendar year — a feat accomplished in the modern era only by Tiger Woods.

McIlroy enters at +1200 odds and with three runner-up finishes at the U.S. Open since his 2011 victory. His 2011 U.S. Open win at Congressional — a 16-under 268, by 8 strokes — remains one of the most dominant major performances of the past 20 years. Recreating anything close to that at Shinnecock would require a different kind of golf entirely.

The Course Factor

Shinnecock Hills is not a course that rewards aggressive play in the traditional sense. The exposed Southampton layout — situated along the coastline where wind off the Atlantic and Long Island Sound creates constantly changing conditions — punishes poor club selection and rewards patience, course management, and the ability to accept bogeys without cascading damage.

The last time the U.S. Open came here, in 2018, no player in the 156-man field finished under par for the week. The winning score was +1. The USGA had to water greens mid-round on Saturday to keep the course playable after it baked faster than anticipated.

Today's afternoon wave faces the same structural challenge. Those who drew afternoon tee times on Thursday may be competing on a course playing three to four strokes harder than the morning wave. The leaderboard structure after 36 holes will reflect that disparity.

What's Next

Round 1 play continues through this afternoon. The 36-hole cut is expected to fall after Round 2 on Friday, with the top 60 and ties advancing to the weekend. Round 3 is Saturday, June 20. The final round is Sunday, June 21.
