LIV Golf's Survival Race: Saudi Money Gone, CEO in Emergency Fundraising Mode

By Jake Torres | Golf Fanrecap | June 18, 2026

When LIV Golf launched in 2022, backed by Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund and the promise of unlimited capital, the question was whether the sport establishment could withstand the financial pressure. Four years and more than $5 billion later, the question has inverted: can LIV Golf survive without the money that built it?

Saudi Arabia's PIF has confirmed it will end funding for LIV Golf after the conclusion of the 2026 season. The league has already postponed its New Orleans event. CEO Scott O'Neil is conducting an emergency fundraising campaign, seeking $250 million to $350 million in new private investment to keep the league operational into 2027 and beyond.

The math behind the league's existence is stark. Annual losses have been estimated at $500 million to $600 million. Total Saudi investment since launch exceeds $5 billion. The model — massive player salaries, guaranteed contracts, and a closed no-cut format that struggled to attract major sponsors — never came close to profitability.

Where the Money Stands

Saudi Arabia committed $600 million to fund LIV Golf's 2026 season. As of the time O'Neil's CNBC interview was published in early June, only $200 million of that commitment had been delivered. The remaining $400 million is still outstanding and not guaranteed.

O'Neil told CNBC he has conducted five formal investor meetings and has 18 more planned. He characterized the response as "positive" and expressed confidence that fundraising would be complete by the end of summer. Whether that confidence is warranted — and whether the investors he has approached have done the math on those annual losses — remains to be seen.

The league has hired financial advisers Ducera Partners to manage the process. A new independent board has been established, led by Gene Davis and Jon Zinman, following the departure of PIF chairman Yasir Al-Rumayyan from his role as LIV Golf's board chairman.

Operational Impact

The most visible consequence of the financial uncertainty is the New Orleans event, scheduled for June 25-28, which has already been postponed. Four remaining 2026 events are considered at risk, including the LIV UK tournament (July 23-26) and three U.S. events scheduled for August.

O'Neil has acknowledged he cannot guarantee all remaining 2026 tournaments. That statement — from a sitting CEO about events on his own calendar — represents an extraordinary level of public uncertainty about a league that was supposed to represent a permanent alternative structure for professional golf.

The league is exploring a downsized operating model: potentially 10 events with substantially reduced purses, rather than the current expanded schedule at current salary levels. Any downsizing would require renegotiating player contracts — some of which were structured as multi-year guarantees specifically to attract talent from the PGA Tour.

The Players' Position

Bryson DeChambeau has said publicly that he is involved in helping O'Neil chart a path forward. Jon Rahm has focused on his golf and left the business questions to O'Neil and the board. Both represent the range of player responses: some engaged, some waiting.

The fundamental risk for LIV's player pool is straightforward: if the league folds or reduces to a minimal schedule, where do these players go? PGA Tour reinstatement is the most likely pathway, but reinstatement terms — after years of legal battles and the complicated framework that emerged from the framework agreement negotiations — would almost certainly favor the Tour rather than the returning players.

What's Next

O'Neil's self-imposed timeline for completing the fundraising process is summer 2026. If private investors materialize with the capital needed to cover losses and fund future seasons, the league continues in some form. If they don't, the financial runway from the remaining Saudi commitments may carry LIV Golf to the end of 2026 — and no further.

The U.S. Open this week features several LIV Golf players who received special exemptions or qualified through their world rankings. Their presence at Shinnecock Hills is a reminder of what's at stake: the world's best golf is being played at a USGA major, not a LIV event. That context hasn't changed. The financial reality behind it has.
