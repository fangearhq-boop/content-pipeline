### STORY 2: LIV Golf Crisis

#### Long-Form Post — 10:30 AM ET

The math on LIV Golf's survival doesn't add up — and the timeline is getting short.

Here's where things stand as of this week:

Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund has confirmed it will end funding for LIV Golf after the 2026 season concludes. Of the $600 million committed for 2026, only $200 million has been delivered so far. The remaining $400 million is still outstanding and not guaranteed.

Annual losses are estimated at $500 million to $600 million. Since launching in 2022, Saudi Arabia has invested more than $5 billion in total.

PIF chairman Yasir Al-Rumayyan — a LIV Golf founder — has stepped down from his role as chairman. A new independent board has been established, led by Gene Davis and Jon Zinman. Their job: find investors willing to keep the league running.

CEO Scott O'Neil told CNBC that he's seeking between $250 million and $350 million in new funding. He has completed five formal investor meetings and has 18 more planned. He said the response has "been positive" and expects to complete fundraising by the end of summer.

Meanwhile, the operational impacts are already visible:

• The New Orleans event (scheduled for June 25-28) has been postponed.
• The LIV UK event (July 23-26) and three additional U.S. events remain at risk.
• O'Neil has said he can no longer guarantee all remaining 2026 tournaments.

The league is exploring a downsized model — potentially 10 events with reduced purses — if new funding doesn't materialize.

Players like Bryson DeChambeau and Jon Rahm are staying focused on golf while O'Neil handles the business side. But the uncertainty affects every contract extension discussion and every sponsor conversation.

The question no one in the industry wants to answer publicly is now unavoidable: what happens to LIV Golf's players if the league folds? PGA Tour reinstatement is the most likely outcome — but on terms that would almost certainly favor the Tour.

### STORY 4: Shinnecock Hills Brutality

#### Long-Form Post — 3:30 PM ET

No player finished under par at the 2018 U.S. Open at Shinnecock Hills.

Not one. In a field of the best golfers on the planet.

Brooks Koepka won the tournament — defending his 2017 U.S. Open title — with a score of +1 (281 total). His final round of 68 (-2) was the lowest round in the final four groups on Saturday. The Saturday scoring average was 75.3 strokes. The USGA had to water the greens mid-round on that same Saturday to keep the pins playable.

Shinnecock Hills is uniquely brutal because of where it sits. The course runs along the exposed coastal landscape of Southampton, New York — the Hamptons, essentially — where wind off the Atlantic and Long Island Sound can turn every hole into a different test depending on the direction. The layout is long, the rough is penal, and the greens are fast and sloped.

The 2026 version is forecasting the same kind of afternoon chaos. Sustained winds of 12-24 mph with gusts reaching 35-40+ mph by late afternoon. The USGA moved up tee times by 30 minutes this morning specifically to give the morning wave a better chance before the wind fully arrives.

Here's the brutal truth: the players who tee off before noon today are competing on a completely different course than those who tee off after noon. The draw matters as much as the skill set at Shinnecock.

If history repeats itself, the leaderboard after Round 1 will show a handful of players in red figures from the morning wave — and most of the afternoon field fighting to stay at even par.

That's Shinnecock Hills. That's the U.S. Open. That's why it's the hardest major.
