The Blue Monster at Doral: 50 Years of PGA Tour History Returns to the Big Stage

By Marcus Cole | Golf Fanrecap

April 30, 2026

Before it was called the Blue Monster, before Tiger Woods made it his personal playground, before the long absence that ended today, there was just a golf course rising out of the South Florida flatlands — one that would grow into one of professional golf's most enduring venues.

Dick Wilson designed the original Blue Monster, and when it opened in 1962 and immediately hosted a PGA Tour event, it began a streak of consecutive Tour stops that would last more than 50 years. Today, with Round 1 of the 2026 Cadillac Championship underway, it is back for what is technically the 56th PGA Tour event in the club's history.

The Early Years

The Doral Open — as it was known through most of its early history — was one of the Tour's signature non-major events for decades. It attracted the game's biggest names to Miami each winter or early spring, giving television audiences and galleries a marquee event in a marquee setting. The course was demanding enough to separate the field, but not so punishing that low scores were impossible, which made it ideal for compelling leaderboard battles.

Jack Nicklaus won at Doral. So did Lee Trevino, Raymond Floyd, Ben Crenshaw, Nick Faldo, and Tom Weiskopf. The list of champions reads like a Hall of Fame roll call, which is appropriate given that several of them are actually Hall of Famers. Doral's winner's list is one of the most impressive in Tour history outside of the majors themselves.

The Tiger Years

No player left a bigger mark on the Blue Monster than Tiger Woods. He won at Doral four times — in 1997, 2005, 2006, and 2013 — and made it feel like a home court during his periods of dominance. His 2013 performance was the most extraordinary: a score of 19-under par that still stands as the course record. That performance came when Woods was 37 and in the midst of what appeared to be a late-career renaissance. The image of him walking the 18th at Doral in peak form is one of the iconic visuals from his post-2000 career.

By the time the event evolved from the Doral Open into the WGC-Cadillac Championship — part of the World Golf Championships series that brought the world's top players together for elite events outside the major structure — it had become one of the most consistently watched events on the Tour calendar.

The Decade Away

The Tour's last visit to Doral came in 2016, when Adam Scott won the WGC-Cadillac Championship. The event then moved to Mexico City, which hosted the WGC-Mexico Championship for several years. Trump National Doral went through ownership changes and renovations during the intervening decade.

The renovation that preceded this year's return was overseen by Gil Hanse and Jim Wagner, the same team responsible for the redesign of the Olympic course in Rio and several other high-profile projects. Their work at Doral added new lakes on two critical holes: the par-3 15th and the drivable par-4 16th. Both additions increase the risk-reward calculations on the back nine and give the course's most dramatic stretch additional teeth.

What the Blue Monster Demands

The course plays 7,739 yards from the tips with a par of 72. Four par-5s give the field legitimate scoring opportunities, while four par-3s demand precision off the tee — particularly now, with the new lake on the 15th penalizing any miss that finds water. The ten par-4s range in length and character, testing every element of a player's game from driving accuracy to distance control on approach shots.

The wide fairways are one of Doral's most discussed features. They allow bombers to swing freely and create separation from the field, but the Bermuda rough surrounding them is thick enough to negate length if a player misses badly. The combination rewards aggressive players who can also control their misses — a profile that fits Scheffler almost perfectly.

What's Next

The 2026 Cadillac Championship runs through Sunday, May 3, at Trump National Doral. Round 1 is underway today with coverage on Golf Channel beginning at 3 PM ET. The week will determine whether a new generation of players can add their names to the Blue Monster's legendary winner's list.
