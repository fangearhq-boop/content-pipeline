### STORY 2: Scheffler Misses Cut at Scottish Open

**Long-Form Post:**
Scottie Scheffler missed his first cut in nearly four years on Friday.

The streak was remarkable: 78 consecutive weekends making the cut at PGA Tour events — the fifth-longest streak in the history of the sport, behind only Tiger Woods (142), Byron Nelson (113), Jack Nicklaus (105), and Hale Irwin (86). The last time Scheffler had missed a cut was the 2022 FedEx St. Jude Classic — 1,428 days ago.

It ended at the Genesis Scottish Open at The Renaissance Club. Scheffler shot 68 in Round 1 and 72 in Round 2 for an even-par 36-hole total, finishing two strokes outside the cut line. He hit only 11 of 26 fairways across the two rounds and 23 of 36 greens — losing nearly two strokes on approach shots compared to the field.

This comes one week before The Open Championship at Royal Birkdale, where Scheffler will attempt to claim the one major trophy missing from his collection (he has Masters, PGA, and The Open already, or is bidding for The Open specifically). The missed cut is his worst result of 2026 and arrives at the worst possible moment.

He remains World No. 1 — starting his 163rd week at the top position on July 5, his 197th week overall. One missed cut doesn't change that. But it does mean he arrives at Royal Birkdale without the tune-up he wanted.

### STORY 3: Evian Championship — Korda Out, Woad Leads

Nelly Korda's bid for three consecutive major championships is over.

Round 3 is ongoing today at Evian-les-Bains, France, with Woad positioned as the clear title favorite. The tournament offers $9.1 million in total purse money.

### STORY 4: LIV Golf Files Layoff Notices

**Long-Form Post:**
LIV Golf's financial crisis has reached a new stage.

The Saudi-backed circuit, which has received more than $5 billion in investment from Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund (PIF) since its founding, filed Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification (WARN) Act notices in both the United States and the United Kingdom this week. The WARN Act requires employers to give employees 60 days' notice before potential mass layoffs — a legal formality that signals the situation has become serious enough to trigger mandatory disclosure.

A LIV spokesperson confirmed the filings but stated that there are currently no changes to operations or schedule. The reality underneath that statement: the league is urgently seeking between $250 million and $400 million in outside investment to cover player contracts, operating costs, and the prize purses for its remaining 2026 events. Some reports indicate the final two events of the season could be canceled if the funding doesn't arrive.

The PIF announced in April 2026 that it will no longer fund LIV beyond the current season — a significant shift after years of essentially unlimited backing that absorbed estimated annual losses of $500-600 million. Ducera Partners is handling the fundraising effort on LIV's behalf, with CEO Scott O'Neil conducting an active investor roadshow.

Adding to the pressure: English companies World Golf Group and Premier Golf League have filed suit against LIV Golf, PIF, and associated entities, alleging breach of confidence and unlawful conspiracy, and seeking damages of $210 million to $630 million. The plaintiffs claim LIV Golf was "a bald facsimile" of their original concept.

LIV's most recent event — the Maaden LIV Golf Virginia event at Trump National Washington, D.C. — was won by Australia's Lucas Herbert (24-under, four shots clear of Sergio Garcia). Herbert became the 27th different winner of a LIV individual title. The next event is scheduled for July 23 in England.

### STORY 5: Open Championship Preview

**Long-Form Post:**
The final men's major of 2026 is five days away.

The 154th Open Championship returns to Royal Birkdale in Southport, England on Thursday July 16, offering golf's oldest major trophy to a field of 156 players. The championship hasn't been held at Birkdale since Jordan Spieth's dramatic 2017 victory — the one that included his famous penalty area escapade at the 13th hole.

The course has been updated since 2017 by architects Tom Mackenzie and Mike Howard. The former par-3 14th was removed; the par-5 15th was relocated and renumbered the 14th; and a new par-3 15th was inserted. The result is a layout that routing through valleys in the dune system has always characterized Birkdale — firm and fast, accurate approach play required, putting surfaces among the most challenging on the Open rota.

The field includes Scottie Scheffler — World No. 1 despite his missed cut at the Scottish Open — along with Rory McIlroy, who carries genuine momentum after his 66 at The Renaissance Club yesterday. LIV Golf's Joaquin Niemann and Lucas Herbert (winner of LIV Virginia) earned direct exemptions. Tom McKibbin, who won the LINK Hong Kong Open to earn Masters and Open invitations through the LIV pathway, is also in the field.

McIlroy won the 2026 Masters in April. If he wins The Open, it would be his second major of 2026 and his fifth overall. He's 35 years old, in the form of his life, and playing in links conditions that have always brought out his best.

Weather forecasts for Southport in late July carry the usual caveats. What's certain: the 154th Open Championship will deliver. They always do.
