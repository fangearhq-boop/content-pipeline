LIV Golf Files Layoff Notices — Saudi PIF Is Walking Away After 2026 Season

By Ryan Calloway | July 11, 2026

LIV Golf

LIV Golf's financial crisis has moved from speculation to formal legal disclosure.

The Saudi-backed golf circuit filed Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification Act notices in both the United States and the United Kingdom — a legal requirement for employers planning potential mass layoffs, requiring 60 days' notice to affected employees. The filings represent the clearest signal yet that the organization is preparing for the possibility of significant operational contraction.

A LIV Golf spokesperson confirmed the filings while stating that there are currently no changes to operations or schedule. The statement does not change what the filings mean.

Why the Money Is Running Out

Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund announced in April 2026 that it will no longer bankroll LIV Golf beyond the current season. This represents a fundamental shift in the organization's financial structure. Since LIV's founding in 2022, the PIF invested more than $5 billion into the circuit — covering player guaranteed contracts (some worth tens of millions annually), operating costs, purse money, and the expense of building a touring infrastructure from scratch.

The investment absorbed estimated annual losses of $500-600 million without meaningful revenue to offset them. LIV Golf built significant broadcasting partnerships and ticket revenue, but the scale of its cost structure — guaranteed contracts, private jet logistics, premium venues, and premium purse money of $25-30 million per event — made profitability a distant prospect.

The PIF's decision to step back reflects a calculation that four years of losses without a clear path to sustainability requires a different approach to either the investment model or the scale of operations.

What LIV Needs

According to reporting from Sportico, Golf Digest, and OutKick, LIV Golf is seeking between $250 million and $400 million in outside investment to cover its immediate obligations. That capital would need to fund player contracts through the end of the season, operating costs for remaining events, and prize purses that LIV's brand depends on maintaining.

Without the funding, according to some reports, the final two events of the 2026 season could be canceled. The circuit's next scheduled event is set for July 23 in England — shortly after The Open Championship at Royal Birkdale.

Ducera Partners is handling the fundraising effort as LIV's financial advisor. CEO Scott O'Neil has conducted an active roadshow with potential investors. As of this writing, no outside investor has been announced publicly.

The Legal Complications

LIV's financial challenge is compounded by active litigation. English companies World Golf Group and Premier Golf League have filed suit against LIV Golf, PIF, and associated entities, alleging breach of confidence and unlawful means conspiracy. The plaintiffs claim LIV Golf is "a bald facsimile" of their original concept for a breakaway golf league. They are seeking damages of $210 million to $630 million.

A ruling against LIV in that suit — at any level of the claimed damages range — would significantly alter the financial picture for any potential outside investor.

What's Next

LIV Golf's most recently completed event — the Maaden LIV Golf Virginia at Trump National Washington, D.C. — was won by Australia's Lucas Herbert, who led wire-to-wire at 24-under par. The event proceeded without disruption and the 4Aces GC team won the team competition in a playoff.

The July 23 England event is the immediate test of whether LIV Golf can continue its current schedule. Investor announcements, if any, are expected before or shortly after that date. The WARN Act notices give employees and observers a concrete timeline: 60 days from filing, or roughly early September, as the outer boundary of the current uncertainty.

LIV Golf began as the most disruptive force in professional golf in a generation. Its current situation represents the natural consequence of building an organization at extraordinary expense without a sustainable revenue model. What it becomes next depends on capital that hasn't been committed yet.
