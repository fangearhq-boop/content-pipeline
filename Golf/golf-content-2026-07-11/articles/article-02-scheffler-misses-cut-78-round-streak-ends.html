Scheffler's 78-Round Made-Cut Streak Ends at Scottish Open — 5th Longest in PGA History

By Jake Torres | July 11, 2026

PGA Tour

The streak lasted 78 weekends. 1,428 days. It ended Friday at The Renaissance Club in North Berwick, Scotland.

Scottie Scheffler missed the cut at the Genesis Scottish Open — his first missed cut since the 2022 FedEx St. Jude Classic — ending the fifth-longest made-cut streak in PGA Tour history. Only Tiger Woods (142 consecutive made cuts), Byron Nelson (113), Jack Nicklaus (105), and Hale Irwin (86) rank higher on the all-time list.

The timing is not ideal. The Open Championship at Royal Birkdale begins Thursday.

How It Ended

Scheffler shot 68 in Round 1 on Thursday — a respectable score, but one that left him outside the lead pack in a field that was scoring well. Friday brought a 72. His 36-hole total of even par finished two strokes outside the projected cut line of 2-under, sending him home ahead of the weekend without the tournament practice he came to Scotland to get.

The statistics tell the story of an off week: Scheffler hit only 11 of 26 fairways across the two rounds — below his normal standards from tee to fairway — and 23 of 36 greens in regulation. According to strokes gained metrics, he lost nearly two strokes on approach shots compared to the field average. For a player who has led the PGA Tour in strokes gained: approach in recent seasons, that represents a significant deviation from his baseline.

Links golf has its own particular demands. The firmer, faster conditions at The Renaissance Club ask for different trajectory control and wind management than most American courses. Scheffler has competed well in links conditions before, but this week the adjustment didn't come.

The Streak in Context

Seventy-eight consecutive made cuts represents an extraordinary level of sustained competitiveness. Making the cut at a PGA Tour event requires finishing in roughly the top 65 players in the field — a threshold that eliminates a significant percentage of tour members every week, including accomplished veterans having difficult weeks.

To maintain that standard for 78 consecutive events, through injuries, off weeks, personal challenges, and the natural variance of professional golf, requires both exceptional talent and a certain mechanical consistency that allows a player to produce acceptable results even when their best isn't present. Scheffler has possessed both for several years now.

The four players ahead of him on the all-time list — Woods, Nelson, Nicklaus, Irwin — represent the foundational tier of the sport's history. Scheffler at 29 was building toward the upper ranges of that company. The streak was always going to end. Its ending at the Scottish Open, one week before a major, makes it a story in a way that a routine missed cut in September would not.

What It Means for The Open

Scheffler remains the World No. 1 — beginning his 163rd week at the top of the world rankings on July 5, and his 197th week overall at No. 1. A missed cut at one event doesn't change the consensus view of him as the sport's best player or the favorite entering any major.

The practical concern is preparation. Players typically use the week before a major to sharpen tournament form — feeling competitive pressure, calibrating their distance control on European links conditions, and building rhythm through 36 holes of genuine stakes. Scheffler won't have that this week. He arrives at Royal Birkdale having played only two rounds of links golf since the US Open and coming off his worst recent result.

The counterargument: a few days of rest and focused practice at Birkdale may serve him as well as 72 holes of Scottish Open golf would have. The 30-year-old (his birthday was in June) has demonstrated the ability to reset and compete at the highest level regardless of recent form.

What's Next

Scheffler is expected to travel to Royal Birkdale for preparation ahead of The Open Championship's first round Thursday. His game will be under extra scrutiny — not because one missed cut changes who he is, but because the timing amplifies everything.

Royal Birkdale has been modified since its 2017 Open and presents a somewhat different challenge than The Renaissance Club. The conditions will differ. So will Scheffler's preparation. The streak is over. The next question is whether The Open becomes the place he answers the week's narrative.
