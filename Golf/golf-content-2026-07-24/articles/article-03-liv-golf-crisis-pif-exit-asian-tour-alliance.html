LIV Golf's Financial Crisis in Full: $5 Billion Gone, Asian Tour Alliance Kills the Pipeline, and $250M Needed to Survive

By Marcus Cole | July 24, 2026

Saudi Arabia's PIF is pulling out after 2026. The chairman is gone. The Asian Tour is now aligned with the PGA Tour. The league needs $250 million in new investment and doesn't have it yet. This is where LIV Golf stands heading into its final months of funded competition.

The players competing at JCB Golf & Country Club in England this week do not know if their league will exist next year. That is the honest context behind every leaderboard update coming out of LIV Golf UK — and it is worth stating plainly.

Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund, which invested approximately $5 billion in LIV Golf since the league launched in June 2022, announced in April 2026 that it would withdraw all funding after the current season. PIF Governor Yasir Al-Rumayyan, who had served as LIV Golf's chairman since the league's founding, subsequently stepped down from the role. The league is operating on loans for the remainder of 2026. Operating costs reportedly exceed $100 million per month.

The Survival Plan

LIV Golf CEO Scott O'Neil has confirmed the 2026 season will be completed. He has pointed to a $100 million increase in revenue for the year, driven by new sponsorship deals with HSBC and Salesforce, as evidence that the league has commercial momentum. The organisation has established a new independent board — led by Gene Davis and Jon Zinman, described as experienced hands at navigating complex investment situations — with a mandate to raise up to $250 million in new capital.

No new investor has been announced.

PIF's stated reason for the exit was framed as a strategic portfolio decision: "The substantial investment required by LIV Golf over a longer term is no longer consistent with the current phase of PIF's investment strategy." The framing suggests the withdrawal is not about LIV's specific failures but about PIF's broader reallocation of capital. Whether that framing changes the mathematical reality — $250 million needed, none secured — is a separate question.

The Asian Tour Blow

The funding crisis would be difficult enough on its own. The news that arrived on July 21 made it harder.

On the opening day of The Open Championship at Royal Birkdale, the PGA Tour, DP World Tour, and Asian Tour announced a formal strategic alliance, effective through at least 2029. The announcement was made without LIV Golf's knowledge — CEO O'Neil was reportedly caught "completely by surprise" by it.

The significance is structural. LIV Golf had invested approximately $300 million in the Asian Tour's International Series over four years, building the series into LIV's primary global development pipeline. The International Series offered prize pools up to $2 million per event and served as a pathway for players outside the major tours to build their games and access LIV Golf fields. The Asian Tour's alignment with the PGA Tour closes that pathway. It eliminates LIV's most functional feeder system and removes the ranking credibility that the International Series provided for developing players.

For a league already operating without its primary funder, losing its development infrastructure at the same time is a compounding problem.

What Happens Next

The next three LIV Golf events — UK, New Jersey, and Indianapolis — are expected to proceed. Players have contracts. Venues are booked. Prize money for the current season is reportedly secured. The question is what happens after the 2026 season ends and PIF's funding runs out.

O'Neil and the new board have until the end of 2026 to either secure new investment or negotiate an outcome — whether that is a restructured league, a reduced footprint, a merger with another organisation, or something that has not been publicly discussed. The $250 million target is ambitious in context. The league that was supposed to force a reckoning in professional golf is now the one facing existential pressure.

The players competing for titles and prize money this week are professionals doing their jobs in a legitimate competition. The results on the leaderboard are real. The uncertainty about what comes next is equally real. Both things are true simultaneously at JCB Golf & Country Club in the summer of 2026.

What's Next

The 3 remaining 2026 LIV Golf events are LIV Golf UK (JCB, July 23-25), LIV Golf New Jersey, and LIV Golf Indianapolis. After that, the season ends and the funding clock runs out. The new independent board's fundraising timeline will determine whether there is a 2027 season to plan for.
