Coughlin and Shin Co-Lead Women's Scottish Open After Round 1 — Only 13 Players Break Par at Windswept Dundonald Links

By Ryan Calloway | July 24, 2026

Lauren Coughlin, the 2024 defending champion, and Jenny Shin share the Round 1 lead at -6. World No. 1 Nelly Korda sits tied for 14th after shooting even par on a day when 29 players couldn't break 80.

The ISPS Handa Women's Scottish Open at Dundonald Links in Ayrshire, Scotland is the final event before the AIG Women's British Open. Thursday's Round 1 served as an immediate reminder of what links golf at its most punishing looks like.

Only 13 players in a 144-player professional field broke par. Twenty-nine players couldn't break 80. The conditions — windswept, firm, and fast — reduced one of the strongest women's fields of the summer to a survival exercise for most of the day. The few who navigated Dundonald Links cleanly earned their position on the leaderboard.

Co-Leaders and the Top of the Board

Lauren Coughlin shot a 66 (-6) to share the lead with South Korea's Jenny Shin, who also posted 66. Coughlin made 8 birdies against 2 bogeys — a clean, aggressive card that reflects the knowledge of a player who won this same event in 2024. She knows what Dundonald Links asks for. Her Round 1 answered those demands directly.

A Lim Kim is alone in third at -5. Behind her, the leaderboard grows sparse. Lina Pettersson sits at -3 in fourth. A group of four players — Anna Nordqvist, Momoko Yamashita, Eimi Hara, and Esther Henseleit — are tied at -2 in fifth. The drop-off after that reflects how few players were able to manage the conditions.

Shin's co-leadership is notable: she has historically shown comfort on links-style courses, and her 66 at Dundonald suggests she did not need prior wins here to understand the demands of the layout.

Korda's Difficult Day

Nelly Korda, the World No. 1, shot 72 and sits tied for 14th. Even par on this day in these conditions is not a catastrophic result — it represents staying functional on a course that cost most players strokes they could not recover. But it does leave Korda 6 shots behind the leaders heading into Round 2 of a tournament that serves as a form guide for the major to follow.

Korda reportedly had 30 putts in Round 1, an unusually high count that cost her scoring opportunities she would expect to convert on a calmer day. Links golf punishes putting more than most surfaces — the greens are unpredictable, the slopes are subtle, and the wind that affects ball flight on approach carries over to the pace of the greens as well. Korda's 72 is recoverable, but she will need a significantly lower round on Friday to enter the weekend in realistic contention.

Why This Event Matters Beyond the Leaderboard

The Women's Scottish Open is the last start before the AIG Women's British Open at Royal Porthcawl. For players whose form has been inconsistent, or who are trying to peak at the right moment, the Scottish Open carries additional weight as a competitive indicator. Coughlin's defending-champion advantage is real — she knows the course, has performed on it before, and enters Round 2 in a position that gives her two full rounds to dictate her own preparation for the major ahead.

The $2 million prize fund is meaningful. The course reading and competitive confidence that come from leading the Scottish Open are more meaningful still.

What's Next

Round 2 is Friday. Coughlin and Shin will play in the final groups. Korda will be trying to move up the board. The 36-hole cut will follow Friday's round, with the weekend reserved for players who have managed Dundonald Links well enough to make it through. The conditions may ease for Round 2 — or they may not. Links golf in Ayrshire in July offers no guarantees.
