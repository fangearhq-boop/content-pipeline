### STORY 3: LIV Golf Financial Crisis

#### Long-Form Post — 12:00 PM ET

The players competing at JCB Golf & Country Club this week don't know if their league will exist next year.

That's the honest context behind every leaderboard update coming out of LIV Golf UK.

In April 2026, Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund — which invested approximately $5 billion in LIV Golf since the league launched in June 2022 — announced it would pull all funding after the 2026 season. PIF Governor Yasir Al-Rumayyan, who had served as LIV's chairman, has since stepped down from his role. The league is operating on loans for the remainder of 2026. Operating costs reportedly exceed $100 million per month.

LIV CEO Scott O'Neil has confirmed 2026 will be completed, pointed to a $100 million revenue increase from new sponsorships with HSBC and Salesforce, and established a new independent board (led by Gene Davis and Jon Zinman) to seek up to $250 million in new investment. No new investor has been announced.

The news got worse on July 21. Just before The Open Championship at Royal Birkdale, the PGA Tour, DP World Tour, and Asian Tour announced a formal strategic alliance through at least 2029. LIV Golf had spent approximately $300 million building the Asian Tour's International Series over four years — using it as the global development pipeline and ranking pathway for players unable to access major tour status. That pathway is now closed. LIV CEO O'Neil was reportedly caught "completely by surprise" by the announcement.

PIF's exit statement described the investment as no longer consistent with "the current phase of PIF's investment strategy." The league that upended professional golf in 2022 and attracted players worth hundreds of millions in guaranteed contracts is now in existential territory heading into its off-season.

The next three events — UK, New Jersey, and Indianapolis — are expected to proceed. What happens after that depends on finding $250 million that isn't there yet.

### STORY 4: Women's Scottish Open Round 1

#### Long-Form Post — 2:00 PM ET

Twenty-nine players in a professional golf tournament field of 144 could not break 80. In Round 1.

That's what Dundonald Links in Ayrshire, Scotland did to the women's game on Thursday.

The ISPS Handa Women's Scottish Open — the final warm-up event before the AIG Women's British Open — played at its most brutal in recent memory. Windswept, firm, and fast, the links course punished anything wayward. Only 13 players in the entire field broke par.

Lauren Coughlin was one of them. The 2024 defending champion shot a 66 (-6), making 8 birdies against 2 bogeys, to share the lead with South Korea's Jenny Shin, who also posted 66. A Lim Kim sits alone in third at -5.

World No. 1 Nelly Korda is not near the top. Korda shot 72 — even par — and sits tied for 14th. In conditions this severe, even par is a functional result. Thirty putt after thirty putt on unfamiliar links greens will do that.

The $2M event carries real importance beyond its prize fund. The Women's Scottish Open is the last start before the AIG Women's British Open at Royal Porthcawl — meaning every round here is also a form guide for the major to come. Coughlin's defending-champion history at Dundonald Links is a genuine edge. She knows what the course demands. After Round 1, she is leading it by demonstrating that.

Round 2 is Friday.
