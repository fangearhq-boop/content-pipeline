Asian Tour Joins PGA Tour and DP World Alliance, Leaving LIV Golf Without Its Feeder System

By Marcus Cole

July 22, 2026

In a move that caught LIV Golf "completely by surprise," the Asian Tour announced Tuesday that it has joined a strategic alliance with the PGA Tour and DP World Tour running through at least 2029. The deal was negotiated at Royal Birkdale during The Open Championship last week. LIV CEO Scott O'Neil was not informed until Monday.

The announcement lands as a significant structural blow to a circuit already dealing with a financial crisis of its own. LIV Golf had invested approximately $300 million in the Asian Tour's International Series over the past several years. That money built the Asian Tour into LIV's primary development and promotion pathway — the mechanism through which players could earn their way into the LIV roster and through which LIV could project a credible competitive ecosystem beyond its 48-player core. That architecture is now gone.

What the Alliance Does

The new arrangement between the PGA Tour, DP World Tour, and Asian Tour creates a pathway for top Asian Tour members to earn access to the DP World Tour or HotelPlanner Tour during the 2027 season. The DP World Tour will also resume co-sanctioning selected events with the Asian Tour starting in 2027.

LIV Golf can still operate its own qualifying school. What it loses is the promotion-and-relegation system it had constructed using the Asian Tour as its developmental tier. Players who aspired to graduate from the Asian Tour's International Series into LIV's main event roster will now have a different option — one that keeps them in the mainstream professional golf ecosystem rather than routing them through a circuit whose long-term future is uncertain.

How the Deal Came Together

According to multiple reports, the alliance was negotiated during The Open Championship week at Royal Birkdale. The timing — at a major championship where golf's most prominent figures gather — is consistent with how significant deals in the sport tend to take shape, through conversations at the venue margins rather than formal negotiation tables.

What stands out is the secrecy. LIV Golf's leadership was apparently kept out of the loop entirely. The circuit — which had $300 million invested in the Asian Tour — was not informed of discussions that would eliminate that investment's strategic value until after the agreement was in place. That is a notable breach of any expectation of partnership that might have existed.

The Broader Context

The Asian Tour defection is separate from LIV Golf's primary existential challenge: the circuit's ongoing need for Saudi Public Investment Fund disbursements to complete its 2026 schedule. Those two crises together paint a picture of a circuit that is being squeezed from multiple directions simultaneously.

The financial story has dominated coverage of LIV Golf's difficulties. The Asian Tour development is perhaps less visible but arguably more significant in the long run. A circuit that cannot fund its existing events can raise money. A circuit that has lost the infrastructure through which it was supposed to grow and develop future talent faces a more fundamental structural problem.

The 2026 LIV Golf UK event begins Thursday at JCB Golf & Country Club in Staffordshire, England — the same day the PGA Tour's 3M Open opens in Minnesota and the LPGA Women's Scottish Open tees off in Scotland. The circuit will be competing for attention with a week's worth of news working against it.

What's Next

The immediate practical effect of the alliance announcement will unfold in 2027, when co-sanctioned events between the Asian Tour and DP World Tour resume and the new player pathway goes live. LIV Golf will need to determine how to replace the development infrastructure it has lost — if it chooses to remain in the player development business at all — without the Asian Tour as a partner.

For the broader golf world, the alliance represents a consolidation of the mainstream circuit structure around three established tours, each with clear geographic and competitive identity. The deal works against LIV by making the alternative pathway more attractive, not just by removing the Asian Tour from LIV's orbit.

The PGA Tour dealt with this week's news by gaining, not losing. That asymmetry captures where the sport's balance of power currently sits.
