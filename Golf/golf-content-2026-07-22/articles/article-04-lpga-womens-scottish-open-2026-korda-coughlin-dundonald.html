LPGA Women's Scottish Open 2026: Korda Headlines a Final Tune-Up Before the AIG Women's Open

By Jake Torres

July 22, 2026

The week before a major championship is different from any other week in professional golf. Players arrive at the event before the event, shaped by the knowledge that what happens here — how they ball-strike, how they putt, how they manage a links course in Scottish weather — flows directly into the tournament that matters most. That is the defining quality of the 2026 ISPS Handa Women's Scottish Open at Dundonald Links.

The AIG Women's Open, the final major of the 2026 season, follows immediately. Before it arrives, the world's best women golfers have this week at Dundonald Links in Gailes, Scotland (July 23-26) to sharpen their games, identify what needs work, and establish the form they want to carry into the biggest event of the year's final stretch.

A Field Worth Watching

World No. 1 Nelly Korda leads the 144-player field. Korda has been the dominant force in women's golf for two seasons, and her entry transforms an already-strong event into a proper showcase. She does not coast into major weeks; she competes into them.

Defending champion Lauren Coughlin returns. Coughlin won at Dundonald Links in 2025 and arrives this week as someone with both course knowledge and the confidence that comes from having already claimed the title at this venue. She is a legitimate threat to repeat.

The field extends well beyond those two names. Seven of the world's top ten are entered. Lottie Woad (No. 4) brings the energy of a player who has played her way to the top of the world rankings in the span of a single season. Charley Hull (No. 5), who last year made her case as one of the best links players in the world, is a particular watch on Scottish terrain. Hyo Joo Kim (No. 3) and Hannah Green (No. 9) add international dimension to the draw, as do Minjee Lee and Jin Young Ko further down the field.

The field includes 19 major champions with a combined 35 major titles. That is not background texture — it is a genuine representation of the top of women's professional golf in a single 144-player draw.

Dundonald Links

Dundonald Links in Gailes, Ayrshire sits on Scotland's west coast, close to the Firth of Clyde. It is a genuine links course: exposed to the elements, with the kind of natural terrain that rewards creativity and imagination over power. Wind direction at Dundonald can change the character of the course entirely, which means that reading conditions accurately on any given day becomes as important as the quality of the swing.

It is the kind of course where links experience matters — where players who grew up on exposed coastal terrain, or who have invested in understanding how to manage their ball flight in variable conditions, find edges that don't show up in conventional practice settings. Charley Hull, as an English player with deep roots in links golf, has a legitimate knowledge advantage here. Korda, whose success at links events has been built on ball-striking precision rather than course-specific familiarity, offers a contrasting model.

What's Next

The ISPS Handa Women's Scottish Open runs July 23-26 at Dundonald Links in Gailes, Scotland. After this event, the attention of women's golf turns entirely to the AIG Women's Open — the final major of 2026. Players who build momentum at Dundonald carry it directly into the season's most important week. Players who leave Scotland with questions to answer have one week to find answers before the biggest stage arrives.

Korda enters as the favorite to claim both titles. Whether she does so, or whether someone like Coughlin, Woad, or Hull uses this week to announce themselves heading into the major, is the story that will define the Women's Scottish Open when it ends on Sunday.
