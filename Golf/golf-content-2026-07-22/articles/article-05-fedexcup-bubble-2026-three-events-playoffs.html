FedEx Cup Bubble Watch 2026: Three Events Left, Careers on the Line

By Marcus Cole

July 22, 2026

The math becomes unforgiving in the final stretch. Three PGA Tour regular-season events remain before the FedEx Cup Playoffs begin in Memphis. For the players with comfortable cushion in the standings, these weeks are about positioning. For the players on the wrong side of the playoff cutoff, they are about something much more immediate: keeping a season alive.

The 3M Open at TPC Twin Cities begins Thursday as the first of those three final events, with 500 FedExCup points available. After Blaine comes two more chances. Then the FedEx St. Jude Championship in Memphis opens the Playoffs — and only the players who made it through the regular season get to play.

The Top and the Bubble

Scottie Scheffler leads the FedEx Cup standings with 3,823 points. He is safe. Scheffler's position at the top of the standings reflects a season of sustained dominance even after missing the cut at The Open — his first missed cut in four years — last week at Royal Birkdale.

Tom Kim sits 35th in the standings on the strength of recent form, providing a useful illustration of what is possible over a short stretch of good golf. Kim has played himself from bubble anxiety into a comfortable playoff position, which is exactly what the players currently below him need to replicate.

The names below the playoff cutoff include players whose career accomplishments dwarf their 2026 seasons. Jason Day has been a world No. 1, a major champion, and one of the most dominant players in the game at his peak. Brian Harman has a major title. Tony Finau has won multiple PGA Tour events and played Ryder Cup golf. Brandt Snedeker has been one of the best putters in the game for more than a decade. None of that history matters at the FedEx Cup cutoff. Only points count, and right now these players don't have enough.

The Points Math

The 3M Open carries 500 points for the winner — a meaningful allocation in the context of a FedEx Cup bubble. A player who wins or finishes in the top three this week can move significantly in the standings with a single result. The risk for bubble players is that the same tournament also has Scheffler and other FedEx Cup leaders competing, meaning the points race within the event is happening on a field that isn't artificially thinned at the top.

The formula is simple in concept: play well twice in the next two weeks at the 3M Open and the following regular-season event, then get into the Playoffs and compete from there. In practice, executing that plan while managing the pressure of a season on the line is the challenge that separates players who save their years from those who don't.

The Stakes Beyond the Points

The FedEx Cup playoffs are not just a competitive goal — they determine PGA Tour card status for the following season. Players who miss the Playoffs face re-qualifying scenarios that can be disruptive and career-defining. For veterans like Snedeker and Finau, a missed Playoff would represent a stark marker against their careers' decline. For younger players who haven't yet established the exemption categories that allow for a runway, missing the Playoffs can mean losing playing privileges entirely.

The stretch run tends to produce some of the PGA Tour's most emotionally charged golf of the season for exactly this reason. The player who drains a birdie putt on Sunday at TPC Twin Cities to move from 127th to 70th in the FedEx Cup standings is experiencing a different kind of moment than the player who does the same to move from 3rd to 1st.

What's Next

The 3M Open at TPC Twin Cities runs July 23-26. After this event, two regular-season events remain before the FedEx Cup Playoffs begin at the FedEx St. Jude Championship in Memphis. The 3M Open is the largest immediate opportunity — both in points available and in the sheer number of bubble players in the field who need to seize it.

By Sunday evening in Blaine, the shape of the FedEx Cup stretch run will be considerably clearer. For the players who came in on the bubble and left with results, the pressure eases. For the players who don't deliver, two chances remain. That is not much runway, and the clock is running.
