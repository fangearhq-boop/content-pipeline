Zurich Classic Final Round Preview: Why the Fitzpatricks' 4-Shot Lead Isn't as Safe as It Looks

By Marcus Cole | Golf Fanrecap | April 26, 2026

The scoreboard says four shots. In stroke play, that margin would make the Fitzpatrick brothers near-prohibitive favorites heading into a final round. But Sunday's Zurich Classic is not stroke play. It is foursomes — alternate shot — and four shots in that format is a lead that can vanish before anyone reaches the turn.

The mechanics of the format create a specific kind of tension that does not exist elsewhere in professional golf. Partners alternate shots on each hole. When the drive finds trouble, the other partner faces a recovery from a position they did not create. When chemistry breaks down — when one player's rhythm is off and the other can't compensate — holes that should be straightforward become unpredictable. A double bogey in foursomes happens faster than it does in stroke play, and it costs both players the same.

The Chasers' Best Case

Alex Smalley and Hayden Springer have already proved they belong. Their tournament-record 58 in Thursday's first round established them as the most dangerous team in the field — a record that stood for less than 48 hours before the Fitzpatricks beat it. They have the form, the experience in the format, and the motivation of players who have come this far and are not prepared to simply hand the trophy to the leaders.

Davis Thompson and Austin Eckroat sit in the same position — four back, tied with Smalley and Springer — and they bring a different but equally capable game. Doug Ghim and Jeffrey Kang, five back, are the longest shot among the realistic contenders but cannot be dismissed in a format where variance is high and momentum shifts matter enormously.

For any of these teams to win, the math requires either a Fitzpatrick collapse — multiple bogeys or worse — or a run of five or more birdies combined with clean play. Neither is impossible in foursomes. Both have happened at this tournament in previous years.

The Fitzpatricks' Strengths

Matt Fitzpatrick enters Sunday playing the best golf of his career. He won the RBC Heritage seven days ago, shot a 65 in Friday's alternate shot round, and contributed to Saturday's record 57 that separates the brothers from the field. His iron play is elite — he consistently hits greens in foursomes situations, which keeps the format's inherent randomness from becoming catastrophic.

Alex Fitzpatrick has matched that standard all week. The brothers' decision-making in alternate shot — who tees off on even holes, who on odd, how they manage the sequencing of shots to maximize each other's strengths — reflects preparation and partnership that goes beyond one week's collaboration. They have been building toward this kind of moment for years.

What's Next

The final round begins Sunday morning at TPC Louisiana. Four shots is a lead worth having — and in the hands of two players who have demonstrated this week that they belong at the top of the leaderboard, it is a lead worth respecting. But anyone watching should stay through every hole. In foursomes, the tournament is never truly over until the last putt drops.
