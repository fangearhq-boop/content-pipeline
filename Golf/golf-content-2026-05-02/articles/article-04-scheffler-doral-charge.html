Scheffler's Doral Charge — World No. 1 at 6-Under After R2, Seven Back of Young Heading Into Moving Day

By Ryan Calloway | May 2, 2026 | Golf Fanrecap

Scottie Scheffler did not arrive at Trump National Doral looking like the world No. 1. His opening 71 on Thursday left him seven shots behind Cameron Young's blistering 64, and for a brief moment it appeared the revived Blue Monster might not suit Scheffler's methodical, precision-based game.

Then came Friday.

Scheffler answered his tentative first round with a 67 in the second, moving to 6-under and into a tie for sixth place at the $20 million Cadillac Championship. He remains seven shots behind Young, and the mathematics of a Blue Monster charge are daunting. But the world No. 1 has found his form, and moving day at Doral is precisely the kind of stage where Scheffler tends to announce himself.

What Changed in Round 2

The primary story of Scheffler's second round was ball-striking. The opening 71 felt uncharacteristically errant for a player whose iron game has been the defining tool of his rise to No. 1. On Friday, that precision returned. He found more fairways, converted birdie opportunities with greater efficiency, and navigated the Blue Monster's demanding closing stretch without the lapses that characterized his Thursday round.

Six-under through 36 holes is not a bad position at most PGA Tour events. At this particular Cadillac Championship, with Young at 13-under and five players bunched between 6- and 8-under, it places Scheffler in a pack that needs a historic moving day to get into the conversation.

The Math and the Man

To win on Sunday, Scheffler likely needs to close the seven-shot gap on Young entirely over 36 holes — meaning he would need to average roughly 3.5 shots better per round than the tournament leader. On one of the most demanding courses in professional golf, against a player who has been nearly flawless, that is an enormous ask.

But context matters. Scheffler has won multiple times from worse positions. He is paired for Saturday's third round with Brian Harman, another player who understands how to compete in pressure situations. And the Blue Monster has a history of producing dramatic moving-day shifts.

He is also tied for sixth alongside Alex Fitzpatrick, who fired one of Friday's better rounds at 66. The competition at 6-under is real, which means Scheffler cannot simply focus on catching Young — he also needs to separate himself from the chasing pack to stay relevant for Sunday.

What's Next

Round 3 begins at 1 PM ET on Golf Channel, with Scheffler and Harman in the same pairing. The question Saturday is not whether Scheffler can shoot low — he clearly can — but whether Young has another low round in him that renders the deficit insurmountable by Sunday morning.

If Young falters, the Cadillac Championship opens up in ways that benefit every player in the top ten. Scheffler has won enough times to know that leads evaporate, and his task for Saturday is simple: shoot the lowest number possible and let the leaderboard sort itself out.
