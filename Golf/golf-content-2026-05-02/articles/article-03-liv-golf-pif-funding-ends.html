LIV Golf's Existential Moment — Saudi Arabia Ends Funding After 2026, Players Call PGA Tour

By Marcus Cole | May 2, 2026 | Golf Fanrecap

When LIV Golf launched in 2022 with Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund writing essentially unlimited checks, the question most people in golf were asking was: how long can this go on?

Now we have an answer. The PIF has confirmed it will end its financial support for LIV Golf at the conclusion of the 2026 season. The statement from the sovereign wealth fund was as direct as it was consequential: the "substantial investment required is no longer consistent" with the current phase of the PIF's investment strategy.

After four years, dozens of tournaments, hundreds of millions in player guarantees, and the complete restructuring of professional golf's landscape, the Saudi era is drawing to a close.

Immediate Consequences

The announcement triggered immediate organizational changes. Yasir Al-Rumayyan — the PIF governor who was the primary architect of LIV Golf and served as its board chairman — is stepping down from that position. A new independent board has been named, led by Gene Davis and Jon Zinman, with a mandate to find replacement financial partners before the PIF's support expires.

LIV Golf's CEO has said the league is "funded through the season," offering a measured reassurance that 2026 events will continue as scheduled. The organization has already postponed its planned New Orleans event, and its future beyond 2026 depends entirely on whether Davis, Zinman, and the new board can attract significant new investment from sources willing to take on a project that the world's largest sovereign wealth fund has decided to exit.

The PGA Tour Calls

While the administrative picture is turbulent, the most revealing development may be what is happening in private conversations. Multiple representatives for LIV Golf players have reportedly reached out to the PGA Tour to discuss potential return pathways.

The PGA Tour is not expected to offer the Returning Member Program in the form it was previously structured, and any returns would come under more restrictive terms. The players who left for LIV in 2022 and 2023 — many of whom accepted tour bans in exchange for guaranteed contracts — will not simply walk back through the door. But the conversations are happening, and that matters.

LIV Golf can point to genuine business progress: sponsorship revenue up 40% year-over-year, ticket sales rising 129%, and four events projected to be profitable in 2026. These are real numbers. But they don't represent the kind of scale that replaces the PIF's foundational financial commitment.

What's Next

The immediate future for LIV Golf is a race against time — find new investors before 2027, or face the possibility of significant contraction or dissolution. For the players still under LIV contracts, this creates genuine uncertainty about their careers beyond the current season.

For the broader golf landscape, this moment represents an inflection point. Whether the sport ultimately consolidates — with LIV players returning to the PGA Tour under some structured agreement — or fractures further will be determined in the coming months. The board changes, the PGA Tour conversations, and the search for new funding are all happening simultaneously. The outcome will define professional golf for the next decade.
