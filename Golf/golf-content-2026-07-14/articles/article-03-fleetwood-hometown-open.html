Tommy Fleetwood's Once-in-a-Career Chance: The Open Championship at the Course He Played as a Kid

By Marcus Cole | July 14, 2026

Tommy Fleetwood grew up in Southport, next door to Royal Birkdale. He used to climb fences to play it. Now the whole town is watching him tee off there in the Open Championship.

Tommy Fleetwood used to climb fences to get onto Royal Birkdale. He grew up in Southport, the town that sits adjacent to the course, and when you grow up that close to a golf course like Birkdale, you find ways to walk its fairways whether you have permission or not. The kid from the mural on the wall in town — that kid knew, somehow, that the course next door was going to matter one day.

On Thursday, that day arrives. Fleetwood tees off in the Open Championship at Royal Birkdale, the major on the course he knows more intimately than any other player in the 156-man field. His hometown crowd will be louder than any section of spectators at Birkdale this week. And the weight of what this moment means — for him, for Southport, for English golf — will be present on every hole.

Thirty-Four Years

Nick Faldo won The Open Championship in 1992 at Muirfield. He is the last Englishman to lift the Claret Jug. That was 34 years ago. In the decades since, English golfers have come close — Lee Westwood, Luke Donald, Paul Casey, and others have carried the expectation — but none have converted it into a major title at The Open.

Fleetwood has the best chance of any English player in years to end that drought. He has the game: his long iron play and ball control on links turf are elite. He has the history with the course: nobody in the field has walked Birkdale's fairways more, from childhood sneaking sessions to professional preparation rounds. And he has the preparation: Fleetwood skipped the Genesis Scottish Open last week, an unusual decision for a top player the week before a major, specifically to spend additional time at Birkdale. While the rest of the field was in Edinburgh, Fleetwood was getting extra rounds in on the course that matters.

The Group He Is In

Fleetwood draws a Round 1 group that is a storyline on its own. He is paired with Jordan Spieth and Jon Rahm, teeing off at approximately 10:09 AM BST.

Spieth won The Open Championship at Royal Birkdale in 2017 — the last time the major was held here. He knows this course in victory, knows how it plays in championship conditions, and brings a links game built for links weather. Rahm has developed into one of the world's best links players since his move to LIV Golf, and his shotmaking creativity suits Birkdale's demands.

The three-ball has the feel of a genuine early contenders' group. Whoever separates from the others by the end of Thursday will have made a statement.

The Burden of Possibility

Fleetwood has finished runner-up in major championships twice. He has been close. But this week is categorically different from those close calls, because this is not a major played on a course he happens to be good at. This is The Open Championship played on the course that shaped him. That distinction carries a weight that is difficult to compare to anything else in professional golf.

When Fleetwood steps onto the first tee Thursday, the noise from the Southport crowd — people who watched him grow up, who saw his face go up on a mural in town, who have been imagining this week for as long as he has — will be unlike anything he has experienced before. The question is not whether Fleetwood can handle the course. The question is whether he can handle the magnitude.

Players who win at home, in front of their people, tend to describe the experience as something that transcends the game. And players who come close but cannot convert — who feel the crowd and crumble under the weight of what winning would mean — describe it as something that stays with them for the rest of their career.

Thursday is the beginning of finding out which story Tommy Fleetwood is going to tell about Royal Birkdale, 2026.
