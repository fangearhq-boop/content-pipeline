Scottie Scheffler Missed the Cut at the Scottish Open — Here's What It Means for His Open Defense

By Jake Torres | July 14, 2026

The defending Open champion's 78-event made-cut streak ended at the worst possible time. But Scheffler skipped his final warmup to prepare specifically at Birkdale — and history says defending champions respond.

For almost four years, Scottie Scheffler did not miss a cut. Seventy-eight consecutive events on the PGA Tour without going home early — a streak that ranked fifth in Tour history. Then, the week before the Open Championship he is defending, the streak ended.

Scheffler shot 68 in the first round of the Genesis Scottish Open, a round that looked fine. Then came a second-round 72 at The Renaissance Club. He hit only 11 of 26 fairways across both rounds, missed the cut, packed his bags, and headed south to Royal Birkdale.

The Concern: Iron Play on a Precision Course

Royal Birkdale is not a course that forgives wayward driving. Its 100-plus bunkers are placed with surgical intent, and the links fairways — firm and fast — do not funnel errant shots to safety. You need to find the short grass at Birkdale, or you spend your round manufacturing escape routes from sand and rough.

Scheffler hitting 11 of 26 fairways is not disqualifying. One week's statistics don't define a player's ball-striking ability, and the Scottish Open's setup and conditions were their own challenge. But for a player whose iron precision has been the backbone of his dominance — Scheffler routinely ranks among the Tour's best in Strokes Gained: Approach — the timing of a tee-to-green regression is worth noting.

The Positive Case

Here is the reframe that the Scheffler camp would offer, and it is not a weak one: by missing the cut at the Scottish Open, Scheffler gained time at Birkdale that almost no other player in the field has had. While competitors were grinding through the weekend at The Renaissance Club, Scheffler was at Royal Birkdale — walking the course, hitting shots, learning the modifications, getting comfortable.

He knows this course from 2017, when The Open was last held here. He won the 2025 Open at Royal Portrush by four shots. He is not a player who struggles on links golf; he is one of the best links players in the world, and the missed cut — however unusual — does not change that underlying reality.

Defending major champions have come off poor tune-up performances and delivered before. The course memory, the institutional confidence that comes from having held the Claret Jug, and the ability to isolate one bad week from the week that actually matters — these are skills Scheffler has demonstrated.

The Historical Angle

Scheffler arrives at Birkdale chasing something that has not been done in nearly two decades. Back-to-back Open Championship titles. The last player to accomplish it was Padraig Harrington, who won at Carnoustie in 2007 and then defended at Royal Birkdale in 2008 — the same course Scheffler is now attempting to make his second. Tiger Woods won at Hoylake in 2006 but did not successfully defend the following year.

If Scheffler wins this week, he enters a conversation with the very best who have played this game. That stakes level is a motivator unlike anything a Monday morning missed cut can introduce as noise.

What to Watch Thursday

Scheffler tees off in the morning wave, grouped with Bryson DeChambeau and Tyrrell Hatton at approximately 9:58 AM BST. The conditions Thursday morning will differ from the afternoon — The Open's early wave often plays in calmer air before the coastal winds build — so Scheffler's draw could represent an advantage if the wind picks up later in the day.

Watch his driving. If the fairways are finding him — or if he is finding the fairways — the missed cut becomes a footnote almost immediately. If the erratic ball-striking from The Renaissance Club follows him to Birkdale, the conversation deepens.

Either way, Scheffler is the defending Open champion. A player of his caliber with a week of extra Birkdale preparation, chasing a slice of golf history, is not someone to dismiss because of one missed cut in Scotland.
