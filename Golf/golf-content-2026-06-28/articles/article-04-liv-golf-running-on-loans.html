LIV Golf Running on Loans: Four Events Left, Financial Future Unclear

By Jake Torres | Golf Fanrecap

LIV Golf is finishing its 2026 season on borrowed money — and that fact is no longer a rumor. The Saudi Arabia–backed breakaway circuit is operating on loans as it works to complete four remaining events, with the Public Investment Fund's financial backing confirmed only through the end of 2026. What comes after is, at this point, an open question.

The Numbers Behind the Story

PIF has invested more than $5 billion into LIV Golf since the league launched, but reports indicate that only $200 million of the $600 million needed to continue operations has been received for the current period. The circuit is bridging the gap through loans to stay solvent and get through the schedule.

The Louisiana event, which was postponed citing summer heat, provided a window into the financial reality. Louisiana had invested $7 million into securing the event; under the terms of its contract it received $3.2 million back — and the event still didn't happen. That kind of outcome, where a host market absorbs millions in losses, makes future host recruitment substantially harder.

What's Left on the Schedule

Four events remain in the 2026 LIV Golf calendar: a UK event, a New York event, an Indianapolis event, and the $40 million Team Championship in Michigan. The Team Championship is the marquee end-of-season event and represents the largest single prize pool on the circuit — finishing it would allow LIV to close the year with a headline number, whatever the backdrop.

Jon Rahm has been the circuit's most dominant performer in 2026, winning in Hong Kong by 3 shots and in Mexico City by 6 shots through 9 of the season's 13 events at the time of reporting. Rahm's form on the course hasn't dimmed — but stars playing well can only carry a league so far when the structural questions are this significant.

Two New Board Members Can't Fix the Core Problem

LIV Golf added two new board members — Gene Davis and Jon Zinman — in recent months. Board composition changes don't address the underlying challenge: the circuit needs a business model that doesn't depend indefinitely on PIF subsidy. The merger framework with the PGA Tour that was announced in principle in 2023 never fully materialized into the binding deal that would have given LIV players a structured path back to the main tour, and the legal and financial landscape since then has remained unresolved.

Without a new rights deal, expanded sponsorship revenue, or a concrete plan for 2027, LIV players and their management teams face real uncertainty about whether events exist next year — and at what funding level.

The Human Element

The financial story isn't abstract. It affects players who signed guaranteed contracts with the expectation that the league would be operating at full capacity for years, caddies and support staff whose livelihoods are tied to the schedule running, and the host venues and communities that have invested in building infrastructure around LIV events.

The cockpit fire and the electrical failures at events earlier this year — difficult production problems that drew significant media attention — are a separate issue from the financial one. But together they paint a picture of a circuit under pressure from multiple directions simultaneously.

What's Next

LIV Golf's next event is the UK event later in July. Whether the season's remaining four events proceed on schedule depends on the league's ability to manage its loan obligations and maintain relationships with venues and broadcast partners. Any announcement about 2027 plans — from PIF, from league management, or from individual players — is the next significant signal to watch. Until that clarity arrives, the four remaining events of 2026 are what the circuit has to work with.
