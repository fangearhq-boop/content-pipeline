McIlroy's Grand Slam Bid Is on Life Support After a +4 First Round at Aronimink

By Ryan Calloway | Golf Fanrecap | May 15, 2026

Rory McIlroy came to Aronimink carrying the weight of golf's greatest narrative. He won the Masters in April, becoming the first player in more than a decade to win the year's first two majors in a single calendar year — and a PGA Championship victory would have put him squarely on the path toward a Calendar Grand Slam that no player has ever truly completed in a single year.

After Thursday's opening round, that dream is in serious trouble.

McIlroy shot a 4-over-par 74, sitting seven shots behind the leaders and in real danger of missing the cut. With the projected cut line around even par, he will need a round in the mid-to-low 60s on Friday just to play the weekend.

A Difficult Day in Every Respect

The details of McIlroy's Thursday were difficult to watch. He dealt with foot discomfort throughout the round — a physical issue that affected his setup and mobility on certain shots. His driver, one of the most reliable weapons in the game on his best days, was erratic at Aronimink, where the firm fairways and tight approaches punish imprecision off the tee.

The most damaging stretch came at the end of his round. McIlroy made five bogeys in his final six holes — a closing collapse that transformed a difficult but manageable round into something far more serious. He left the course quickly and said little to the gathered media, described by reporters as speaking tersely about his performance.

McIlroy has bounced back from difficult opening rounds in major championships before. He famously recovered from difficult positions to win multiple majors. The question is whether the combination of physical discomfort, driver struggles, and an already demanding projected cut number leaves him enough room to work with in Round 2.

What the Grand Slam Would Mean

The stakes for McIlroy at this tournament extend far beyond a single result. Jordan Spieth won the Masters and the US Open in 2015 and came close to completing the Grand Slam but could not finish it. Tiger Woods' version of consecutive major wins crossed two calendar years (2000-01) and was never a formal single-year Grand Slam. A McIlroy Calendar Grand Slam in 2026 would be one of the most celebrated achievements in the sport's history.

That achievement now requires, as a first step, making the cut at Aronimink. McIlroy has the talent to shoot 65 or 66 on Friday — but Aronimink in its major championship setup has proven it will not gift that kind of round easily. Every shot will need to be committed. Every decision will carry consequence.

The Field Moves On

While McIlroy's situation dominates the narrative, the rest of the field has positioned itself for a genuinely competitive major weekend. Scheffler, Potgieter, Hisatsune, Min Woo Lee, and Jaeger all share the Round 1 lead at 3-under. The group just behind them — Spieth, Koepka, Rahm, Thomas at 2-under — contains multiple major champions who know exactly how to navigate a second-round day when the cut pressure is high.

Whatever happens this Friday, the 2026 PGA Championship is already delivering the drama that major golf is supposed to produce.

What's Next

Round 2 is Friday at Aronimink Golf Club. McIlroy will need one of the great recovery rounds of his career to survive the projected cut and keep his Grand Slam alive. The cut is expected to fall around even par. A McIlroy miss would eliminate the most anticipated story in men's professional golf from the weekend. A survival round — even at just making the number — would keep the dream technically alive heading into Saturday, though a substantial gap from the leaders would remain. All of major golf is watching.
