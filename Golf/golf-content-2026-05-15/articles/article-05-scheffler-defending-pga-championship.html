Scottie Scheffler Is Defending His PGA Championship — and He's in the Perfect Position to Do It

By Ryan Calloway | Golf Fanrecap | May 15, 2026

Back-to-back major championships are extraordinarily rare in professional golf. The list of players who have successfully defended a major is short enough to remember by name: Tiger Woods, Jack Nicklaus, Curtis Strange, Ben Hogan. The ability to peak again on the same stage, against a field that has spent twelve months studying your tendencies, is a test that even the greatest players rarely pass.

Scottie Scheffler is attempting to pass it. And after Round 1 of the 108th PGA Championship at Aronimink, he is in precisely the position he needs to be.

Leading From the Front

Scheffler's Round 1 67 put him in a seven-way tie for the lead at 3-under par — and it did something else that deserves specific attention: it was the first time in Scheffler's career that he has held at least a share of the lead after an opening round at a major championship. For a player who has built much of his major championship portfolio through patient accumulation across 72 holes, having the lead in his hands from day one changes the psychological dynamic.

His four birdies came on holes 6, 7, 10, and 11 — a cluster in the middle of the round that positioned him well before a patient finish. His lone bogey was absorbed without collateral damage. That is Scheffler's signature: mistakes happen, but they rarely compound.

Why Aronimink Suits Scheffler

Donald Ross designed Aronimink to reward precision over power, and course management over aggression. The routing forces players to think through their shot selection from the tee, to leave themselves in the right parts of the fairway for approach shots into greens that punish even slight miscalculations. Scheffler's game — built around his exceptional iron play, his distance control, and his ability to grind through difficult stretches without losing composure — maps directly onto what Aronimink demands.

He does not make the flashy, high-risk decision when the conservative play is available. He accepts his bogeys when they come and moves forward. At a course where trying to force birdies leads to double bogeys, that discipline is genuinely valuable.

The Competition Around Him

The six other co-leaders are not names Scheffler can dismiss. Aldrich Potgieter has the power to attack Aronimink in ways others cannot. Ryo Hisatsune and Min Woo Lee bring elite international ball-striking. Stephan Jaeger is a meticulous ball-striker who thrives in demanding conditions. And the group just behind — Spieth, Koepka, Rahm, Thomas at 2-under — contains multiple major champions who know how to produce low weekend rounds when the field is bunched.

Scheffler is not running away from the field. He is exactly where a defending champion should be after 18 holes: at the top, well-positioned, with the game to capitalize if others stumble.

What's Next

Round 2 of the 108th PGA Championship is underway Friday at Aronimink Golf Club. If Scheffler holds his position through Friday — staying within one or two shots of the lead regardless of what the field produces — he enters the weekend as the favorite. His scoring average in Rounds 3 and 4 of major championships across his career is exceptional. The moment the PGA Championship reaches Saturday and Sunday at Aronimink, the defending champion's experience becomes a formidable advantage. The question is simply whether his Round 1 position holds through Friday.
