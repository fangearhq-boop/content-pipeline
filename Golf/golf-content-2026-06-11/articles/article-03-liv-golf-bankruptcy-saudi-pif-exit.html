LIV Golf Faces Collapse: Saudi Arabia Ends Funding, Bankruptcy Plans Underway

By Ryan Calloway | June 11, 2026 | Golf Fanrecap

The experiment that upended professional golf is facing the most serious threat to its survival since the league launched four years ago.

Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund, which has invested more than $5 billion into LIV Golf since its founding, has confirmed it will withdraw all funding at the conclusion of the 2026 season. The announcement has forced LIV Golf's leadership to begin preparations for a potential U.S. bankruptcy filing — a contingency that, until recently, existed only in private conversations.

It is now official company policy.

O'Neil Speaks — and Doesn't Guarantee the Season's Finish

Scott O'Neil, LIV Golf's chief executive, made his first public statements since the PIF withdrawal was announced at the LIV Virginia event — a lengthy press availability described as tense and closely watched across professional golf.

His guidance to reporters: take the Public Investment Fund "at their word." The message was clear. The Saudis are leaving, and the timeline for their departure is the end of the 2026 season.

When asked whether the league's final four scheduled events would take place, O'Neil could not provide a guarantee. That answer — or more precisely, the absence of a confident "yes" — sent a message through the league's players, staff, and broadcasters that the situation is more precarious than had previously been acknowledged.

The Search for New Investors

LIV Golf's leadership is pursuing parallel tracks simultaneously: seeking new investors who can fund continued operations, while preparing for the legal and financial consequences of a collapse. Bloomberg reported that the management team is actively laying bankruptcy groundwork in the United States. The league has already postponed an event in New Orleans as part of the financial restructuring process.

O'Neil described the types of investors who had expressed interest, suggesting the investor pool exists but that closing a deal of sufficient size — reports have cited a fundraising target of between $250 million and $350 million — remains a challenge. Whether any investor is willing to commit at that scale without Saudi backing providing the floor beneath the league is the central question of LIV Golf's immediate future.

What It Means for the Players

The consequences for the players who built their careers around LIV Golf are significant. Many left the PGA Tour — and with it, Ryder Cup eligibility, world ranking points, and the infrastructure of traditional professional golf — in exchange for guaranteed contracts and the league's promise of a long-term future. That future is now uncertain.

Whether any pathway back to the PGA Tour opens up for those players — and on what terms — will be one of the most consequential governance decisions in professional golf in years. The PGA Tour has not publicly committed to any reintegration pathway, though pressure from players, agents, and sponsors will likely force the conversation as LIV's situation becomes clearer.

What's Next

LIV Golf still has events scheduled through the August conclusion of its 2026 season — though O'Neil's comments cast doubt on whether all of them will be played. The league's new board and management team are expected to present a revised business model to prospective investors in the coming weeks. A deal that ensures the season's completion would stabilize the immediate situation; without one, the bankruptcy timeline accelerates.

Professional golf is entering one of its most consequential periods. How the established tour structures absorb the players and consequences of LIV's potential collapse will shape the sport's landscape for years.
