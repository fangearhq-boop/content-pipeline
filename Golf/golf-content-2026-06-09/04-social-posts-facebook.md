### STORY 2: U.S. Open Preview

#### Long-Form Post — 1:00 PM ET

The 126th U.S. Open is nine days away, and the storylines building around it are extraordinary.

Shinnecock Hills Golf Club in Southampton, New York, hosts the championship for the first time since 2018, when Brooks Koepka won with a score of 1-over par — a reminder of how brutally the USGA can set up a golf course at this venue. The greens are described as "extraordinarily difficult." The rough is penalizing. The wind comes off the water. Shinnecock was designed to test, not flatter.

Into this environment walks Scottie Scheffler, the world's No. 1 player and the man with the most compelling individual narrative in major championship golf. Scheffler has won the Masters twice, the PGA Championship, and The Open Championship. A U.S. Open title would make him just the seventh player in history to complete the career Grand Slam.

And the timing? The final round is scheduled for June 21. Scheffler's 30th birthday. Also Father's Day.

If Scheffler wins the U.S. Open on June 21, he will complete the career Grand Slam on the most personally significant day of his year.

The defending Masters champion Rory McIlroy also arrives with momentum. The field will be full of major winners, contenders, and dreamers who have qualified their way to one of the most punishing venues on the planet.

The U.S. Open doesn't just test golf games. It tests character.

### STORY 3: Dow Championship LPGA

#### Long-Form Post — 3:30 PM ET

Every year, the LPGA Tour runs an event unlike anything else in women's professional golf. This week, the Dow Championship returns to Midland Country Club in Midland, Michigan, starting Thursday.

The format sets it apart: 72 two-player teams compete over four rounds in a stroke-play structure that alternates between foursomes (alternate shot) in Rounds 1 and 3 and fourballs (better ball) in Rounds 2 and 4. It is the tour's only team event, and the partnerships it creates generate storylines that standard stroke-play tournaments rarely produce.

The defending champions are Jin Hee Im and Somi Lee, who both became Rolex First Time Winners at the 2025 Dow Championship and return to Midland as the team to beat.

The field has compelling storylines of its own. LPGA Hall of Famer Juli Inkster is making her first 2026 LPGA Tour start, teaming with her long-term mentee Angel Yin. The Jutanugarn sisters — Ariya and Moriya, former world No. 1 players and 2021 Dow champions — are back together. And twins Aki and Chizzy Iwai represent another sister pairing in the field.

With Nelly Korda fresh off her U.S. Women's Open victory at Riviera, the LPGA Tour enters an exciting stretch of summer events. The Dow is one of the most enjoyable to watch.

### STORY 4: LIV Golf Financial Crisis

#### Long-Form Post — 6:00 PM ET

LIV Golf continues to run events. But behind the scoreboards and the team colors, the financial foundation of the Saudi-backed tour has cracked significantly.

Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund, the sovereign wealth fund that funded LIV Golf's launch and has supported it since its 2022 debut, withdrew its financial backing earlier in 2026. The tour has since been searching for new investors and has reportedly held internal discussions about filing for bankruptcy in the United States.

These are not small developments. The PIF was not just a sponsor — it was the fundamental financial structure of LIV Golf. Without it, the tour is operating without its founding lifeline, and the $25 million-per-event prize purses and player contracts that defined its early years are now in question.

LIV Golf made a significant format change heading into 2026, switching from a 54-hole format to 72 holes to "align with the historic format recognized globally." The change was meant to signal legitimacy. The financial crisis it is navigating simultaneously tells a different story.

For the sport of golf, the implications are significant. A LIV Golf bankruptcy would reopen every conversation about a potential PGA Tour merger that has been dormant since the preliminary framework agreement of 2023. The players who left the PGA Tour for LIV would face uncertain futures. The Saudi investment in the sport would be evaluated very differently.

None of this is resolved. But the story is developing, and the outcome will shape professional golf for years.

### STORY 5: Poston Memorial Win Earns Him Shinnecock

#### Long-Form Post — 8:30 PM ET

J.T. Poston played 33 holes of golf on Sunday at Muirfield Village. He probably never wants to do that again. He also probably never wants to forget it.

The rain-delayed Memorial Tournament turned Sunday into a marathon. Poston began the day needing to complete 13 holes of Round 3, then play a full 18-hole Round 4 on the same day. He entered Round 4 tied for the lead with Ryan Gerard and played 18 holes that culminated in one of the most stressful moments of the entire PGA Tour season.

Standing on the 18th green in regulation, Poston faced a 7-foot birdie putt to force a playoff. He made it. The playoff went two holes. On the second extra hole, Gerard missed a 6-foot par putt, and Poston rolled in a 3-footer to win.

His fourth PGA Tour title. A $4 million winner's check from the $20 million Memorial purse. And — perhaps most meaningfully — a U.S. Open exemption to Shinnecock Hills, along with exemptions to the next three major championships.

Poston, 31, now heads to one of the most demanding venues in golf for the first time as a champion. The Memorial is a Jack Nicklaus-hosted Signature Event, a major in everything but name. Winning it, the way he won it, on a Sunday that asked everything of him, puts Poston on a different level as a player than he occupied at the start of the week.
