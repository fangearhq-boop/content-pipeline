33 Holes, a 7-Foot Birdie, and a Playoff: Poston Earned His U.S. Open Spot the Hard Way

By Jake Torres | June 9, 2026 | Golf Fanrecap

The weather didn't cooperate. The leaderboard didn't cooperate. Ryan Gerard made a 40-foot birdie putt on the 17th hole of regulation to briefly take the lead. J.T. Poston, in what had already been an extraordinarily long and demanding Sunday at Muirfield Village Golf Club, responded with a 7-foot birdie putt on the 18th to force a playoff.

He won on the second extra hole, when Gerard missed a 6-foot par putt and Poston rolled in a 3-footer to become the 2026 Memorial Tournament champion.

His reward: a check for $4 million, a fourth PGA Tour title, exemptions into the next three major championships, and — most immediately meaningful — a guaranteed spot in the field at the 126th U.S. Open at Shinnecock Hills, beginning June 18.

The Sunday That Asked Everything

To understand what Poston accomplished on Sunday, you have to understand what the day actually required. Rain delays had suspended play on Saturday, leaving 13 holes of Round 3 to be completed Sunday morning before Round 4 even began.

Poston and Gerard were among the leaders when play was suspended. They returned Sunday morning, completed Round 3, and then immediately began Round 4. By the time the playoff was settled, Poston had played 33 competitive holes in a single day at one of the PGA Tour's Signature Events, under leaderboard pressure from the first shot to the last.

The physical and mental demands of a 33-hole Sunday are significant at any level of golf. At the Memorial — the Jack Nicklaus-hosted Signature Event with a $20 million purse — the pressure is layered on top of the fatigue. Poston's ability to make a birdie from 7 feet on the 18th hole after that day, to force extra holes when he could have gone home with a runner-up finish, says something definitive about the quality of his competitive character.

A Fourth Win and a Career Statement

The Memorial is not a regular Tour stop. It is one of the handful of events below the majors in prestige but above the average Tour event in competitive significance. Past Memorial champions include some of the greatest names in the sport's history. Poston's name now sits alongside them.

His four Tour wins represent a career trajectory that places him firmly in the tier of reliable, successful PGA Tour professionals. The Memorial win, the biggest of his career, elevates him beyond that. A Signature Event victory earns ranking points that move players toward world ranking positions that affect major exemptions and Ryder Cup considerations for years.

At 31, Poston is entering what many would consider the prime competitive years of a professional golfer's career. The Memorial win, rather than being a career summit, may instead turn out to be the event that preceded a decade of consistent major contention.

What the U.S. Open Means

Shinnecock Hills is one of the most demanding venues in major championship golf. The course is exposed to ocean winds, punishing in the rough, and relentless on its greens. It rewards patience, precision, and a short game capable of saving pars from positions that would destroy most players' scores.

Poston's Memorial win came from exactly those qualities: patience through a marathon Sunday, precision at critical moments, and a short-game clutch factor that showed up when the tournament was on the line. If those same qualities travel to Shinnecock Hills on June 18, he will be more than a name at the bottom of the leaderboard.

What's Next

J.T. Poston heads to Shinnecock Hills for the U.S. Open, which opens Thursday, June 18, and concludes Sunday, June 21. He arrives as a Memorial champion, a four-time Tour winner, and a player who demonstrated last Sunday that he can perform under the kind of pressure that defines major championship golf.

Nine days separate the end of Poston's hardest golf day and the beginning of his most important golf week. The reward was earned. Now comes the biggest test.
