LIV Golf Is in Financial Crisis: What the Saudi PIF Withdrawal and Bankruptcy Talks Mean

By Ryan Calloway | June 9, 2026 | Golf Fanrecap

The scoreboards are still posting results. The prize money is still being distributed. The teams are still wearing their colors and competing. But behind the surface of LIV Golf's 2026 season, the financial architecture that built the tour from scratch in 2022 has cracked in a way that raises fundamental questions about the tour's future.

Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund — the sovereign wealth fund that provided the financial backbone for LIV Golf's launch and has sustained it through years of legal battles, player recruitment, and an ongoing struggle for world ranking recognition — withdrew its funding earlier in 2026. The tour has since been searching for new investors and, per multiple reports, has held discussions about filing for bankruptcy in the United States.

The Scale of the Problem

The PIF was not simply one of LIV Golf's backers. It was the reason LIV Golf existed. The fund's deep pockets enabled the tour to offer $25 million purses at every event, guarantee contracts worth hundreds of millions of dollars to marquee players, and operate the kind of global schedule that required infrastructure no independent sports property could maintain without sovereign wealth behind it.

Without PIF backing, each of those financial commitments is in question. Player contracts that were guaranteed under the assumption of continued Saudi investment may be at risk. Prize purses that redefined the economics of professional golf can only continue if a replacement source of funding is found.

LIV Golf changed its format for 2026 — switching from the 54-hole, three-round format that had been its signature to a standard 72-hole, four-round structure. The change was presented as a move toward aligning with "the historic format recognized globally." In hindsight, it may also have been an attempt to enhance the tour's credibility to potential investors who viewed the shorter format as a barrier to mainstream acceptance.

What Bankruptcy Would Mean

A bankruptcy filing by LIV Golf would not necessarily mean the end of the tour. Chapter 11 bankruptcy protection in the United States allows businesses to reorganize, renegotiate contracts, and continue operating while they restructure their finances. It is a legal tool, not a death sentence.

But a filing would have profound consequences for the players who departed the PGA Tour for LIV, many of whom gave up their PGA Tour membership and the associated eligibility to compete on the world's richest tour. A bankruptcy proceeding that interrupted player payments or voided contracts would leave those players in an extremely difficult position.

It would also reopen, in the most urgent possible way, the conversation about a merger between LIV Golf and the PGA Tour. The preliminary framework agreement of 2023 never fully materialized into a concrete deal. If LIV Golf is financially insolvent, the negotiating dynamics change completely — the PGA Tour would be in a position of strength rather than a reluctant partner.

The Tour Is Still Running

Despite the financial uncertainty, LIV Golf's 2026 season has continued without visible interruption. The most recent event — LIV Andalucia at Real Club Valderrama in Spain — concluded last weekend with Tyrrell Hatton winning at 11-under, his teammate Jon Rahm finishing second, and Legion XIII claiming the team title. The quality of the golf and the caliber of the players remain high.

That continued operation in the face of financial turmoil is either a sign of genuine resilience or a delay of the inevitable, depending on how the investor search unfolds.

What's Next

The LIV Golf 2026 schedule continues with events through the remainder of the year. Whether all of those events occur as planned depends on the investor search and the outcome of any bankruptcy discussions.

For professional golf broadly, the resolution of LIV Golf's financial situation is the most consequential pending development in the sport. However it resolves — revival through new investment, restructuring through bankruptcy, or a negotiated end — the outcome will reshape the landscape that has defined professional golf since 2022.
