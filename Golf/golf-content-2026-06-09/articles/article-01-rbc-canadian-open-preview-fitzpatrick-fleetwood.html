RBC Canadian Open Preview: Fitzpatrick Chases Fourth Win of 2026 as Scheffler and McIlroy Rest

By Ryan Calloway | June 9, 2026 | Golf Fanrecap

The PGA Tour's final event before the U.S. Open tees off Thursday at TPC Toronto at Osprey Valley's Hoot Course in Caledon, Ontario, and the absence of the two most recognizable names in world golf has made the RBC Canadian Open exactly what late-major-prep weeks often become: a wide-open tournament where momentum and form matter more than marquee names.

Scottie Scheffler and Rory McIlroy are both resting this week, preparing for Shinnecock Hills and June 18. The field they left behind is still strong — strong enough that the Canadian Open winner will earn no asterisk — but the absence of the world's No. 1 player and the defending Masters champion reshapes the betting market entirely.

Fitzpatrick and Fleetwood as the Market Leaders

Matt Fitzpatrick and Tommy Fleetwood are co-favorites at +1200. Fitzpatrick's position at the top of the market is a direct reflection of the best season of his career. He has already won three times in 2026 — a pace that would make him the Tour's most prolific winner if it continues — and he arrives in Canada with the kind of momentum that makes him dangerous on any course in any conditions.

Fitzpatrick's game suits the demands of a tournament played on a design that rewards precision over pure power. The Hoot Course at TPC Toronto is not a bomber's paradise. Approach-shot accuracy and iron control are the skills that separate contenders from leaders in the final round, and Fitzpatrick has both in abundance.

Fleetwood brings his own case. He finished just outside the Memorial playoff last week — T4 alongside Sam Burns — and his European tour experience and ball-striking ability make him a consistent threat at any event where fairway iron play matters.

The Wider Field

Four players ranked in the world's top ten are teeing it up at TPC Toronto. Beyond Fitzpatrick and Fleetwood, Collin Morikawa and Justin Rose round out the top-ranked group in the field. Morikawa and Rose represent a combined eight major championships and the kind of game management that tends to show up on leaderboards at demanding venues.

Sam Burns (+1500) is fresh off a Memorial week that almost produced a playoff appearance and arrives in Canada in excellent form. Wyndham Clark, who won on Tour a few weeks ago, also features in the market at the same price as Morikawa at +2500.

The Monday qualifying results — which produced a handful of tour card hopefuls, including the remarkable story of UPS driver Nick Barrett, who earned a U.S. Open spot through sectional qualifying — serve as a reminder that professional golf always has an underdog waiting.

The U.S. Open Dimension

For everyone in this field who is also playing Shinnecock Hills in nine days, the Canadian Open serves a dual purpose. A win earns ranking points, prize money, and exemptions. But equally important is the feel of competitive golf — four rounds of tournament pressure — heading into the most demanding major of the calendar year.

The players skipping this week, like Scheffler and McIlroy, will be scouting Shinnecock and preparing their course management strategies. The players in Toronto are betting that competitive sharpness outweighs the benefits of extra rest and course reconnaissance.

What's Next

The RBC Canadian Open runs June 11-14 at TPC Toronto at Osprey Valley (Hoot Course) in Caledon, Ontario. The U.S. Open begins at Shinnecock Hills in Southampton, New York, on June 18. The two events are separated by exactly three days of travel, recovery, and preparation.

For Matt Fitzpatrick, a win on Sunday would set up the most consequential final-round-of-major Sunday of his career, nine days later, as a four-time Tour winner. The ambition is within reach.
