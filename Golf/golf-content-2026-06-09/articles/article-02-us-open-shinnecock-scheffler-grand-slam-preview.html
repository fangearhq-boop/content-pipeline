Nine Days to Shinnecock: Scheffler's Grand Slam Bid and the Perfect U.S. Open Stage

By Jake Torres | June 9, 2026 | Golf Fanrecap

The 126th U.S. Open is nine days away. Scottie Scheffler is already at Shinnecock Hills, scouting the course, walking the greens, preparing for what would be the most significant competitive day of his career.

Scheffler is the world's No. 1 player. He has won the Masters twice, the PGA Championship, and The Open Championship. A U.S. Open title would make him just the seventh player in history to complete the career Grand Slam — a list that includes Jack Nicklaus, Tiger Woods, Ben Hogan, Gary Player, Gene Sarazen, and Rory McIlroy.

And the final round is scheduled for June 21. Scheffler's 30th birthday. Also Father's Day. If there is a golf script written by someone with a flair for theater, this is it.

Shinnecock Hills: The Punisher

Shinnecock Hills Golf Club in Southampton, New York, is not a course that flatters. It sits on elevated ground above Southampton, exposed to ocean winds and designed without the comfortable margins that modern courses sometimes offer. The rough is penalizing. The greens are, as Scheffler himself described after his recent scouting session, "extraordinarily difficult." The USGA can pin the flagsticks anywhere it wants and produce scores that would embarrass Tour players at lesser venues.

The last time Shinnecock hosted the U.S. Open was 2018. Brooks Koepka won at 1-over par — a positive score for a U.S. Open that serves as a reminder of the scoring environment this course creates. Before Koepka, the course produced Retief Goosen (2004), Corin Pavin (1995), and Raymond Floyd (1986) as champions — all players who combined precision with patience and managed the course rather than attacking it.

Scheffler's assessment was characteristically level-headed. He noted that the fairways are generous enough to allow scoring opportunities for players who keep it in play, but that missing fairways at Shinnecock removes any chance of a manageable approach. The rough is the penalty. The greens are the test. The combination of the two makes it the most demanding venue in major golf.

Scheffler's Grand Slam Case

The career Grand Slam is golf's most demanding individual achievement — not just winning all four majors, but winning all four at some point in a career. Only six players in the history of the sport have done it. Scheffler, at 29 turning 30 on final-round Sunday, has the Masters twice (2022, 2024), the PGA Championship, and The Open Championship. The U.S. Open is the missing piece.

His scouting session at Shinnecock Hills is a serious preparation signal. Scheffler is treating this as the defined opportunity it is. The world's No. 1 player, with a full major collection in sight, does not scout a venue like Shinnecock and then play tentatively.

The birthday-and-Father's Day layering on final-round Sunday is remarkable for its concentration of personal significance. Scheffler won the 2024 Masters and then became emotional talking about fatherhood. The idea of completing the career Grand Slam on his 30th birthday, as a father playing in front of his family, is a scenario that the sport could not have scripted more elegantly.

The Field Around Him

Scheffler is not the only story at Shinnecock Hills. Rory McIlroy arrives as the defending Masters champion, rested after skipping the Canadian Open, with a game that has been in strong form through the first half of 2026. The U.S. Open is one of the two majors remaining on McIlroy's career Grand Slam list — so he and Scheffler share the same defining goal from different points on the same trajectory.

J.T. Poston, fresh off his Memorial win, will make his U.S. Open debut as a confirmed champion rather than an aspirant. Brooks Koepka, the Shinnecock conqueror from 2018, knows the venue better than anyone in the field.

What's Next

The RBC Canadian Open runs this week, June 11-14, as the final tune-up event before the major. The 126th U.S. Open opens at Shinnecock Hills on Thursday, June 18, and concludes on Sunday, June 21.

Nine days of anticipation. Then Scotttie Scheffler, Shinnecock Hills, and the most consequential 72 holes of his career — on his birthday, on Father's Day, with the career Grand Slam in play.
