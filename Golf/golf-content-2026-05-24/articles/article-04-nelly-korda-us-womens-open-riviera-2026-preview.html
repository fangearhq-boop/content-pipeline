Nelly Korda's Missing Major: Can She Finally Win the US Women's Open at Riviera?

By Marcus Cole | Golf Fanrecap | May 24, 2026

There is a list of things Nelly Korda has accomplished in professional golf. It is a long list. Three wins already in 2026. World No. 2 ranking. An Olympic gold medal. A title run that has made her the most discussed player in the women's game for several years running. She has won on different continents, in different conditions, against different fields, and she has done it with the kind of controlled, methodical excellence that the best players in any sport use to separate themselves from their peers.

There is one thing not on that list: a US Women's Open title. That absence has become one of the most compelling storylines in professional golf heading into the 81st U.S. Women's Open Championship Presented by Ally, which takes place June 4-7 at Riviera Country Club in Pacific Palisades, California.

The One That Got Away

At the 2025 US Women's Open at Erin Hills in Wisconsin, Korda came close. She finished two shots behind winner Maja Stark — her best result in 11 appearances at the championship — but close only counts in a handful of sports, and professional golf is rarely generous with near-misses. At Erin Hills, Korda's putter was inconsistent in the final rounds. Opportunities created by ball-striking did not convert the way they needed to on a Wisconsin course that demanded confidence on the greens as much as precision through the air. Stark played exceptional golf that week. Korda played well enough to win against almost any other field. It wasn't enough.

She carries that result to Riviera. Not as a weight, exactly — Korda's mental resilience has been demonstrated too many times to suggest she is damaged by a near-miss — but as information. She knows what Erin Hills took from her. She knows where the putter has to perform when the championship comes down to its final holes.

What Riviera Demands

Riviera Country Club is a very different examination from Erin Hills. The course, designed by George Thomas and Billy Bell and opened in 1927, is a classic of the golden era of American golf course design. Tight fairways. Tricky, undulating greens. Deep rough that penalizes anything less than accurate approach play. Riviera rewards patience and precise shotmaking over raw aggression — qualities that suit Korda's game considerably.

The men's tour event at Riviera — The Genesis Invitational — consistently reveals which players manage their emotions and their shot selection under pressure. The women's game has historically seen similar patterns at US Women's Open venues: players who attack indiscriminately tend to fall back into the field by the weekend. Players who commit to a disciplined game plan, and execute it over 72 holes, tend to be there on Sunday afternoon.

Korda is the right player for that kind of tournament. She does not need to force anything. Her game is complete enough that she can take the course's offer rather than demanding more from it than it is willing to give.

The Competition

Maja Stark defends the title she won at Erin Hills. The field for the 81st edition is expected to feature the top players in the women's game from around the world — the US Women's Open draws the broadest and deepest international field of any championship in women's golf. The USGA's flagship event has produced champions from across the globe, and the Riviera setting will attract attention from players who want to add their name to one of the sport's most storied titles.

But the conversation heading into June 4 begins and ends with Korda. Three wins in 2026 across a variety of conditions have underlined why she is considered the most complete player in the women's game. If her putter performs at Riviera the way it does in her best weeks, she will be there on Sunday afternoon. And if she is there on Sunday afternoon, she is the favorite.
