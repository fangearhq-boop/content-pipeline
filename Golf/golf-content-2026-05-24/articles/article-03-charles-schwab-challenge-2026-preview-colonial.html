Hogan's Alley at 80: The Charles Schwab Challenge Returns to Colonial Country Club

By Jake Torres | Golf Fanrecap | May 24, 2026

Professional golf has had many long relationships with great venues. Augusta National and the Masters. Pebble Beach and the U.S. Open. St. Andrews and the Open Championship. But for sheer longevity of an uninterrupted PGA Tour event at a single location, nothing matches Colonial Country Club in Fort Worth, Texas.

The Charles Schwab Challenge begins tomorrow — Monday, May 25 — at Colonial, marking the 80th consecutive year that professional golf's elite have descended on this particular piece of Texas ground. Since 1946, the tournament now known as the Charles Schwab Challenge has been played at Colonial without interruption. No other venue in PGA Tour history can claim that record.

Hogan's Alley

Colonial earned its nickname the way all great nicknames are earned: through repeated, dominant performance by one player on one stage. Ben Hogan, the Fort Worth native whose name is stamped on the entire history of the game's golden era, won the Colonial event five times. He did it with the same precise, methodical approach to course management that defined his career — not overpowering Colonial, but understanding it, playing to its demands, finding the lines that others missed.

The nickname "Hogan's Alley" has followed Colonial for decades. It is a tribute to what Hogan did here, and it is also a reminder of what this course demands. Colonial is not a course that rewards raw distance alone. It demands accuracy off the tee, precision with approach play, and patience. The greens are not large, the rough is unforgiving, and Colonial's closing stretch — holes 16, 17, and 18, known collectively as the "Green Mile" — has ended more championship bids than any other closing sequence in Texas golf.

Jack Nicklaus has won here. Arnold Palmer. Lee Trevino. Tom Watson. Phil Mickelson. Ben Crenshaw. The names inscribed on Colonial's Wall of Champions represent a cross-section of professional golf's greatest champions across eight decades of competition.

The 2026 Field

The Charles Schwab Challenge carries a $9.9 million purse in 2026. The field is headlined by Ludvig Aberg and Justin Thomas — two of the most compelling players currently competing on the PGA Tour and two men with very different relationships to a venue of this vintage and character.

Aberg, the young Swede who has emerged as one of the most complete players of his generation, represents the modern game's power-precision blend. Thomas, a former world No. 1 and PGA Championship winner, has the course management instincts that serve players well at demanding tracks like Colonial. Seven of the top 20 players in the Official World Golf Rankings have committed to play, making the field genuinely representative of where the professional game sits right now.

Ben Griffin defends the title he claimed here last year — his most significant PGA Tour win and the result that established him as more than a promising player but as someone who can deliver when the moment demands it. Griffin's return to Colonial carries obvious significance, and the defending champion's comfort with the course could prove to be an advantage in a week where knowledge of the layout pays dividends.

The Green Mile

Any Colonial preview is incomplete without discussing the back-nine finish. Holes 16, 17, and 18 at Colonial form a closing sequence that players and observers have long considered among the most demanding in professional golf. The "Green Mile" label understates it only slightly. These three holes have produced leaderboard chaos in almost every edition of this tournament. A player two shots ahead standing on the 16th tee is not safe. A player two shots behind standing on the 16th tee is not finished.

The closing holes at Colonial reward nerve as much as skill, and that combination makes Sunday afternoons in Fort Worth reliably compelling. This year's 80th edition is unlikely to be any different.
