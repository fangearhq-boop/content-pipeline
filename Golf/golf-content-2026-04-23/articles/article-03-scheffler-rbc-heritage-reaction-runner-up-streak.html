Scheffler's Spring of Near-Misses: World No. 1 Reacts to Back-to-Back Runner-Up Finishes

By Marcus Cole | Golf Fanrecap | April 23, 2026

The most frustrating thing about Scottie Scheffler's 2026 spring is that he is playing extraordinary golf. And it keeps not being quite enough.

At the Masters, Scheffler entered the weekend 12 shots behind the leaders and fired back-to-back 66s to climb all the way into contention — only to finish one shot behind Rory McIlroy's historic back-to-back Masters victory. The effort was remarkable. The result was a runner-up finish.

Last week at the RBC Heritage at Harbour Town Golf Links, Scheffler did it again. He battled Matt Fitzpatrick to a playoff — only to produce one of the most uncharacteristic swings of his year on the first playoff hole. Facing a straightforward approach to the 18th green, Scheffler fanned his 6-iron so severely that the ball finished 37 yards short of the hole. Fitzpatrick, composed as ever, hit a 4-iron from 204 yards to 13 feet and made the putt. The tournament was over.

The Reaction

What came next from Scheffler was a reminder of why he is universally respected in the sport — not just for his game, but for his character.

"In both weeks, I put myself behind the 8-ball going into the weekend and had really nice Saturdays and Sundays in order to get myself into contention," Scheffler said after the playoff loss. "On Sunday, it's a shot here or there that makes a difference. This was one of those weeks where any time Fitzy needed something to happen, he made something happen. He definitely earned the win, and he just played great golf."

No excuses. No suggestions that fortune went against him. Just straightforward acknowledgment that his opponent played better when it mattered.

The Numbers Tell a Different Story

For all the runner-up heartbreak, Scheffler's world ranking remains No. 1. He is not collapsing; he is not in a slump. His consistency is exactly what a world No. 1 is supposed to show — contending at every major event, giving himself chances to win week after week.

The issue is conversion. Two top-level near-misses in consecutive starts is unusual for the most dominant player of his era. But the margins were tiny: one shot at Augusta, one playoff hole at Harbour Town. Different breaks on different days, and Scheffler might be discussing two 2026 victories rather than two close calls.

What's Next

Scheffler is not in the field at this week's Zurich Classic. His next scheduled start remains to be confirmed. When he does return, the expectations will be the same as always: compete at the highest level and give himself a chance to win. After two spring near-misses, the next Scheffler victory — whenever it comes — will feel overdue. And when it arrives, it will serve as a reminder that the world No. 1 ranking is held by someone who earns it every week, even when he doesn't take home the trophy.
