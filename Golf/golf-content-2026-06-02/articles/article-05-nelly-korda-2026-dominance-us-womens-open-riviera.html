Nelly Korda's 2026 Dominance: Three Wins, Three T2s, and One Major Still to Win

By Marcus Cole | Golf Fanrecap | June 2, 2026

In seven starts on the LPGA Tour in 2026, Nelly Korda has finished first or second six times. Three wins and three runner-up finishes define a season of dominance that has no parallel in the current era of women's professional golf.

One of those wins was the Chevron Championship, the third major of her career. She is the undisputed World No. 1 on the LPGA Tour.

She has never won the US Women's Open.

The Numbers Behind the Dominance

Three wins in seven starts is elite. Three wins and three T2s in seven starts is something rarer — a level of sustained proximity to the top of leaderboards that indicates a player is operating in a different gear from her competitors. Korda is not just winning. She is finishing second when she does not win, which reflects a competitive floor that most players cannot approach.

The Chevron Championship win in 2026 added a third major to a collection that already included two others. She is one of a small group of active players with three or more major championships. The résumé, at this stage of her career, is already remarkable.

The Missing Piece

The US Women's Open is the one gap. Korda finished T2 at the 2025 edition — close enough to feel the occasion, not close enough to hold the trophy. That near-miss, at the most prestigious event in her sport, carries a specific weight.

She arrives at Riviera Country Club as the +350 favorite. The venue is historic: the first time a USGA women's major has been held at Riviera. The par-71, 6,699-yard course is a ballstriker's examination — precise, demanding, and unforgiving of mechanical errors. Korda's ball-striking is among the best in the world. The setup rewards her strengths.

What's Next

The 81st US Women's Open begins Thursday at Riviera Country Club. If Korda wins, she completes a major collection that will be among the most accomplished in LPGA history and silences any remaining question about whether she can win the sport's most prestigious event. If she falls short again, the question becomes part of her story until she answers it. Thursday's first round begins the four-day answer.
