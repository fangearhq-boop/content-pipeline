Fitzpatrick the Dark Horse: Hot Form and Augusta-Perfect Ball-Striking Make Him Dangerous

By Jake Torres April 2, 2026

The Player Nobody Is Talking About

When golf fans discuss the 2026 Masters field, the usual names dominate the conversation: Scheffler's three-peat bid, McIlroy's title defense, the Grand Slam chasers, Koepka's dramatic return. Matt Fitzpatrick rarely leads those discussions. That, historically, is when Fitzpatrick tends to perform best.

The Englishman arrives at Augusta National following a stretch of form that, examined carefully, suggests he is playing some of the best golf of his 2026 season. He produced consecutive bogey-free rounds over the weekend at the Valspar Championship and followed that with a runner-up finish at The Players Championship — the PGA Tour's unofficial fifth major, played at TPC Sawgrass earlier this year.

The Course Profile

Augusta National was not designed for the biggest hitters. Distance helps on the par-5s, but the par-4s that line the back nine — the ones that decide Masters championships on Sunday afternoons — reward iron play, course management, and the willingness to take what the course gives rather than force decisions into problematic positions.

Fitzpatrick won the 2022 U.S. Open at The Country Club by playing this kind of golf over four days in the most demanding conditions American major championship golf provides. He hit fairways when fairways mattered, threaded approach shots to the right quadrant of greens that punished the wrong side, and grinded through a tense final day without leaking shots at the moments that mattered most. The same qualities translate at Augusta — perhaps more directly than at any other major venue.

The Dark Horse Case

Every Masters produces a player — or two, or three — who enters the week underrated, executes a game plan perfectly, and contends over the final nine on Sunday. The conversation around Augusta's outcome rarely starts with that player's name on Monday. It ends with it on Sunday.

Fitzpatrick is in form. He won a U.S. Open. His game fits Augusta's demands. He is not the favorite, the defending champion, or the Grand Slam chaser with the most compelling storyline. He may be exactly the kind of player who is most dangerous when everyone else's eyes are on the bigger names.

What's Next

Fitzpatrick will prepare for the Masters through the standard week of practice rounds at Augusta. His preparation approach is characteristically detailed — charting shot shapes, memorizing pin positions, understanding where Augusta's slopes feed and where they penalize. If that preparation aligns with the form he has shown through the early 2026 season, Thursday morning in Georgia will be a very good time to pay attention to wherever his ball lands on the first hole.
