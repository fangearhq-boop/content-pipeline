Koepka Returns to PGA Tour — Four-Time Major Champion Back Just in Time for Masters

By Jake Torres April 2, 2026

The Return

Brooks Koepka has left LIV Golf. The announcement — framed by both parties as an "amicable and mutually agreed" early release from his contract — closes one chapter and opens another. Koepka, 35, has rejoined the PGA Tour, returning to the circuit where he won four major championships and established himself as one of the most clutch performers in the modern game.

The timing is remarkable. One week from now, the Masters begins at Augusta National. Koepka, as a past Augusta champion, holds a lifetime invitation to the tournament. He does not need a Tour card to tee it up on Thursday. But his return to the PGA Tour is about more than one week — it is about rebuilding the competitive schedule that allows him to peak for the moments he has always owned.

The Grand Slam Within Reach

Among the handful of active players who have won at least two different majors, Koepka occupies a singular position. He has won the PGA Championship twice (2018 and 2019) and the U.S. Open twice (2017 and 2018). He needs only the Masters and The Open Championship to complete the career Grand Slam.

Of those two remaining titles, Augusta represents his most plausible near-term opportunity. His previous Masters appearances have included strong final-round positioning, and the tournament's emphasis on power and iron play — Koepka's precise physical profile — suits his game better than almost any other major venue. The career Grand Slam conversation became more theoretical during his LIV years, when his path to a Masters title was limited only by his invitation. Now that he is back on the PGA Tour, the competitive context surrounding any Augusta run is fully restored.

The Stakes at Augusta

Koepka joins Collin Morikawa and Xander Schauffele as the three players most seriously positioned to complete the career Grand Slam at Augusta in 2026. All three need only the Masters. All three have the major-championship pedigree to contend.

The difference is narrative weight. Morikawa and Schauffele have been building toward this moment steadily. Koepka arrived — dramatically, on the week of the Masters — after a cross-tour journey that has been the sport's defining business story for three years. Augusta has always been a place where the biggest names produce the biggest moments. Koepka's return adds another thread to a week that was already rich with storyline.

What's Next

Koepka will be eligible to compete at Augusta National starting April 9 as a past champion invitee. His immediate competitive priority is this year's Masters, after which he can begin building a PGA Tour schedule around his remaining major calendar. The question — as it has always been with Koepka — is not whether he can perform at Augusta. It is whether his preparation, his health, and the timing align in the way that only seems to happen when the biggest trophies are on the table. His record suggests it happens more often than most.
