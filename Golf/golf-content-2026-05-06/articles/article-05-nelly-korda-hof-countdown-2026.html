Nelly Korda Is 4 Points From the LPGA Hall of Fame — and Her 2026 Season Is Already Historic

By Marcus Cole | Golf Fanrecap | May 6, 2026

Three wins and three runner-ups in six starts. Eighteen career titles. The comparison to Annika Sorenstam's 2001 dominance is no longer a stretch — and the Hall of Fame is closing in.

The LPGA Hall of Fame is widely considered the hardest sports Hall of Fame to earn entry into among the major professional tours. The requirements are strict, the standards are high, and the path to 27 qualifying points typically takes a decade of elite performance.

Nelly Korda has 23. She needs four more. She is 27 years old. And she is having the most dominant early-season run since Annika Sorenstam.

Through six starts in the 2026 LPGA Tour season, Korda has not finished outside the top two. Not once. Three victories and three runner-up finishes — a record that, in its opening-season consistency, has only been matched by Sorenstam in 2001, a year many golf historians regard as the greatest individual campaign in the history of women's professional golf.

The Three Wins

Korda's 2026 has already delivered one major championship. She won the Chevron Championship — the LPGA's traditional first major of the year — with a performance that showcased both her elite ballstriking and the clutch putting that has defined her career in its peak years.

She followed with a wire-to-wire victory at the Riviera Maya Open at Mayakoba in Mexico, winning by four shots in a performance that Golf Channel described as "crushing another LPGA field." The win was her 18th career LPGA title and her second in back-to-back starts.

Those 18 career victories make her the youngest American player to reach that milestone since Nancy Lopez won her 18th LPGA title at age 23 in 1980. Lopez would go on to be inducted into the LPGA Hall of Fame and is widely regarded as one of the most important figures in the history of women's golf. At 27, Korda is closing in on similar legacy-level territory.

The HOF Math

The LPGA Hall of Fame points system is complex, but the numbers for Korda are becoming straightforward. She has 23 of the required 27 points. A major championship victory earns three points. A regular LPGA victory earns one point. Player of the Year earns two points. At her current pace, she could reach 27 points during the 2026 season itself — potentially as early as her next major victory.

The window for induction mid-season — which would be historically early for a 27-year-old — seems genuinely realistic. Of the roughly 30 players in LPGA Hall of Fame history, virtually none entered at this age. Korda is performing at a level that compresses timelines that are typically measured in decades.

The Sorenstam Parallel

The comparison to Annika Sorenstam's 2001 season is not merely rhetorical. Sorenstam is the standard against which all LPGA dominance is measured. In 2001, she started the season without finishing outside the top two through her first six events — the exact parallel to Korda's 2026 performance.

Sorenstam went on to win eight times in 2001 and broke the LPGA 72-hole scoring record. She is the only women's player to have shot a competitive 59. She is the definition of Hall of Fame greatness on the LPGA Tour.

Korda is matching her statistical footprint in the early going of 2026.

What's Next

The LPGA Tour continues its 2026 schedule with several more events before the next major. Each week Korda competes represents another opportunity to add to her point total and close the final gap to LPGA Hall of Fame induction. Four points may sound like a large number, but at her current scoring pace — and with at least one more major on the calendar before midseason — the timeline is shorter than anyone outside her team probably expected at the start of the year. We are watching a potential all-time great season unfold in real time.
