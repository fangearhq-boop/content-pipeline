Scheffler's Three-Runner-Up Streak: Can the PGA Championship at Aronimink Be Where It Finally Ends?

By Jake Torres | Golf Fanrecap | May 6, 2026

Scottie Scheffler has finished runner-up in his last three starts — the first player to do so since Sergio Garcia in 2014. He's also the defending PGA champion at a Donald Ross course that may suit him better than any other major venue this year.

There is a particular kind of torture in being the world's best golfer and consistently finishing second.

Scottie Scheffler has now done it three times in a row. He lost to Rory McIlroy by one shot at the Masters. He lost to Matt Fitzpatrick in a playoff at Hilton Head. He lost to Cameron Young by six strokes at the Cadillac Championship at Doral. The PGA Tour officially recognized the achievement — if that's the right word — noting that Scheffler is the first player to post three consecutive runner-up finishes since Sergio Garcia accomplished the same feat in 2014.

Garcia, it should be noted, went on to win the 2017 Masters after years of near-misses. The streak, in hindsight, was a prologue.

The Numbers Behind the Streak

To be clear about what Scheffler's run represents: he is not playing badly. He is playing some of the best golf of his career. His Strokes Gained metrics across all three runner-up events rank him comfortably inside the top three in the field. He is hitting greens at an extraordinary rate and his driving accuracy remains elite.

He is simply being beaten by opponents who, in those specific moments, found their best golf. McIlroy's back nine at Augusta was transcendent. Fitzpatrick in a playoff at Hilton Head was clutch. Young in a wire-to-wire Cadillac performance was dominant. Scheffler did not lose those tournaments by playing poorly. He was outdone.

That distinction matters enormously heading into a week when his game is in excellent shape and the venue could not suit him better.

Why Aronimink Suits Scheffler

The PGA Championship at Aronimink Golf Club is not a power golf tournament. Donald Ross's 1928 design — widely considered his masterpiece — rewards precision, shot shaping, and the ability to work the ball to specific targets rather than simply maximizing distance.

Scheffler's game is built exactly for this. His driver accuracy ranks among the best on tour. His iron play is the most consistent in the field when measured for proximity to the hole from specific distances. And his ability to recover — to manufacture pars when he misses greens slightly — is what separates elite major championship performers from very good ones.

He is also the defending PGA champion. Whatever the course asks of him, he has done it before on a major stage. That context doesn't guarantee anything, but it does guarantee that Aronimink holds no mysteries for him that it hasn't held for his major experience.

The Truist Championship First

Before Aronimink, Scheffler must navigate this week's Truist Championship at Quail Hollow — a course that also suits his precision-based game. If he wins at Quail Hollow, the runner-up streak ends before it even becomes a major championship conversation. If he finishes second again, the narrative builds further.

But Scheffler has never seemed like a player driven by narrative. He plays holes, not stories. And right now, his game is too good for the runner-up streak to last much longer — regardless of which course it ends at.

What's Next

Scheffler tees off in the Truist Championship at Quail Hollow starting Thursday, May 7. If the world No. 1 has not broken the runner-up streak by May 10, the PGA Championship at Aronimink follows May 14-17 — an event where he is the defending champion and the favorite at +350 according to early odds. The streak will end. The question is simply where and when.
