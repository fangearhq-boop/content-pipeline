McIlroy's Grand Slam Wait Continues: Three Legs, One Miss, and the Road to the US Open

By Ryan Calloway | Golf Fanrecap | May 21, 2026

Rory McIlroy left Aronimink Golf Club on Sunday afternoon carrying something that almost no golfer in history has carried: three legs of the Grand Slam and a clear path to the fourth.

He had won the 2026 Masters at Augusta National. He holds the US Open and The Open Championship from previous seasons. When he arrived at the 108th PGA Championship, he needed only to win this one tournament to complete the most coveted achievement in men's golf — the Grand Slam. He finished T-7, five strokes behind Aaron Rai, in a final round that never produced the opening he needed.

The Grand Slam waits. The pursuit continues. And the next major — the US Open — is now the destination where everything resumes.

What Happened at Aronimink

The PGA Championship's final round took shape in the early afternoon around a leaderboard that featured Rai, Smalley, Rahm, and a cluster of players within striking distance. McIlroy was one of them heading into the day, five strokes back — not out of reach, but requiring a sequence of events that didn't materialize.

Rai's brilliant final round — particularly the nearly 70-foot putt on the 17th that extended his lead to three shots — removed any realistic path for the players chasing him. McIlroy's T-7 finish reflects a week where his game was good enough to contend but not good enough to win, on a day when the eventual champion played his best golf at exactly the right time.

There is no shame in T-7 at a major where the winner shot 65 in the final round. The narrative around McIlroy is not one of failure. It is one of continuation.

The Schedule Before the US Open

McIlroy will not play at the Byron Nelson this week. He is also skipping the Charles Schwab Challenge. Both decisions follow his typical post-major pattern — a period of recovery and preparation rather than immediate reentry into competition.

His next scheduled appearance is at the Memorial Tournament hosted by Jack Nicklaus at Muirfield Village Golf Club in Dublin, Ohio, June 4-7. The Memorial is one of the strongest non-major events on the PGA Tour calendar and the kind of competitive test that prepares a player for the demands of a major. It also follows Muirfield Village's reputation as one of the more demanding courses in the game — a track that rewards the precise, controlled ball-striking that defines McIlroy at his best.

After the Memorial, the US Open awaits.

The Grand Slam Is Not Closed

It would be easy to frame a T-7 at the PGA Championship — after already holding three of four Grand Slam legs — as a devastating miss. But the circumstances don't support despair. McIlroy is 36 years old. He is the defending Masters champion. He is one of the most reliable major contenders in golf history, with the mental toughness and game quality to compete for trophies at any course in the world.

The US Open is a tournament he has won before. He knows what it takes. The Grand Slam, if it comes, will be achieved through exactly the kind of calculated, patient pursuit McIlroy has always shown in major seasons. Aronimink was one missed opportunity in a career that has learned to absorb missed opportunities and use them.

What's Next

The Grand Slam story continues through the summer. McIlroy at the Memorial in June. McIlroy at the US Open in June. The narrative that began at Augusta National in April is not finished — it is approaching its most compelling chapter.

Three legs of the Grand Slam is where he was in April. He converted Augusta. The question now is whether the US Open delivers the same kind of result. McIlroy's next start: Memorial Tournament, June 4-7, Muirfield Village Golf Club, Dublin, Ohio.
