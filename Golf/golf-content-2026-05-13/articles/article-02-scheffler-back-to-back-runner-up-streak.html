Scheffler Has Been Runner-Up Three Times in a Row. Now He's Defending His PGA Title.

By Jake Torres

May 13, 2026

There is something almost absurd about Scottie Scheffler's current predicament. The world's No. 1 player has been runner-up in three consecutive events. Not third, not fifth — runner-up, three times in a row, in three of the most competitive fields in professional golf. And now, the defending PGA champion walks onto Aronimink's first tee Thursday carrying both the weight of that streak and the expectation of a title defense.

He is the most logical winner on the course. He is also overdue for a win by almost any statistical measure. Something has to give at Aronimink.

The Streak Is Historically Unusual

Scheffler's run of runner-up finishes — which includes his loss to Rory McIlroy at the Masters and a playoff defeat to Matt Fitzpatrick at the RBC Heritage — puts him in unusual company. According to multiple reports, he is the first player to post three consecutive solo second-place finishes since Sergio Garcia did it in 2014. For a player of his caliber, the consistency is almost more remarkable than the outcomes — he keeps finishing second because he keeps putting himself in position to win, and somehow the wins keep slipping away.

That kind of sustained, high-level performance is not a symptom of a player slipping. It is a symptom of a player who is good enough to nearly win every week but whose competitors are occasionally able to match him at the crucial moments. The gap that is missing is small. At Aronimink, with a defending title and a course that suits him perfectly, that gap may close.

The Course Was Made For Him

Aronimink Golf Club puts a premium on iron accuracy and precise putting on undulating greens — Scheffler's two strongest attributes as a golfer. The course is not a bomber's paradise that rewards the longest hitters. It rewards the most precise. Among all active PGA Tour players, few combine Scheffler's level of iron accuracy with his ability to navigate complex putting surfaces.

A victory here would accomplish something else entirely: winning back-to-back PGA Championships in stroke play, which only Brooks Koepka (2018-19) and Tiger Woods have done in the modern era. That would be remarkable company even for a player of Scheffler's stature.

What's Next

Round 1 at Aronimink begins Thursday, May 14. If the pattern of these last three events holds, Scheffler will again be in contention on Sunday afternoon. The question is whether, on this course and at this major, he can finally convert. Based on the evidence, there is every reason to believe the answer is yes.
