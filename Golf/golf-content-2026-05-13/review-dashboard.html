### Long-Form Post One of the most compelling weeks in recent major championship history begins tomorrow, and we are here for it. The 108th PGA Championship tees off Thursday at Aronimink Golf Club in Newtown Square, Pennsylvania — a course that hasn't hosted a PGA Championship since Gary Player claimed the Wanamaker Trophy back in 1962. It's a precision golf course, one that rewards iron accuracy and demands delicate putting on enormous, undulating greens. That description could not suit the current favorites more perfectly. Scottie Scheffler is the defending champion and the overwhelming favorite at the top of the betting board, but he enters on a streak of three consecutive runner-up finishes that would be remarkable at any talent level. Rory McIlroy seeks another major to add to his Grand Slam collection. Cameron Young — No. 3 in the world and hunting his first major — has been in career-best form. Jon Rahm, Xander Schauffele, and a field of 156 players complete a week that is loaded with storylines. And then there's Jordan Spieth. The 32-year-old needs only the PGA Championship to complete the career Grand Slam. He said this week he "feels closer than ever." Maybe this is finally the week. ### Long-Form Post Scottie Scheffler is playing like the best golfer in the world. The scorecard just keeps lying about it. Three consecutive events, three consecutive runner-up finishes. He finished second at the Masters when Rory McIlroy held on. He lost a playoff at the RBC Heritage to Matt Fitzpatrick. And then second again in the most recent event. No player has finished in that many consecutive solo second-place results since Sergio Garcia pulled off the same curious feat back in 2014. Now he arrives at Aronimink as the defending PGA Champion, the world's top-ranked player, and a man who is clearly overdue for a victory. The course suits his game exceptionally well. Aronimink's large greens and tight fairways demand exactly the kind of elite iron play that has made Scheffler the finest ball-striker of his generation. Winning back-to-back PGA Championships in stroke play would put Scheffler in the company of Brooks Koepka (2018-19) and Tiger Woods. There is an argument to be made that no active player is better equipped, statistically, for this specific course. Do you think the runner-up streak ends this week? ### Image Post Caption Runner-up. Spieth's Grand Slam Quest — Aronimink Is His Next Best Chance
NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post The moment Jordan Spieth won The Open Championship at Royal Birkdale in 2017, he joined one of golf's most exclusive clubs — a player who had won three of the four major championships. He was 24 years old, with a long career ahead and what felt like an inevitable fourth title just around the corner. Nine years later, he's still waiting for the PGA Championship. Spieth arrives at Aronimink having won the 2015 Masters, the 2015 US Open, and the 2017 Open Championship. The PGA is the only one missing from his collection, and completing the career Grand Slam would make him just the seventh player in the modern era to hold all four major championship titles. He would join Jack Nicklaus, Tiger Woods, Ben Hogan, Gary Player, Gene Sarazen, and Rory McIlroy in that group. The 32-year-old has tried before, more than once, with his best result being a tie for third at the 2019 PGA Championship. But he has said this week that he "feels closer than ever," and Aronimink — a course that rewards precision iron play rather than raw power — may be more suited to his game than the bombers' courses that have sometimes hurt his chances. History. Redemption. A story nine years in the making. It starts tomorrow. ### Image Post Caption Masters ✅ US Open ✅ The Open Championship ✅ PGA Championship ❓ Spieth needs one more.
