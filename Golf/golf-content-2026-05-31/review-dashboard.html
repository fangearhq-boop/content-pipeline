The Charles Schwab Challenge enters its concluding round today at one of the most storied venues in professional golf — and the leaderboard looks nothing like most people predicted when the week began. US Women's Open Preview — Korda Targeting a 4th 2026 Title at Historic Riviera
NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post One of the most anticipated women's majors in recent memory is four days away. The 81st U.S. Women's Open Presented by Ally tees off at Riviera Country Club in Pacific Palisades, California on June 4 — and it marks the first time in USGA history that a women's major championship has been played at Riviera. The course known as "The Riviera of the West" — a venue that hosts men's PGA Tour events every year — will now test the best women's golfers in the world. And those golfers know who they're chasing. Nelly Korda arrives at Riviera as the overwhelming tournament favorite, following one of the most dominant starts to an LPGA season in decades. In six starts in 2026, she has won three times and finished runner-up in the other three. She won the Chevron Championship for her third major title. The week after, she won the Riviera Maya Open at Mayakoba by four shots for her 18th career LPGA title — making her the youngest American to reach that number since Nancy Lopez. She has not finished outside the top two in any event this year, matching a feat previously achieved only by Annika Sorenstam in 2001. World No. 1 Atthaya "Jeeno" Thitikul will look to challenge, as will defending champion Maja Stark, who returns to defend her title. Eleven past US Women's Open champions are in the field, and all 25 of the world's top-ranked players have entered. Riviera is a course where precision matters more than distance, and course management rewards experience. Whether Korda can transfer her current dominance to a major venue she hasn't won at before will be the central question of the week. US Women's Open: June 4-7 at Riviera CC. Don't miss it. ### Image Post Caption Riviera. June 4-7. The first USGA women's major at this iconic venue. Korda enters as the favorite. Thitikul, Stark, and the world's best await. 🏆 #USWomensOpen #LPGA

### Long-Form Post Ludvig Åberg is one of the most talented young players in professional golf. He hits it long, he hits it straight, he sees the game with the kind of clarity that coaches describe when they talk about "seeing the course." And yet, heading into Sunday at Colonial, he sits tied for 12th at seven under par — searching, yet again, for the win that will move him from "promising" to "proven." He arrived at the Charles Schwab Challenge having made a change: a new putter. After multiple top-10 finishes in 2026 without converting, Åberg decided the flatstick needed an upgrade. Through three rounds, the switch showed some results — he's made enough putts to stay in the conversation at Colonial. But being tied for 12th at the 54-hole mark doesn't put a trophy in his hands. For context, Åberg won the Genesis Invitational earlier in 2026, showing that the win is in there. But converting the kind of sustained top-10 form he's shown into more victories has been the challenge. The cut line also told a story this week: Tony Finau (even par, back-to-back 70s) and Rickie Fowler (70-73) both missed the weekend. Colonial can be a course that rewards ball-strikers who can scramble — and both found themselves without the form to capitalize. The 2026 PGA Tour season is providing exactly the kind of wide-open competition that makes the sport compelling.
