### STORY 5: Alex Smalley's 1.07-Stroke Short Game Flip

There is a concept in golf analytics that separates the elite from the very good: strokes gained around the greens. It measures how much better or worse a player performs relative to the field on shots from within 100 yards and on the putting green. Great putters and wedge players gain fractions on every hole. Poor short game players give them back.

Alex Smalley has been one of the best iron players on the PGA Tour for years. His ball-striking is genuine — he ranks seventh in the Charles Schwab Challenge field in strokes gained tee-to-green. The problem, until recently, was that the scoring touch that converts elite ball-striking into low rounds wasn't there. In 2024, Smalley lost 0.62 strokes per round around the greens compared to the tour average.

In 2026, he is gaining 0.45 strokes per round. That is a 1.07-stroke improvement — one of the most dramatic single-season short game turnarounds in recent tour history.

The results have been immediate and concrete. Six consecutive top-25 finishes. Two T2 finishes at the Zurich Classic and the PGA Championship. A run of form that has made him one of the most discussed players on the PGA Tour heading into this week's Charles Schwab Challenge at Colonial Country Club in Fort Worth.

Colonial suits Smalley's profile. The narrow fairways (average 27 yards — third-tightest on tour) require the kind of controlled ball-striking that is his baseline. The approach angles into small greens demand precision that he has always had. And now, with the short game transformation complete, he has the scoring capability to convert those approach shots into birdies and pars rather than bogeys.

He is listed at +4000 this week — a genuine long-shot price for a player who arrives with the most momentum in the field. That disconnect between his price and his form is the most interesting value story in golf right now.
