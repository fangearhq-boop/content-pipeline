Riviera Comes to Women's Golf: The 2026 US Women's Open Preview

By Marcus Cole | May 27, 2026 | Golf Fanrecap

Riviera Country Club has hosted men's professional golf for decades — the Los Angeles Open, the Nissan Open, the Genesis Invitational. Its place in the canon of iconic American golf venues is beyond debate. But on June 4, 2026, it will host something it has never hosted before: the United States Women's Open Championship. The 81st edition of one of golf's most prestigious events arrives at a venue that will challenge every player in the field in ways they have not been challenged at a women's major before.

The USGA has made a statement by bringing the US Women's Open to Riviera. It is a declaration that the women's game deserves the same historic venues that have defined the men's major circuit, and a bet that the quality of the current women's field is compelling enough to fill Riviera's amphitheater galleries.

The Field

The entry numbers support that bet. The 2026 US Women's Open drew 1,897 entries — tied for third all-time — and every player ranked in the world's top 25 has confirmed participation. This is the deepest gathering of women's golf talent the US Women's Open has seen in recent years, and the storylines at the top of the leaderboard are genuinely compelling.

Nelly Korda enters as the clear betting favorite. The American has won three LPGA events in 2026 — including the Chevron Championship, her third career major title — and has established herself as the dominant force in women's golf through the first half of the season. She was runner-up at the 2025 US Women's Open at Erin Hills, where Maja Stark denied her by two shots in a final-round performance that announced the Swede's arrival as a major champion. Korda will arrive at Riviera with unfinished business and the best form of any player in the field.

World No. 1 Jeeno Thitikul gives Korda her most dangerous challenger. The Thai star has been the most consistent performer in women's golf over the past 18 months, and her all-around ball-striking ability is well-suited to a Riviera layout that rewards precision over power. Eleven past US Women's Open champions are entered, including Stark as the defending champion — a group of former winners that adds historical weight to every leaderboard position.

What Riviera Demands

Riviera is not a punishing course in the traditional sense. The fairways are generous enough, the yardage manageable by modern standards. What it demands instead is intelligence — the ability to read how the terrain, the wind, and the kikuyu rough interact to create risk that is not always obvious from the tee.

The barranca — a dry creek bed that runs through several holes — is the defining strategic element at Riviera. It forces players to make decisions about how much to challenge it, how far to lay back, and what happens when a shot that looked safe ends up in the wrong place. The barranca has turned good rounds into scrambles and scrambles into disasters at every men's event Riviera has hosted. It will do the same in June.

The kikuyu rough is unlike anything most players in the women's field will have encountered at a major venue. Kikuyu is thicker and clumpier than bent grass or fescue rough at traditional major sites. It grabs clubfaces, limits spin, and makes clean contact from difficult lies genuinely difficult. Players who have spent their careers preparing for traditional major setups will need to adapt their approach play and their expectations around the greens.

Elevation change is the third challenge Riviera presents. The course's routing through mature trees and significant grade changes requires careful management of distances, trajectories, and how approach shots will release on greens built into hillsides. It rewards players who can visualize shots in three dimensions rather than simply executing yardages from flat lies.

The Historic Significance

Beyond the competitive storylines, the 2026 US Women's Open at Riviera is a landmark moment for the women's game. The USGA has committed to elevating the championship's venues to match the prestige of the US Open, and Riviera — one of the most beloved and recognizable courses in American golf — is the clearest statement yet of that commitment.

Players who have grown up watching men's golf at Riviera, who know the 10th hole and the amphitheater green and the views across Pacific Palisades, will compete there for a major championship trophy for the first time. That is something worth acknowledging. Whatever happens on the leaderboard over four rounds starting June 4, the week itself represents a step forward for how the women's game is presented to the world.

Korda has the form. Thitikul has the game. Stark has the experience. And Riviera has the stage. The combination should produce a US Women's Open worth watching from the first shot to the last putt.
