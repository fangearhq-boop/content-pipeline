The 1.07-Stroke Flip: How Alex Smalley Fixed the One Thing That Was Holding Him Back

By Ryan Calloway | May 27, 2026 | Golf Fanrecap

Professional golf analytics have a way of identifying the exact problem a player needs to solve before the player himself will admit to it. In Alex Smalley's case, the numbers were unambiguous for two years: elite ball-striker, good enough iron player to compete at any venue on the PGA Tour, but a short game that consistently gave back what the tee-to-green game built. In 2024, Smalley lost 0.62 strokes per round around the greens compared to tour average. The irons were producing birdie looks. The wedges and putter weren't converting them.

In 2026, Smalley is gaining 0.45 strokes per round around the greens. That is a swing of 1.07 strokes — not over a career, not across multiple seasons of gradual improvement, but in the span of one offseason and one season of tournament play.

What Changed

The specifics of what any player works on in an offseason are private by nature, and Smalley has not detailed a single breakthrough moment. What the statistics show is that the change is real and sustained. He is not getting luckier around the greens or benefiting from favorable scoring conditions. He is making cleaner contact from tight lies, controlling his distances on partial wedge shots more precisely, and converting a higher percentage of the birdie looks his iron play continues to produce.

The result is a player who was already good enough to compete for wins now operating as a legitimate contender. Six consecutive top-25 finishes is not an accident. Back-to-back second-place finishes at the Zurich Classic and the PGA Championship — two events as different in format and venue as any two events on the tour calendar — confirm that the improvement is not course-specific or format-specific. It is a change in how he plays the game.

Why Colonial Suits Him Specifically

The Charles Schwab Challenge at Colonial Country Club is as good a venue as Smalley could ask for given what his game currently looks like. The course rewards the specific combination of skills that define his profile: controlled tee shots on narrow fairways, precise approach play into small greens, and the short game conversion that he lacked before this season.

At 27 yards average fairway width — third-narrowest on the PGA Tour — Colonial separates players by their ability to hit fairways, not by how far they can drive the ball. Smalley ranks seventh in the Charles Schwab Challenge field in strokes gained tee-to-green. His ball-striking has always been the foundation of his game. Colonial puts that foundation to maximum use.

The Hanse renovation lowered Colonial's greens and made them more receptive to approach shots, which plays directly into the strength of a player with elite approach play. And the firm, fast Bermuda surfaces around the greens are precisely the conditions where the difference between a 0.62-loss short game and a 0.45-gain short game becomes visible in the scorecard.

The Value Question

Smalley is listed at +4000 entering the week at Colonial. That price deserves attention from anyone who follows golf closely enough to understand what the statistics are showing.

He is arriving with two runner-up finishes in his last several starts. He is gaining strokes in every phase of the game. He is playing a course that matches his ball-striking profile. The field is without Scottie Scheffler and Rory McIlroy. And yet the market has him priced as a 40-to-1 long shot — a player you bet on if you're looking for upside, not one the oddsmakers expect to contend.

That disconnect between his form and his price is exactly the kind of inefficiency that gets corrected when a player converts the numbers into a Sunday leaderboard position. The 1.07-stroke short game flip doesn't guarantee anything. But it has turned Alex Smalley from a good PGA Tour player into one who can win at venues like Colonial. This week, that matters.
