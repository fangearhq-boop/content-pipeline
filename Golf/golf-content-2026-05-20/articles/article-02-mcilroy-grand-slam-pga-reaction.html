McIlroy's Grand Slam Bid Falls Short at Aronimink — Now the Focus Shifts to Shinnecock Hills

By Jake Torres | Golf Fanrecap | May 20, 2026

Rory McIlroy arrived at the 2026 PGA Championship carrying the season's most compelling storyline: a chance at the calendar Grand Slam, having won the Masters in April. He departed Aronimink Golf Club tied for seventh at 4-under par — five shots behind the champion, Aaron Rai — with his Grand Slam bid on hold and the US Open at Shinnecock Hills now the next chapter.

The numbers from Sunday tell the story concisely. McIlroy shot a final round of 1-under 69. He made one birdie on the front nine. He bogeyed the drivable par-4 13th, where even players of shorter length target the green in the final round. He failed to birdie either par-5 on the back nine — the holes that most directly create the scoring separation between champions and also-rans at major championships.

"Not birdieing the two par 5s and making the bogey at the drivable par-4 13th," McIlroy said afterward, summarizing where the round — and the Grand Slam bid — came apart.

What Went Wrong

McIlroy's season has been remarkable. His Masters win in April was his first at Augusta National after years of close calls and near-misses. It silenced a decade of questions about whether he could win there and put his name on the most famous trophy in golf. That achievement was authentic and complete.

At Aronimink, the circumstances were different. Being in contention at a major with a Grand Slam on the line generates a specific kind of pressure — the awareness of what a win would mean is constant, and it is exceptionally difficult to separate that awareness from the moment-to-moment execution that winning golf requires.

McIlroy is 36, with multiple major victories on his resume. He has handled major pressure better than almost any player of his generation. Sunday at Aronimink was not a collapse — a 69 on a demanding final round in a major championship is respectable. But the specific failures on the holes where he needed to take advantage — the par-5s, the drivable par-4 — were the difference between contention and a distant T7 finish.

What's Next

The calendar Grand Slam is still alive. McIlroy needs three more majors — the US Open, The Open Championship, and the PGA Championship — to complete the set in a single year. The US Open at Shinnecock Hills (June 18-21) is his next opportunity. Shinnecock has historically rewarded precise ball-striking and course management — qualities McIlroy possesses at a level that few players match.

The goal is unchanged. The process continues. And for Rory McIlroy, the story of his 2026 season is far from written.
