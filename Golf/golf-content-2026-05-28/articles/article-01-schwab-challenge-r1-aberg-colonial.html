Charles Schwab Challenge Opens at Colonial: Åberg Leads Favorites at Hogan's Alley

Round 1 of the 2026 Charles Schwab Challenge is underway at Colonial Country Club in Fort Worth, Texas, and the field of contenders gathering at Hogan's Alley represents some of the best storytelling in professional golf. The tournament runs May 28-31, carries a purse of approximately $9.9 million, and centers around a question that has followed the betting market's favorite all season: when does Ludvig Åberg finally win?

Åberg opened as the favorite at +900 and has since moved to +800-+1000 range at most sportsbooks. Six top-10 finishes in his last seven PGA Tour starts, a solo-third at this very tournament last season, and the week's best strokes-gained tee-to-green numbers all point to a player who should be adding to his win column. So far in 2026, he has not.

Colonial Country Club and the Hanse Touch

Colonial Country Club's relationship with Ben Hogan — who dominated the event in its early years — gives it a nickname that carries more weight than most: Hogan's Alley. The course demands precision over power, rewards players who can work the ball both ways, and punishes loose swings with thick rough and deep greenside bunkers.

Golf architect Gil Hanse completed a renovation following the 2023 tournament that has been settling into the competitive landscape over the subsequent years. Hanse lowered the majority of the greens to make them more receptive and introduced dramatic changes to several par-3 holes, restoring the course closer to its original Perry Maxwell design intentions. The result is a test that continues to reward ball-striking excellence over raw length — which benefits players like Åberg, Fowler, and Thomas who rely on their iron play and short game rather than overpowering the layout.

The Contenders

Ben Griffin, who won the Charles Schwab Challenge in 2025 for his second career PGA Tour title, returns to defend. Griffin showed at Colonial what a peaking performance from a streaky player can produce — a final-round 60 that included the low score in recent tournament history.

Rickie Fowler enters as perhaps the most compelling human interest story in the field. He has not won on the PGA Tour since the summer of 2023, but 2026 has produced three top-10 finishes in his last four starts — including three consecutive top-10s in signature events before a PGA Championship week that was, by his own admission, a poor fit for his putting game. At Colonial, where he has not missed a cut in five consecutive appearances and has gained nearly six strokes on average against the field over his last three visits, Fowler's historical relationship with this course borders on exceptional.

Justin Thomas has shown what Golf Digest described as "a promising trend" at Colonial, making him a legitimate contender with the course knowledge and elite ball-striking to navigate the layout's demands. Russell Henley rounds out the group of players analysts consistently identify as capable of contending through the weekend.

The Åberg Equation

Åberg's statistical profile this season is one of the most analyzed in professional golf precisely because the gap between his performance metrics and his win count is so unusual. He leads or ranks near the top of virtually every strokes-gained category that predicts future success. He finishes inside the top 10 consistently. He handles major championship venues and regular Tour events with equal competence.

The most common explanation for the win drought is variance — in professional golf, elite players routinely go through stretches of strong but winless form before the sample size catches up with the expectation. Åberg's 2025 Colonial T3 suggests this course fits his game particularly well. If there is a week on the 2026 schedule where the metrics and the scoreboard align for him, Colonial Country Club in late May has as much supporting evidence as any.
