Wyndham Clark: Two US Open Titles, Three Years Apart — What It Means for His Career

By Jake Torres | June 23, 2026

When Wyndham Clark made his final putt at Shinnecock Hills on Sunday, he joined an exclusive club. Two US Open titles in three years — 2023 at Los Angeles Country Club and 2026 at Shinnecock Hills — place Clark among the rare players who have mastered golf's most demanding major test more than once. And on Monday morning, he was already on his way to Connecticut and the Travelers Championship.

The Wire-to-Wire Victory

Clark's 2026 US Open performance at Shinnecock Hills was a study in sustained excellence under pressure. He opened with a 64 — the second-lowest round in the course's storied history and a figure that established an immediate command of a field that included every major contender in men's golf. He held that lead through four days on one of the most demanding venues on the planet.

His final margin was one shot over Sam Burns, who posted a 73 on Sunday in pursuit of his first major title. The Shinnecock crowd — historically one of the most demanding and at times hostile galleries in American golf — tested Clark throughout the week. He held his composure and closed without incident.

Clark's wire-to-wire win was the first in US Open history since Germany's Martin Kaymer led from start to finish at Pinehurst No. 2 in 2014. He is now the ninth player in US Open history to win the championship while never relinquishing the lead.

What Two US Opens Mean

Two US Open victories in three years is a mark of elite, repeatable excellence at the hardest major in the world. The US Open is specifically designed to identify the best golfer in the field rather than reward fortunate bounces or birdie festivals — tight fairways, fast greens, punishing rough, and firm conditions that expose every weakness. Winning it once is an achievement. Winning it twice confirms a player's status as one of the defining talents of his era.

Clark's 2023 win at LACC established him as a major champion. His 2026 win at Shinnecock confirms he belongs in the conversation with the sport's best. At 32, he is entering what many consider the prime window for professional golfers — old enough to have course management and composure, young enough to have the physical tools for continued success.

Immediately Back to Work

Clark's decision to enter the Travelers Championship this week reflects both the financial reality of professional golf at the signature-event level and the competitive mentality that produced two US Open titles. The $3.6 million winner's check at TPC River Highlands is significant. More importantly, signature events carry world-ranking points and FedEx Cup implications that matter when the season's full picture is tallied.

Against a field that includes world No. 1 Scottie Scheffler, Jordan Spieth, Tommy Fleetwood, and Xander Schauffele, Clark will face a genuine test of whether US Open momentum translates to the tour's most birdie-friendly venues. It rarely does directly — but Clark has proven more than capable of adapting.

What's Next

The Travelers Championship begins Thursday at TPC River Highlights. Clark tees off as a player carrying significant momentum and historical validation. After that, his summer calendar will fill with the remaining majors and the FedEx Cup playoffs. A third major title at some point in his career is not beyond the realm of possibility for a player who has won the most demanding test in golf twice before age 33.
