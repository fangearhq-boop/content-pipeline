Aaron Rai Wins PGA Championship at 290-1 — First English Winner in 107 Years

By Marcus Cole | May 18, 2026 | Golf Fanrecap

Nobody expected this. That is not a cliché. It is a verifiable fact.

Aaron Rai walked into Aronimink Golf Club on Thursday morning as a 290-1 long shot. He had never finished inside the top 15 at a major championship in his career. He had won exactly once on the PGA TOUR — the 2024 Wyndham Championship — and had spent most of his professional life as a solid but anonymous presence on the DP World Tour.

On Sunday evening, he walked out of Aronimink holding the Wanamaker Trophy, having shot a final-round 65 to win the 108th PGA Championship by three shots, finishing at -9 overall. According to ESPN Research, Rai is the biggest long-shot winner in a major in at least the past 20 years — surpassing Phil Mickelson's 2021 PGA Championship win and Louis Oosthuizen's 2010 Open Championship as the most improbable result of the modern era.

He also became the first Englishman to win the PGA Championship since Jim Barnes in 1919 — a gap of 107 years.

How He Did It

Rai's final round was a masterclass in momentum management. He made an eagle on the par-5 ninth hole, rolling in a 40-foot putt that ignited a run of extraordinary putting. He one-putted seven consecutive greens in a stretch that turned a competitive leaderboard into Rai's private stage. Every time a competitor threatened to close the gap, another red number appeared next to his name.

The dagger came at the 17th hole, where Rai holed a 68-foot birdie putt to virtually seal the championship. By the time he reached the 18th, the only remaining question was the margin of victory. He won by three shots over Jon Rahm and Alex Smalley, who both finished at -6.

The round produced one of the cleanest performances under pressure in recent major championship history. Rai made no bogeys. He gave nothing back. He played each hole as if the situation demanded it — because it did.

The Historical Context

The Wanamaker Trophy has been awarded since 1916. In that time, English golfers have claimed it twice: Jim Barnes in 1919 and now Aaron Rai in 2026. Barnes was the winner of the first two PGA Championships (1916 and 1919, with the event suspended during World War I). Rai's name sits alongside his on the trophy's list of champions.

For Rai personally, the win is a transformation. He turned professional in 2012, built a solid career on the DP World Tour with three victories, and made his way to the PGA TOUR through persistence rather than prodigious talent. At 31, he arrived at his first major championship win having spent his entire career as a respected professional who had never threatened at the game's highest level. Sunday changed the conversation permanently.

He will collect $3.6 million from the record $20.5 million purse — and a place in major championship history that no one anticipated he would ever occupy.

What's Next

Aaron Rai is a major champion. That sentence, written before Thursday, would have seemed impossible. It is now an unalterable fact. As a PGA Championship winner, Rai is exempt from qualifying for major championships for the next five years and will compete in the Masters for the first time in 2027. For the rest of the golf world — McIlroy, Scheffler, Rahm, and every other player who spent the week at Aronimink — the next major, the US Open, arrives in June. For Rai, the next event comes with a different kind of pressure: the weight of being a major champion, and the knowledge that every future leaderboard will be measured against what he accomplished at Aronimink this week.
