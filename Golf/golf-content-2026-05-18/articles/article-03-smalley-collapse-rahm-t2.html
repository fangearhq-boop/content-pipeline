Smalley's Sunday Collapse Opens the Door — Rahm and Smalley Share T2 as Rai Runs Away

By Jake Torres | May 18, 2026 | Golf Fanrecap

Alex Smalley had one job on Sunday morning: stay on the leaderboard long enough to give himself a chance to win.

He managed it for several holes. Then came the 6th.

Smalley, who had entered the final round as the 54-hole leader at -6, made a double bogey on the par-4 6th hole. He followed it with a bogey at the 8th. In the span of three holes, the lead evaporated. The door that swung open is the one Aaron Rai walked through — and Rai never looked back.

Smalley finished tied for second at -6, sharing the position with Jon Rahm. Three shots separated them from champion Aaron Rai, who shot a final-round 65 to claim the Wanamaker Trophy.

What Could Have Been

Smalley's week at Aronimink was remarkable for reasons that extend beyond Sunday's collapse. He arrived at the 108th PGA Championship without a PGA TOUR win on his record, yet managed to navigate one of the toughest fields in golf through three rounds in the lead. He showed composure under circumstances that would have undone many players in his position.

The collapse on the front nine Sunday morning does not erase that accomplishment. It does, however, stand as the defining sequence of a final round that he will revisit. Golf at the major championship level is merciless — a double bogey in Round 4, when you hold the lead, rarely gets recovered from. Smalley couldn't recover from this one.

He leaves Aronimink with a T2 finish that is the best major result of his career. It is also the kind of result — so close, so visible, so tied to a specific mistake — that will motivate him for years.

Rahm's Patience Rewarded

Jon Rahm, sharing second place at -6, played the more consistent final round. The Spaniard has become a constant presence near the top of major championship leaderboards, and Aronimink continued that pattern. He shot a steady Sunday to put himself in position to win if the leaders faltered. Smalley faltered. But Rai did not, and Rahm finished two shots short of matching the improbable Englishman.

The T2 continues a stretch of near-misses for Rahm at the majors. He is one of the most consistent performers in the sport's biggest events, and that consistency almost certainly means more contention — and more chances at a second major title — in the months ahead.

Ludvig Åberg, Justin Thomas, and Matti Schmid finished tied for fourth at -5, forming a group that had every opportunity on Sunday and couldn't close the gap on Rai.

What's Next

For Smalley, the question is what he does with this experience. The best players in history have used near-misses at majors as fuel — Mickelson famously used his 2004 disappointment to win the Masters in 2004, and McIlroy channeled his 2011 Masters collapse into a dominant US Open win weeks later. Smalley has demonstrated he belongs at the top of major leaderboards. The first win — on the regular Tour or in the majors — now seems closer than it did a week ago. The US Open is the next major on the calendar.
