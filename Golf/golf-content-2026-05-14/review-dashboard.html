Aronimink Golf Club — The Course That Will Separate the Field at the 2026 PGA Championship
NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post Not all major championship courses are built the same. Aronimink Golf Club is a different kind of test. The Donald Ross design from 1926-1928 — located in the western suburbs of Philadelphia, just 13 miles from where America was founded — last hosted the PGA Championship in 1962. Gary Player won it. This year, Rory McIlroy, Scottie Scheffler, Cameron Young, and 151 other players will find out what made Player's achievement special. Here's what the course demands: **The rough** — There is no intermediate cut. You are in the fairway (cut to 3/8 of an inch) or you are in 3.5-inch fescue and bluegrass that can swallow a golf ball and a scorecard together. This is not a course that allows for "bomb-and-gouge" golf. Accuracy off the tee is essential. **The greens** — They average 8,100 square feet, roughly a third larger than a typical Donald Ross green. They slope boldly and punish shots hit to the wrong quadrant. Position, not just proximity, defines success on these surfaces. **The bunkers** — 75 of them, placed strategically by Ross to collect wayward approaches and demand precise sand play. This championship also carries special historical significance: it coincides with America's 250th anniversary, taking place just miles from Philadelphia, where the nation's founding documents were signed in 1776. Aronimink is ready. Who do you think wins this week? ### Image Post Caption No intermediate rough. Greens averaging 8,100 sq ft. 75 bunkers. Aronimink will be unforgiving. #PGAChampionship

McIlroy's Grand Slam Bid in Historical Context — What Winning Back-to-Back Majors Takes
NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post What Rory McIlroy is trying to do this week has barely been done before. He won the 2026 Masters. He now arrives at the PGA Championship — the second major of the year — as the defending major champion. If he wins this week, he will become the first player since Jordan Spieth in 2015 to claim the first two major championships of the calendar year. That's a rare enough achievement. But the larger narrative goes even further. A win at Aronimink would give McIlroy seven career major titles, moving him past Arnold Palmer on the all-time list. The top of that leaderboard — Jack Nicklaus (18), Tiger Woods (15), Walter Hagen (11), Ben Hogan (9), Gary Player (9) — would become a more distant, but more imaginable, conversation. Then there is the Grand Slam: winning all four major championships in a single calendar year. No player has ever done it in the calendar-year sense. Tiger Woods won four consecutive majors across 2000-2001 — called the "Tiger Slam" — but never within a single calendar year. Rory would need to win here, then the US Open at Shinnecock Hills, and then the Open Championship at Royal St. Andrews. No player in history has cleared that bar. For now, the focus is Aronimink and Round 1. The rest of the story gets written over four days.
