Scottie Scheffler walked onto the first tee at Augusta National on Thursday carrying more than just a golf bag. His son Remy, born on March 27, is at Augusta this week — 13 days old, watching his father attempt to win a third Masters title before he is old enough to understand what a Green Jacket means.

Scheffler's wife Meredith, described by the World No. 1 as "a trooper," is also at Augusta for the week. So is the couple's older son, Bennett. It is the kind of family picture that exists at the intersection of professional achievement and personal life — a reminder that behind the +500 betting line and the strokes-gained statistics, there is a 29-year-old man who just became a father of two weeks ago and is now trying to win one of the most prestigious tournaments in sports.

What a Third Jacket Would Mean

Scheffler won his first Masters in 2022, running away from the field in one of the more dominant Augusta performances of recent memory. He added a second in 2024 in similarly convincing fashion. He did not win in 2023 (Jon Rahm did) or 2025 (Rory McIlroy did), but two titles in three years put him on a pace that demands historical comparison.

Jack Nicklaus won six Masters titles — 1963, 1965, 1966, 1972, 1975, and 1986. Tiger Woods won five — 1997, 2001, 2002, 2005, and 2019. Arnold Palmer won four. A third title for Scheffler before the age of 30 would place him firmly on the trajectory of those legends, even if the raw number still trails them considerably.

More immediately: three Masters titles in five years (2022, 2024, 2026 if he wins) would be a pace of sustained Augusta dominance that only Nicklaus and Woods have matched in the modern era. That is the company Scheffler is working toward.

The Form Question

Heading into the 2026 Masters, Scheffler has had three consecutive starts outside the top 10 — an unusual run of inconsistency for a player whose default setting is elite. It is a small sample size in the context of a career marked by consistency at the highest level, but it is notable enough that even his most ardent supporters are watching his Round 1 performance Thursday with some attention.

The +500 Masters favorite designation reflects the market's broader confidence in Scheffler's Augusta pedigree. His game — driving accuracy, iron precision, methodical course management — is well-suited to Augusta National's demands regardless of form fluctuations in early-season stroke play events.

He teed off at 1:44 PM ET on Thursday with Robert MacIntyre and Gary Woodland. The afternoon wave brought Augusta's biggest remaining names to the tee sheet, and Scheffler's round set a tone for how the tournament's favorite would handle the 2026 conditions.

Remy Scheffler will not remember this week. His father may remember it forever.
