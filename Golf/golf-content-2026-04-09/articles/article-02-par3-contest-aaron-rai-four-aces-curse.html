England's Aaron Rai won the 2026 Par 3 Contest at Augusta National on Wednesday, April 8, finishing 6-under par after closing with four consecutive birdies on the iconic 9-hole course. It was a clean, dominant performance — and it came on one of the most eventful Par 3 Contest afternoons in recent memory.

Four players made holes-in-one during Wednesday's event: Tommy Fleetwood, Justin Rose, Keegan Bradley, and Wyndham Clark. Four aces in a single Par 3 Contest is a remarkably high total and produced the kind of viral moments that make this particular Augusta tradition must-watch television every April.

Family Caddies, Celebrity Appearances, and Frankie Fleetwood

The Par 3 Contest has always been as much about the personalities off the course as the shots on it. Wednesday continued that tradition in memorable fashion.

Kevin Hart appeared as a celebrity caddie during the event, drawing large crowds at every hole and generating the expected chaos of a comedian navigating Augusta's manicured lawns with a bag on his shoulder. NFL Hall of Famer Jason Kelce looped for Akshay Bhatia — a pairing that brought the worlds of professional football and golf together in the most Augusta-appropriate way possible.

Among the family caddie moments, none drew more attention than Tommy Fleetwood's son Frankie, who attempted to drive the No. 9 green. The moment — a child sizing up an Augusta Par 3 with the confidence of a tour player — became an immediate fan favorite and will be remembered long after Round 4 scores are posted.

The Curse: 66 Years and Counting

Aaron Rai now carries a very specific burden heading into Round 1 of the 2026 Masters.

In 66 years of Par 3 Contests at Augusta National, no winner has ever gone on to win the Masters in the same week. Not one. The streak dates to the very first Par 3 Contest in 1960 and has survived the eras of Palmer, Nicklaus, Watson, Norman, Woods, and every other dominant player to walk Augusta's grounds. Sixty-six consecutive winners. Sixty-six Masters outcomes that produced a different champion.

Rai is now the 66th man to carry that weight into tournament week. He is a solid professional on the European Tour and DP World Tour — capable of competing at Augusta — but he is not among the betting favorites to break the streak. Which is entirely the point. The streak's persistence is rooted not in any mystical force but in the simple statistical reality that winning a Par 3 contest and winning a 72-hole major are two very different athletic achievements.

The curse is never going away. It just needs a new name attached to it for another year.

What the Par 3 Contest Actually Is

For the uninitiated: the Masters Par 3 Contest is an unofficial, pre-tournament event played on Augusta National's 9-hole Par 3 course every Wednesday. Players invite family members as caddies, the atmosphere is relaxed relative to the week's competitive stakes, and children roaming Augusta's fairways in caddie bibs are a reliable annual highlight.

No prize money is awarded. The result has no bearing on the Masters itself. And yet the Par 3 Contest routinely generates more social media engagement than several regular PGA Tour events — because it shows the human side of players who spend most of the year operating under enormous competitive pressure.

Aaron Rai shot 6-under, closed with four birdies, and won the whole thing. His reward is a title no one has ever successfully parlayed into a Green Jacket. Round 1 tees off Thursday. The real test has begun.
