LIV Golf's Louisiana Event Is Cancelled. The League's Future May Be Next.

By Ryan Calloway | Golf Fanrecap | June 26, 2026

The LIV Golf Louisiana event will not be played. The cancellation is the most visible symptom of a crisis that has been building for months and now threatens the existence of a league that promised to transform professional golf.

Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund has confirmed it will end its funding of LIV Golf after the 2026 season. CEO Sean O'Neil is publicly seeking between $250 million and $350 million in outside investment to keep the league operational. He has acknowledged that he cannot guarantee the four remaining 2026 events will be held. A lending facility is in place as bridge financing, but it is not a solution to the structural problem.

Four years after its founding, LIV Golf is fighting for survival.

The Numbers Behind the Crisis

PIF's investment in LIV Golf since the league's 2022 launch has exceeded $5 billion by multiple reports. The return on that investment, measured in the traditional metrics of professional sports — television deals, fan engagement, sustainable revenue — has not materialized at the scale the original projections suggested it would.

In the most recent funding cycle, LIV Golf needed to receive $600 million. It received approximately $200 million. The gap — $400 million — reflects the degree to which the league's financial model depends on ongoing Saudi backing that PIF has now elected to withdraw.

The new independent board installed to govern LIV Golf — with Gene Davis and Jon Zinman replacing the previous leadership structure and effectively ending Greg Norman's era of influence — has the authority to chart a new course. Whether there is a course to chart depends on whether O'Neil can close the investment gap in the next 60 days.

The Failed Merger and Its Aftermath

The most plausible off-ramp for LIV Golf's structural problems was always a merger or integration agreement with the PGA Tour. Talks between the two organizations reached significant levels in 2023 and continued into 2024 before collapsing without a deal. The framework for integration — how LIV players would be ranked, how events would coexist, how prize money would be structured — proved too complex to resolve under the conditions both sides required.

The collapse of those talks left LIV Golf without the legitimacy that an affiliation with the PGA Tour would have provided. Without that legitimacy, major equipment deals and broadcast partners approached the league cautiously. The fans who follow professional golf closely continued to follow the PGA Tour and the DP World Tour rather than shifting allegiance to a competing circuit.

LIV's model required changing those habits at scale. It did not change them at scale. The result is where the league finds itself today.

The Players Caught in the Middle

The players who accepted LIV Golf contracts — in many cases, guaranteed deals worth hundreds of millions of dollars — made decisions based on the assumption that the league would be a going concern for the foreseeable future. Some of those players, Bryson DeChambeau and Joaquin Niemann among the most prominent, hold LIV-only status that complicates their ability to compete on the PGA Tour even as LIV's future becomes uncertain.

The restoration of paths back to traditional golf would require negotiation between the tours and individual players, a process that moves slowly even under favorable conditions. If LIV Golf ceases operations before those paths are established, the players caught in the middle face a genuinely difficult professional situation.

What's Next

O'Neil's $250-350 million fundraising effort is the immediate variable. The pool of potential investors willing to commit that level of capital to a professional golf league with LIV's current profile is limited. Sovereign wealth funds in the Middle East, private equity groups with sports portfolios, and media companies interested in live sports rights are the most plausible candidates. None have publicly stepped forward.

The four remaining 2026 LIV events — their locations and dates already in question after the Louisiana cancellation — depend entirely on whether bridge financing holds and whether new capital arrives in time. O'Neil has been transparent about the uncertainty. That transparency is unusual and may be the clearest signal yet about how precarious the situation has become.

LIV Golf was founded on a promise to change professional golf. The PGA Tour adapted, raised purses, and competed harder for players and fans. LIV Golf is now the organization fighting to survive while the tour it hoped to challenge goes on as if the disruption never fully arrived.

Whether LIV reaches 2027 will be known within weeks.
