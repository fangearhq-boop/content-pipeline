Ina Yoon Tied the Record. Korda Is Seven Back. The KPMG Is Just Getting Started.

By Jake Torres | Golf Fanrecap | June 26, 2026

Ina Yoon fired a 63 at Hazeltine National on Thursday. Nine under par. A score that tied the course record for the KPMG Women's PGA Championship. She leads the tournament at -9, two strokes ahead of Karis Davidson and seven ahead of Nelly Korda, who is somewhere between a defending champion-class contender and a history-chasing phenomenon still finding her footing at this venue.

Round 2 is the tournament's first real test. Can Yoon protect a lead that few expected her to build? Can Korda begin the comeback that would make 2026 the greatest single season in women's major golf history?

Who Is Ina Yoon?

Ina Yoon's 63 arrived without significant advance warning. She is a South Korean player ranked inside the LPGA Tour's top 40 — a legitimate professional with a solid record, not an unknown, but also not a household name entering the week. Players in her position occasionally produce magical rounds at major championships. What they do with those rounds over the following three days is the question the sport has learned to ask carefully.

Her score was built on fairways hit and irons that found greens in the right positions. Hazeltine's length — over 6,800 yards — demands distance and accuracy off the tee. Yoon managed both, and she made putts when she needed to. It was a complete round, not a fluke built on holed chips and perfect lie bounces.

She enters Round 2 as the leader of a major championship. That is a different experience from everything that preceded Thursday. How she processes that experience will be visible in her scorecard by Friday afternoon.

Davidson's Quiet Bid

Karis Davidson sits at -7, two behind Yoon and well-positioned to contend through the weekend. Davidson is a measured, intelligent player who tends to perform consistently rather than spectacularly. She has been knocking on the door of major championship contention for several seasons, and this leaderboard position is the most prominent she has occupied at a major.

Davidson's game at Hazeltine has been precise — she plays within herself, avoids the mistakes that cost strokes at long, demanding courses, and manufactures pars when birdies are not available. At -7, she may be the most dangerous player in the field: close enough to lead, experienced enough to manage her game, and flying under the radar of the Korda-Yoon narrative that will define most of the coverage.

The Korda Math

Seven shots over 54 holes is a hill. It is not a wall. The KPMG Women's PGA Championship has produced leaderboard movement in the middle rounds before — Hazeltine's length can expose players whose iron play regresses from a first-round peak, and conditions changes can shift scores dramatically.

For Korda to win, she needs to gain roughly 2.5 strokes per round on the leader over three days. That is an aggressive pace, but it is not outside her documented capabilities at major venues. She did something similar in her US Women's Open run earlier this year. The difference is she began that tournament closer to the pace, not seven strokes back.

The scenario that benefits Korda most is a Yoon Round 2 that hovers near par — a natural regression from an extraordinary opening round — while Korda posts something in the 64-66 range. If that happens, the gap enters the weekend in a range that keeps the history narrative alive and forces the leaderboard to reckon with her presence.

Hazeltine's Role in What Comes Next

Hazeltine National has a long history of producing drama at major championships. The course favors players with the length to attack par-fives and the precision to manage the iron tests on the longer par-fours. It is not a course that easily surrenders low scores — Yoon's 63 was exceptional specifically because the layout makes that number genuinely difficult to achieve.

The greens at Hazeltine become more difficult as the week progresses. Pin placements tuck into corners that were accessible earlier in the week but become severe by the weekend. A player who shot 63 in Round 1 will not necessarily shoot 63 in Round 4 — the course adjusts, and the pressure adjusts alongside it.

What's Next

Round 2 at Hazeltine will establish whether this tournament becomes Yoon's coronation, Korda's comeback, or Davidson's quiet conquest. By Friday evening, the gap will have either grown too wide for history or closed into something that makes the KPMG weekend genuinely electric.

Korda has already secured her place in the 2026 story. Two majors, both won with conviction. If she adds a third — if she finds 63 holes of golf that rival what she produced at the Chevron and the US Women's Open — the 2026 KPMG becomes the tournament that completed one of the most extraordinary seasons in the history of women's professional golf.

That outcome requires Yoon to yield and Korda to charge. Three rounds remain to find out which player sets the terms.
