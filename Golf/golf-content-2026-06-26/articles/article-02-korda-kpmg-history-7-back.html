Korda Is Seven Back. The KPMG Has Three Rounds Left. History Is Still Alive.

By Jake Torres | Golf Fanrecap | June 26, 2026

Nelly Korda walked off Hazeltine National on Thursday at -2, seven strokes behind Ina Yoon's record-tying 63. She double-bogeyed the 16th hole when her ball found the pond. She has won two majors already in 2026. She needs one more.

The KPMG Women's PGA Championship is three rounds away from a Sunday finish. The gap Korda faces is real. The history she is chasing is real. And this is Nelly Korda in 2026, which means neither fact alone tells you how this story ends.

What She Has Already Done

The 2026 season has already produced two extraordinary chapters in Korda's career. She won the Chevron Championship in the spring, her first title at Mission Hills. Then she came to the US Women's Open and delivered a performance that removed all doubt — wire-to-wire, dominant, the kind of golf that wins by a margin that makes the tournament feel settled before Sunday.

Two majors in a calendar year is an achievement that any player would frame as the pinnacle of a career. For Korda, the conversation has moved to three.

The record she is chasing belongs to Inbee Park. In 2013, Park won the Kraft Nabisco Championship — now the Chevron — the KPMG Women's PGA, and the US Women's Open in sequence. Three consecutive major championships in a single calendar year. No player has done it since. No player has come close.

Until now.

The Hole 16 Moment

Korda's Round 1 at Hazeltine was moving along within striking distance of the leaders until the 16th hole. Her ball found the pond. Double bogey. The scorecard shifted from something manageable to a seven-shot gap against a player who looked completely in control of her game.

In tournament golf, a single hole can change everything. Korda knows this better than most — she has been on both sides of critical moments in major championships. The double bogey on 16 is not a disqualifying event in a 72-hole tournament. It is, however, a number that makes the remaining math significantly harder.

She finishes the day at -2. Seven back. A gap that has been closed before at this tournament, at this course, but one that demands excellence over the next three rounds rather than just competence.

Why Hazeltine Suits Her

If any course gives Korda a path back into contention, Hazeltine National is a strong candidate. The layout stretches beyond 6,800 yards, making it one of the longest tests in women's major golf. Distance matters here. Korda is one of the longest drivers on the LPGA Tour, and her iron play from mid-range distances is among the best in the world.

The course rewards power paired with precision — exactly Korda's profile. She posted a T-3 at the 2019 KPMG, demonstrating she has the game for Hazeltine. The conditions in Round 2 will matter, but the raw fit between Korda's skill set and this layout is real.

The Yoon Factor

Ina Yoon is the player standing between Korda and history. Her 63 in Round 1 was not a round anyone predicted — Yoon is a talented player, but a 9-under opening round at a major on a 6,800-yard course is not something that appears on most forecasting sheets.

Yoon leads by two over Karis Davidson entering Round 2. The question of whether her round represents who she is as a golfer or who she was on one exceptional Thursday will be answered over the next 54 holes. Leaders who have built their advantage on one extraordinary round sometimes tighten when the pressure builds. Sometimes they hold every bit of it.

Korda's path to the KPMG title almost certainly requires Yoon to give back at least some of her lead. Whether she does is the central variable in the tournament.

What's Next

Korda needs a Round 2 that changes the conversation. A 65 or better would cut the gap in half, put her inside three shots, and shift the psychological weight of the leaderboard. Anything near or above par sends a different message entirely.

The 2026 season has already ensured Korda's place among the best players of her generation. Two majors by late June is a career-defining year for virtually anyone who has ever played this game.

But at Hazeltine National, with three rounds remaining and history seven shots away, the question is not what Korda has already done. It is what she does next.
