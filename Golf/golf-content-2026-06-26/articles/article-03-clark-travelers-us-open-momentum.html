Clark Survived a Triple Bogey. The US Open Champion Is Still in the Travelers Hunt.

By Marcus Cole | Golf Fanrecap | June 26, 2026

Wyndham Clark won the 2026 US Open Championship three weeks ago at Shinnecock Hills, playing four rounds of wire-to-wire golf at -4 that was clean, controlled, and ultimately decisive. He arrived at TPC River Highlands for the Travelers Championship carrying momentum and expectations.

Round 1 gave him a test he did not anticipate. He made triple bogey on hole 12, a mistake that can define a round the wrong way, and he responded by finishing at -2. He is five back of leader Eric Cole. He is alive.

The US Open as Context

Clark's 2026 US Open victory at Shinnecock Hills was his second major title and his most convincing. He held the lead from the opening round, never surrendered it, and finished two shots clear of the field despite conditions that challenged every player in the draw. Shinnecock Hills is one of the most demanding tests in golf — narrow fairways, thick rough, and greens that punish anything less than precise approach play.

Winning there wire-to-wire signals something beyond hot putting or favorable weather. It signals a player in the kind of form that can carry across multiple events. The Travelers, contested just weeks later, is the immediate test of whether that form has staying power.

Clark has also won a previous major. Two major titles by age 31 puts him in a genuine conversation about the best players of his generation.

What Happened on Hole 12

Triple bogey in a tournament round is a number that demands explanation. On hole 12 at TPC River Highlands, Clark found trouble that cost him three shots against par. The specifics of what went wrong matter less than what happened next: he made no further significant errors and posted a -2 finish.

The ability to absorb a damaging hole and continue playing quality golf is a marker of mental resilience. Clark has demonstrated that resilience at the highest level — major championships do not reward players who fold under pressure. That he finished at -2 despite the triple bogey on 12 speaks to his competitive composure.

In a 72-hole tournament at TPC River Highlights, a course where birdies are available across multiple holes, five shots back is not an insurmountable position. It is a position that requires a strong Round 2.

The Back-to-Back Narrative

The question hovering over Clark's week is whether a major champion can immediately follow with a PGA Tour victory. It is not common. The physical and mental toll of major week preparation, the performance itself, and the travel that follows create conditions that make the next tournament feel like a recovery exercise for many players.

Clark is not most players. He has spent the past 18 months building his game toward peak consistency — not just winning occasionally, but performing at a high level across consecutive events. The Travelers represents an opportunity to demonstrate that the US Open form was not a peak but a baseline.

The field he faces is formidable. Scheffler at -6, one behind Cole. Bradley at -5 on his home course. Cole himself at -7, playing the best golf of his career. Clark at -2 has work to do.

What's Next

Round 2 is Clark's opportunity to reset the narrative from the triple bogey and position himself for the weekend. A 64 or better would bring him to -6, back inside the conversation. A round in the mid-60s, the type of round he posted at Shinnecock Hills regularly, would put him within two or three shots of whatever the R2 lead looks like.

The Travelers Championship has a $20 million purse and a field that represents the best of what the PGA Tour offers in late June. A Clark win here, following his US Open title, would make a statement about the kind of player he has become in 2026.

Triple bogeys happen. US Open champions have bad holes. What matters is the next shot, and the one after that. At -2 at TPC River Highlands, Wyndham Clark is still writing this week's story.
