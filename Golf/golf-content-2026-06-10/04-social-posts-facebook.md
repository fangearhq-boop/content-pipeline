### STORY 2: LIV Golf Survival Crisis

#### Long-Form Post — 10:30 AM ET

LIV Golf's future has never looked more precarious — and the league's own CEO is struggling to offer guarantees.

Speaking to CNBC on Tuesday, CEO Scott O'Neil addressed the existential uncertainty hanging over the breakaway circuit. The Saudi Public Investment Fund, which has funded LIV Golf since its founding and has invested more than $5 billion in the league, is withdrawing its financial backing at the conclusion of the 2026 season.

The league's response has been to launch a fundraising campaign seeking between $250 million and $350 million from outside investors. According to O'Neil, he has had five formal meetings with potential investors and had 18 more planned for the week of June 9. Whether those conversations produce results is the central question.

But the most alarming detail in O'Neil's CNBC interview was what he couldn't say: he could not guarantee that LIV's final four tournaments of the 2026 season will actually take place. The organization is taking PIF "at their word" that funding continues through the year — but the word of the organization that just said it's leaving is not a firm guarantee.

Meanwhile, Bloomberg has reported that LIV is actively laying the groundwork for a potential US Chapter 11 bankruptcy filing. The league has established a new independent board led by Gene Davis and Jon Zinman, and PIF chairman Yasir Al-Rumayyan — one of the founding figures of the circuit — has stepped down from his board position.

For the players who left the PGA Tour with financial guarantees and for the fans who invested in a new era of golf, this week's news represents a significant moment of reckoning.

### STORY 3: Scheffler's Grand Slam Date With Destiny

#### Long-Form Post — 1:00 PM ET

The US Open at Shinnecock Hills is eight days away. And the sport of golf is waiting to see if Scottie Scheffler can write one of its greatest chapters.

Scheffler has won two Masters titles. He won the PGA Championship and The Open Championship last year. He is, by most measures, the best golfer in the world. The only major left for him to complete the career Grand Slam — the honor of winning all four majors over the course of a career — is the US Open.

That tournament begins June 18 at Shinnecock Hills Golf Club in Southampton, New York. If Scheffler wins, he would become just the seventh player in history to complete the career Grand Slam. The final round is on June 21 — his 30th birthday.

Scheffler recently made a scouting trip to Shinnecock Hills and came away with an assessment that's worth listening to. The course's fairways, he noted, are exceptionally wide by US Open standards — averaging 48 yards across, the widest setup the tournament has seen in 50 to 75 years. That doesn't mean it will play easy. It means the challenge comes from somewhere else.

"The green complexes there are extremely difficult," Scheffler said, "and that's where the greatest challenge comes from."

For a player of Scheffler's precision, those complex greens and the ability to miss fairways and still have angles — that's a setup that plays to his strengths. Rory McIlroy, who won the 2026 Masters, will also be in the field looking to add a second US Open title.

**Is Scheffler going to complete the Grand Slam? Or does someone else steal Shinnecock?**

### STORY 4: JT Poston WD From Canadian Open

#### Long-Form Post — 3:30 PM ET

JT Poston withdrew from the RBC Canadian Open this week, and honestly — he earned the rest.

Last Sunday at the Memorial Tournament in Ohio, Poston played 33 holes in a single day because weather delays pushed the final round's start to the morning, leaving the field unable to finish before darkness. He completed his round and then played three playoff holes against Ryan Gerard, finally ending it on the second extra hole to claim victory.

The reward for that marathon: a $4 million winner's check, his fourth PGA Tour title, a spot in The Open Championship, and a US Open exemption. His world ranking jumped from 94th to 39th in a single week.

So when the Canadian Open field notice showed Poston as a withdrawal, it required exactly zero explanation. There are weeks in professional golf where you've simply done enough, and the week after playing 33 holes in a playoff battle that ends in a title is definitely one of them.

His absence does create a meaningful shift in the Canadian Open field. Poston had arrived at TPC Toronto on hot form with fresh momentum. Without him, the door opens a little wider for the favorites — and for the kind of surprise winner that the Canadian Open has a habit of producing.

Poston will be back soon enough. He has the US Open to prepare for.

### STORY 5: Dow LPGA Championship Preview

#### Long-Form Post — 8:30 PM ET

While the men head to Toronto for the RBC Canadian Open, the women take center stage in Michigan for one of professional golf's most unique events.

The Dow Championship returns to Midland, Michigan on Thursday for a June 11-14 tournament that brings together 72 two-player LPGA teams in a format unlike anything else on the women's tour calendar. The $3.3 million purse makes it one of the season's stronger prize pools, and the team competition creates a dynamic that pure stroke-play events simply cannot replicate.

The Dow Championship has carved out a distinct identity over the years because the team format demands a different kind of game. Communication, partnership, strategy, and the ability to trust your partner become as important as raw scoring ability. Pairings that complement each other — balancing risk-taking with steadiness — often outperform teams of two individual stars who don't quite mesh.

This week at Midland, the leaderboard will tell a story not just about which players are in form, but about which partnerships have the chemistry to perform under pressure. It is one of the most enjoyable viewing experiences in women's golf — a different kind of competition that rewards watching from the first hole.

Coverage of the Dow Championship is available on Golf Channel and the PGA Tour app throughout the week.
