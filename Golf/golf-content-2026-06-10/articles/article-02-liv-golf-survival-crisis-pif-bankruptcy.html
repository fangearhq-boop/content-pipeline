LIV Golf's Survival Crisis: CEO Can't Guarantee Final Four Events as Bankruptcy Plans Take Shape

By Ryan Calloway | June 10, 2026 | Golf Fanrecap

LIV Golf's CEO went on national television this week and said that he cannot guarantee his league's final four tournaments of the season will take place.

That sentence, delivered by Scott O'Neil in an interview with CNBC on Tuesday, represents the most transparent acknowledgment yet of how precarious LIV Golf's situation has become. The Saudi Public Investment Fund — the organization that created LIV Golf, funded it through its launch and first years, and invested more than $5 billion in a bid to reshape professional golf — is withdrawing its financial backing at the conclusion of the 2026 season.

What comes after that is, as of this week, genuinely unknown.

What O'Neil Said

O'Neil's position is one that requires holding two contradictory realities simultaneously. He told CNBC that LIV Golf must take PIF "at their word" that funding will continue through the remainder of the 2026 season. But he also said he cannot guarantee that the final four tournaments will take place.

That is not a reassuring combination of statements. Taking an organization "at their word" while being unable to guarantee the outcomes that word is supposed to produce suggests the actual financial picture is unclear even to LIV's leadership.

O'Neil did offer some detail on the fundraising effort underway. He said he had held five formal meetings with potential investors as of Tuesday and had 18 more planned for the current week. LIV Golf has engaged investment banking advisor Ducera Partners to help identify and pursue those investors. The target: $250 million to $350 million in outside funding to replace the departing PIF investment.

Bankruptcy Plans and Leadership Changes

Bloomberg has reported that LIV Golf is actively laying the groundwork for a potential US Chapter 11 bankruptcy filing. According to the report, the league is even considering relocating its headquarters to the United States specifically to access the Chapter 11 process, which provides structured protections for reorganizing debt.

At the same time, the organization has made significant governance changes. PIF chairman Yasir Al-Rumayyan — one of the founding architects of LIV Golf — has stepped down from his role on the league's board. A new independent board has been established, led by Gene Davis and Jon Zinman, tasked with finding the long-term financial partners that PIF's exit leaves the organization needing.

By the end of the 2026 season, PIF's total investment in LIV Golf is expected to exceed $6 billion. That figure represents one of the most extraordinary financial commitments in sports history. The decision to end it reflects a strategic pivot — or a judgment that the investment thesis no longer holds — that will reshape professional golf regardless of whether LIV survives or not.

What It Means for Players and Fans

The players who left the PGA Tour to join LIV Golf did so under guarantees of financial security that no traditional tour could match. Whether those guarantees hold through the end of the season — and what happens to player contracts if the organization enters bankruptcy — are open questions that will have real consequences for the sport's landscape.

For fans who followed LIV Golf, this week's news is a signal that the era of unlimited Saudi backing is ending, and what replaces it will depend on whether O'Neil's investor conversations produce results over the next several months.

What's Next

O'Neil has said he hopes to conclude the fundraising process this summer. Whether LIV Golf can secure the capital it needs to operate independently of PIF — and whether the remaining four 2026 tournaments take place — will be determined in the coming weeks and months.

Professional golf is watching closely. The PGA Tour, which has been in ongoing framework discussions with PIF about a broader merger or partnership, now faces a different counterpart than it did a year ago. Whatever form LIV Golf's future takes, the sport's landscape is about to change again.
