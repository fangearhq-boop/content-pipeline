The good news for Scheffler — and the drama for the rest of the field — is that the next major is right around the corner. He will take a week off before returning to defend his PGA Championship title at Aronimink Golf Club in Newtown Square, Pennsylvania, May 14-17. He has now surpassed $110 million in career PGA Tour earnings. He is world No. 1. He has wins at golf's biggest events. A fourth victory is coming. ### Long-Form Post Rory McIlroy's return to competitive golf this week comes with a certain kind of weight. On April 12, McIlroy claimed his second consecutive Masters title at Augusta National, joining Jack Nicklaus, Nick Faldo and Tiger Woods as the only men to win back-to-back green jackets. He now holds six major championships — tied with Lee Trevino, Faldo and Phil Mickelson for 12th on the all-time list. He has not competed since. His first start since that historic day is at Quail Hollow Club in Charlotte, North Carolina, for the Truist Championship (May 6-10). And if history is any guide, this is as comfortable a venue as he could have chosen for a return. McIlroy has won this event four times: in 2010, 2015, 2021 and 2024. He is seeking a record fifth victory at a single non-major PGA Tour venue. The field is strong. Cameron Young arrives after his wire-to-wire Cadillac Championship win, having ascended to World No. 3. Matt Fitzpatrick, who has two wins this season himself, is expected. Seven of the top-10 players in the world are in the field. But none of them arrive carrying what McIlroy carries: two green jackets and the momentum of back-to-back wins at the most storied major in golf. If there is a favorite at Quail Hollow this week, it is him — and it is by a considerable margin. ### Image Post Caption McIlroy back at Quail Hollow. Two straight Masters wins. Six majors. ### Long-Form Post Six starts. Six top-two finishes. Three wins and three runner-ups. That is Nelly Korda's record through the first six events of the 2026 LPGA Tour season — and it stands as one of the most dominant early-season stretches in the recent history of women's professional golf. Her three wins include the AIG Women's Open, the Chevron Championship (her third major title), and last weekend's Riviera Maya Open — a four-shot victory at 17-under in Mexico that was never seriously in doubt from the closing stretch of the final round. Her three runner-up finishes have come against equally elite competition. Hyo Joo Kim, who has had a remarkable season herself, denied Korda twice at the top of leaderboards. The losses in this context are not failures — they are what happens when two elite players compete at the same events. What makes Korda's consistency remarkable is not just the wins but the floor. She has not finished lower than second in any event she has entered in 2026. For a sport known for its volatility — where conditions, courses, and random breaks can derail even the best players — maintaining a top-two floor across six tournaments is a statistical rarity. At 22 of the 27 required Hall of Fame points, Korda is five away from LPGA Hall of Fame eligibility. At the pace she is accumulating points, that threshold may be reached before the year is out.
