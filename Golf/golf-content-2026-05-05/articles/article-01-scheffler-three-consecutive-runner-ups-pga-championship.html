Three Straight Runner-Ups: Scheffler's Remarkable Rut and Why the PGA Championship Awaits

By Marcus Cole | Golf Fanrecap | May 5, 2026

Scottie Scheffler has finished second in three consecutive starts — the first player since Sergio Garcia in 2014 to do so. He's not playing poorly. He's just running into great golf. And now his focus turns to defending the PGA Championship at Aronimink.

Consider, for a moment, what Scottie Scheffler's last three weeks in professional golf have produced. He finished runner-up at the Masters, losing to a Rory McIlroy who was winning back-to-back green jackets. He finished runner-up at the RBC Heritage, losing to Matt Fitzpatrick in a playoff. He finished runner-up at the Cadillac Championship, losing to Cameron Young by six shots in a wire-to-wire performance that was simply dominant.

Three runner-up finishes in three consecutive starts — the first player since Sergio Garcia in 2014 to accomplish that frustrating feat. In another context, back-to-back-to-back silver medals would define a career stretch. For Scheffler, they define a problem that is not entirely of his making.

The Wrong Kind of Historic

Scheffler is the world No. 1 golfer. He has surpassed $110 million in career PGA Tour earnings. He is the defending PGA Champion. None of that has protected him from the specific frustration of watching three different players each produce rounds that he simply could not match at the precise moments that mattered most.

McIlroy at Augusta was at a level that perhaps only McIlroy himself could reach. Fitzpatrick at Harbour Town was dialed in during a playoff hole when margins are their smallest. Young at Trump National Doral was the most dominant wire-to-wire performance of the PGA Tour season, leading all four rounds and finishing 19-under in what amounted to a masterclass in sustained ball-striking.

Scheffler was not absent from those contests. He was there, near the top, playing well enough to win in a normal week. This has not been a normal stretch of weeks in professional golf.

The Cadillac Chapter

At Doral, Scheffler's final-round frustration was visible. Reports from the course described exchanges with his caddie that suggested the grinding reality of another near-miss landing heavily. He played well — the kind of well that in most circumstances earns a trophy. Young was simply better across every round.

The six-stroke margin in the final standings is perhaps misleading — the gap between them was not six shots at any single point, but rather a function of Young's cumulative excellence over four days. Still, the result is the result.

Scheffler will take a week off before the PGA Championship. It is a deliberate decision, the kind of measured preparation that characterizes how he approaches his major championship schedule.

Aronimink Is Next

The PGA Championship at Aronimink Golf Club in Newtown Square, Pennsylvania, runs May 14-17. Scheffler is the defending champion, having won last year's edition. Aronimink — a Donald Ross design, hosting its first PGA Championship since 1962 — will provide a different test than Doral or Harbour Town: a classic parkland layout where control off the tee and precision approach play are rewarded above raw distance.

Those are attributes Scheffler possesses in abundance. If any venue can end this particular streak of near-misses, a course that plays to his strengths under major championship pressure is the logical candidate.

The three runner-up finishes will be forgotten immediately the moment Scheffler signs a winning scorecard. That moment, based on everything the rest of 2026 has suggested, will come. The question is whether it comes at Aronimink, where he would become just the fourth player since 1950 to defend a PGA Championship title.

What's Next

Scheffler will be absent from this week's Truist Championship at Quail Hollow. He will return to competition at the PGA Championship, Aronimink Golf Club, Pennsylvania, May 14-17. Three consecutive runner-up finishes create enormous motivation in an elite competitor who has won at every level of professional golf. Expect the world's top-ranked player to arrive in suburban Philadelphia with something to prove — and the game to back it up.
