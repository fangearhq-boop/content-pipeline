### STORY 1: Clark Wins 2026 US Open

**Long-Form Post:**
🏆 WYNDHAM CLARK IS A TWO-TIME US OPEN CHAMPION.

In one of the most dramatic finishes at Shinnecock Hills in years, Clark shot a 3-over 73 on Sunday — battling a gallery that mocked his tee shots, cheered his errant approaches, and celebrated every missed putt — and still found a way to win.

Final score: 4-under par. Winning margin: one stroke over Sam Burns.

Clark came into Sunday with a six-shot lead. By the time he reached the back nine, that cushion had all but evaporated. But Clark held firm when it mattered most, making the crucial putts down the stretch.

THE HISTORY:
Clark became only the ninth wire-to-wire US Open champion in history — leading after all four rounds. The last player to accomplish that was Martin Kaymer at Pinehurst No. 2 in 2014. Clark also won the US Open in 2023 at Los Angeles Country Club, making him a repeat champion at the US's most demanding major.

The Shinnecock Hills crowd — notoriously among the most difficult in American golf — did everything in their power to get inside Clark's head. They failed.

Wyndham Clark: two US Open trophies, zero regrets.

### STORY 2: Yamashita Wins Meijer LPGA Classic in Playoff

**Long-Form Post:**
DRAMA AT THE MEIJER LPGA CLASSIC — AND A HISTORIC WINNER.

What happened at Blythefield Country Club in Michigan on Sunday was the kind of finish that makes professional golf unforgettable.

Japan's Miyu Yamashita came out of nowhere in the final round, firing a stunning 64 — the low round of the day — and set the clubhouse target at 17-under par. All she could do was wait.

England's Lottie Woad, who had led for much of the week and was in the final group, came to the 18th hole needing a par to win. She had a putt from 50 feet. She three-putted. Bogey. Playoff.

On the first hole of sudden death — Yamashita won.

THE SIGNIFICANCE:
✨ First LPGA Tour win in the United States for Yamashita
🏆 First Japanese winner in Meijer LPGA Classic history
💔 Woad's heartbreak: the 22-year-old English rookie had the tournament in her hands

At just 24, Yamashita has shown the game to compete at the highest level. And Lottie Woad, despite the gut-wrenching loss, has put herself on the map as a future LPGA star. This won't be the last time we hear about either player.

The LPGA heads now to Hazeltine for the KPMG Women's PGA Championship beginning Thursday.

🏆 KORDA'S 2026 MAJOR RESUME:

### STORY 4: LIV Golf Running on Loans

**Long-Form Post:**
LIV GOLF'S SURVIVAL RACE: LOANS TO FINISH 2026, AND THEN THE REAL TEST BEGINS.

The financial picture for LIV Golf has clarified — and it's not comfortable.

According to multiple reports, LIV Golf has a lending facility in place with its primary backer, Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund, to keep operations running through the end of the 2026 season. The PIF will then end its financial support.

That means CEO Scott O'Neil has until the end of 2026 to find a new money source or LIV Golf as we know it likely ceases to exist.

Here's the restructured plan O'Neil is pitching to investors:

📋 THE "LIV 2.0" PITCH:
• Reduced schedule: ~10 events (down from the current format)
• Reduced prize funds: ~$5-10M per event (down from ~$30M)
• International focus
• Fundraising target: $250-350M
• Financial advisor: Ducera Partners hired

📊 THE NUMBERS:
LIV has received approximately $200M of a ~$600M PIF commitment for 2026. Annual losses in the range of $500-600M have been the reality since launch. The scale is unsustainable without either a massive new investor or a fundamentally different business model.

O'Neil has been on the road pitching institutional investors and private equity. The response has been described as "positive" — but positive meetings and signed checks are two very different things.

The future of LIV Golf — and the players whose contracts depend on it — may be decided in the next few months.

### STORY 5: Scheffler's Grand Slam Bid Falls Short

**Long-Form Post:**
THE GRAND SLAM THAT WASN'T — AND WHY SCHEFFLER'S STORY ISN'T OVER.

Scottie Scheffler entered Sunday's final round of the 2026 US Open at Shinnecock Hills six shots behind Wyndham Clark. He needed a historic charge — something close to what Clark himself had built earlier in the week — just to get in position.

It didn't happen. Clark held on to win at 4-under, Sam Burns was second at 3-under, and Scheffler's bid to become only the seventh player ever to complete the career Grand Slam in a single calendar year fell short.

THE WHAT-IF:
Scheffler entered this US Open holding the Masters, the PGA Championship, and The Open Championship. He has been the most consistent major champion in the world over the last 18 months. A US Open win would have been the final piece — completing a calendar Grand Slam that had not been achieved in the modern era of professional golf.

But Shinnecock Hills is unforgiving. A 6-shot deficit on Sunday in a US Open is essentially insurmountable — and Clark's resilience under pressure made it impossible.

THE BIGGER PICTURE:
What Scheffler has accomplished is already remarkable. World No. 1. Multiple Masters titles. PGA Champion. Open Champion. He's rewriting the record book one week at a time.

The calendar Grand Slam is gone for 2026. But Scheffler is 29 years old and in the prime of his career. Another opportunity will come.

Does the Grand Slam miss sting — or is Scheffler's record still extraordinary? 👇 #PGATour #USOpen #Scheffler
