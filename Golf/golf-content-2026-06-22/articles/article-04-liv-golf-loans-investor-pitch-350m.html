LIV Golf Running on Loans: CEO O'Neil Pitches $350M Investor Rescue as PIF Era Ends

By Jake Torres | Golf Fanrecap | June 22, 2026

LIV Golf is operating on borrowed money — literally — and the organization's future depends on a fundraising effort that must succeed before the 2026 season ends.

Multiple reports have confirmed that LIV Golf has a lending facility in place with its primary backer, Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund, to cover operations through the rest of the current season. When 2026 concludes, PIF ends its financial support. CEO Scott O'Neil is on the road pitching a restructured business model to outside investors in hopes of keeping the tour alive into 2027 and beyond.

The Numbers Are Stark

LIV Golf has been among the most expensive sporting ventures in history. Since its launch, the organization has absorbed annual operating losses estimated at $500-600 million. The PIF, which committed approximately $600 million for the 2026 season, has reportedly delivered around $200 million of that total. The remainder is being bridged by the current loan arrangement.

The core financial problem is fundamental: LIV's prize pools — approximately $30 million per event — and player contracts at that scale simply cannot be supported by current television revenue, ticket sales, and sponsorship income. The model requires either a drastic reduction in costs or a major new investor to underwrite the gap.

The "LIV 2.0" Pitch

O'Neil's investor roadshow is built around a restructured version of the league. According to reports from Golf Channel and Golf Digest, the proposed "LIV 2.0" model includes a reduced schedule of approximately 10 events — down from the current format — with prize funds cut to a range of $5-10 million per event, significantly lower than the $30 million that made LIV famous (and expensive).

The target fundraising range is $250-350 million. LIV has hired Ducera Partners as financial advisor, assembled a new board of directors, and reshuffled senior leadership roles to present a more investor-ready face to potential backers.

O'Neil has characterized the response in investor meetings as "positive." But in the fundraising world, positive meetings without signed term sheets are not a rescue plan — they are a starting point.

The Players' Perspective

For the hundreds of golfers under LIV contracts, the uncertainty is real. Many signed multi-year deals at significant guaranteed salaries that made LIV financially irresistible compared to the performance-based PGA Tour model. If LIV downsizes dramatically or collapses entirely, those players face questions about where they will play, whether their contracts will be honored, and what avenues exist for a return to the major tours.

The PGA Tour has made some accommodating noises over the years about potential pathways for LIV players, but the political and contractual complexities remain significant. A disorderly collapse of LIV would create a complicated situation for everyone involved.

What's Next

The remaining 2026 LIV Golf schedule continues under the PIF lending facility. O'Neil and his team continue their investor conversations. The critical question is whether a deal is signed before the current season ends — and whether any agreement can be reached on terms that would allow a credible 2027 schedule. The answer will be known before the year is out.
