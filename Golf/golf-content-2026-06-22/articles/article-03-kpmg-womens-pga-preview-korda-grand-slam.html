KPMG Women's PGA Preview: Nelly Korda Chases Grand Slam and Hall of Fame at Hazeltine

By Ryan Calloway | Golf Fanrecap | June 22, 2026

Three days from now, women's golf gathers at Hazeltine National Golf Club in Chaska, Minnesota, for one of the most anticipated major championships in recent LPGA history. The KPMG Women's PGA Championship runs June 25-28, and the story at the top of the leaderboard before a single shot is struck is already enormous.

Nelly Korda, the world No. 1 from the United States, arrives having won the first two major championships of the 2026 season. A victory at Hazeltine would put her in pursuit of the women's calendar Grand Slam — an achievement that has been completed only once in LPGA Tour history — and simultaneously push her to LPGA Hall of Fame eligibility.

The stakes could not be higher.

Korda's Grand Slam Context

The women's calendar Grand Slam requires winning all four LPGA major championships in the same calendar year. Korda has won the first two: the Women's Masters and the US Women's Open at Riviera Country Club. Two majors remain: the KPMG Women's PGA at Hazeltine, and the Chevron Championship later in the season.

The last time a women's calendar Grand Slam was completed was Babe Zaharias in 1950, more than 75 years ago. The feat is extraordinarily difficult even for the best players in the world — major championships, by definition, attract the deepest, most competitive fields in professional golf, and winning four in a year requires sustained excellence across wildly different course conditions and formats.

Korda, at 27, is playing the best golf of her career. Hazeltine is the next test.

The Hall of Fame Equation

Alongside the Grand Slam hunt is another milestone. Korda currently has 25 of the 27 points required for LPGA Hall of Fame induction. Winning a major championship earns two points. A KPMG title would push her to 27 — meeting the threshold at age 27.

LPGA Hall of Fame induction is among the most exclusive honors in professional sport. The points system ensures that only players of sustained excellence over a career — not just a single hot season — qualify. If Korda reaches 27 points this week, she will be eligible for induction as one of the youngest players in the modern era to achieve the mark.

The Competition

Korda will not have the course to herself. World No. 2 Atthaya Thitikul — known as Jeeno — is one of the most talented players on the LPGA Tour and is still chasing her first major championship. Thitikul has the game to win at Hazeltine, and she enters this week as Korda's primary challenger in most projections.

The field also includes 12 past KPMG champions, among them defending champion Minjee Lee. The depth of the Hazeltine field means Korda will need to play her best golf to claim the title, not simply show up as the frontrunner.

Hazeltine as a Challenge

Hazeltine National is known as one of the most demanding courses on the women's major calendar. Long, with significant rough and a course routing that punishes conservative play, Hazeltine demands both length and precision. Players who can shape the ball both ways and manage their misses effectively tend to thrive. Players who rely on one ball-flight pattern off the tee tend to struggle.

Course management will be as important as raw talent in determining who finishes with the low score on Sunday.

What's Next

Practice rounds begin Monday and Tuesday. The first competitive round is Thursday, June 25. By Sunday evening, we'll know whether Nelly Korda is two-thirds of the way to one of sports' rarest achievements, or whether the grand slam bid will need to wait for the Chevron. Either way, Hazeltine this week is unmissable.
