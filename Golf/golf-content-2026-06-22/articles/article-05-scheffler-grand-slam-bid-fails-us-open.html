Scheffler's Grand Slam Bid Falls Short — Clark Wins US Open as History Goes Unclaimed

By Marcus Cole | Golf Fanrecap | June 22, 2026

For a brief moment this week, it felt possible. Scottie Scheffler, the world No. 1, had already won the Masters and the PGA Championship in 2025 and The Open Championship earlier in 2026. The US Open at Shinnecock Hills was the one missing piece — the final step toward the most exclusive achievement in professional golf: the calendar Grand Slam.

Entering Sunday's final round six shots behind Wyndham Clark, the odds against Scheffler closing the gap were long. In the end, they proved too long. Clark held on at 4-under-par, Sam Burns finished second at 3-under, and the Grand Slam — the hold of all four major championship titles simultaneously — remained beyond reach.

What Scheffler Was Chasing

The career Grand Slam in golf requires winning the Masters, the PGA Championship, The Open Championship, and the US Open. Only six players in the history of the game have completed it: Gene Sarazen, Ben Hogan, Gary Player, Jack Nicklaus, Tiger Woods, and Rory McIlroy.

Scheffler already owned the first three. A US Open title at Shinnecock would have added his name to that list and done it in a single calendar year — something no player in the modern era has achieved. The calendar Grand Slam requires winning all four majors in the same year, a feat last accomplished in any form by Tiger Woods who won four consecutively spanning 2000 and 2001.

The combination of the existing collection and the calendar opportunity made this US Open week uniquely charged. Every round Scheffler played, every shot he hit, carried the weight of those six names ahead of him in the record books.

Six Shots Was Too Far

Golf is a game where six shots can sometimes be overcome. Final-round collapses — even significant ones — are part of the sport's history. But Wyndham Clark, who shot 73 on Sunday and still held on to win by one, didn't collapse in the way that would have opened a door for Scheffler. He bent without breaking.

A six-shot deficit in a US Open final round is also particularly difficult to overcome because the course setup is designed to protect leads, not surrender them. Shinnecock Hills is as unforgiving a major venue as exists in American golf. The rough, the greens, the firmness — all of it favors caution. Going for broke in those conditions is easier said than done.

The Legacy Is Already Remarkable

Missing the calendar Grand Slam does not diminish what Scheffler has built. He enters the second half of 2026 as the consensus world No. 1, the holder of multiple major titles, and one of the most dominant players of his generation. His major championship collection is already one of the most impressive in the modern era.

Scheffler is 29 years old. He has years, perhaps decades, of major championship contention ahead of him. The US Open title will come eventually — the only question is which Shinnecock, Pebble Beach, or LACC will host the moment when it does.

What's Next

The next opportunity for Scheffler is The Travelers Championship, and beyond that, major season continues later in the summer. The specific combination of the four titles in a calendar year is no longer possible in 2026. But the career Grand Slam remains live, and with his current trajectory, the only question is when — not whether.
