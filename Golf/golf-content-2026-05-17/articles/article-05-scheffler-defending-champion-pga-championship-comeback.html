Scheffler Seeks Back-to-Back PGA Titles with Early Tee Time and Five-Shot Mountain to Climb

By Jake Torres | Golf Fanrecap | May 17, 2026

Scottie Scheffler arrived at Aronimink Golf Club this week as the defending PGA Championship winner. He leaves Sunday needing to produce something extraordinary to stay that way.

The world's No. 1 player enters the final round at -1, five shots behind 54-hole leader Alex Smalley. After a third round his own caddie might generously describe as workmanlike — Scheffler himself called it "a bit of a ho-hum" showing — the defending champion faces a deficit that, on most courses and in most fields, would be considered prohibitive.

This is not most fields. Twenty-two players enter Sunday within four shots of the lead. The leaderboard is so compressed that a single great round could move almost anyone into championship position. Scheffler, with his record and his game, is among those players.

The Early Tee Time Strategy

Scheffler tees off at 12:05 PM ET, paired with LIV Golf's David Puig — well ahead of the leaders, who don't tee off until 2:05 and 2:35 PM. Early tee times in a major final round are double-edged. The player sets a target but then waits in the clubhouse, watching as the afternoon field tries to beat them.

For Scheffler, the opportunity is clear: go deep — 64, 65, or better — post a number at 9 or 10 under, and let the pressure of that target fall on McIlroy, Smalley, Schauffele, and the rest of the late wave. Major championships have been won this way by players who seemed out of contention at the end of 54 holes. Scheffler has the game to do it.

His putter, which Golf Channel reported "went cold" in the third round, will be the key variable. If the flat stick cooperates the way it did when Scheffler won here last year, the story of the day could change entirely by mid-afternoon.

Why Scheffler Remains Relevant

Five shots is not a small deficit. But the world No. 1 ranking is not assigned to players who only perform when the road is easy. Scheffler has been in difficult situations in major championships and produced remarkable golf. He defended a major title with the same steady excellence that won it. He knows Aronimink's demands as well as any player in the field.

The course, per Scheffler's own description, has not shown him at his best this week. He noted that the challenge here is "different," and has suggested he may not be playing his finest golf. And yet he arrives at Sunday one round from another Wanamaker Trophy. That is not the position of a golfer who has lost his way. It is the position of a golfer who has had a slightly off week and still has a chance.

What's Next

Scheffler tees off at 12:05 PM ET at Aronimink Golf Club. The defending champion's window to repeat is real, if narrow. Watch what number he posts — because everything that follows this afternoon will be measured against it. If he's at the top of the leaderboard by 3:00 PM, the final pairings will feel every shot of that pressure.
