LIV Golf's Saudi Backer Is Walking Away — What Happens After 2026?

By Ryan Calloway | Golf Fanrecap | June 30, 2026

Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund is done funding LIV Golf.

The sovereign wealth fund that has bankrolled the breakaway tour since its 2022 launch is ending its financial backing after the 2026 season. The decision closes a chapter in golf's most disruptive financial experiment — and opens a new set of questions about what LIV Golf becomes without the source of money that built it.

The Financial Picture

PIF has committed more than $5 billion to LIV Golf since the tour launched. Annual losses have been estimated at $500 to $600 million — numbers that reflect both the cost of player contracts and the scale of events the tour has staged across the globe.

For 2026, PIF has been operating as a lending facility rather than an outright funder. LIV has received approximately $200 million of the $600 million it was promised for this season. That gap — $400 million still outstanding — is part of the financial uncertainty surrounding the tour's current operations.

The New Orleans event, which was scheduled as part of LIV's 2026 calendar, has been postponed as the organization explores fall dates. It is the most visible consequence of the financial pressure so far.

The Leadership Position

CEO Scott O'Neil, who took over the organization earlier this cycle, has been consistent in his public stance: the 2026 season continues. The tour completed a format overhaul this year — moving from 54-hole events to 72-hole events and expanding the field to 57 players per event. A $40 million Team Championship in Michigan remains on the schedule.

LIV has a new board in place, including Gene Davis and Jon Zinman. Ducera Partners is serving as the tour's financial advisor as it pursues $250 to $350 million in outside investment to replace PIF backing. Jon Rahm, the tour's most prominent winner, has taken victories in Hong Kong and Mexico City in 2026.

There is no PGA Tour merger in sight. O'Neil and the PGA Tour's leadership have not engaged in formal discussions, and the golf landscape — which looked briefly like it might consolidate following the 2023 framework agreement — remains divided.

What LIV Golf Built — and What It Faces

LIV Golf attracted major champions, changed the economics of professional golf, and forced the PGA Tour to significantly increase its own prize money. It created a competing structure with team elements, international events, and a different broadcast model. Whether those accomplishments translate into a self-sustaining business without PIF is the open question.

Professional golf has absorbed expensive experiments before. Some survive by finding new economics. Some contract. Some disappear entirely. LIV Golf is trying to find investors willing to believe the tour has value beyond its Saudi origins.

What's Next

The 2026 LIV Golf season continues. Events are scheduled. Players are under contract. O'Neil's message is stability. But the $250 to $350 million investment round that the tour is seeking represents a fundamental test of whether LIV Golf can attract capital without PIF as a guarantor. The answer to that question will determine whether there is a 2027 LIV Golf season — and what it looks like.
