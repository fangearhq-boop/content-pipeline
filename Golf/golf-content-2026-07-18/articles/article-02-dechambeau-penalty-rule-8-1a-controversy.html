DeChambeau Shot 66. The R&A Took Two Strokes Back. Then He Said He Might Withdraw. Then He Showed Up.

By Ryan Calloway | Golf Fanrecap | July 18, 2026

Bryson DeChambeau had a brilliant Friday at Royal Birkdale. He shot 66 in the second round of the Open Championship and climbed toward -7, positioning himself near the top of the leaderboard in one of the year's most compelling major scenarios.

Then the R&A announced a two-stroke post-round penalty. And things got complicated.

The Ruling

R&A chief referee Grant Moir issued the penalty under Rule 8.1a — "Improving Conditions Affecting the Stroke." Specifically, DeChambeau was penalized for inadvertently improving the area of his intended swing on the 5th hole, where he was observed stomping down tall fescue grass near his ball before playing the shot.

The R&A's position was unambiguous. Intent, Moir stated explicitly, is irrelevant under Rule 8.1a. Whether a player means to improve their conditions or not, if the conditions are improved, the penalty applies. DeChambeau's round of 66 became a round of 68 in the official scoring — dropping him from -7 to -5 and reshaping his position on the leaderboard.

DeChambeau's Reaction

DeChambeau was furious. His camp made comments suggesting he was considering withdrawing from the championship rather than continuing. The golf world spent Friday evening debating Rule 8.1a, intent versus result, and whether the R&A's enforcement was appropriate.

Somewhere around midnight, DeChambeau confirmed he would play Round 3. He took the course Saturday at -5, five strokes behind leader Lucas Herbert. The Yahoo Sports live blog for Round 3 opened with the headline: "Can anyone fend off Bryson DeChambeau after his chaotic penalty?" — signaling that DeChambeau is competing hard through moving day despite the controversy.

A Debate That Won't End Quickly

The ruling puts Rule 8.1a back under the microscope. The rule is designed to protect the integrity of the playing conditions by preventing players from gaining advantages through changes to the lie, stance, or swing area — even inadvertent ones. Its strict application has been controversial in the past.

DeChambeau is one of the most analytically minded players in the sport and has historically engaged deeply with the rules of the game. That makes the situation particularly ironic. Whether the R&A's ruling will be debated for years or will stand as a straightforward application of a clear rule is a discussion that will continue long after the 154th Open Championship concludes.

What's Next

DeChambeau is in Round 3 at Royal Birkdale at -5, five back of leader Lucas Herbert. He needs a strong Saturday and a close-to-perfect Sunday to contend for the Claret Jug. The penalty cost him position, but it did not end his week.
