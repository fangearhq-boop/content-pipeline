### Long-Form Post Haeran Ryu stood on the 18th green at Hazeltine National on Sunday and became a major champion for the first time in her career. Ryu finished at -13, a two-shot victory over Ina Yoon, to win the KPMG Women's PGA Championship — the richest women's major in history with a record $13 million purse. Her winner's check: $1.95 million. The result was a story in two parts. In one sense, it was a breakthrough win for Ryu — a well-regarded player whose talent has long been visible but whose major breakthrough had not yet arrived. This was her moment, and she delivered with a composed final round that held off a competitive field throughout Sunday. In another sense, the story at Hazeltine was what did not happen. Nelly Korda entered the KPMG looking to win her third consecutive major — a historic and nearly unprecedented achievement in women's golf. She had already won the ANA Inspiration earlier this year and was the defending KPMG champion. A Sunday 73 ended that pursuit. Korda finished T8 at -6, unable to sustain the brilliance that had defined her recent months. Brooke Henderson and Olivia Weber finished at T3 at -10. The leaderboard reflected a genuinely contested championship decided by the player who best handled Sunday's pressure. Ryu is a major champion. ### Long-Form Post The 154th Open Championship is three weeks away — and the field for the oldest major in golf is almost complete. Royal Birkdale in Southport, England, hosts the Open Championship this year from July 16-19, returning to one of England's great links venues for the first time since 2017, when Jordan Spieth famously scrambled to one of the most dramatic wins in major championship history. Final Qualifying for the Open Championship takes place tomorrow, June 30 — the Last Chance Qualifier in which the final spots in the 156-player field are decided. For players who have not yet secured their place through world ranking, tour victories, or prior exemptions, tomorrow is the last opportunity. A few things worth knowing about this year's field and event: Scottie Scheffler is the defending champion, having won his first Open title last year in a performance that cemented his status as the world's best player. He arrives at Birkdale as the prohibitive favorite. Tiger Woods is not entered in the 2026 field. His continued battle with physical limitations means that the game's most marketable name will be absent from Royal Birkdale's links. The course itself is a classic links test — wind-dependent, bump-and-run friendly, punishing to those who fly the ball high and land it soft. LIV Golf's Uncertain Future — Saudi Funding Ending, Season Under Pressure
NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post LIV Golf is facing a moment of reckoning that could reshape professional golf more dramatically than any event since the league's founding in 2022. The Saudi Public Investment Fund — the sovereign wealth fund that has bankrolled LIV Golf from its inception — is reportedly ending its financial support after the conclusion of the 2026 season. The signal is not subtle: the New Orleans tournament has already been postponed, and the Greg Norman-era leadership structure that originally built the league has been dismantled and restructured. Scott O'Neil, who took over as CEO in late 2025, has publicly insisted that the 2026 season will continue as planned. The league made a genuine structural change this year, shifting from its distinctive 54-hole format to a 72-hole model that more closely mirrors the PGA Tour. The change was intended to improve broadcast appeal and competitive credibility. But the larger problem has not gone away: the PGA Tour merger that was supposed to unite the two circuits has never materialized. After years of reported framework agreements, negotiations, and announced deals that later unraveled, no actual merger exists. The players who departed the PGA Tour for LIV Golf — Rahm, DeChambeau, Koepka, and dozens of others — did so with the understanding that their futures would be secured by either ongoing Saudi funding or a merged entity that legitimized their choice. Neither has definitively arrived.
