LIV Golf's Uncertain Future: Saudi Funding Reportedly Ending After 2026

By Jake Torres | Golf Fanrecap

June 29, 2026

When LIV Golf launched in 2022 with the backing of Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund, it promised to reshape professional golf permanently. Four years later, the league faces an existential question: what does its future look like if the Saudi money stops?

Reports indicate that PIF is planning to end its financial support for LIV Golf after the conclusion of the 2026 season. The signal is concrete rather than theoretical: the New Orleans tournament has already been postponed, the leadership structure built under founding CEO Greg Norman has been dismantled and restructured, and the no-merger scenario that backers once hoped would never arrive has become the working reality of the sport.

The Merger That Never Happened

The framework agreement between the PGA Tour and LIV Golf announced in the summer of 2023 was supposed to resolve the sport's fundamental division. It did not. After years of negotiation, announced deals, reported concessions, and political complications on both sides, no actual merger exists. The two circuits still operate separately, with separate schedules, separate world ranking implications in some cases, and the same fundamental tension between them that existed when LIV first launched.

For the players who left the PGA Tour for LIV Golf — Jon Rahm, Bryson DeChambeau, Brooks Koepka, and many others who signed guaranteed money contracts — the premise was that their financial security was guaranteed and that the sport's structure would eventually resolve in their favor. The 2026 situation challenges that premise on its most basic level.

If PIF withdraws and no merger materializes, LIV Golf's players face a future where the league that guaranteed their financial security may not exist in its current form after this year — and their paths back to the PGA Tour, if they want them, are complicated by the ongoing distance between the two organizations.

What Changed in 2026

LIV Golf's 2026 season arrived with a genuine structural change: the shift from the distinctive 54-hole, no-cut format that defined the league's early identity to a 72-hole format that more closely mirrors conventional stroke-play tournament golf. The change was designed to improve broadcast appeal, attract more traditional sponsors, and strengthen the argument that LIV Golf was a credible competitive circuit rather than an exhibition series.

The New Orleans postponement was the most visible crack in the structure this year. Officially, the reasons given were logistical. The subtext — that the financial and organizational pressure on LIV Golf is producing scheduling difficulties that would not affect a fully funded operation — was not subtle.

Scott O'Neil, who became CEO in late 2025 after taking over from the Norman-era leadership, has been consistent in his public messaging: the 2026 season continues. Events are scheduled. Players are competing. The league is operational.

What Happens Next

The uncertainty extends in multiple directions. If PIF does end its funding commitment after 2026, LIV Golf faces a choice: find alternative investment, scale down dramatically, or cease operations. Each option produces dramatically different outcomes for the players whose contracts and careers are built around the league's existence.

The PGA Tour, for its part, has maintained that players who left for LIV Golf made a choice that carries consequences for their Tour membership. The details of how — and under what conditions — those players might return to the mainstream tour have been a recurring but unresolved topic in golf's ongoing governance conversations.

The most high-profile LIV Golf players — Rahm, DeChambeau, Koepka, Phil Mickelson — are established enough to find competitive platforms regardless of what happens to the league. For the broader LIV roster, particularly the younger or less globally prominent players whose PGA Tour status was affected by their LIV commitments, the stakes are considerably higher.

What's Next

The 2026 LIV Golf season is scheduled to continue through the calendar year. Whether it does — and what comes after — is the most significant governance story in professional golf right now. Watch for any official statement from PIF, any merger development, or any player movement that signals how the sport's leadership expects this to resolve.

Golf's fractured structure has defined the sport for four years. One way or another, 2026 may be the year that definitively answers what comes next.
