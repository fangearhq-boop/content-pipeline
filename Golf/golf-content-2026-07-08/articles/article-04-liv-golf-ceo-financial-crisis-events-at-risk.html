LIV Golf CEO Can't Guarantee the Final Four Events Will Happen — The $400M Shortfall Is Real

By Ryan Calloway | July 8, 2026

LIV Golf / Business

LIV Golf CEO Scott O'Neil said this week that he cannot guarantee the league's remaining four events on the 2026 schedule will be played. The UK event and Bedminster, New Jersey, are expected to proceed. But Indianapolis and Michigan are now officially in doubt — and the admission that even a partial schedule cannot be assured reflects a financial reality that LIV Golf's own filings have documented in stark terms.

The league is running on loans from Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund (PIF), the sovereign wealth fund that has funded LIV Golf since its 2022 launch. Debenture filings show a shortfall estimated at $400 million. New Orleans — another scheduled 2026 LIV event — was postponed earlier in the year. The pattern is consistent: the calendar is shrinking because the money is no longer covering the cost of the ambition.

The Financial Structure

LIV Golf has never operated on a conventional commercial model. From its first season, the league was funded directly by PIF — the entity that controls over $700 billion in assets on behalf of the Saudi Arabian government. The initial investment was justified, in public statements from Saudi officials, as part of a broader diversification strategy: using sports as a vehicle for international engagement and domestic entertainment development under the Vision 2030 framework.

The problem, documented now across multiple financial cycles, is that LIV Golf has not generated the commercial revenue that would justify the investment on financial terms alone. The league has no domestic broadcast deal of significant scale in the United States. It lacks a title sponsor at the league level. Individual events have attracted local sponsors, but the overall revenue picture falls well short of what a $6 billion cumulative investment would require to service, let alone recover.

PIF has signaled, through sources familiar with the league's internal planning, that direct financial support for LIV Golf will end after 2026. That does not mean the league shuts down on January 1, 2027 — but it does mean the financial model that has sustained it for four years will no longer be available in the same form.

Events at Risk

The four remaining events on the 2026 LIV Golf schedule are: the UK event, Bedminster (New Jersey), Indianapolis, and Michigan. O'Neil's public statement drew the line at the first two — those appear secure for now. Indianapolis and Michigan are identified as the events most vulnerable to postponement or cancellation.

New Orleans was already postponed before the midpoint of the season, marking the first time a full LIV event had been removed from an announced calendar without being rescheduled within the same year. The optics of a CEO publicly stating on the record that he cannot guarantee his own schedule are significant — it is the kind of admission that creates uncertainty for players, caddies, venue staff, and sponsors who have commitments tied to specific event dates.

Where the Players Stand

LIV Golf's player roster still includes several of the highest-paid athletes in the sport. The guaranteed contracts that drew players like Phil Mickelson, Dustin Johnson, Bryson DeChambeau, and others to LIV in 2022 and 2023 have not been publicly renegotiated, though the terms of those deals were always multi-year. The league's commitment to player payments through the end of existing contracts is a separate question from whether the events themselves run.

Some players have returned to the PGA Tour. Brooks Koepka and Patrick Reed — two of the highest-profile LIV signees — are both competing in the Genesis Scottish Open this week, having re-entered the mainstream tour ecosystem. The direction of player movement, which once ran heavily toward LIV, has shifted.

What's Next

The broader PGA Tour–LIV framework discussions, which generated extensive coverage in 2023 and 2024, remain unresolved. PIF's signaled withdrawal of direct support does not necessarily mean an end to the Saudi government's involvement in golf — it could precede a restructured deal, a merger mechanism, or a different investment vehicle. What it does mean, based on current evidence, is that the model that put LIV Golf on the calendar in 2022 is not the model that will carry it past 2026. Whether it survives in a different form, or whether the remaining four events of 2026 represent the final chapters of LIV Golf as currently constituted, is the question the sport will spend the rest of this year answering.
