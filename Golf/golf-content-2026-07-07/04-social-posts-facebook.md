### STORY 4: LIV Golf Financial Crisis Deepens

#### Long-Form Post — 3:30 PM ET

When LIV Golf launched in 2022 with seemingly unlimited Saudi sovereign wealth funding, the conventional wisdom was that money could simply buy its way into golf relevance. Four years later, the financial picture is dramatically more complicated.

Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund, the sovereign wealth fund that backs LIV Golf, reportedly withdrew significant funding for the 2026 season in April — providing only approximately one-third of its pledged annual commitment. CEO Scott O'Neil confirmed publicly that there is a "significant shortfall" but insisted the season would continue "as planned, uninterrupted and at full throttle." He declined, notably, to confirm the status of individual remaining tour stops when pressed.

The evidence that something is wrong is visible in the calendar. LIV Golf Dallas and Miami (Trump Doral) are not on the 2026 schedule. The New Orleans event was postponed to the fall. Reports indicate a $400 million funding gap. With four events remaining in 2026, the question isn't whether LIV Golf has a financial problem — it clearly does. The question is how severe, and whether the tour can stabilize before it causes permanent damage to the players who signed multi-year deals with it.

Those players made calculated bets when they left the PGA Tour. Some, like Jon Rahm and Bryson DeChambeau, were established major champions who could afford to take a financial risk for a guaranteed payday. Others are less established players for whom LIV represented a career-altering financial choice. If the tour contracts significantly or collapses entirely, their paths back to the PGA Tour or DP World Tour are complicated by eligibility rules that were never designed for this situation.

The PGA Tour-LIV merger talks, meanwhile, are effectively dead. Two White House meetings produced no agreement. Rory McIlroy has called a merger "irrational." The two tours are competing in a market that, it turns out, may not be big enough for both of them to thrive simultaneously.

### STORY 5: Amundi Evian Championship Preview

#### Long-Form Post — 6:00 PM ET

If you follow professional golf, this Thursday is twice as important as a normal Thursday. The Genesis Scottish Open tips off at Renaissance Club in Scotland. And 800 miles south in Évian-les-Bains, France, the Amundi Evian Championship begins at the Evian Resort Golf Club — one of the LPGA's five major championships.

The Evian Championship is unlike any other major on the golf calendar. The setting is extraordinary — a course perched above Lake Geneva, with the Alps visible on clear days and a village that turns into a celebration of the sport for four days each July. It's been a full LPGA major since 2013 and has produced some of the most dramatic finishes in women's golf.

Haeran Ryu enters the week as the sport's most recent major winner, having captured the Women's PGA Championship at Hazeltine National by two strokes over Ina Yoon. Major momentum matters in golf — players who have recently won majors often arrive at the next one with a different kind of confidence, a belief that the game they're playing can withstand the pressure that separates major champions from everyone else.

Alongside Ryu, the Evian field will include the full complement of world-class talent that the LPGA Tour has developed over the past decade. The course rewards precision over power, which opens the door to players who might not dominate on longer, more open courses.

The Evian Championship starts Thursday. This is golf's most beautiful major week, and this year the field has something to say. ⛳
