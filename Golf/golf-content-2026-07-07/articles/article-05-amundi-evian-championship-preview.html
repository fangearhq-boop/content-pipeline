Amundi Evian Championship Preview: LPGA's French Major Begins Thursday in the Alps

By Ryan Calloway | Golf Fanrecap | July 7, 2026

The golf world's attention tends to congregate around the men's majors in the summer — The Open Championship, the U.S. Open, the Masters. But Thursday, simultaneously with the Genesis Scottish Open across the continent, one of women's golf's five major championships gets underway in one of sport's most extraordinary settings.

The Amundi Evian Championship begins at the Evian Resort Golf Club in Évian-les-Bains, France — a hillside course perched above Lake Geneva with the Swiss Alps visible on clear days and a resort village that transforms into a celebration of the sport for four days in July. Since earning full major status on the LPGA Tour in 2013, the Evian Championship has produced some of the most memorable moments in women's golf and established itself as a genuinely unique event in the sport's global calendar.

The Form Entering Week

Haeran Ryu arrives at Évian-les-Bains as the most recent major champion in women's golf, having won the Women's PGA Championship at Hazeltine National by two strokes over Ina Yoon. Major momentum is a real phenomenon in golf — players who have recently stood on a winner's podium at a major carry something intangible into the next week that's difficult to replicate through ordinary competitive results. For Ryu, the challenge is channeling that confidence without allowing it to become complacency.

The Evian Championship course is distinctive in ways that reward a specific kind of player. The Evian Resort Golf Club is a precision course — not the longest test in women's golf, but one that demands consistent ball-striking, intelligent course management around a layout with elevation changes and greens that require precise approaches. Players with strong iron games and creative short games tend to find success here.

The Setting Makes It Special

What separates the Evian Championship from most other major championships is the environment it creates. The venue sits between the Chablais Alps and Lake Geneva, with the Dents du Midi massif visible in the distance on ideal conditions. The town of Évian-les-Bains, famous globally for its mineral water sourced from the surrounding mountains, becomes a tournament village during championship week — a European atmosphere unlike any other major in the sport.

For international players — many of whom grew up following the LPGA Tour from Asia and Europe — the Evian Championship represents something beyond a major score. It's a cultural event on a stunning European canvas, the kind of tournament that draws fans and global media coverage that extends well beyond the sport's core audience.

What's Next

The Evian Championship begins Thursday and concludes Sunday July 12. First-round scoring on a course that tends to yield low scores in ideal Alpine summer conditions will tell us who came prepared and who is still working through their game. Ryu enters as the story, but the Evian Championship has a history of producing first-time major champions — players who arrived under the radar and left with the title that defines a career. Watch closely for which names are at the top of the leaderboard after Thursday's play. In a venue this beautiful, with a major title this rare, the story that emerges could be one of golf's best of the year.
