Aberg Is Playing the Best Golf of His Career. His Final Rounds Say Something Different.

By Ryan Calloway | Golf Fanrecap | June 4, 2026

Ludvig Aberg is the most compelling player at the 2026 Memorial Tournament who is not Scottie Scheffler. Six top-10 finishes in his last nine PGA Tour starts. A T5 here at Muirfield Village in 2024. A form line that suggests a player who is closing in on his first significant win.

There is just one problem. In five of his last seven final rounds, Aberg has shot 70 or higher. He ranks 43rd on the PGA Tour in final-round scoring in 2026. For a player whose overall game makes him a pre-tournament contender at nearly every event he enters, the Sunday numbers reveal a gap between what he is capable of and what he has produced when it matters most.

The Memorial Tournament gives Aberg a specific opportunity to answer that question. It is also the tournament where the question is loudest.

Why Muirfield Village Suits Aberg

Muirfield Village rewards the qualities that Aberg has in abundance. Precision off the tee. Elite iron play. Distance control into small greens. The course punishes the kind of shot that Aberg rarely hits — the wild miss, the poorly committed approach, the rushed short game attempt. His game is built for the disciplined, methodical scoring that Nicklaus' design demands.

His 2024 Memorial result — a T5 — was not a fluke. He played four consistent rounds and finished in the top five at one of the most demanding courses on the calendar. He came back in 2025 and posted a T16 — still respectable but suggesting a regression. The 2026 edition arrives with Aberg playing better than he did in either previous appearance.

At +1500, the market is acknowledging his ability while pricing in the risk. The implied probability suggests that roughly one in every sixteen outcomes has Aberg hoisting the trophy Sunday evening. His course history, his recent form, and the structural match between his game and Muirfield Village all argue for him being better than that number suggests.

The Closing-Round Question

The closing-round data is specific enough to be meaningful rather than noise. Seventy or higher in five of his last seven Sundays means something about how Aberg processes the pressure of a final round with a leaderboard visible. It might be a mental pattern. It might be a fatigue issue. It might be a strategic problem — playing conservatively when the right approach is to attack, or attacking when the right approach is to protect.

Whatever the root cause, the symptom is consistent. Players who lead or contend through 54 holes at Muirfield Village, then post rounds of 70-73 on Sunday, do not win the Memorial. They finish in the middle of the top ten and leave Dublin knowing the course liked them without returning the full affection.

Aberg's challenge this week is not to get into contention through Saturday. He does that routinely. His challenge is to carry his Thursday-through-Saturday quality into the fourth round and refuse to let the closing pressure change how he plays golf.

What a Win Would Mean

Aberg is 24 years old in 2026. He is already in the early stages of a career that has exceeded expectations since he turned professional. A Memorial Tournament title would be a signature event victory — a PGA Tour event with history, tradition, and a field of elite players. It would answer the question his closing rounds have been raising all season and establish him as a player capable of winning when the stakes are highest.

The case for Aberg this week is straightforward: he is good enough to win here, the course fits his game, and he is playing as well as he ever has. The case against him is equally straightforward: his closing rounds have not matched his overall quality this season, and Muirfield Village's demanding Sunday conditions will expose any weakness in his fourth-round composure.

What's Next

Round 1 tees off today at Muirfield Village. Watch where Aberg sits after Thursday's round — his first-round scores have generally been consistent with his overall form this season. Round 2 and Saturday's third round will build the leaderboard that Sunday's final round resolves.

If Aberg is within three strokes of the lead with 18 holes to play, the closing-round question gets its most direct test of the season. The Memorial Tournament runs June 4-7. The answer to whether Aberg can break his Sunday pattern may come by Sunday evening at Muirfield Village.
