### STORY 1: Memorial Tournament Opens

#### Long-Form Post — 8:00 AM ET

Round 1 of the Memorial Tournament is underway at Muirfield Village Golf Club in Dublin, Ohio — and the most compelling storyline heading into this week is one that has not happened on the PGA Tour in 15 years.

Scottie Scheffler won the Memorial in 2024. He won it again in 2025. Now he tees off Thursday as the +310 favorite trying to become the first player since Steve Stricker at the 2011 John Deere Classic to win the same PGA Tour event three consecutive times.

Scheffler has dominated Jack Nicklaus' course. In addition to back-to-back wins, he also finished third in each of the two years prior — four straight top-three finishes at Muirfield Village. His iron play, course management, and putting on Nicklaus' bentgrass greens have made him the perfect fit for what the Memorial demands.

But the field is strong. Rory McIlroy is back, having detoured through Shinnecock Hills this week to preview the US Open course on his way to Ohio. McIlroy's best Memorial finish is T4 — and that is in 13 attempts. He has never solved Jack's Place. This week could finally be the one.

Ludvig Aberg enters at +1500 with momentum — six top-10 finishes in his last nine starts. Cameron Young, Xander Schauffele, Jordan Spieth, and Matt Fitzpatrick are also in a field that features 47 of the top 50 FedEx Cup point earners. Viktor Hovland (2023 champion) withdrew.

Muirfield Village is unforgiving. The rough is penal, the greens are small and undulating, and missing fairways turns pars into double-bogeys. Scheffler has navigated it better than anyone. But three in a row is a different kind of challenge entirely.

That changes this week, if Rahm has anything to say about it.

The change gives elite players more time to separate themselves from the field.

This is also the last LIV event before the US Open at Shinnecock Hills on June 18-21. For LIV players — who now have US Open qualifying pathways — this week at Valderrama serves as the final competitive preparation before golf's second major of the calendar year.

The No. 1 player in the world owns the PGA Championship (2025) and The Open Championship (2025). He has never won a US Open. The final round of this year's championship falls on his 30th birthday — a detail that feels scripted for a career that has already produced one improbable chapter after another.

This week, on his way to the Memorial Tournament in Dublin, Ohio, Scheffler made a detour to Shinnecock Hills in Southampton, New York, to walk the course and assess what the 2026 US Open will require. Rory McIlroy made the same detour. Both players wanted to see the course before the field of 156 arrives for championship rounds on June 18-21.

Shinnecock is hosting the US Open for the sixth time. The 126th US Open is expected to be one of the most demanding in recent memory — the USGA typically presents Shinnecock at its most difficult, using the natural contours of the links-style layout to punish even slightly missed shots.

J.J. Spaun is the defending champion, having won at Oakmont in 2025 with a dramatic 64-foot birdie putt on the 72nd hole. He returns to defend in a field that includes nearly every major winner of the last decade.

For Scheffler, the US Open represents the most tantalizing prize still on the shelf. For McIlroy, who has not won a major since back-to-back Masters victories, it represents an opportunity to end a different kind of drought.

Both of them are already thinking about June 21. Golf's Longest Day final qualifying is June 8 — which determines the final entries in the 156-player field.

### STORY 5: Aberg's Closing-Round Problem

#### Long-Form Post — 3:30 PM ET

Ludvig Aberg is playing some of the best golf of his career. Six top-10 finishes in his last nine PGA Tour starts. A T5 at Muirfield Village in 2024. Entering the Memorial as the second biggest talking point behind Scheffler himself.

But there is a persistent question mark attached to his name that this week at Jack's Place could either answer or amplify.

Five of Aberg's last seven final-round scores are 70 or higher. He ranks 43rd on the PGA Tour in final-round scoring in 2026. For a player who is consistently putting himself in contention through three rounds, the Sunday slide is a pattern that has cost him victories he seemed positioned to take.

At +1500, Aberg is a reasonable bet if — and it is a significant if — he can maintain his form when the pressure peaks on Sunday afternoon. Muirfield Village has historically rewarded complete players who make birdie on the par-fives, avoid bogeys on the tough par-fours, and manage their scoring on the final nine holes with discipline. Aberg has shown the ability to be that player through 54 holes. The question is what happens at holes 55 through 72.

The course suits his skill set. His iron play and distance control match what Nicklaus' design rewards. He has track record here. And he is in form.

The only thing standing between Aberg and a first major-level win is a Sunday round that matches his Thursday-through-Saturday play. The 2026 Memorial begins today. We will know the answer by Sunday evening.
