## STORY 1: McIlroy's "Liberated" 65 Headlines Scottish Open Tie

### Long-Form Post
There is a word Rory McIlroy has been using since he won the career Grand Slam at Augusta last year: liberated. On Thursday at the Renaissance Club in North Berwick, that word took the shape of a bogey-free 65.

McIlroy shares the Round 1 lead at the Genesis Scottish Open at -5 with four others — Patrick Cantlay, Tom Kim, Rasmus Hojgaard, and Bernd Wiesberger. He made an eagle on the par-5 10th, never made a bogey, and described it afterward as "a complete performance." He told Sky Sports he feels "very liberated" since completing the career Grand Slam, while being careful to add that he "can't get complacent."

The round could have been surpassed by Brooks Koepka, who was arguably hitting it better than anyone in the field. Koepka hit 14 greens in regulation and made 7 birdies — but missed 4 putts inside 7 feet and came within 15 inches of a hole-in-one on the par-3 9th. "It could have been a very, very good round," he said. It was still a 66, leaving him one back.

Scottie Scheffler, the defending Open champion, shot a 68 and sits 3 back. He admitted something "hurt" him about the round without specifying what. He's still in position, but not quite the form of a man who's won consecutive Claret Jugs.

And then there was Rahm. Jon Rahm came to the Scottish Open as LIV Golf's biggest active name — his first PGA Tour start outside majors since joining LIV in January 2024. He was expected to contend. He opened with a bogey on the par-5 first hole. Made 4 more bogeys in his first 11. Finished +3 (73) and T137 — beating only 12 players in the field.

R2 is underway now. McIlroy is playing golf with something that looks like freedom. There is one week until the Open Championship at Royal Birkdale.

## STORY 2: Evian R2 — Korda at +3, 11 Back, Needs Miracle

### Long-Form Post
Nelly Korda came to the Amundi Evian Championship needing a win. Her career Grand Slam bid — she's already won the Chevron Championship and the US Women's Open in 2026 — requires the Evian and the AIG Women's British Open. She is, to put it plainly, halfway there.

She is also 11 shots back after one round.

Korda opened Thursday with a 74 — double-bogeys and bogeys in the early holes, a round that never found its footing. She sits at +3, staring at Aki Iwai's dominant 63 at the top of the leaderboard. The Japanese player was bogey-free, made 8 birdies, and is in the middle of what may be the round of this major.

Below Iwai, the story gets interesting. Perrine Delacour of France shot a 65 and sits in second at -6. She's ranked 116th in the world. She made a birdie on the 1st, an eagle on the par-5 15th, and birdied the final two holes to send the French galleries home delighted. It was the best first-round score by a French player at Evian in tournament history.

The T3 group at -5 includes Charley Hull, Haeran Ryu, Jeongeun Im, Mao Saigo, and Maja Stark. Lottie Woad, the young English star, sits at -4 after an eagle putt from 20 feet on the par-5 9th.

Korda can still make up ground — she won back-to-back Evian titles and knows this course. But she needs Iwai to come back to the field, and she needs to play nearly flawless golf for three rounds starting now. R2 is underway in France.

## STORY 3: LIV Golf Files WARN Act Layoff Notices

### Long-Form Post
The clearest signal yet that LIV Golf's financial crisis is not a negotiation tactic came this week in the form of legal paperwork.

LIV Golf has filed WARN Act notices — the U.S. law that requires any company with more than 100 employees to give 60 days' advance notice of likely mass layoffs. The notices, filed approximately July 8-9, cover both U.S. and U.K. employees. They are not a definitive workforce reduction. They are, however, a legal acknowledgment that one is probably coming.

The financial context has been developing all week. The Saudi Public Investment Fund committed roughly $600 million to fund the remainder of LIV's 2026 season. It has delivered approximately $200 million — $66 million in May and $130 million in June. The $400 million gap has not been filled. Without it, the final two events of the 2026 season are at risk.

CEO Scott O'Neil has said he cannot guarantee those events will proceed. Two English companies — World Golf Group and Premier Golf League — are suing LIV for between $210 million and $630 million, claiming breach of confidence and unlawful means conspiracy. The league is seeking a new long-term investor (using Ducera Partners as an advisor) and targeting $250 million to $350 million.

LIV's captain of Legion XIII, Jon Rahm, provided the most striking comment of the week. After his disastrous +3 return to co-sanctioned golf at the Scottish Open, Rahm told media he "refuses to rule out investing his own money" to help save LIV Golf. Whether that comment reads as devotion or desperation depends on what the next 60 days look like.

## STORY 4: Royal Birkdale Preview — Rebuilt Course, 6 Days Out

### Long-Form Post
The 154th Open Championship begins in six days at Royal Birkdale — and some of what made the 2017 Birkdale so memorable will not be there when the field arrives.

Architects Tom Mackenzie and Mike Howard have been working on the course since Jordan Spieth's famous win nine years ago. The changes are substantial. The bunkering throughout the course has been naturalized — less formal, more integrated into the dunescape. More sand has been exposed between the dunes, making the rough terrain a more constant visual and strategic presence. On the par-4 5th, the blind tee shot that once defined the hole has been eliminated. And the course now has a new par-3 15th that plays at 241 yards.

The field chasing the Claret Jug is one of the more compelling in recent memory. Scottie Scheffler is the defending champion (he won at Royal Portrush in 2025) and would become the first player since Tiger Woods to win consecutive Open Championships. But Scheffler has not won a tournament since January 2026 and admitted something "hurt" him about his opening round at this week's Scottish Open.

Rory McIlroy, who completed the career Grand Slam at Augusta last year, is currently co-leading the Scottish Open and has described feeling "liberated" playing golf without a Grand Slam to chase. His links credentials are strong.

Jordan Spieth skipped the Scottish Open specifically to spend the week preparing at Royal Birkdale. The nostalgia factor is real — Spieth has spoken about the 2017 win as one of the defining moments of his career. Tommy Fleetwood, a Southport native, also skipped Scotland to prepare for his home Open.

Last Chance Qualifier is Monday at Birkdale — 12 players, one spot. Then six days of waiting. Then the Open.

## STORY 5: Rahm's Nightmare Return Analysis

### Long-Form Post
Jon Rahm had not started a PGA Tour event outside of a major in over 18 months. When he returned to co-sanctioned competition Thursday at the Genesis Scottish Open, the expectation — at least the narrative expectation — was that he would remind people what LIV Golf's biggest star looks like when he competes against the world's best.

He shot a 73. He beat 12 players. He sits T137.

The round did not go gradually wrong. It went immediately wrong. Rahm bogeyed the par-5 first hole — the type of hole a player of his caliber is expected to birdie or at worst par. He then made four more bogeys in his first 11 holes. By the time he found anything resembling his game, he was deep enough into red figures that two back-to-back birdies on 15 and 16 were cosmetic at best.

Before he teed off, Rahm described it as "just another event" and said he was "happy the DP World Tour let me have the opportunity." That language was modest and measured. The scorecard was harsh.

The irony is that Rahm's deal to return — paid fines to the DP World Tour, committed to a set number of events, earned "member in good standing" status at the Scottish Open as his fifth qualifying start — was hailed as a potential template for LIV players to come back and compete. The first data point is +3/73 beating 12 players.

Rory McIlroy, who shot a bogey-free 65, was gracious about Rahm's presence: "Great for the tournament." The scorecard was less gracious.

And after it all, Rahm told media he refuses to rule out investing his own money to help save LIV Golf, which filed WARN Act layoff notices this week. Whatever Thursday meant for Jon Rahm the golfer, Jon Rahm the LIV captain is not walking away.
