T137: Rahm's Return to the PGA Tour World Started With a Nightmare Round — and He Still Won't Walk Away From LIV

By Marcus Cole July 10, 2026

The Number That Tells It

T137. Beat 12 players. Those two statistics describe Jon Rahm's opening round at the Genesis Scottish Open on Thursday — his first co-sanctioned PGA Tour start outside of a major championship in over 18 months, and the round that was supposed to announce that LIV Golf's biggest name could still compete with the best players on the planet on any stage. Instead, +3 (73). T137. Beat 12 people.

The narrative attached to Rahm's return was significant. He paid his fines to the DP World Tour, committed to a set number of co-sanctioned events as part of a negotiated agreement, and played the Scottish Open as his fifth qualifying event under that arrangement, earning "member in good standing" status. The possibility existed — and was discussed — that Rahm could become the first LIV player to win a regular-season PGA Tour event. His game, at its best, is the equal of anyone in the world.

How the Round Unraveled

It did not go gradually wrong. It went immediately wrong. Rahm bogeyed the opening hole — a par-5, the type of hole that players of his caliber are expected to birdie or at worst par. He made four more bogeys in his first 11 holes. By the time he found anything resembling the golf he is capable of, back-to-back birdies on 15 and 16 were the only positives in a round that finished five over the par-5 opportunities and three over the course rating. The two birdies in the closing stretch were not salvage. They were punctuation on a round that had already decided its own story.

Rory McIlroy — who shot a bogey-free 65 and co-leads the tournament — was gracious in his response to Rahm's presence in the field. "Great for the tournament," McIlroy said. The result on the scorecard was less gracious. McIlroy and Rahm played on the same golf course on Thursday. The distance between -5 and +3 is the distance between a player who says he feels "liberated" and one who opened with a bogey on a par-5.

What He Said Before

In the days before his round, Rahm described the Scottish Open as "just another event." He told media he was "just happy that the DP World Tour let me have the opportunity" and called the Renaissance Club "one of my favourite weeks" in previous years. The language was measured and modest — a player trying to frame a historically significant return as ordinary, perhaps to relieve the weight of expectation. The problem with describing it as ordinary is that the scorecard measures it the same way regardless of how you frame it.

The cut line at the Scottish Open is projected at approximately -1. Rahm at +3 would need an extraordinary second round to make the weekend. The tournament that was supposed to demonstrate what a LIV player looks like in a regular PGA Tour environment is currently demonstrating something considerably less flattering.

Despite Everything, He Won't Walk Away

After his round on Thursday, Rahm was asked about LIV Golf's financial crisis — the WARN Act layoff notices, the $400 million PIF funding gap, the league's uncertain future. His response was reported by Golf Monthly. He told media he "refuses to rule out investing his own money" to help save LIV Golf. The statement is either remarkable loyalty or remarkable stubbornness depending on your perspective. The man who went T137 beating 12 players in his first regular PGA Tour start since leaving also told reporters that his financial commitment to the league that brought him there is not off the table.

That is the version of Jon Rahm's Scottish Open that matters beyond the scorecard. The round was a disaster. His position on his league's future was resolute. Whether LIV Golf is still running events when those 60 days of WARN Act notice expire will determine whether his loyalty turns out to be the sentiment that kept the league alive or the attachment that went down with it.

What's Next

Rahm tees off in Round 2 on Friday needing an extraordinary score to make the cut at the Genesis Scottish Open. His Ryder Cup eligibility — one of the primary reasons he pursued the DP World Tour agreement in the first place — depends on maintaining "member in good standing" status through 2026 and 2027. A missed cut is not a disqualifying outcome for that goal. But a missed cut in his first co-sanctioned return, in a week when the league he plays for is filing layoff notices, makes the gap between LIV Golf's ambitions and the reality of professional golf in 2026 about as visible as it has ever been.
