LIV Golf Files Layoff Notices as $400M Crisis Deepens — Rahm Won't Rule Out Personal Investment

By Ryan Calloway July 10, 2026

The Legal Signal

LIV Golf has filed WARN Act notices. The Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification Act requires any U.S. employer with more than 100 employees to provide 60 days' advance notice before implementing mass layoffs. LIV Golf notified both its U.S. and U.K. employees approximately July 8-9. The notices do not confirm that layoffs are happening — they confirm that the company believes they probably will. It is the clearest legal signal yet that the $400 million funding gap between what the Saudi Public Investment Fund promised and what it has delivered is not resolving itself.

The financial architecture has been visible for some time. PIF committed roughly $600 million to fund the remainder of LIV Golf's 2026 season. It has delivered approximately $200 million — $66 million in May and $130 million in June. The $400 million shortfall has not been filled, and without it, LIV Golf CEO Scott O'Neil has said he cannot guarantee the final two events of the 2026 season will proceed.

The Legal Exposure

The WARN Act filings are not the only legal pressure LIV Golf is managing. Two English companies — World Golf Group and Premier Golf League — are suing the organization, seeking damages of between $210 million and $630 million. The claims include breach of confidence and unlawful means conspiracy. The lawsuits compound a financial picture that was already stretched before the litigation entered.

LIV is simultaneously searching for a new long-term investment partner, using Ducera Partners as its financial advisor and targeting between $250 million and $350 million in outside capital. The business case it is presenting to potential investors includes a revenue figure that doubled from 2024 to 2025 and a projection of profitability within three years under a 10-event annual schedule. The challenge is persuading investors to put capital into an organization that has spent more than $5 billion of PIF money since 2022 and is now filing layoff notices while two companies pursue it in court.

Rahm's Remarkable Offer

The most striking statement to emerge from the LIV story this week came not from a financial advisor or an executive, but from a player. Jon Rahm, who captains Legion XIII and returned to co-sanctioned competition at the Scottish Open on Thursday, told media after his opening-round 73 that he "refuses to rule out investing his own money" to help save LIV Golf. The comment was reported by Golf Monthly.

Whether the offer is realistic depends on what LIV Golf's actual capital requirements are and whether Rahm's financial position makes a meaningful contribution possible. As a statement of commitment to the league, however, it is essentially without precedent. The player who receives the most attention when LIV Golf's survival is discussed is expressing willingness to put personal money on the line for its continuation. That is either a sign of genuine belief in the project or a sign of how worried the people inside LIV Golf are about what the WARN Act notices mean.

The 60-Day Countdown

Under the WARN Act, 60 days of notice are required before layoffs can proceed. That clock creates a deadline for LIV Golf's investor search. If the funding gap does not close and a new partner is not secured within that window, the workforce reductions the notices anticipate become real. LIV Golf's most recent tournament result — Lucas Herbert's wire-to-wire victory at LIV Virginia, where he shot a final-round 69 to win by four shots at -24 and earn $4 million — suggests the league is still functioning. But the distance between tournament golf proceeding and a league that can sustain itself through the end of 2026 and into 2027 is now a legal filing narrow.

What's Next

The 60-day WARN Act window creates urgency for LIV Golf's investor search. If Ducera Partners can secure the $250 million to $350 million target from a new partner, the layoff notices become unnecessary. If the search fails, the workforce reductions will proceed and the final two events of the season become increasingly unlikely. LIV's UK event and the Trump National Bedminster event have been identified as the events most likely to survive if the financial situation deteriorates further. The two events reported most at risk are Indianapolis and Michigan. Watch for any announcement from Ducera Partners or O'Neil about a new investment agreement — that is the development that would change this trajectory.
