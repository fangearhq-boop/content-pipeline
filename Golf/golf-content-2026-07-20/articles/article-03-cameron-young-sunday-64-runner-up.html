Cameron Young Shot 64 on Sunday. Ryan Fox Shot 68. One Putt Separated Them.

By Marcus Cole

July 20, 2026

Cameron Young played one of the best final rounds at a major championship in 2026. He shot a 9-under-par 64 on Sunday at Royal Birkdale, charging through the leaderboard while Sam Burns gave strokes back and the field compacted around him. At points during the back nine, Young was the outright leader of The Open Championship.

Then Ryan Fox made birdie on the 72nd hole.

Young finished at -9, one stroke behind Fox, with a round that deserves to be recognized on its own terms rather than only as "the second-best score on the final day." A 64 in a major championship is rare. A 64 while chasing down a major championship lead is rarer. Young delivered one of the most compelling final rounds of the year at Royal Birkdale and ended the day without the Claret Jug.

That is not a failure. It is a missed opportunity, and there is a difference.

How the Round Unfolded

Young entered Sunday outside the immediate lead group, with Sam Burns holding a 2-shot advantage at -10. A runner making a charge at The Open needs to post a number and wait — the nature of links golf means you often cannot watch the leaders in real time while playing. Young posted a number.

His 64 was built on consistent birdie-making across the Birkdale course. Young has long been regarded as one of the PGA Tour's best iron players and one of the most accurate drivers in the field. At Royal Birkdale — a course that rewards precision off the tee and from the fairway — those skills were on full display.

When Burns made his three consecutive bogeys on holes four through six, the leaderboard moved in real time in Young's favor. He briefly occupied the top spot, the entire tournament tilting toward him. What he could not control was what Fox was doing simultaneously on the back nine.

One Putt, One Shot

Fox birdied four of his final six holes and then made a 12-foot birdie putt on the 72nd hole to go to -10. Young was already in the clubhouse at -9. He had done everything he could do. The margin was a single stroke — the difference between winning and runner-up was one birdie putt by another player at the 18th.

Young has not won a major championship yet. Sunday at Birkdale reminded the golf world that he is fully capable of competing for one. His game holds up under Sunday pressure at the sport's highest level. He played 18 holes of near-flawless golf and finished second to an inspired champion. That combination of talent and near-miss creates a player who is squarely in the major conversation for the rest of his career.

What's Next

Young will continue his season knowing he came one shot from a major title on one of the game's most historic courses. The lesson runners-up carry out of moments like Sunday's is not always a discouraging one. For some players, a near-miss is the clearest confirmation that they belong — that the level of their game is where it needs to be.

Cameron Young belongs. Sunday at Royal Birkdale proved it. The major that puts his name on a trophy is coming. It just was not at Birkdale in 2026.
