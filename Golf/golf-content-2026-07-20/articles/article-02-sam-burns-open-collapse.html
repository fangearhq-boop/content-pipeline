Sam Burns Had the Open Championship. Three Holes on Sunday Took It Away.

By Jake Torres

July 20, 2026

The sequence will be replayed on highlight reels for decades. Sam Burns on the first tee Sunday at Royal Birkdale, two shots ahead of the field, his daughter Belle born just days before the tournament, the lowest two consecutive rounds in men's major championship history sitting in his scoring history from earlier in the week. He had everything he needed to win.

He birdied the second hole to reach -11 and lead by three.

Then holes four, five, and six.

Three consecutive bogeys. Three shots gone in three holes. Burns went from -11 to -8, from commanding leader to just another name on a suddenly crowded leaderboard. He parred his final 12 holes — steady golf, respectable golf, the kind of golf that wins tournaments when you are not collapsing. When Ryan Fox sank his birdie putt on 18 to finish at -10, Burns was watching from -8, the Claret Jug already gone.

What Happened on the Front Nine

The specific nature of Burns' collapse on holes four through six on Sunday has not been fully detailed in post-round reports, but the result is plain: three bogeys in a row, in a sequence where he had been leading the Open Championship. Whether the cause was wind, nerves, poor shot selection, or the particular cruelty of links golf on a Sunday afternoon, the outcome was the same.

Analysts noted that Burns' collapse at The Open matched a pattern not seen at this major in nearly three decades — a 54-hole leader giving away the tournament on the final day in such dramatic fashion. The specificity of that historical comparison reflects how rare and how significant Sunday's sequence was.

Burns shot a final-round 2-over 72 and finished at -8. He came to Royal Birkdale to win his first major. He leaves with a third-place finish and a story that will define how his career is discussed for years to come — whatever follows.

The Week That Was

Before Sunday, Burns had produced one of the most extraordinary three-round performances in major championship history. His second-round 62 and third-round 65 were the lowest two consecutive rounds ever shot in a men's major. He had arrived at the 54-hole mark having broken records and carrying momentum that would have seemed unassailable.

Burns had also carried something else into the week. His daughter Belle was born two days before the tournament began. He had chosen to play rather than withdraw, and he carried the weight of that decision with him across four days at Birkdale. The emotional arc of his week — joy, record-setting performance, Sunday collapse — is the kind of story that golf produces uniquely among major sports.

What's Next

Burns will face the question that every major-championship runner-up faces: how do you process what happened and come back stronger? For players who have led a major at 54 holes and walked away without a trophy, the psychological aftermath varies. Some come back energized and win the next one. Some carry the moment for years.

Burns is among the best players in the world. He will have more opportunities. He will stand on another Sunday tee with another major lead at some point in his career. Sunday at Royal Birkdale will inform how that moment unfolds — either as a shadow he has overcome or a reference point he draws on.

Golf is one of the few sports where losing the right way still gets discussed. Burns held the lead into Sunday. He did not win. But he was there, and that is not nothing.
