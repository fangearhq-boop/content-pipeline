McIlroy's Open Week: Masters Glory in April, Birkdale Frustration in July

By Jake Torres

July 20, 2026

Rory McIlroy arrived at Royal Birkdale as the 2026 Masters champion — his second consecutive Green Jacket, following his 2025 title. He had also won the Scottish Open in the week prior, arriving at The Open with momentum and one of the sport's most watchable storylines: a former world No. 1 on a run that had already produced one of 2026's best stories.

It did not carry through to the Claret Jug.

McIlroy shot a 2-over 72 in his opening round, placing him well behind the day's leaders. A second-round 67 got him to -1 and safely through the cut, but at a distance from the leaders that left him without a realistic shot at contention heading into the weekend. He was never in the conversation for Sunday's final groupings.

The History McIlroy Carries

The Open Championship holds particular significance for McIlroy. His 2014 victory at Royal Hoylake was one of the most dominant major performances of the decade — a wire-to-wire display of precision and confidence that announced him as the sport's premier player. It was his fourth major title. The expectation was that more would follow quickly.

It has been twelve years since Hoylake. McIlroy has added no further majors to his record since 2014 — until the 2025 and 2026 Masters titles, which restarted his major tally after a long drought. He now has six major championships. For a player of his ability and consistency, that number is both impressive and perhaps slightly below what his talent suggests was possible.

The Open — played often in conditions that suit his links golf background — is the major he is most frequently cited as being overdue to win again. Birkdale was not the occasion. The next links major will be.

The DeChambeau Comment

McIlroy generated attention at Royal Birkdale beyond his scorecard. When asked about Bryson DeChambeau's reaction to his second-round two-stroke penalty — the result of DeChambeau stepping on tall fescue near his ball and improving his swing area, a violation of Rule 8.1a — McIlroy was direct.

"I'm not particularly fond of him," McIlroy said, adding that he found DeChambeau's public reaction to the penalty "performative" and "for attention." DeChambeau, who had briefly threatened at -7 before the penalty dropped him to -5, had expressed frustration about the ruling in public.

McIlroy's comment was not isolated — it reflected a broader wariness many tour players carry about how DeChambeau's public persona intersects with competitive situations. Whether the exchange will have lasting implications in the locker room or fade as a tournament week footnote is difficult to predict.

What's Next

McIlroy's season, viewed in full, remains one of the best on the PGA Tour. Two Masters titles — back-to-back — plus a Scottish Open victory represent a remarkable stretch of major championship success for any player in the modern era. The Open at Birkdale was a disappointment, not a disaster.

He will be back at links courses. He will chase the seventh major again. Whether that pursuit brings him back to The Open's winner's circle remains the most compelling ongoing question in golf for the rest of his career.
