Saudi Arabia Is Ending LIV Golf Funding. The Final Four Events May Be at Risk.

By Ryan Calloway

July 20, 2026

As the 154th Open Championship concluded at Royal Birkdale — an event where several LIV Golf players competed through the year's final major — the structural future of the breakaway tour has reached a moment of genuine crisis.

Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund confirmed in April 2026 that it will end its financial backing of LIV Golf after the current season. The numbers behind that decision reflect the scale of the undertaking: PIF reportedly invested more than $6 billion into the tour since its 2022 launch, absorbing annual losses estimated between $500 million and $600 million per year. After four years of those losses, the sovereign wealth fund has determined the investment is not sustainable.

The consequences are materializing faster than the calendar suggests.

The Immediate Problem

Reports from inside LIV Golf's financial structure indicate that PIF may cut payments to the tour before the 2026 season formally concludes — putting the final four events of the year's schedule at risk before the season reaches its planned end. LIV CEO Scott O'Neil, when asked whether those remaining events would take place, was unable to offer assurances.

That is a significant admission from the head of a professional sports organization. It reflects the gap between the tour's official schedule and its ability to fund that schedule through existing resources.

LIV Golf has responded by establishing an independent board to lead the search for new financial partners. Gene Davis and Jon Zinman have been named to that board. The investment bank Ducera Partners has been retained to assist in identifying potential investors and restructuring options. The stated goal is to find replacement funding that can sustain the tour's operations into 2027 and beyond.

What It Means for Players and the Game

LIV Golf's uncertainty has direct implications for the players on its roster. Prize purse guarantees, appearance fees, and the competitive structure that attracted many of those players away from the PGA Tour depend on financial backing that is now in question. Players who signed long-term LIV contracts may find those commitments complicated if the tour cannot sustain operations.

For the broader golf ecosystem, LIV's funding crisis is a complicated development. The PGA Tour, which absorbed years of pressure and legal challenges from LIV's well-financed emergence, now watches its rival face a financial reckoning. Whether that produces competitive recalibration or further fragmentation of professional golf's structure remains to be seen.

The 2027 LIV schedule, according to reports, is "still up in the air." No planning has been confirmed for events beyond 2026.

What's Next

The search for new investors will define the next several months. Ducera Partners has experience in sports-sector financing and restructuring, but finding a partner willing to absorb the kind of losses that PIF sustained — or willing to restructure the tour's cost model significantly — is a significant challenge.

LIV Golf entered the sport's landscape with ambitions to reshape professional golf. It spent lavishly, attracted major names, and forced the PGA Tour into structural changes it had resisted for years. Now, the funding model that made all of that possible is ending, and the question of what comes next is one of the most consequential in the sport's near-term future.
