Saudi Arabia Ends LIV Golf Funding After 2026 — Golf's Biggest Business Story Reaches a Turning Point

By Ryan Calloway | May 1, 2026 | Golf Fanrecap

The Saudi experiment in professional golf is entering its final funded phase.

Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund confirmed this week that it will end its financial support of LIV Golf at the conclusion of the 2026 season, setting off a cascade of questions about the league's survival, the future of its players, and what a reorganized professional golf landscape might look like. The PIF has reportedly invested more than $5 billion into LIV since its 2022 launch — a staggering sum that has now reached its planned expiry date.

LIV's Response and Path Forward

LIV Golf responded quickly, announcing the formation of a new independent board and signaling its intention to seek new investment from private equity firms, other sovereign wealth funds, family offices, and wealthy individuals. The league's CEO maintained that the current 2026 season is unaltered — events will proceed as scheduled, including next week's LIV Golf Virginia tournament at Trump National DC.

But finding private investors willing to assume what the PIF was funding is a dramatically different proposition. The PIF wrote checks that no private market entity was expected to match indefinitely. LIV has never generated the kind of television revenue or sponsorship base that would justify those investments on commercial terms alone. The next investor — if one arrives — will expect a real return.

Players and the PGA Tour Question

The development has immediately accelerated conversations that had been theoretical just weeks ago. Multiple representatives of LIV Golf players have reached out to the PGA Tour about exploring a path back to the circuit. The interest is real — but so are the obstacles.

The template for returning was established by Brooks Koepka, who negotiated his way back to the PGA Tour after leaving LIV. The terms were stringent: no access to equity grants for five years, a $5 million charitable donation, and no bonus money in the first year. In February, the PGA Tour extended similar offers to Jon Rahm, Bryson DeChambeau, and Cam Smith, who had all won major championships since departing. All three declined.

That February offer may no longer be on the table. The PGA Tour is in a stronger negotiating position now — if the funding spigot is off, the alternative for LIV players is considerably less attractive than it was when oil money was flowing.

Rahm continues to compete on LIV, having won the Mexico City event by six shots earlier this month. DeChambeau, nursing a wrist issue that forced him out of the Mexico City final round, is expected to compete at LIV Virginia. The show goes on — for now.

What Jordan Spieth Said

At Doral on Thursday, reporters naturally sought out Spieth — one of the PGA Tour's most eloquent voices — for his reaction. His response was careful, honest, and immediately quotable. "Olive branches were given out a couple months ago — Brooks took them up on it — so I'm not sure what would change now," he said, referencing Koepka's return. "There are just too many unknowns for me to have a good gauge on what would happen."

When asked if he had thoughts on who should be welcomed back, Spieth offered a laugh: "I'm glad I'm not in the decision-making room."

It's a sentiment that captures the mood of many tour players: relieved to not be making decisions, but acutely aware that the results of those decisions will shape the game they play for years to come.

What's Next

The immediate future is LIV Virginia, May 7-10 at Trump National DC, which proceeds as planned. Longer-term, watch for any PGA Tour communications about modified reinstatement terms and whether any high-profile LIV players publicly announce intentions to pursue a return. The golf world is watching closely — and so is everyone in the decision-making room that Spieth is happy to avoid.
