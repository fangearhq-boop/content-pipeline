The Tournament Rory McIlroy Hasn't Won — And Why That Changes at Muirfield Village This Week

By Jake Torres | Golf Fanrecap | June 3, 2026

Rory McIlroy's golf resume is, by any reasonable measure, one of the greatest ever assembled by a player in the modern era.

Four major championships. Back-to-back Masters titles — only the fourth player in history to win at Augusta National in consecutive years. A career Grand Slam completed when he won the Masters. Multiple Race to Dubai titles. Wins across five continents. A record that has put him in the conversation alongside golf's all-time greats while he is still playing the best golf of his career.

And yet, for all of that, he has never won the Memorial Tournament at Muirfield Village Golf Club.

The Strange History

Golf Digest described it as McIlroy's "strange history with Jack's Place" ahead of this week's Memorial Tournament — an acknowledgment that his inability to win at Muirfield Village is not a reflection of any technical weakness, but rather the kind of specific circuit-and-player mismatch that golf produces sometimes, inexplicably, even for elite performers.

McIlroy has had opportunities. He has come close. He has played the Memorial regularly throughout his career. But the trophy has stayed in other hands every year — including the last two, when Scottie Scheffler claimed back-to-back victories in 2024 and 2025.

For a player who has won on virtually every significant stage in the game, the Memorial represents the largest event-shaped hole in his record on the PGA Tour.

Why This Week Is Different

McIlroy has arrived at this Memorial in deliberately good shape. He skipped the Byron Nelson and Charles Schwab Challenge — two events where his form could have been valuable — to prepare specifically for Muirfield Village. That kind of targeted schedule management suggests a player with a specific goal in mind, not a routine tournament appearance.

His 2026 form has been extraordinary. Two Masters victories. A third-place showing at the PGA Championship despite Aaron Rai's remarkable closing round. He is playing golf at the highest level of his career, and the physical and mental tools that have delivered four majors are fully intact.

At +900 odds, he is second on the betting board behind Scheffler. That reflects respect for the defending champion, not any doubt about McIlroy's capability on this course or in these conditions.

The Scheffler Rivalry

The McIlroy-Scheffler rivalry has been the defining narrative thread of 2026 golf. They have traded wins at the biggest moments, pushed each other to performances that neither might achieve without the other's presence in the field, and elevated the standard of men's professional golf to a level that draws comparisons to the Nicklaus-Watson and Faldo-Norman eras.

At Muirfield Village this week, that rivalry takes another turn. Scheffler is attempting a three-peat at a course he owns. McIlroy is attempting to close a chapter that has remained open too long for a player of his standing.

Whatever happens Thursday through Sunday — whether Scheffler extends his record or McIlroy finally breaks through — the Memorial in 2026 delivers exactly the match-up that the sport's best storylines demand.

What's Next

Round 1 begins Thursday. McIlroy's tee time and early-round pace will be the most-watched development on the leaderboard. If he opens with a low score — his 2026 form suggests he is entirely capable of posting under 65 on a good day at a receptive Muirfield Village — then the conversation immediately shifts to whether this is finally the week.

The Memorial has been on Rory McIlroy's list for a long time. This week, with the best form of his career and a course he knows and has studied, is the best chance he's had. Jack Nicklaus watches from the club he built. The Golden Bear's tournament. The question of whether it will finally bear Rory McIlroy's name gets answered this week.
