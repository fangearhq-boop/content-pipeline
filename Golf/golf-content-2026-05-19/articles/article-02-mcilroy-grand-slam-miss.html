McIlroy's Grand Slam Dream Denied Again: What Went Wrong at Aronimink

By Ryan Calloway | Golf Fanrecap | May 19, 2026

Rory McIlroy has won the Masters Tournament. He has won the U.S. Open Championship. He has won The Open Championship. Those three titles, alongside a PGA Championship triumph, constitute what golfers call the career Grand Slam — the rarest achievement in the sport, claimed by only five players in history.

After Sunday at Aronimink, the PGA Championship still occupies the empty space on McIlroy's career record. He finished tied-seventh at four-under par, five shots behind champion Aaron Rai. A one-under 69 in the final round — two birdies, one bogey, no momentum — was not the performance the moment required. He leaves Pennsylvania with the Grand Slam still uncompleted.

The Final Round That Wasn't

McIlroy entered the final round three shots behind 54-hole leader Alex Smalley. The math was workable. Championship golf has been turned on its final day by players in similar situations throughout the sport's history. He needed a low number — something in the five or six-under range — and enough chaos at the top of the leaderboard to deliver the title.

The bogey at the par-four 13th was the moment that effectively closed the door. McIlroy had managed two birdies to that point and maintained contact with the leaders, but the mistake at 13 came at precisely the wrong moment. Rai, playing ahead of him, was making his move — eagle at nine, birdies through the back nine. McIlroy needed to go forward; instead, the 13th sent him backward.

Two birdies, one bogey, and a round completed in one-under 69. Against a leaderboard where the winner played his final 10 holes in six-under, it was not enough.

The Grand Slam Story That Follows Him

McIlroy's relationship with the PGA Championship — the title he needs — has been one of the persistent subplots of modern golf. He has come close. He has led into final rounds. He has entered weeks with the game and form to win. And he has left without the trophy each time.

The sport's history books offer some comfort: the career Grand Slam has been achieved by Gene Sarazen, Ben Hogan, Gary Player, Jack Nicklaus, and Tiger Woods. Jack Nicklaus did not complete it until the 1966 Open Championship, years into his career. The journey, for the players who ultimately complete it, is rarely linear.

At 36, McIlroy has time. His game has not deteriorated — he contended here at a major championship, and his ball-striking remains elite. The question is whether the circumstances align — the right major, the right week, the right final round — before the window narrows further.

Looking Forward

McIlroy's next major opportunity is the U.S. Open, which he has already won once in his career. The focus will shift there after a week's reflection on what happened at Aronimink. For now, the narrative that has followed McIlroy for years continues: extraordinary player, extraordinary career, one extraordinary title still missing. The search goes on.

What's Next

The next major championship on the calendar is the U.S. Open. McIlroy, as a former champion and perennial contender, will begin preparation for another chance at the history that continues to elude him. The PGA Tour also continues this week at the CJ Cup Byron Nelson at TPC Craig Ranch in McKinney, Texas, starting Thursday May 21.
