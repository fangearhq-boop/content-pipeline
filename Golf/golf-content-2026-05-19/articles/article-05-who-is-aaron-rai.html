Who Is Aaron Rai? The Story Behind Golf's Most Unlikely Major Champion

By Ryan Calloway | Golf Fanrecap | May 19, 2026

Before Sunday, most casual golf fans knew Aaron Rai for two things: the two gloves he wears on the course, and the 2024 Wyndham Championship — his first PGA Tour victory. After Sunday, they know him as the 2026 PGA Champion, the first Englishman to win the Wanamaker Trophy in 107 years, and the man who beat 290-1 opening odds to claim the sport's most prestigious title from a packed Aronimink Golf Club leaderboard.

Who is he? Here is the answer.

The Beginning

Aaron Rai was born on March 3, 1995, in Wombourne — a small town in the English West Midlands, situated a few miles southwest of Wolverhampton. His father, Amrik Singh, was born in England to parents who immigrated from India. His mother, Dalvir Shukla, immigrated to England from Kenya as a teenager. The family's roots span three continents, and the quiet determination that characterizes Rai's approach to his profession seems to reflect a household where working hard without making noise was simply the expected standard.

He turned professional in 2012, when he was 17 years old. The professional golf landscape for European players starts in the developmental structures beneath the DP World Tour, where young players grind for status and experience across circuits that receive minimal mainstream coverage. Rai went through that process over years, learning how to manage courses, conditions, and competition without the benefit of the spotlight.

The European Tour Years

Rai built his game and his record on the European Tour before transitioning to the PGA Tour. The experience accumulated on courses across the UK, continental Europe, and the Middle East — circuits that demand precise ball flight management and a disciplined approach to risk — shaped the kind of player who could handle the demands of a final round at a major without being overwhelmed.

The two gloves are a trademark. Most golfers wear a single glove on the non-dominant hand to improve grip. Rai's choice to wear one on each hand sets him apart visually from every other player on any practice range or tournament tee. He has never made the gloves a story — they are simply how he plays. The sport's tradition-minded observation of this detail has given journalists a shorthand for identifying him in broadcast coverage, but Rai himself treats the gloves as incidental.

The Wyndham Breakthrough

In 2024, Rai won the Wyndham Championship — the final event of the PGA Tour's regular season before the FedEx Cup playoffs. It was his first PGA Tour title and the result that established him as a competitive presence at the top level. The win came in a manner consistent with his personality: methodical, well-constructed over four rounds, without a dramatic final-round collapse or surge that attracted significant attention outside dedicated golf coverage.

He was ranked outside the top 50 in the world rankings heading into the 2026 PGA Championship at Aronimink.

What Sunday Produced

A 40-foot eagle at the ninth. A 70-foot putt at the 17th. Six holes of back-nine golf played in six under par, from three behind to three clear. The PGA Championship, on a day when every other contender had their chance and could not sustain it, belonged to the player nobody expected.

The $3.69 million first-place prize changes the practical realities of his career. The five-year exemption into every major championship changes the calendar. The Wanamaker Trophy in his possession changes the conversation around his name in perpetuity. Aaron Rai, from Wombourne, England, is now a major champion — and the two gloves are the smallest detail anyone will remember about him from this week forward.

What's Next

Rai is in the field at the CJ Cup Byron Nelson at TPC Craig Ranch in McKinney, Texas, starting Thursday May 21 — his first tournament as a major champion. However he plays this week, the story of who he is has already been told. The rest of his career will be written in comparison to Sunday at Aronimink.
