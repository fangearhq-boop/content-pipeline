Aaron Rai Wins the PGA Championship: The 290-1 Longshot Who Rewrote History at Aronimink

By Marcus Cole | Golf Fanrecap | May 19, 2026

On Thursday morning at Aronimink Golf Club, DraftKings Sportsbook had Aaron Rai at 290-1 to win the 108th PGA Championship. By Sunday evening, he was holding the Wanamaker Trophy. His 9-under par total — the product of a final-round 65 that stands as one of the great back-nine charges in major championship history — gave the 31-year-old Englishman a three-shot victory over Jon Rahm and Alex Smalley.

Described by CBS Sports as the biggest opening-day longshot to win a major in at least 20 years, Rai became the first Englishman to claim the PGA Championship since Jim Barnes in 1919 — a gap of 107 years. His victory will be spoken about whenever the sport talks about results nobody predicted, rounds nobody expected, and champions who simply refused to accept the narrative the odds had written for them.

The Final Round That Changed Everything

Rai began the final round two shots behind the leader. With 10 holes to play, the gap had grown to three. What happened next is the kind of sequence that makes major championship golf unlike any other sport.

At the par-five ninth, Rai holed a 40-foot eagle. One shot, one swing of momentum, and the leaderboard shuddered. He followed it with birdies at the 11th and 12th, birdies at the 16th, and then — in the defining moment of his golf career — he read and rolled a 70-foot putt into the hole at the par-three 17th. The lead was his, and it would not be challenged again.

His final 10 holes were played in six under par without a blemish. From three behind to three clear, in the space of a back nine that will be replayed and studied for years.

The Historic Significance

The last Englishman to win the PGA Championship was Jim Barnes, who took the title in 1919 when the tournament was contested at match play. Barnes was an immigrant to the United States from Cornwall, England. Rai's triumph is the first by an England-born and raised golfer since that era — 107 years of American, Australian, South African, and Northern Irish champions separating the two.

Rai will also receive a five-year exemption into every major championship as a result of his win, changing the course of his career immediately. The $3.69 million first-place check — from a record $20.5 million total purse — provides financial security to match the competitive credibility. He arrived at Aronimink with one PGA Tour victory to his name; he left as a major champion.

What Made Rai Different

The betting public missed him at 290-1. The narrative of the week focused on Smalley, McIlroy's Grand Slam pursuit, and Scheffler's defending champion bid. Rai was the player who quietly shot his way up the leaderboard, made no dramatic statements, and simply kept scoring. His two-glove habit — unusual enough to be a defining visual characteristic — was the only thing many casual observers knew about him heading into Sunday.

What he displayed was the ability to manufacture scoring when the tournament demanded it. His eagle at nine was not a product of superior length — it was a well-read putt across a sloped green, struck with the confidence of someone who had prepared for this moment whether the world expected it or not.

What's Next

Aaron Rai is in the field at the CJ Cup Byron Nelson at TPC Craig Ranch in McKinney, Texas, starting Thursday May 21. How he mentally transitions from the highest moment of his career to a regular PGA Tour event within 96 hours will be one of the most fascinating storylines of the week. For now, though, the Wanamaker Trophy is in his possession — and no odds will underestimate him again.
