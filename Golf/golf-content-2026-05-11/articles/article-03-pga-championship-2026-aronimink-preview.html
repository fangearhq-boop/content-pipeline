PGA Championship 2026 Preview: McIlroy's Grand Slam Bid, Scheffler's Defense, and the Test of Aronimink

By Jake Torres | Golf Fanrecap | May 11, 2026

The PGA Championship begins Thursday at Aronimink Golf Club in Newtown Square, Pennsylvania, and it arrives carrying the weight of a question that has rarely felt more legitimate in modern golf: can a single player win all four major championships in one calendar year?

Rory McIlroy already has the Masters. He successfully defended the green jacket in April, becoming the first player to retain the title since Tiger Woods in 2002. He is the only player in 2026 with a mathematical chance at the calendar Grand Slam. Every major from this point forward is a new chapter in either an achievement for the ages or a road that ends somewhere short of history.

The PGA Championship is the second chapter. It begins May 14 and runs through May 17, with coverage available on ESPN and ESPN+.

The Course: Donald Ross's Masterpiece

Aronimink Golf Club was designed by Donald Ross, who described it as the finest course he ever built — high praise from an architect whose portfolio includes Pinehurst No. 2, Oakland Hills, and Seminole. The course plays as a par 70 and can be stretched to 7,600 yards for championship conditions. Seventy-five bunkers, a continuous series of slopes and valleys, and multiple doglegs create a layout that demands precision over power. Position from the tee, distance control into greens, and the ability to read complex putting surfaces are what Aronimink rewards.

For the major championship contenders, this setup suits the technicians — the players who manage the golf course rather than overpowering it. That profile fits McIlroy, whose iron play has been among the best on tour for years. It also fits Matt Fitzpatrick, who built his game around Aronimink's demands before winning the 2022 US Open at Brookline with a similar approach.

The Field: Contenders and Storylines

Scottie Scheffler arrives as the defending PGA champion, having won last year's edition by five shots. Scheffler spent the 2026 season racking up runner-up finishes — at the Masters, the RBC Heritage, and the Cadillac Championship — without adding a win. He skipped the Truist Championship last week specifically to prepare for Aronimink. His form is sharp. His motivation to defend is high. He is the player most likely to challenge McIlroy directly for the title.

Cameron Young enters the week having won twice in 2026 already — the Players Championship and the Doral event. The 29-year-old American has been on the doorstep of a major breakthrough across multiple events. Young is the kind of complete player who can handle major conditions, and the trajectory of his season suggests this might be the week the door finally opens.

Fitzpatrick is perhaps the most underrated threat. He has three wins in his last four PGA Tour starts — the kind of form that earns respect regardless of world ranking. As a former major champion who knows how to manage difficult courses, he is precisely the player that tournament conditions at Aronimink will reward.

Bryson DeChambeau, Jon Rahm, and Dustin Johnson join the field from LIV Golf, as the annual major exemption unites the sport's divided competitive landscape for one week. DeChambeau, in particular, has shown an ability to peak for majors that belies his results in regular-season LIV events.

What's Next

McIlroy's Grand Slam pursuit is the story of this major and potentially the story of this golfing era. If he wins at Aronimink, the question will immediately shift to the US Open at Oakmont in June and then The Open Championship at Royal Troon in July. No player has completed the calendar Grand Slam. The most recent attempt was Tiger Woods in 2000-01, who held all four simultaneously across two seasons but did not win all four in the same calendar year.

First, though, Aronimink. First, 72 holes on Donald Ross's masterpiece. First, four days that could redefine what we expect from one of the best players of his generation.
