Brandt Snedeker Ends 8-Year Win Drought at Myrtle Beach Classic, Earns PGA Championship Spot

By Marcus Cole | Golf Fanrecap | May 11, 2026

Eight years is a long time to wait. In professional golf, where careers are measured in Tour cards and sponsor exemptions and weeks spent wondering if the form will ever return, an eight-year win drought is a full professional lifetime for many players. For Brandt Snedeker, 43, it ended on Sunday at The Dunes Golf and Beach Club in Myrtle Beach, South Carolina.

Snedeker closed with a 5-under 66 to win the 2026 ONEflight Myrtle Beach Classic at 18-under 266, his first PGA Tour victory since approximately 2018 and the 10th of his career. Beau Hossler and Kevin Roy tied for third, two shots back, unable to match Snedeker's combination of precision and composure in the final round.

The victory was not just a win. It was a statement about what professional golfers are capable of when they refuse to accept that the good years are behind them.

How the Week Unfolded

Aaron Rai had led the Myrtle Beach Classic after the second round, posting the kind of scores that suggested another young player might be heading toward a breakthrough. The leaderboard shifted across the weekend, as it tends to do on courses that punish mistakes harshly and reward precise iron play generously.

By Sunday, Snedeker had climbed near the top, and when he made his move — a 5-under closing 66 on a course that demanded that score from the winner — the rest of the field had no response. Snedeker made the putts he needed to make, hit the approaches that mattered, and played with the controlled aggression that characterized his best seasons over a decade of consistent Tour play.

"This is everything," Snedeker said after the round. "You keep working, you keep believing it can happen. And then it does."

The PGA Championship Bonus

Beyond the trophy and the Tour points, Snedeker earned something he had not necessarily expected to be playing for this week: a starting spot in the PGA Championship at Aronimink Golf Club, which begins Thursday in Newtown Square, Pennsylvania.

The Myrtle Beach Classic offers an automatic major exemption for the winner, providing a path to the second major of the season for players who might not otherwise have qualified. For Snedeker, it means trading Sunday's celebration in South Carolina for a tee time at one of Donald Ross's architectural masterpieces just four days later.

It is a remarkable turn of events. A veteran who spent years grinding at the margins of Tour eligibility now walks into a major championship field on the strength of a closing 66 and the best version of himself appearing at the right moment. That is what professional golf can do — and why even the players with decade-long win droughts keep showing up every week.

What's Next

Snedeker will join a field that includes Rory McIlroy (chasing the calendar Grand Slam), Scottie Scheffler (defending champion), Cameron Young, Matt Fitzpatrick, and the top players from both the PGA Tour and LIV Golf circuits. His win at Myrtle Beach ensures that he arrives at Aronimink not as a sentimental favorite but as someone who closed out a professional golf tournament with a 66 less than a week ago. That form is real, and Aronimink's demanding layout will test whether he can carry it into major competition.

For now, the 10th win. The rest can wait until Thursday.
