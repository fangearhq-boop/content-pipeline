### Long-Form Post A 28-year-old Norwegian came to Quail Hollow Club in Charlotte and left with the most significant win of his life. Kristoffer Reitan won the 2026 Truist Championship on Sunday, shooting a final-round 2-under 69 to finish at 15-under 269 and beat Rickie Fowler and Nicolai Hojgaard by two shots. It was Reitan's first PGA Tour victory in his 15th career start, and he earned $3.6 million from the tournament's $20 million purse. The final round was as dramatic as anyone could have hoped. Alex Fitzpatrick had led after Round 3 at 14-under. Cameron Young had fired a stunning 63 on Saturday to charge into contention at 12-under. Rory McIlroy, who had a dismal third-round 75 on Saturday, came roaring back with a final-round 67, showing the kind of form that will be useful heading into the PGA Championship at Aronimink this week. But none of them could match Reitan on the day that mattered most. The Norwegian's story is one of persistence. He grinded his way through his first 14 PGA Tour starts without a victory before breaking through in Charlotte on one of the Tour's biggest regular-season stages. The win also secured his status on the Tour and placed his name among the first-time winners of 2026 alongside some notable company. McIlroy finished approximately T19 after his final-round revival, heading to Aronimink with his long game in better shape than it looked on Saturday. The PGA Championship begins Thursday at Aronimink Golf Club in Pennsylvania. ### Image Post Caption Kristoffer Reitan: 15-under, first PGA Tour win, $3.6M. He didn't use it as an excuse. He played 3-under 69 on Sunday, extended his lead, and won the tournament at 24-under par, four shots clear of the field. In the playoff, Kim and Thomas Detry made par on the 18th hole to clinch the team title. ### Long-Form Post The second major of 2026 is here, and the storylines could not be richer. The PGA Championship begins Thursday at Aronimink Golf Club in Newtown Square, Pennsylvania — a Donald Ross masterpiece that the legendary architect himself described as "my masterpiece." The par-70 course stretches to 7,600 yards and features 75 bunkers, enough slopes, valleys, and doglegs to test every aspect of a player's game over 72 holes. The centerpiece of the week is Rory McIlroy. The Northern Irishman successfully defended his Masters title in April, becoming the first player to retain the green jacket since Tiger Woods in 2002. That win made McIlroy the only player in 2026 with a genuine shot at the calendar Grand Slam — winning all four majors in one year. He has the Masters. The PGA Championship at Aronimink is next. McIlroy arrives having bounced back at the Truist Championship with a final-round 67 after a rough Saturday. He is healthy, focused, and — by his own admission — more motivated than ever after the Masters defense. A second consecutive major would make 2026 arguably the greatest individual season the sport has seen in decades. Standing in his way is defending champion Scottie Scheffler, who won last year's PGA by five shots and has spent 2026 in runner-up purgatory — three second-place finishes at the Masters, RBC Heritage, and Cadillac Championship. Scheffler skipped last week's Truist Championship specifically to prepare for Aronimink. He is ready. Cameron Young, 29, arrives with two wins already in 2026 (the Players Championship and the Doral event) and no major championship on his resume. Young is precisely the kind of in-form, explosive player who could break through at Aronimink. Matt Fitzpatrick, the English former US Open champion, has three wins in his last four starts and brings a Ryder Cup veteran's course management to one of the most technical tracks in major championship golf. LIV Golf players are also in the field. Bryson DeChambeau, Jon Rahm, and Dustin Johnson join their former PGA Tour colleagues for major championships — where the two tours briefly set aside their competition for four weeks a year. The PGA Championship broadcasts May 14-17 on ESPN/ESPN+. ### Image Post Caption McIlroy's Grand Slam bid. Scheffler defending. Cameron Young chasing his first major. ### Long-Form Post Some stories in professional golf are about prodigies and breakout moments. This one is about patience. Brandt Snedeker, 43 years old and nine-time PGA Tour winner heading into the week, closed with a 5-under 66 on Sunday at The Dunes Golf and Beach Club in Myrtle Beach, South Carolina, to win the 2026 ONEflight Myrtle Beach Classic at 18-under 266. It was his first PGA Tour victory in nearly eight years — and his 10th career title. The wait between wins doesn't dull the feeling. Snedeker, who has been a staple of the PGA Tour for nearly two decades, showed on Sunday that his game still has the precision and the composure required to close out a professional golf tournament. Beau Hossler and Kevin Roy tied for third, two shots back, unable to match Snedeker's final-round charge. The victory also earned Snedeker something he hadn't anticipated: a start in the PGA Championship at Aronimink this week. The Myrtle Beach Classic, like other recent PGA Tour events, offers an automatic berth in the following week's major for first-time qualifiers. Snedeker goes from celebrating in Myrtle Beach to teeing it up in Pennsylvania — a week he will remember for the rest of his career. "Never count out the veterans" is the cliché that fits. This week, it earned its place. ### Image Post Caption Brandt Snedeker: 10th career win, first in nearly 8 years.
