Young's 65 Is Already One of the Great Augusta Rounds. Now He Has to Win Sunday.

By Ryan Calloway | Golf Fanrecap

April 12, 2026

Cameron Young walked onto Augusta National's first tee on Saturday carrying the weight of eight shots. Rory McIlroy's lead after two rounds had been the largest in Masters history at the 36-hole mark — a six-stroke cushion that made the Saturday morning pairings feel like a formality before the champion's coronation on Sunday.

Young left the 18th green having erased all of it.

His third-round 65 — seven under par, built on eight birdies and just one bogey — is one of the most remarkable single rounds in recent Masters history. Not because of the score itself, but because of the deficit he overcame to post it. Young became the first player in Masters history to share the 54-hole lead after entering the day eight shots behind. The record had never been within reach before Saturday. Now it belongs to Young.

How He Did It

Augusta National on Saturday was not easy — the winds that arrived in the afternoon made scoring difficult for nearly everyone in the field. McIlroy struggled to a 73 under those same conditions, and his fall from six shots ahead to tied was as much a product of the course's resistance as it was Young's excellence.

But Young's excellence is the more instructive story. He made birdies at strategic moments — taking advantage of the par-5s, converting putts that players further up the leaderboard left short. He made his one bogey early and did not let it derail him. The back nine at Augusta, which so often separates contenders from champions, treated Young with the deference it reserves for players who are truly playing well.

By the time the final group finished their rounds Saturday, Young had moved from an afterthought to the co-leader. The tournament had been rewritten in a single afternoon.

The Task on Sunday

Erasing a six-shot deficit to tie is one thing. Winning the Masters is another. The history of Augusta is populated with players who arrived at Sunday co-leading and left without a green jacket — close calls and near-misses that made the tournament what it is. Young will need to do on Sunday what he did on Saturday: make birdies, avoid the serious mistakes, and trust his game under the specific pressure that Augusta National creates in a way no other venue can replicate.

He will do all of that in the final pairing alongside McIlroy — which is its own form of psychological test. McIlroy has been in more major-Sunday final groups than Young. The experience imbalance is real. But then again, the person who shot 65 on Saturday when he was eight shots back is not somebody who collapses under pressure.

What's Next

The 2026 Masters final round begins Sunday afternoon, with McIlroy and Young teeing off together at 2:25 PM ET. Coverage is on CBS from 2:00 PM. Whoever wins — whether McIlroy defends or Young completes one of the great late surges in major championship history — Saturday's round will be remembered as the day the Masters became a real competition. Cameron Young made sure of that.
