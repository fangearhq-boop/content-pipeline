### STORY 7: Augusta Traditions

#### Long-Form Post вЂ” 8:30 PM ET

There is no golf tournament in the world quite like the Masters.

The green jacket ceremony is one of sport's most recognizable rituals. When the final putt drops on Sunday, the previous year's champion slips the green jacket onto the new winner's shoulders in the Butler Cabin. If the same player wins back-to-back вЂ” as Tiger Woods did in 2001-02 вЂ” a club official presents the jacket instead, since the champion cannot place it on himself.

If Rory McIlroy wins today, Cameron Young (or whoever finishes as runner-up) won't be in the ceremony. McIlroy's 2025 green jacket will stay on the rack, and Augusta National's chairman will step in.

But the jacket is only the most famous part of the experience. Augusta National is built on traditions that casual fans might not know:

The leaderboards at Augusta are run by hand. Volunteers climb ladders and slide numbered panels into slots on enormous yellow scoreboards positioned around the course. There are no electronic displays visible to spectators on the course.

The pimento cheese sandwich has been a Masters staple for decades and is famously priced well below what you'd pay at any comparable sporting event. The butter pecan ice cream sandwich is a quiet cult favorite.

The azaleas bloom for the Masters every April вЂ” the pink and white blossoms are as much a part of the Augusta aesthetic as the fairways themselves.

Today marks the end of another Masters. The azaleas are in full bloom. The jacket is waiting. The tradition continues.
