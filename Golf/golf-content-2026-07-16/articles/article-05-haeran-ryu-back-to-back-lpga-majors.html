Haeran Ryu Has Won Back-to-Back LPGA Majors — and Her Evian Round of 60 Is Historic

By Jake Torres | Golf Fanrecap | July 16, 2026

The South Korean has won the KPMG Women's PGA Championship and the Evian Championship in consecutive major starts, with a third-round 60 at Evian standing as the lowest round in LPGA major championship history.

The golf world's attention is on Royal Birkdale this week, but the most dominant player in the game right now might not be in the Open Championship field. Haeran Ryu has won back-to-back LPGA major championships — the KPMG Women's PGA Championship followed by the Amundi Evian Championship on July 12, where she defeated Brooke Henderson in a playoff.

To understand the scale of what Ryu has accomplished, start with the third round at Evian. She shot a 60 — eleven under par, bogey-free — at a major championship. That is the lowest single round in the history of any LPGA major championship. The previous records had stood for years. Ryu walked onto the 18th green of the final round with a three-shot lead built on the back of a performance that golf has rarely, if ever, seen on its biggest stage.

Two Majors in Three Weeks

The KPMG Women's PGA Championship and the Evian Championship are held within weeks of each other, making back-to-back major wins one of the rarest accomplishments in women's professional golf. The physical and psychological toll of contending across multiple major championship weeks, maintaining focus through the pressure of a final round, and then doing it again almost immediately is extraordinary.

Ryu navigated all of it. Her playoff victory over Henderson at Evian was the exclamation point on a stretch of golf that has established her as the clear No. 1 performer on the LPGA Tour. No. 1-ranked Nelly Korda missed the cut at Evian — a result that underlines how thoroughly Ryu has occupied the top of the women's game in recent weeks.

What Comes Next

The LPGA Tour's next major event is the ISPS HANDA Women's Scottish Open at Dundonald Links (July 23-26), followed by the US Women's Open. Ryu heads into that stretch as the most in-form player on tour by a considerable margin. The question now is whether she can maintain this level heading into the back half of the major championship season — a stretch that historically separates short hot streaks from truly dominant careers.

What she has already done is historic. Two consecutive majors. A scoring record that may stand for a generation. The LPGA has a genuine superstar — and the rest of the field has until July 23 to figure out how to stop her.

What's Next

All eyes return to Royal Birkdale this week for the Open Championship, but Ryu's place in the golf calendar is already secured. When the LPGA comes back to Scotland on July 23, the biggest question in women's golf is simple: can anyone beat Haeran Ryu at a major championship? The answer, based on recent evidence, is far from obvious.
