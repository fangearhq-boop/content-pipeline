LIV Golf Crisis: Saudi Arabia Ends Funding After 2026, League Races to Find New Investors

By Ryan Calloway | Golf Fanrecap

June 14, 2026

The league that reshaped professional golf is now fighting to survive to the end of its own season.

Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund (PIF), the financial backer that launched LIV Golf and provided the guaranteed contracts that drew some of the sport's biggest names, has funded only $196 million of the roughly $600 million LIV Golf requires to complete the 2026 schedule. A payment of $66 million came in early May. Another $130 million arrived in early June. The rest is, for now, absent.

CEO Greg O'Neil told CNBC he cannot guarantee that the final events on the 2026 LIV Golf calendar will take place.

What Has Already Been Affected

The LIV Golf event in Louisiana — planned for New Orleans — has been postponed. LIV Indianapolis and the LIV Team Championship in Michigan are at risk of cancellation if additional funding does not arrive. LIV UK and LIV New York are currently expected to proceed, though the instability of the league's finances creates uncertainty even around confirmed events.

PIF governor Yasir Al-Rumayyan has stepped down from his role as LIV Golf's chairman — the highest-profile leadership departure since the league's founding. His exit signals a broader shift in the Saudi government's relationship with the project it created.

The End of an Era

Saudi Arabia has announced it will cease funding LIV Golf after the 2026 season concludes. The statement confirms what many in the golf world had suspected: the PIF's appetite for subsidizing a league that has not achieved the commercial success or merger progress originally envisioned has reached its limit.

LIV Golf has invested more than $5 billion since its 2022 launch, paying out guaranteed contracts that traditional tour structures do not offer, while operating at a significant loss. The business model required either a merger with the PGA Tour — which has not materialized in any formal way — or private investment that could sustain the league independently.

The league is now seeking $250 to $350 million from private investors to fund what it is calling "LIV 2.0" — a restructured version of the circuit that would operate without the Saudi financial backstop that defined its first four years.

What's Next

The next significant test is whether LIV UK and LIV New York proceed as scheduled. Investment conversations are ongoing. The players who signed guaranteed contracts now face uncertainty about whether the league they joined will finish the year — let alone extend beyond it.

For the broader golf landscape, the LIV crisis reopens questions about what professional golf's structure looks like after 2026. The PGA Tour, DP World Tour, and the players who never left them will be watching closely.
