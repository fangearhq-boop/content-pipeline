Cameron Young Is Playing the Best Golf of His Life — Can He Win His First Major at Aronimink?

By Marcus Cole | Golf Fanrecap

May 12, 2026

Cameron Young arrived at the Players Championship in March with something to prove. He left with the trophy. Then he went to Trump National Doral for the Cadillac Championship and won by six shots in wire-to-wire fashion. He is now the world's No. 3-ranked golfer, the best position of his career. He has two wins in 2026, and the season is barely four months old.

There is exactly one thing Young has not done: win a major championship. That could change starting Thursday at the PGA Championship at Aronimink Golf Club.

The Form of His Life

At the Players Championship, Young trailed heading into the weekend before manufacturing a signature moment on Sunday. He birdied the island green 17th hole at TPC Sawgrass and then watched Matt Fitzpatrick — who had been leading — bogey the 18th to hand him the victory. It was the kind of clutch performance that separates good players from great ones.

At Doral, there were no late drama moments. Young led from start to finish, shooting 19-under 269 for the week and winning by six shots over Scheffler. It was a dominant performance that bore the hallmarks of a player who had completely stopped worrying about results and was simply playing golf.

Why Aronimink Suits Young

Course analysts projecting the 2026 PGA Championship have identified approach play as the most critical statistical category. Aronimink's massive, sweeping greens — averaging around 8,300 square feet — mean that missing in the wrong spot creates long, treacherous putts. Players who consistently hit the right part of the green will separate themselves from the field over 72 holes.

Young's iron play has been among the best on Tour this season. At Doral and the Players, his approach game was the difference-maker — and that's before accounting for his length off the tee, which opens up Aronimink's par 5s in ways that shorter hitters cannot match.

The Company He Keeps

The obvious complication is the competition. Rory McIlroy arrives as the Masters champion seeking back-to-back majors, one of the most historically charged storylines in recent golf memory. Scheffler is the defending champion with three second-place finishes fueling a quiet determination. Fitzpatrick has won three times in his last four starts.

Young is not the favorite. But heading into a major, he may have the most complete and confident game of anyone in the field outside McIlroy. That means something.

What's Next

Young tees off Thursday, May 14 at Aronimink Golf Club. His pairing and tee time will be announced Wednesday afternoon. Follow the leaderboard through the weekend — if Young is in contention through 54 holes, Sunday at Aronimink could be the moment his career is defined by a major title rather than near-misses.
