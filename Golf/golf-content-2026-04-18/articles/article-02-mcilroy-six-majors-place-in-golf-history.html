Rory McIlroy's Six Majors and the Question That Now Defines His Legacy

By Jake Torres | Golf Fanrecap | April 18, 2026

One week after winning his second consecutive Masters, Rory McIlroy sits with six career majors and a path forward that looks like nothing in modern golf. The only question left is how high he climbs.

Rory McIlroy is at home this week, not in Hilton Head Island. He withdrew from the RBC Heritage before it began — a decision made before he won the Masters, unrelated to any fatigue — and is taking a rare week away from professional competition. He has earned it.

Last Sunday at Augusta National, McIlroy rolled in his final putt to claim his second consecutive Masters title, becoming only the fourth player to win back-to-back green jackets. Jack Nicklaus did it in 1965 and 1966. Nick Faldo did it in 1989 and 1990. Tiger Woods did it in 2001 and 2002. Now Rory McIlroy, in 2025 and 2026, is in that company.

Six Majors and What They Mean

The back-to-back victory at Augusta brought McIlroy to six career major championships. The count breaks down as follows: two Open Championships, two US Opens, and two Masters titles. His career Grand Slam, completed at Augusta in 2025, was already one of the landmark achievements in modern golf. The 2026 victory added a second layer to it.

Six majors ties McIlroy with Lee Trevino, Nick Faldo, and Phil Mickelson. It also makes him the European player with the most major championships in the sport's history, drawing even with Faldo — who won his sixth major at The Masters in 1996. For a player who grew up in Northern Ireland idolizing the great European players of the 1980s and 1990s, that record has particular resonance.

The next number on McIlroy's horizon is seven. That would tie him with Arnold Palmer, Sam Snead, Gene Sarazen, and Bobby Jones. Palmer's seven majors make him one of the immortals of American golf. Matching that number — which would require just one more win at a major — is within McIlroy's reach in a way that would have seemed improbable five years ago, when he was stuck at four titles and unable to win the Masters to complete his Slam.

The LIV Effect, Seen Clearly

McIlroy's continued excellence on the PGA Tour has had an uncomfortable context this week. At the 2026 Masters, LIV Golf sent 10 players — the fewest since the league was founded — and received just one top-30 finish in return. Tyrrell Hatton tied for third. Jon Rahm, who won the 2023 Masters and joined LIV with considerable fanfare, finished tied for 38th.

McIlroy himself has been measured in his public comments about LIV Golf for the past two years. He has privately argued against a merger — or at least been skeptical of one — and his performance across the majors continues to demonstrate what staying on the PGA Tour has meant for his game and his place in history. The contrast is not flattering for those who left.

The Road Ahead

When McIlroy returns to competition, the schedule in front of him reads like a dream for a player of his particular profile. The Open Championship at St Andrews — his favourite course in golf, where he has already won an Open — is the next major of the year. The Ryder Cup in Ireland at The K Club follows. For a Northern Irishman who has made no secret of his emotional investment in playing for Europe, these are the events that give him goosebumps.

He will be 37 by the time the Open rolls around. The idea that a golfer at 37 is approaching the end of his competitive window no longer applies to the modern game, not when technology, fitness, and the example of players like Mickelson and Watson at that age have rewritten the timeline. McIlroy is in the prime of his career by any modern definition — and he now has a chance to add to a major total that is already among the greatest in golf history.

What's Next

McIlroy will return to competition after his week off. The exact schedule has not been confirmed, but the major calendar will shape his season: the PGA Championship in May, the US Open in June, The Open at St Andrews in July. Each event is an opportunity to add to a tally of six that already puts him among the immortals of the sport.

One week out from his Masters victory, the question is no longer whether Rory McIlroy belongs in the conversation about golf's all-time greats. It is how high he climbs, and how many of the record books he rewrites before he is done.
