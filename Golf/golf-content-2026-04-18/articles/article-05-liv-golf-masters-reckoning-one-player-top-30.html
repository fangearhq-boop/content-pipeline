LIV Golf's Masters Reckoning: Ten Players, One Top-30 Finish, and a $5 Billion Question

By Jake Torres | Golf Fanrecap | April 18, 2026

The 2026 Masters sent LIV Golf's 10-player delegation home with one top-30 finish and a T-38 from its biggest star. With Saudi PIF reportedly reconsidering its investment, the circuit faces its most consequential weeks in its four-year history.

The numbers from LIV Golf's performance at the 2026 Masters are worth setting out plainly. Ten players from LIV Golf qualified for the field at Augusta National — the fewest since the league was founded in 2022, down from 18 in 2023, 14 in 2024, and 12 in 2025. Of those 10 players, one finished under par and inside the top 30: Tyrrell Hatton, who tied for third after a Sunday 66 recovered from a 72 in the third round.

The rest of the LIV delegation finished outside the top 30. Jon Rahm, who won the Masters in 2023 and was arguably the most prominent active LIV player entering the week, finished tied for 38th. For context: in the three years since Rahm won at Augusta, he has contended in just one major championship. The competitive arc is not moving in the right direction.

The PIF Uncertainty

LIV Golf's on-course results at the Masters arrive against a backdrop of financial uncertainty that the league's management has been working to contain. The Saudi Public Investment Fund — which has invested more than $5 billion in LIV Golf since the league's founding — is reportedly reconsidering the scale of its commitment, according to reporting from The Athletic and the Wall Street Journal.

LIV Golf CEO Scott O'Neil addressed the reports directly in an email to staff, telling them the 2026 season would continue "as planned, uninterrupted and at full throttle." The Mexico City event is proceeding on schedule. Players are competing. But the gap between O'Neil's confidence and the reported uncertainty in Riyadh is visible, and it has not gone unnoticed in golf's broader community.

The Merger That Never Was

When LIV Golf and the PGA Tour announced a framework agreement in June 2023, many in the sport believed — or hoped — that a resolution was at hand. Two and a half years later, a formal merger has never materialized. Rory McIlroy, who has spoken more candidly than almost any other prominent figure about the state of negotiations, said in March 2026 that a deal "doesn't feel like it's any closer."

McIlroy then went out and won his second consecutive Masters. The irony of that juxtaposition — the PGA Tour's most prominent player winning the sport's most prestigious event while LIV Golf struggles to produce top-30 finishes in the same field — has not been lost on anyone watching the sport closely.

The Competitive Question

The debate about whether LIV Golf's 54-hole, shotgun-start format is keeping players sharp for major championship preparation is now several years old. Supporters of the format argue it preserves players' bodies and allows for a different kind of mental engagement. Critics argue that the lack of cuts — and the format's fundamental difference from traditional stroke play — means LIV players arrive at majors without recent experience of the specific pressure those events create.

One year's data is not a definitive answer. But four years of Masters results, with LIV's representation declining from 18 to 14 to 12 to 10, and its competitive results inside the top 30 remaining limited in each of those years, is a pattern that is increasingly difficult to explain away.

What's Next

LIV Golf Mexico City continues through Sunday, April 19. Jon Rahm leads the individual competition at 10 under par through two rounds, with two rounds remaining. A Rahm victory in Mexico City would provide a positive narrative heading into whatever announcement or development comes next for the league.

The PIF's reported reconsideration of its investment does not have a confirmed timeline for resolution. LIV Golf CEO O'Neil has been categorical that the season will continue. For the players competing in Mexico City and preparing for the rest of the 2026 schedule, the message from leadership is that business continues as usual.

Whether that message holds through the summer — and what it means for the competitive landscape of professional golf — is the story that will define the remainder of 2026 for a league that has spent four years promising a revolution and produced, at Augusta at least, one top-30 finish.
