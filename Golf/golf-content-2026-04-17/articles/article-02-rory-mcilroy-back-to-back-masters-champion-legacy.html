Rory McIlroy's Back-to-Back Masters: Six Majors, One Untouchable Legacy

By Jake Torres | Golf Fanrecap | April 17, 2026

The week after a landmark victory has a way of clarifying things. Rory McIlroy is home. The green jacket is presumably in a safe place. The PGA Tour's Signature Event in Hilton Head is underway without him, and that is perfectly fine, because there is nothing left to prove this week.

McIlroy's 12-under 276 at Augusta National on April 12 — a one-shot win over Scottie Scheffler — was the culmination of a decade-long arc that began with the 2011 US Open, ran through the Career Grand Slam completed in 2025, and now ends this chapter with consecutive green jackets and a spot in the smallest club in golf history.

The Exclusive Company

Only three men had won back-to-back Masters titles before McIlroy. Jack Nicklaus did it in 1965 and 1966. Nick Faldo did it in 1989 and 1990. Tiger Woods did it in 2001 and 2002. Each of those names represents the elite tier of the sport's history. McIlroy has now joined them in the most literal way possible — not by comparison or debate, but by doing the same thing they did.

With six major championships, McIlroy is now tied with Nick Faldo for the most major titles won by any European golfer. He and Faldo are the only two Europeans ever to win six majors. The only man ahead of them on the European list is Harry Vardon, who won seven — the last of them in 1914. McIlroy is 36 years old. The conversation about whether he can reach Vardon is not unreasonable.

The Masters Victory Itself

The 2026 Masters had its share of drama. McIlroy built a six-shot lead through 36 holes — the largest 36-hole lead in Masters history — then saw it evaporate in a turbulent third round where Cameron Young made the most stunning move in recent major championship memory. Entering Sunday tied, McIlroy shot a final round that included a double-bogey on the fourth hole and a bogey on the sixth, but held on with key birdies on holes seven, eight, twelve, and thirteen. His final-hole scramble from the trees through a bunker to a chip and two-putt was, in retrospect, very Rory McIlroy.

The Numbers Behind the Trophy

McIlroy's $4.5 million winner's check from the $22.5 million purse comes with a significant caveat: United States tax regulations will claim roughly $1.9 million of it, applying a combined rate of approximately 41.99 percent. The calculation is widely known among touring professionals and does nothing to diminish the achievement, but it adds a sharp financial edge to the story for those keeping track.

More relevant is what McIlroy said afterward: "I want more." Whatever rest this week brings, the competitive intent is clear.

What's Next

McIlroy is skipping the RBC Heritage, taking a full week away from competition after the Masters. His next start has not been confirmed at the time of writing. The PGA Tour season continues with the RBC Heritage this week and the Wells Fargo Championship in coming weeks before the US Open. For McIlroy, with six majors and back-to-back Augusta wins already in hand, the only real question is where this remarkable run goes from here.
