Scheffler's OB Opener: World No. 1 Starts Rough, Bounces Back at RBC Heritage

By Marcus Cole | Golf Fanrecap | April 17, 2026

The first swing of Scottie Scheffler's 2026 RBC Heritage went out of bounds. Not deep rough. Not a divot. Out of bounds — penalty stroke, re-tee required, in front of the entire gallery and a watching world. The World No. 1, the Masters runner-up, the man who had just played 72 holes at Augusta National without winning, started his Harbour Town week with the most visible mistake in golf.

What he did next is the more interesting story.

Salvaging Bogey, Then Going to Work

After the out-of-bounds penalty, Scheffler re-teed and struck his next drive cleanly down the fairway. His approach shot landed inside 12 feet of the pin. He calmly drained the putt for bogey — one over par instead of a possible disaster — and walked to the second tee with nothing visibly changed in his demeanor.

Over the next 17 holes, he played at 4-under. The final result was a 3-under 68, which sits him five back of leader Ludvig Åberg at the end of Round 1. At a no-cut Signature Event with 54 holes remaining, he remains entirely in contention.

Context for the Masters Runner-Up

Scheffler arrived at Harbour Town carrying the weight of a near-miss at Augusta. He finished at 11-under 277, one shot behind McIlroy's winning 276. It was his fourth top-four finish in the 2026 season across four starts — a remarkable run of consistency that has not yet produced a win since January. Harbour Town is a course he knows well, having won the RBC Heritage in 2024 and regularly contending here.

The OB opener was a one-swing blip in a round that otherwise demonstrated exactly why Scheffler occupies the No. 1 ranking. His iron play was sharp, his course management sound, and his ability to compartmentalize a penalty stroke on the opening hole speaks to a mental composure that is genuinely elite.

The Course Suits Him

Harbour Town Golf Links, the Pete Dye design at the heart of the Hilton Head Island resort, is one of the tightest and most demanding courses on the PGA Tour calendar. Its small greens — among the smallest on tour — require precise iron play and reward players who can control distance and trajectory. Those are Scheffler's strengths.

Åberg sits at 8-under and is the clear front-runner through 18 holes. But Scheffler has been in worse positions at better courses and found a way through. Five shots with 54 holes remaining is not an insurmountable gap — especially for the best player in the world.

What's Next

Round 2 is underway today at Harbour Town. Scheffler and his playing partner Matt Fitzpatrick were among the morning-round groups heading out today. A low round in Round 2 could bring him back into the conversation by the weekend. With no cut and every player still in the field, Scheffler's week is very much alive despite the opening drama on the first tee.
