Rahm Paid the Fines. Now He's Back. And His Ryder Cup Path Is Open Again.

By Ryan Calloway July 9, 2026

The Return

Jon Rahm teed off at the Genesis Scottish Open on Thursday morning at The Renaissance Club in North Berwick, Scotland. For the players around him on the tee sheet, that is unremarkable information. For Rahm, it represented the end of a two-year absence from co-sanctioned professional golf and the beginning of a formal path back into the sport's mainstream competitive structure.

Rahm joined LIV Golf in January 2024. The DP World Tour, of which he had been a member, suspended him following that move. LIV Golf events are not co-sanctioned by the DP World Tour or the PGA Tour. With the exception of the four major championships — which the relevant governing bodies have continued to allow LIV players to enter — Rahm's appearances since January 2024 had been exclusively on the LIV circuit.

The Scottish Open is different. It is a co-sanctioned event shared by the PGA Tour and the DP World Tour, a designation that predates the current LIV dispute and has survived it. Players who meet the DP World Tour's membership requirements can enter. Rahm met them — but only after reaching an agreement that cleared the outstanding obstacles to his reinstatement.

The Agreement

Rahm and the DP World Tour reached a formal agreement that enabled his return. The terms required Rahm to pay all outstanding fines that had accumulated since his LIV move in early 2024 and to commit to playing a specified number of DP World Tour events in the remainder of the 2026 season, excluding the major championships. The Scottish Open, July 9-12 at The Renaissance Club, counts as his fifth event under that arrangement, which officially classifies him as a member in good standing.

That designation matters. A member in good standing has access to co-sanctioned events. He earns points in the Race to Dubai, the DP World Tour's season-long points competition. And under the terms of European Ryder Cup eligibility, a player who maintains membership in good standing through 2026 and 2027 can be considered for the European Ryder Cup team. Rahm captained Europe to victory at the 2023 Ryder Cup in Rome. His eligibility for the European side had been in question from the moment he left for LIV Golf.

What Rahm Said

Rahm described the Scottish Open as "one of my favourite weeks" when he addressed the media ahead of his first round. He said he was "just happy that the DP World Tour let me have the opportunity and that I can be here and play what's not only a great week but a great golf course, great crowd." On the return to a co-sanctioned environment, he kept it straightforward: "It's just another event."

That framing — just another event — is one way to manage the surrounding noise. There are players in the field who have competed on the PGA Tour and DP World Tour continuously through the period when Rahm was unavailable. For them, this is a Scottish Open like any other. For the conversation around professional golf's ongoing structural dispute, Rahm's presence at The Renaissance Club carries a significance that a single tee time cannot fully absorb.

The Template Question

Rahm's agreement raises a question that others in golf will be watching closely: does this deal create a replicable pathway for other LIV players who want to return to co-sanctioned competition? The agreement involves paying fines, committing to a set number of events, and maintaining a membership standard. Those are specific terms. Whether other LIV players with different fine structures, different contractual commitments to LIV, and different relationships with the DP World Tour could replicate Rahm's arrangement in exactly the same form is a detail-level question that the organizations involved have not fully addressed publicly.

What the agreement demonstrates is that the door is not permanently closed. A path exists. The terms of walking it are negotiable. For players on the LIV roster who have retained interest in major championship golf and in events like the Scottish Open, Rahm's week in Scotland is the most concrete evidence yet that the bridge back can be built.

What's Next

The Scottish Open runs through Sunday, July 12. The Open Championship at Royal Birkdale begins July 16. Rahm is in the Open field as a major championship qualifier. If he competes well at The Renaissance Club and carries that form into the following week, the conversation about his standing in the sport — and where he fits in European golf's future — will intensify considerably.

He said it is just another event. The leaderboard through four rounds will determine whether anyone believes him.
