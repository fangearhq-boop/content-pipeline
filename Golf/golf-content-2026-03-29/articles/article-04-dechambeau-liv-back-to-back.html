DeChambeau Wins Back-to-Back on LIV: 5 Career Titles After Playoff Win Over Rahm

March 29, 2026 | Golf Fanrecap

Bryson DeChambeau has five career LIV Golf titles. He has won back-to-back events. He has beaten the man who leads all male professionals in strokes gained — twice — in the playoff. And barely anyone is talking about it.

The LIV Golf South Africa event at Johannesburg concluded March 22 with DeChambeau winning a playoff over Jon Rahm on the par-5 18th hole. Both players had finished the 72-hole tournament at 26-under 258. Rahm had shot a brilliant final-round 63 to tie DeChambeau, and appeared to have stolen the momentum. On the playoff hole, Rahm found a bunker. DeChambeau made birdie four. That was that.

The Consecutive Wins

The South Africa victory followed DeChambeau's win at LIV Singapore (March 12–15), where he beat Richard Lee in a playoff. Two consecutive LIV wins, two consecutive playoffs — a pattern of closing under pressure that defines his 2026 season.

His five career LIV titles place him among the most decorated players in the league's history. The event at Johannesburg set a record 47 bogey-free rounds across the field and attracted more than 100,000 spectators — the kind of crowd attendance that LIV has worked to build globally.

DeChambeau heads to the 2026 Masters at +1200 as one of the most credible threats in the field. He won the U.S. Open in 2024 and has demonstrated at Augusta before that his power game and improved short game are suited to the demands of Augusta National. His form in March makes him arguably the most dangerous player outside the top two in the odds board.

The Rahm Paradox

The more philosophically interesting story of the LIV Golf spring swing is Jon Rahm's. Per Data Golf, Rahm leads all male professional golfers in strokes gained in 2026 — with a tee-to-green advantage of +2.05 strokes versus field. In five LIV events, he has one win (Hong Kong, March 5–8, -23, three-shot victory) and three runner-up finishes.

The question his 2026 season raises has no clean answer: what does it mean to be the statistically best player in the world and routinely finish second? Some of it is variance — playoffs are essentially coin flips at the elite level, and Rahm has been in three of them. Some of it is something harder to quantify: the ability to close, to protect leads, to convert the best performance into the best result.

Rahm's Data Golf ranking of No. 2 in the world contrasts sharply with his OWGR position of 28th — a discrepancy that reflects the ongoing controversy about how LIV Golf events are weighted (or not weighted) in the official ranking system. His strokes gained numbers suggest a player at the peak of his form. His results suggest someone who has not yet found the way to translate that form into consistent victories.

The Format Change

LIV Golf's switch to 72-hole events for 2026 (up from 54 holes) and the expansion to 57 players has changed the competitive character of the tour. The extra 18 holes reward consistent golf over multiple days rather than weekend heroics, and the depth of the field means that low rounds across the week now carry more meaning. The record bogey-free rounds at Johannesburg are one indicator of how the longer format has changed scoring dynamics.

Both DeChambeau and Rahm arrive at Augusta having played competitive 72-hole events in recent weeks — an advantage over some PGA Tour players who may have had gaps in their competitive schedules. For Augusta, fitness and form going in have historically mattered. Both LIV golfers have both.
