Masters 2026 Preview: Scheffler's Grand Slam, McIlroy Defending, Tiger's Fate Unknown

March 29, 2026 | Golf Fanrecap

The 90th Masters Tournament begins in two weeks — April 9 through 12 at Augusta National Golf Club. The field, the stakes, and the storylines around this particular edition are as rich as any in recent memory: a Grand Slam pursuit, a defending champion with a bad back, a Tiger Woods situation that resolved nothing, and two LIV Golf threats who arrive genuinely hot.

Here is the full picture heading into Augusta.

Scottie Scheffler: The Grand Slam Bid

Scottie Scheffler is the +480 favorite, and the reason goes beyond his world No. 1 ranking. He has already won the Masters twice (2022, 2024), the PGA Championship, and The Open Championship. He needs only a U.S. Open to complete the career Grand Slam — joining Jack Nicklaus, Tiger Woods, Ben Hogan, Gary Player, Gene Sarazen, and Sam Snead as the only men to hold all four major titles.

He arrives at Augusta this year without the final tune-up he might prefer. He withdrew from the Houston Open for the birth of his second child. His results through March — T-3, fourth, 12th, T-24 — show someone who has not been at the peak of his game since the American Express win in January. But Scheffler at a level below his best is still among the most formidable players in the world. He has 20 PGA Tour wins including four majors before turning 30 — matching a threshold previously reached only by Nicklaus and Woods.

Rory McIlroy: Defending on Faith

Rory McIlroy is defending his 2025 Masters title at odds of +1000. Back-to-back Masters wins have only been achieved three times in the tournament's history: Nicklaus (1965-66), Faldo (1989-90), and Woods (2001-02). McIlroy would be the fourth.

The concern is his preparation. He aggravated a back injury at the Arnold Palmer Invitational and withdrew before the third round. At the Players Championship, he barely made the cut and finished T-46. He has since decided to sit out all remaining events before the Masters — skipping the Houston Open and the Valero Texas Open — arriving at Augusta without competitive rounds in his legs.

His new Amazon Prime documentary, "The Masters Wait," offers an illuminating portrait: a champion who admits he "came to resent the majors" during the long wait for his first green jacket, and who found his way back to the title in 2025. Whether that psychological breakthrough survives a compromised preparation in 2026 is the defining question for his Masters campaign.

The LIV Threats: Rahm and DeChambeau

Jon Rahm and Bryson DeChambeau both arrive at Augusta having played competitive golf in the past two weeks. Rahm leads all male professionals in strokes gained per Data Golf — with a tee-to-green advantage of +2.05 — despite finishing runner-up in three of his five 2026 LIV events. At +1200, he is one of the most compelling pre-tournament plays in the field.

DeChambeau won back-to-back LIV events in March (Singapore and South Africa), giving him five career LIV titles. He arrives at Augusta as the most in-form player in the world over the past month. Both men are major champions — Rahm won the 2021 U.S. Open and 2023 Masters; DeChambeau won the 2020 U.S. Open and 2024 U.S. Open — and both have the game for Augusta National.

Tiger's Status

Tiger Woods was arrested on DUI suspicion on March 27. His breath test read 0.00 — no alcohol — and he refused a urine test, with authorities suspecting medication impairment. He was released after eight hours. He had been targeting Augusta as a potential competitive return.

President Trump said publicly on March 27 that Woods "will be there but won't be playing." Whether that proves accurate, or whether Woods attempts to participate in any capacity, remains unresolved. Augusta without Woods playing is a quieter Masters than the one golf fans anticipated three weeks ago.

Other Notable Storylines

Xander Schauffele (+1600), Collin Morikawa, and Brooks Koepka (who returned to the PGA Tour from LIV in December 2025) each need only a Masters title to complete their own career Grand Slams. The 2026 field may have more Grand Slam chasers in meaningful positions than any Augusta edition in decades.

Amazon Prime holds broadcasting rights to the Masters for the first time in 2026, bringing the tournament to a new platform after decades of traditional television partnerships. New qualifying criteria — adding winners of national opens from Scotland, Spain, Japan, Hong Kong, Australia, and South Africa — broaden the international field.

The 90th Masters has more to offer than almost any edition in memory. Augusta tends to provide, regardless of what arrives at its gates. The question on April 9 is who can deliver under the pressure it creates.
