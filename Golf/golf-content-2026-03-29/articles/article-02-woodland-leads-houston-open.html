Woodland Leads Houston Open Into Sunday: Seeking First Win Since 2019 U.S. Open

March 29, 2026 | Golf Fanrecap

Gary Woodland stands at 18-under par, one shot clear of the field, entering the final round of the Texas Children's Houston Open at Memorial Park Golf Course. If he wins, it will be his first PGA Tour victory since the 2019 U.S. Open at Pebble Beach — a span of nearly seven years that has encompassed brain surgery, a PTSD diagnosis, and a story that has nothing to do with golf shots.

The final round is underway Sunday at the $9.9 million event. Woodland leads Nicolai Højgaard by one, with a pack of players including Michael Thorbjornsen, Sudarshan Yellamaraju, Sahith Theegala, and Chris Gotterup within striking distance.

The Comeback Story

Woodland's 2019 U.S. Open victory at Pebble Beach was one of the most emotional wins of that year — a first major championship, held off against Rory McIlroy and Brooks Koepka on a famous California layout. Since then, the wins stopped coming. And then his health intervened in a way that made results seem secondary.

In September 2023, Woodland underwent surgery to remove a brain tumor. The procedure was successful. He returned to the PGA Tour, but the path back to competitive form was slow and nonlinear. Two weeks before the Houston Open, he publicly disclosed a PTSD diagnosis — an acknowledgment of the psychological weight that can accompany a serious medical event.

He arrived at Memorial Park, shot three rounds that have placed him at the top of the leaderboard, and now stands one good Sunday away from something remarkable.

The Højgaard Factor

Nicolai Højgaard is 24 years old and has never won on the PGA Tour. If he wins at Houston, he will become the first Danish-born player in history to win on the PGA Tour — a historic result for a country with deep amateur golf roots but no previous Tour winners. He has played consistent golf through three rounds and enters Sunday with the pressure of history and a one-shot deficit.

His twin brother Rasmus Højgaard also plays professionally in Europe, making them one of golf's more prominent sibling pairings. Nicolai has played well at previous Tour events without breaking through. This is his best opportunity.

Field Context

The absence of Scottie Scheffler (withdrawn ahead of the week for the birth of his second child) and Rory McIlroy (managing a back injury ahead of the Masters) has opened the field at Houston. Neither player's absence diminishes the competitive quality of the event — multiple players in the mix are established Tour winners or rising stars — but it does mean the champion this week will not have beaten the game's top two players.

The Houston Open winner earns a Masters invitation if not already qualified. For players on the bubble — including Chris Gotterup (already qualified via Scottish Open win) and others — the week carries varying levels of Augusta stakes.

For Woodland, the Masters is secondary. He is chasing something more personal: proof that the years since Pebble Beach have not been wasted, and that a brain surgery and a PTSD diagnosis can be the setup to a second act, not the end of one.
