Tiger Woods Arrested on DUI Suspicion — BAC 0.00, Medication Suspected

March 29, 2026 | Golf Fanrecap

Tiger Woods was arrested on suspicion of driving under the influence on March 27 in Jupiter Island, Florida — but the facts of the incident are more complicated than the headline suggests. His breath test registered 0.00. Zero alcohol. Authorities believe impairment, if any, was medication-related.

The incident came two days after his return to competitive golf at the TGL Finals, and two weeks before the Masters Tournament he had been targeting as a potential competitive return. The timing, the circumstances, and the unresolved legal picture make this one of the most significant news events in golf in years.

What Happened

According to Martin County authorities, Woods' Land Rover clipped the back of a truck and trailer on South Beach Road, a road near his home in Jupiter Island, Florida. The vehicle tipped onto its driver's side and slid to a stop. Woods crawled out of the passenger side uninjured.

Martin County Sheriff John Budensiek confirmed that a roadside breath test returned a reading of 0.00 — no detectable alcohol. Woods then refused to submit to a urine test, which resulted in an additional charge alongside the original DUI with property damage. No substances were found in the vehicle. The working theory among authorities is that any impairment was medication-related.

Woods was booked at Martin County jail and released approximately eight hours later. It is his second DUI-related arrest in nine years and the fourth car crash involving him since 2009.

The Timing

The arrest occurred on March 27 — exactly 48 hours after Woods made his return to competitive golf at the TGL Finals. On March 25, his Jupiter Links GC team faced the Los Angeles Golf Club in a nationally televised match. Viewership approached one million, the second-highest TGL rating ever. Woods had reportedly decided to compete just the night before.

The TGL appearance had generated genuine optimism. At 50 years old, having undergone a ruptured left Achilles repair in March 2025 and a lumbar disc replacement in October 2025 — his seventh back surgery — Woods had shown he could still perform under competitive conditions. It was the kind of moment the sport had been waiting for.

Two days later, that narrative collapsed.

Masters Implications

Woods had been building toward the Masters Tournament (April 9–12) as a potential competitive return. A U.S. Senior Open entry had also been filed for July. President Trump addressed the situation publicly on March 27: "He'll be there, but he won't be playing," citing insider knowledge of Woods' situation.

The legal picture remains unsettled. The refusal to submit to a urine test is a separate charge, but the 0.00 BAC complicates a straightforward DUI prosecution. His legal team will have significant arguments to make. The PGA Tour declined to comment on the arrest.

Whether Woods attends the Masters as a patron — watching the tournament he has won five times — or whether he attempts any form of competitive return before or after any legal proceedings is unknown. What is certain is that the path back to Augusta's first tee has become considerably more complicated in the past 48 hours.

Medical and Legal History

Woods' physical history since 2021 is extensive. His February 2021 car crash near Rancho Palos Verdes, California, left him with severe leg injuries that required multiple surgeries and threatened his ability to walk, let alone play golf. He returned to competition in 2022 and 2023 at reduced frequency, playing selected major championships. His last PGA Tour event was the 2024 Open Championship, where he missed the cut.

His first DUI arrest came in May 2017, when he was found asleep at the wheel in Palm Beach Gardens. He pleaded no contest and entered a diversion program. That incident was attributed to a combination of prescription medications — no alcohol was involved, paralleling the circumstances of the March 27 arrest.

The pattern of car incidents and the consistency of the medication narrative will be central to any legal proceedings. For golf, the more immediate question is what happens next — and whether Tiger Woods, at 50, has any competitive chapter remaining.
