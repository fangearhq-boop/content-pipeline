Three Straight Runner-Ups and One Big Prize Ahead: Scheffler's Road to Aronimink

By Marcus Cole | May 7, 2026

Golf · PGA Tour · PGA Championship Preview

Scottie Scheffler has been the second-best player in professional golf for the last month. The world No. 1 has finished runner-up in three consecutive PGA Tour events — the Masters, the RBC Heritage, and the Cadillac Championship — without winning any of them. It is the first time any player has recorded three consecutive runner-up finishes since Sergio Garcia in 2014, and for most players, it would be a remarkable run. For Scheffler, it feels like unfinished business.

The streak began at Augusta, where McIlroy's back-to-back Masters defense proved too strong. Scheffler finished second. The week after, at the RBC Heritage at Harbour Town, Scheffler built a final-round position and was forced into a playoff — which Matt Fitzpatrick won. Second again. Then came Doral and the Cadillac Championship, where Scheffler carded a final-round 71 and finished 13-under, six shots behind a dominant Cameron Young. Three starts, three silver medals.

What the Streak Actually Reveals

Strip away the narrative frustration and what remains is this: Scheffler has been the second-best player in field at three consecutive elite tournaments. That is an extraordinary level of consistency. Since 2022, he has been in the top two through 54 holes of 24 different PGA Tour events and converted 15 of them into victories — a 62.5% conversion rate that is among the highest of any player since Tiger Woods.

The three runner-ups also came in distinctly different ways: beaten by a better opponent at Augusta, lost in a playoff at Harbour Town, outpaced late by a hot competitor at Doral. There is no single correctable flaw in the pattern — just three instances where another player was better, or luckier, on that particular day. That is a sign that the core of Scheffler's game remains elite. The execution has been close but not perfect.

Aronimink: Built for Scheffler

The 2026 PGA Championship at Aronimink Golf Club (May 14-17) is the next major test, and the course architecture could not be more favorable to Scheffler's strengths. Donald Ross designed Aronimink as a precision test above all else — the most elaborate bunkering of any of his layouts, three par-3s measuring over 210 yards, and greens that demand accurate approach play. The course does not punish distance so much as it punishes imprecision.

Scheffler's ball-striking ranks among the best in professional golf. His approach play — measured by proximity to the pin and greens in regulation — is consistently elite. In a field full of power hitters, Aronimink may be the rare major venue that neutralizes raw distance as an advantage and emphasizes the kind of controlled iron play that defines Scheffler's game.

What's Next

Scheffler is not playing this week at the Truist Championship. He has the week off before heading to Newtown Square, Pennsylvania, for the PGA Championship. The three runner-ups have built something — a sharpened edge, an unresolved motivation, and a public narrative that wants to see him win. Aronimink may be exactly the stage where he answers all of it. The PGA Championship begins May 14.
