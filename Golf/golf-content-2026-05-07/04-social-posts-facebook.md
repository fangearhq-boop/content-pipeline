### STORY 3: Scheffler Runner-Up Streak / Aronimink

**Long-Form Post (1:00 PM ET)**
Let's talk about Scottie Scheffler — because what he's been doing for the last month deserves a real conversation.

The world No. 1 finished runner-up at the Masters, losing to Rory McIlroy. Came back the following week and lost a playoff to Matt Fitzpatrick at the RBC Heritage. Then closed out April by finishing 13-under at the Cadillac Championship — six shots behind Cameron Young.

Three consecutive runner-ups. The PGA Tour confirmed it is the first time any player has done this since Sergio Garcia in 2014.

On paper, this is a story about near-misses. But step back and look at what it actually means: Scheffler has been top-2 at three consecutive PGA Tour events, meaning he has been the best or second-best player in the field at every start. The consistency is extraordinary. The conversion rate just hasn't matched it — yet.

Next week is the PGA Championship at Aronimink Golf Club. Scheffler is the heavy favorite. The course is a Donald Ross design that rewards precision, elite ball-striking, and course management — three things Scheffler does better than almost anyone in the world.

If there's a course designed to end the streak and deliver a major, Aronimink is it.

Is this Scheffler's week to break through at the PGA Championship? 🏆

### STORY 4: Nelly Korda HOF Countdown

**Long-Form Post (3:30 PM ET)**
Four points. That is all that stands between Nelly Korda and a permanent place in the LPGA Hall of Fame.

In a season that has been historically dominant from the first tee shot, Korda now has 23 of the 27 points required for induction. She has won three times in 2026 — at the Hilton Grand Vacations TOC, the Chevron Championship, and the Riviera Maya Open at Mayakoba — and finished runner-up in the other three events she has entered.

That is three wins and three runner-ups in six starts. Nothing worse than second place. The last player to start an LPGA season with that kind of consistency was Annika Sorenstam in 2001 — arguably the greatest women's golfer who ever lived.

The historical context only deepens. Korda is now the youngest player since Nancy Lopez in 1980 to reach 18 career LPGA victories. With four more HOF points needed, two more wins this season could put her in the Hall.

This is a historical moment happening in real time — and it's only May.

### STORY 5: Aronimink Deep Dive

**Long-Form Post (6:00 PM ET)**
One week from today, the most historic course in modern major championship golf opens its gates for just the second time.

The PGA Championship begins May 14 at Aronimink Golf Club in Newtown Square, Pennsylvania — and it will be the first time this legendary Donald Ross design has hosted the tournament since Gary Player won there in 1962.

That gap — 64 years — is remarkable. A course that its own architect described as "my masterpiece" has been waiting since the Eisenhower era for its moment back on the biggest stage.

Ross completed Aronimink in 1928. It features what experts consider the most elaborate bunkering of any of his designs — which is saying something, given that Ross is responsible for some of the most revered layouts in golf history. Three of the course's par-3s measure over 210 yards. The greens are undulating and demanding. Errant shots do not get away cheaply.

Scottie Scheffler (+350) is the betting favorite, and it's easy to see why: Aronimink demands the kind of precision ball-striking and course management that defines his game. Rory McIlroy, fresh off back-to-back Masters victories, will be among the top contenders as well.

An estimated 200,000 spectators are expected to attend. For the first time in six decades, the world's best golfers will try to conquer Donald Ross's masterpiece.

Who wins the 2026 PGA Championship at Aronimink? 🏆
