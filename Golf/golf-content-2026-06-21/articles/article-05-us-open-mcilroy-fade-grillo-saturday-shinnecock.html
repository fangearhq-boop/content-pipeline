US Open Moving Day: McIlroy's Back-Nine Collapse, Grillo's 67, and Shinnecock's Cruelty

By Ryan Calloway | Golf Fanrecap | June 21, 2026

Among the most consistent truths in American major championship golf, this stands out: Shinnecock Hills does not give back what it takes. Saturday's third round of the 2026 US Open reinforced that lesson for several players — and elevated one who found something the rest of the field could not.

Wyndham Clark shot even-par 70 and extended his lead to six. But the day's secondary narratives — Rory McIlroy's back-nine collapse and Emiliano Grillo's 67 — deserve their own telling.

McIlroy's Collapse

Rory McIlroy's 2026 US Open will be remembered for what might have been. The 2011 US Open champion — who won his only title in a wire-to-wire blowout at Congressional — arrived at Shinnecock Hills as one of the names most capable of challenging Clark's lead. Through the front nine on Saturday, he looked like a contender: 2-under, moving in the right direction, hitting the kind of ball-striking that makes him one of the most naturally talented players in the sport's history.

Then the back nine at Shinnecock happened. Four bogeys in six holes dismantled the round and left McIlroy eight shots back of Clark entering the final day. It was the kind of collapse that Shinnecock specializes in producing — the course's sloped greens and unforgiving rough punishing even minor ball-striking errors with compounding severity.

McIlroy's patience with his game post-bogey was tested, and Saturday's back nine found those limits. He remains in the field for Sunday's final round, but eight shots is beyond realistic range even for a player of his considerable gifts.

Grillo's Gem

If McIlroy's Saturday was a cautionary tale, Emiliano Grillo's was the day's antidote. The Argentine shot 67 — three under par — in conditions where only two players in the entire field broke par. That kind of scoring on Moving Day at Shinnecock Hills in major championship trim requires exceptional ball-striking, precise distance control to firm greens, and the mental composure to execute when the course is at its most unforgiving.

Grillo's 67 was the low round of the entire day. It moved him meaningfully up the leaderboard and positioned him within striking distance of a final-round charge — not for the title, given Clark's lead, but for a top-five or top-ten finish that would represent a career-defining US Open result.

The Rest of the Picture

Matt Fitzpatrick, the 2022 US Open champion, entered the weekend in contention and has the major pedigree to produce a Sunday worth watching. Xander Schauffele similarly has form at the biggest events. Both are in positions where birdies Sunday would generate leaderboard movement — though catching Clark would require the extraordinary rather than the expected.

Tom Kim, the South Korean star who was one of two players to break par alongside Scheffler on Saturday, also moved up and brings the kind of fearless approach to major championship Sunday that makes him worth tracking in the afternoon coverage.
