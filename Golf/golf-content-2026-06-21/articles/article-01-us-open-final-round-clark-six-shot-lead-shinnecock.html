2026 US Open Final Round: Clark's Six-Shot Lead and the History That Backs Him Up

By Marcus Cole | Golf Fanrecap | June 21, 2026

The 2026 United States Open Championship is one round from completion at Shinnecock Hills Golf Club in Southampton, New York, and everything about today's final round points to a second major championship for Wyndham Clark.

Clark enters Sunday carrying a six-shot advantage over the field — a margin built methodically over three rounds on one of American golf's most punishing courses. His third-round even-par 70 on Moving Day, when only two players in the entire field broke par, extended his lead from four shots to six. The final pairing is set: Clark and Scottie Scheffler go off together at 2:30 PM ET.

The History Behind the Number

Six shots is not just a comfortable lead in golf — it is a historically decisive one. Research across the sport's major championship records finds that 21 players have held a lead of six strokes or more entering a major's final round. Twenty of them went on to win. The only exception is Greg Norman at the 1996 Masters, whose famous collapse against Nick Faldo remains the single most cautionary data point in the game's history of commanding final-round positions.

Clark won the 2023 US Open at Los Angeles Country Club, becoming a major champion for the first time. He understands what this setting demands and what final-round pressure feels like at the year's toughest test. That experience matters at Shinnecock Hills, which has broken the nerve of players who arrived with less dramatic leads than Clark carries today.

Saturday's Vindication

Moving Day at Shinnecock was a battlefield. The course played significantly harder than the opening two rounds, and the scoring landscape reflected it. Only Scheffler and Tom Kim broke par. Clark shot 70 — even par, steady, professional — and the combination of his own consistency and the field's inability to close gaps produced the six-shot buffer he carries into Sunday.

Emiliano Grillo's 67 was the day's low round — a remarkable piece of golf in brutal conditions that moved the Argentine up the leaderboard. Rory McIlroy, who had positioned himself as a credible threat entering the weekend, saw his round unravel on the back nine with four bogeys in six holes. He now trails Clark by eight.

The Course's Voice

Shinnecock Hills is not a passive participant in these final rounds. The Southampton layout — one of the most historic venues in American golf, with five previous US Opens to its name — has a way of making its presence felt when the stakes are at their highest. Wind, firm conditions, and deceptive slope make par a genuine achievement on Sunday afternoon when the course is set up for championship play.

Clark's gameplan for Sunday will prioritize patience above aggression. A six-shot lead doesn't require brilliant golf — it requires error management and the composure to absorb early bogeys without panic. For a player who has navigated these stakes before, the blueprint is clear. Execute, don't overreact, and let the history do its work.

What's Next

Live coverage is available on NBC Sports and Peacock. The trophy presentation is expected late Sunday afternoon. If Clark wins, he joins an exclusive fraternity of two-time US Open champions — and secures his place as one of the defining major winners of the mid-2020s.
