Scottie Scheffler's 30th Birthday: Grand Slam, Father's Day, and Six Shots to Erase at Shinnecock

By Ryan Calloway | Golf Fanrecap | June 21, 2026

There are rounds of golf that carry the weight of a single tournament, and there are rounds that carry the weight of a career. For Scottie Scheffler, Sunday June 21 is the latter.

Today is his 30th birthday. It is Father's Day. And somewhere on the back nine at Shinnecock Hills, with six shots separating him from Wyndham Clark and the one major championship title his career is missing, the world's No. 1 golfer will be given his most dramatic test yet.

The Grand Slam He's Chasing

Scottie Scheffler has already done what most professional golfers spend entire careers failing to accomplish. He has won The Masters. He has won the PGA Championship. He has won The Open Championship. Each of those titles carries a singular distinction in the sport's history.

The US Open is what remains. It is the only major he does not own. Winning it today, on his 30th birthday at Shinnecock Hills, would complete the career Grand Slam — a feat achieved by only five players in the history of men's professional golf: Gene Sarazen, Ben Hogan, Gary Player, Jack Nicklaus, and Tiger Woods. Scheffler's name alongside that list is not a stretch — it is, in fact, the natural continuation of his trajectory.

Saturday's Evidence of What's Possible

Scheffler didn't arrive at Sunday's final round as a passive spectator to Clark's dominance. His third-round 69 was one of just two sub-par rounds in the entire field on Saturday's brutal Moving Day — a demonstration that his ball-striking remains as sharp as anything he's produced this season. The three consecutive birdies he made on holes 14 through 16 at Shinnecock showed the ability to make runs when the course gives an opening.

ESPN summarized the consensus view in their Sunday preview: "If anyone can catch Wyndham Clark at the 2026 US Open, it's Scottie Scheffler." That endorsement is built on what Scheffler has shown all week and what he has done across three previous major championships.

The Math and the Mythology

Six shots is a significant deficit. History says it's nearly insurmountable — only Greg Norman's 1996 Masters collapse provides any blueprint for what would need to happen. For Scheffler to win, he would need not only to play exceptional golf, but to benefit from Clark faltering on a course that has historically rewarded leading players who play conservatively.

That combination — Scheffler playing brilliantly and Clark struggling — is unlikely. But golf's history is populated by unlikely outcomes on days with exactly these kinds of narrative stakes. A 30th birthday. Father's Day. The Grand Slam. These are the ingredients of sporting mythology, not just sports reporting.

Whatever happens at Shinnecock today, Scottie Scheffler will be remembered for chasing it on this particular Sunday.

What's Next

The US Open final round is live today at Shinnecock Hills. The final pairing — Clark and Scheffler — tees off at 2:30 PM ET. Coverage is on NBC Sports and Peacock. Whether Scheffler closes the gap or Clark claims the trophy, this round is already one of the year's most compelling sporting events. If you can watch only one thing in golf today, watch the final pairing from the opening hole.
