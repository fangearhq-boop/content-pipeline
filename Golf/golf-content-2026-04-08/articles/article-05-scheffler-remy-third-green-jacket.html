Scheffler's Third Green Jacket Quest — and the Newborn Son Who Made the Trip to Augusta

By Jake Torres | Golf Fanrecap

April 8, 2026

Scottie Scheffler has a newborn son named Remy who is roughly ten days old. His wife Meredith, described by those around the family as "a trooper," made the trip to Augusta National. Older son Bennett is also in the gallery. And Scheffler himself — the World No. 1, the +500 favorite, the man with two Green Jackets from 2022 and 2024 — is going to walk onto the first tee Thursday afternoon and try to win a third.

That is a lot to carry. Scheffler, by all accounts, carries it well.

The Case for Scheffler

No one in men's professional golf has established the kind of consistency at Augusta that Scheffler has shown in recent years. Two wins in five years — and both of them convincing performances, not lucky runs. His combination of length off the tee, iron precision, and competitive temperament suits Augusta's demands in ways that very few players can match.

He is the No. 1 player in the world, and while that ranking sometimes carries more marketing weight than predictive value, in Scheffler's case it reflects a genuine dominance over the global field. He wins regularly on the PGA Tour, he performs in majors, and he manages the mental game of playing under the weight of expectation better than almost anyone in the current generation.

A third Masters would put him in a rare category. Only a handful of players in the history of the event have won three or more times, and only the legends of the game — Nicklaus, Palmer, Player — have done it multiple times in the modern era alongside Tiger Woods. Scheffler is on a trajectory that, if it continues, will put him in that conversation.

The Concern Worth Noting

Scheffler has had three consecutive PGA Tour starts outside the top 10 — his worst stretch in four years. He skipped the Houston Open to be present for the birth of his son, and that priority is absolutely the right one. But returning from that personal leave and stepping directly into the pressure of defending at Augusta is a genuine adjustment.

Players often respond to extended breaks from competition in one of two ways: either they return refreshed and sharp, or they need a few rounds to rediscover competitive rhythm. Which Scheffler shows up Thursday at 1:44 PM is one of the central questions of Round 1.

What's Next

Scheffler goes out in the afternoon alongside Robert MacIntyre and Gary Woodland. He will have had the benefit of watching the morning scores develop before his first shot of the tournament, which can be a strategic advantage or an additional source of pressure depending on the leaderboard situation.

Remy Scheffler will presumably be somewhere near the gallery ropes, too young to understand what Augusta National is but close enough to a moment that, years from now, people might describe as historic. His father has won here twice before. His father is going to try to do it again with him watching.

That is what sport is for.
