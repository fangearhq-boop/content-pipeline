Scheffler's Week at Doral: What a Comeback From 7 Back Tells Us About the World No. 1

By Jake Torres | May 3, 2026 | Golf Fanrecap

When Scottie Scheffler signed for a first-round 71 at Trump National Doral, seven shots behind Cameron Young's stunning bogey-free 64, the usual questions materialized quickly. Is the world No. 1 simply going through a mid-season flat spot? Is the Blue Monster a course that doesn't suit his game? Has he arrived at a $20 million event without the sharpness required to compete at the top of the field?

Three rounds later, those questions have their answer: none of the above. Scheffler climbed to 9-under through 54 holes, tied second with Si Woo Kim, inside the final group alongside Young for Sunday's fourth round. He didn't win the Cadillac Championship — Cameron Young was categorically the better player this week. But the arc of Scheffler's tournament reveals something worth understanding about how the world's best player responds to adversity.

The Round That Started It

The opening 71 wasn't a collapse. It was an uncharacteristically rough start at a course that punished the three bogeys he made — a combination of back-to-back bogeys on holes 10 and 11 ended his momentum after a promising opening stretch. Scheffler had birdied three of his first five holes, then simply stopped making putts on the back nine. On a day when Young made 8 birdies and no bogeys, the gap accumulated fast.

What Scheffler did in the rounds that followed was exactly what defines elite players: he reset. Not publicly, not dramatically, but functionally. He shot progressively lower numbers to claw his way back from seven shots to six, and he did it while Young continued to play some of the best golf of his career on the same course in the same conditions.

What T2 Means — and Doesn't Mean

Scheffler is not going to win the 2026 Cadillac Championship. Six shots is six shots. Even on a course where low numbers are possible, catching a player of Young's ability and confidence at 15-under in a final round played under the pressure of a $20 million event requires something extraordinary. The math alone makes it improbable.

But being tied second in a field that includes virtually everyone who matters on the PGA Tour — at an event several of the world's top players chose not to attend — is not a bad outcome for a week that started with a stumble. Scheffler has the points, the ranking, and the major championship résumé that mean one tournament finish, good or bad, doesn't define his 2026 season.

The Larger Pattern

Scheffler has won two Masters titles, in 2022 and 2024, and has been the world No. 1 for a sustained period that reflects consistent excellence rather than a few peak results. His weeks don't always follow a smooth trajectory — few players' do — but the through-line of his career has been the kind of baseline level that makes every tournament he enters a potential victory.

What Doral illustrated, for those watching closely, is that a 71 from Scheffler in Round 1 is not a signal that something is wrong. It is a signal that golf remains unpredictable, that even the best players in the world have rounds that don't go according to plan, and that what separates the elite from the good is how they respond to that reality.

What's Next

The PGA Championship is May 14, with Scheffler as the defending Signature Event caliber contender. The Truist Championship — another major Signature Event — arrives before that on the calendar, and Scheffler's Doral week, for all its opening-round complications, should leave him in good competitive shape heading into the spring's most important stretch. Cameron Young may be celebrating the Cadillac Championship title by the end of Sunday morning. Scottie Scheffler will be preparing for what comes next.
