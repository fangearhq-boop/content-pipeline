KPMG Women's PGA 2026: What Hazeltine Demands — and Why Korda Is Still the Favorite

By Ryan Calloway | Golf Fanrecap

Nelly Korda arrives at Hazeltine National Golf Club in Chaska, Minnesota this week having already won two major championships in 2026. A victory at the KPMG Women's PGA Championship would make her only the second woman in history to win three consecutive major titles in a single season. The last player to accomplish it was Inbee Park in 2013.

But Hazeltine does not give anything away. Understanding what this course demands — and how Korda's game lines up against those demands — matters before Thursday's first round.

What Hazeltine Requires

Hazeltine National is not a typical LPGA venue. The course stretches beyond 6,800 yards, longer than most stops on the women's tour, and it applies pressure through length and terrain rather than tight pin positions alone. Long iron play from 180 to 220 yards is tested repeatedly throughout the round. The course has an enclosed, traditional feel — more reminiscent of a men's major venue than the open, pastoral layouts that dominate the LPGA calendar.

In 2019, when the KPMG Women's PGA last visited Hazeltine, the course forced immediate adjustments. Players who could not commit to long irons found themselves falling off the pace quickly. Hannah Green won that year, becoming the first Australian to win a women's major in 22 years.

Korda finished T-3 in 2019. She knows this course and has competed well here before her current peak form.

Why Korda Is Still the Favorite

Korda won the 2026 Chevron Championship by five shots. She won the 2026 US Women's Open by one shot in a back-nine pressure test. Four wins in nine starts this season. The range of winning margins across different pressure situations speaks to the versatility her game has developed in recent years.

Her long iron play has improved significantly since her 2019 KPMG result, and her distance off the tee has increased as well. Both are relevant at Hazeltine. She is also playing with a different level of mental confidence than she had seven years ago — having already built the kind of sustained excellence that removes doubt from the decision-making process on the course.

The Competition

Atthaya Thitikul is the biggest threat. The world No. 2 player from Thailand has the power to handle Hazeltine's length and the technical precision to compete with Korda over four rounds. Thitikul is still seeking her first major championship, which adds a layer of motivation to a player who has spent two years knocking on the door at major events.

Minjee Lee, the defending champion who won the 2025 KPMG Women's PGA, brings course-specific knowledge. Defending at a major is never simple, but Lee has the consistent ball striking to keep herself in contention over the week.

Lydia Ko and Hannah Green — the 2019 Hazeltine champion — round out the list of players with the game and experience to contend.

What's Next

Round 1 begins Thursday morning at Hazeltine National Golf Club. The $13 million purse is the largest in women's golf history. Television coverage is available on Golf Channel and Peacock throughout the week. If Korda wins, she will be two major championships away from completing the career Grand Slam while simultaneously advancing to 26 of 27 required LPGA Hall of Fame points — one win away from eligibility. This week matters beyond the trophy.
