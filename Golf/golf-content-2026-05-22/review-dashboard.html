And the most significant voices to weigh in may be the most significant voices in the sport's history. Jack Nicklaus — winner of 18 major championships, the most of any player ever — praised Rai's composure and performance in the days after the victory. Nick Faldo — six-time major champion and widely considered one of the greatest ball-strikers of his era — added his own words of admiration. That kind of recognition places Rai's win in a specific historical lineage. When the two men whose names define major championship excellence acknowledge what you achieved, it carries a weight that no highlight package or statistic can match. What Rai did was extraordinary by any measure. He was ranked 44th in the world entering the week. He won at 290-to-1 odds — the definition of an upset. He was the first Englishman to win the PGA Championship since Jim Barnes won the first two editions of the tournament in 1916 and 1919. He was the first player of Indian heritage to win a men's major championship. The 70-foot birdie putt at the 17th hole that sealed it. The eagle at the 9th. The final-round 65. All of it happening at Aronimink, in front of a crowd that had arrived expecting someone else to win. Nicklaus and Faldo noticed. Golf noticed. The reverberations of what Aaron Rai achieved on May 17 will echo for years. ### Image Post Caption Jack Nicklaus and Nick Faldo praised Aaron Rai this week. ### Long-Form Post There is a comparison being made in women's golf right now that you do not make lightly. Analysts are invoking Annika Sorenstam's 2001 season — arguably the most dominant performance by any player in the history of the LPGA Tour. And it applies to Nelly Korda in 2026. In six starts this season, Korda has won three times and finished second three times. Six starts. Zero finishes outside the top two. The only player who achieved six consecutive top-2 finishes to begin a season was Sorenstam in 2001. Her wins include the Chevron Championship — her third career major, which returned her to the world No. 1 ranking — and two subsequent victories, including her win at the Riviera Maya Open at Mayakoba by four strokes. She became the first player since Lorena Ochoa in 2008 to win an LPGA tournament by four strokes or more in back-to-back weeks. The statistical picture is similarly remarkable. Her bogey-or-worse rate of 7.97% is not just the lowest on the LPGA Tour — it's more than four full percentage points better than anyone else in the field. She is getting up-and-down at a 75.0% clip, also the best on tour. At 27 years old, she became the youngest American to reach 18 career LPGA victories in 46 years — since Nancy Lopez did it in 1980. The US Women's Open is scheduled for June 4-7 at Riviera Country Club in Pacific Palisades, California. Korda arrives as the overwhelming favorite and the form player of the decade. The only question is whether anyone in the world can disrupt what she's doing right now. ### Image Post Caption 3 wins. 3 runner-ups. 6 starts. World No. 1. Major champion.
