Tommy Fleetwood's Moment: The Open Championship Comes Home to Southport's Favorite Son

By Jake Torres | July 15, 2026

The world No. 3 grew up minutes from Royal Birkdale's first tee, skipped the Scottish Open to prepare here specifically, and has his father on the bag. This is the week his career has been building toward.

Golf does not often hand you a story this clean.

Tommy Fleetwood grew up in Southport. Royal Birkdale Golf Club is in Southport. The 154th Open Championship, which begins Thursday, is being played at Royal Birkdale. The fairways where the world's best golfers will compete this week are the same ones that Fleetwood would have been able to reach on his bicycle when he was a teenager learning the game. His father, Andy Fleetwood, is caddying for him — walking the same sand dunes that Tommy grew up studying, now with his son competing for the most significant prize in golf.

There are weeks in professional golf where the backstory overwhelms the performance. This is one of them — and Fleetwood hasn't even hit a shot yet.

The Best Player Without a Major

That phrase has become the most persistent description attached to Fleetwood's career. He is the world No. 3. He has won extensively on the European Tour and the DP World Tour. He has contended in major championships. He has produced performances in Ryder Cup singles matches that rank among the most electrifying moments in that tournament's modern history.

He has not won a major. Every great player who hasn't is described as the best without one. The ones who eventually break through are remembered differently. The ones who don't carry the phrase like a mark that never quite fades.

Fleetwood has handled this gracefully for years. He has not let the label become a burden visible in his game. He has shown up at major championships and competed, repeatedly, without the kind of collapse that comes from a player pressing too hard. The form heading into Birkdale reflects that: he has not finished worse than 14th in any of his last five events. He skipped the Scottish Open specifically to spend additional preparation time at Royal Birkdale — a choice that communicates exactly how seriously he is treating this week.

England Is Watching

Nick Faldo won The Open Championship for the last time in 1992. The gap between that victory and the present is 34 years — an absence from the Claret Jug that has become one of the recurring storylines in British golf every time The Open comes to an English course. Fleetwood is the most plausible English candidate to end that drought that the sport has produced in recent memory.

He knows the course. He knows the town. He has the game to compete on a links that rewards local knowledge, patience, and creativity — all areas where Fleetwood is gifted. The wind off the Irish Sea that makes Royal Birkdale one of the most demanding venues in major golf is a wind that Fleetwood has played in since childhood.

That doesn't guarantee anything. Golf resists narratives. The Claret Jug goes to the player who executes best across four days, not to the player with the best story. But this week, the best story and one of the most capable players on tour happen to be the same person.

What's Next

Fleetwood tees off Thursday morning in the first round of the 154th Open Championship. By Sunday, the golf world will know whether the story ends with a triumphant finish or another close call. Everything that has happened in his career — the near-misses, the resilience, the accumulated credibility as a great player who has simply not found the right Sunday in the right major — leads to this week at this course in this town. He could not have written a better setup if he tried.
