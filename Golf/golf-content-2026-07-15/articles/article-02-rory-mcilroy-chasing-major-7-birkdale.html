Rory McIlroy Is Chasing Major No. 7 at Royal Birkdale — and the Historical Stakes Could Not Be Higher

By Ryan Calloway | July 15, 2026

With six majors and a chance to set the European record outright, McIlroy arrives at The Open on some of the best form of his career and carrying one of the most compelling storylines in the sport's history.

Rory McIlroy has done almost everything there is to do in professional golf. The career Grand Slam. Back-to-back Masters titles. More than 20 PGA Tour victories and an equal number internationally. Six major championships.

One more would be something unprecedented.

A seventh major championship at Royal Birkdale this week would make McIlroy the European man with the most major victories in the history of golf — more than Nick Faldo, with whom he currently shares the record at six. That number, and what it would represent, is the historical frame through which the entire 154th Open Championship will be viewed for McIlroy's many millions of fans around the world.

The Road to Six

McIlroy's sixth major arrived in April 2026 when he won the Masters for the second consecutive year — holding off Scottie Scheffler by one stroke at Augusta National with a final-round 71. It was a performance defined by composure under maximum pressure, the kind that a player can only summon after winning enough times to trust themselves when everything is on the line.

That back-to-back Masters run, which made him the first player to accomplish the feat since Tiger Woods in 2001 and 2002, completed a full career narrative that had been building for more than a decade. He had the US Open, the Open Championship, and two PGA Championships. The Masters had been the missing piece. He filled it in twice in succession.

Coming to Birkdale, McIlroy is in a different place mentally than he was when he spent years wondering if Augusta would ever give him what he wanted. The hunger is still there — six majors is not a destination, it's a milestone — but the anxiety that once complicated his Augusta performances has been replaced by something that looks very much like confidence.

Why Royal Birkdale and Why Now

McIlroy last won The Open in 2014 at Royal Liverpool — twelve years ago. In the interim, he has contended and he has gone cold, and the Claret Jug has changed hands without him lifting it multiple times. The gap between his last Open win and this week is long enough that the questions about whether he can reclaim the form that made him so dominant on links courses in his early career are legitimate.

The answer, based on his Scottish Open T7 and the trajectory of his entire 2026 season, is that he can. He has demonstrated the complete game that links golf demands — ball control in the wind, creativity around bump-and-run approaches, putting under pressure. Royal Birkdale is a par-70 layout that rewards patience and punishes impatience. Those happen to be the conditions under which McIlroy has historically been most dangerous.

What's Next

The 154th Open Championship begins Thursday. A McIlroy victory would generate more coverage, more discussion, and more analysis than almost anything else that could happen in golf in 2026. That kind of moment rarely announces itself in advance. But the ingredients are present — the player, the form, the historical stakes, and the right kind of course.

The Claret Jug sits in Southport. History is at stake. Thursday morning cannot come soon enough.
