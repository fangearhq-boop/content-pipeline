### STORY 2: McIlroy Chasing Major No. 7

#### Long-Form Post — 10:30 AM ET

Rory McIlroy is six holes, six rounds, or six strokes from something nobody has done before.

A seventh major championship would give McIlroy more major victories than any European man in the history of professional golf — breaking the record outright rather than continuing to share it. He currently stands at six, tied in the historical record books with Nick Faldo.

The path to this moment has been extraordinary. McIlroy won back-to-back Masters titles in 2025 and 2026 — the first player to do so since Tiger Woods in 2001 and 2002. He completed his career Grand Slam with his first Masters victory in 2025. The Open Championship has been his best major historically, and he last won it in 2014 — a gap of twelve years that this week's tournament could close in a way that changes how his legacy is discussed for decades.

He is playing some of the best golf of his career. He arrived at Birkdale after a T7 at the Scottish Open, which is the kind of solid-but-not-spectacular result that tends to precede great major performances — enough competitive reps without burning peak energy on a tune-up event.

At +850, he is the second choice in the market. He is among the most experienced, most accomplished players in the field. And he is one of those rare golfers who appears to rise when the moment is biggest.

If Rory McIlroy wins this week, it's one of golf's greatest career achievements. What would it mean for his legacy?

Golf doesn't often hand you a story like this. When it does, you pay attention.

Tommy Fleetwood grew up in Southport, the coastal town in Merseyside, England, where Royal Birkdale sits. The driving range where he first learned the game is not far from where he'll tee off Thursday in the first round of the 154th Open Championship. His father, Andy Fleetwood, is caddying — the ultimate family-affair element to an already deeply personal week.

Fleetwood is ranked No. 3 in the world. He is widely regarded as the best player on tour without a major title — a description that has attached itself to him for years and that he has carried with more grace than most could manage. He has been close. He has contended. He has not broken through.

This week is different from every other week in one specific way: the course is his home. He skipped the Scottish Open specifically to spend additional preparation time at Royal Birkdale. He knows the breaks on those greens, the way the wind funnels through the dunes on the 17th hole, which way the par-3s play when the conditions shift. That local knowledge is not insignificant on a links course, where course management is the difference between challenging and impossible.

No English-born player has won The Open since Nick Faldo in 1992 — a 34-year gap that has become something of an obsession in British golf media. Fleetwood is the most plausible candidate to end it that golf has produced in years. He has the game, the form (he has not finished outside the top 14 in any of his last five events), and now the course.

The question is whether he can handle the weight of what it would mean.

### STORY 4: Haeran Ryu's Historic Evian

#### Long-Form Post — 6:00 PM ET

It started with a 60.

In the third round of the Amundi Evian Championship on July 11 in France, South Korea's Haeran Ryu carded an 11-under 60 at the Evian Resort Golf Club — the lowest single-round score in the history of LPGA major championships. That kind of performance in a major doesn't just win tournaments; it announces a player as something different from the rest of the field.

Then she had to back it up.

Ryu went into Sunday at -19 and spent the final round fending off Canada's Brooke Henderson, who matched her score and forced a playoff. In sudden death, Ryu birdied the first hole to claim the Evian Championship and $1.4 million — her second LPGA major in three weeks, following a win at the KPMG Women's PGA Championship.

Back-to-back LPGA majors. The 60 in Round 3. The playoff. This is one of the most dominant stretches of golf played by any player — in any tour — in recent memory.

The LPGA's summer has found its defining player. Whether Haeran Ryu can continue this form into the AIG Women's Open at Lytham St. Annes on July 30 will be one of the most closely watched storylines in women's golf for the rest of the summer.

### STORY 5: Tom Kim's Drought Is Over

#### Long-Form Post — 8:30 PM ET

Nearly three years. That's how long Tom Kim had gone without winning on the PGA Tour when he stood on the 18th fairway at The Renaissance Club last Sunday with a chance to close out the Genesis Scottish Open.

He delivered. A bogey-free final round of 64, finishing at -17, two strokes clear of Min Woo Lee. The drought ended at the best possible time — the week before The Open Championship, at a co-sanctioned event that put the entire golf world's attention on Scotland.

Kim had not won since the fall of 2023. At 23 years old, that kind of winless stretch generates questions about whether a player's early promise was a mirage. Kim's answer was a final round with no bogeys, a series of clutch putts when the leaderboard was close, and a tournament performance that put him $1,575,000 richer and back among the winners.

Reports that Tiger Woods reached out to Kim to congratulate him after the victory added another layer to a week that was already meaningful for the young South Korean. When Tiger texts you after a win, it tends to mean something.

Whether Kim carries momentum from North Berwick to Royal Birkdale and into the field for The Open this week remains to be seen. But the win removed a weight that had been on him for nearly three years — and that is its own kind of advantage.
