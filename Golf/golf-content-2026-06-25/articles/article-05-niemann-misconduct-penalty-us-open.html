Club-Throw, 2-Stroke Penalty, Then a 65: Niemann's Wild U.S. Open Story

By Marcus Cole June 25, 2026

What Happened on Hole 6

Joaquin Niemann made a quadruple bogey on the sixth hole during Round 1 of the 2026 U.S. Open at Shinnecock Hills. What happened immediately after is what the incident is about: Niemann threw his club — it traveled approximately 50 yards. The USGA official on the scene applied Rule 1.2b and assessed a two-stroke misconduct penalty on the spot. It was the first such penalty assessed at a major championship in the 2026 season.

What Rule 1.2b Actually Says

Rule 1.2b is the sportsmanship provision in the Rules of Golf. It addresses conduct that is intentionally reckless or endangers the safety of others, or conduct that brings the game into disrepute. It is rarely invoked. The standard accidental snap of a club over a knee, or a club thrown in frustration that stays near the player's feet, typically does not rise to the rule's threshold. A club thrown 50 yards in a deliberate act of frustration, in the judgment of the officiating committee, did.

The two-stroke addition is significant in U.S. Open terms, where scoring is compressed and a single shot can determine whether a player makes or misses the cut, or falls in or out of contention. For Niemann, the penalty compounded what was already a difficult hole into something considerably worse.

The 65 That Followed

What makes the story something other than a cautionary tale is what Niemann did next. He came out in Round 2 at Shinnecock and shot 65 — one of the lowest rounds of the week. He made the cut. The penalty did not follow him into the weekend in any practical sense; it cost him two strokes in Round 1 and then stopped mattering.

The contrast between the Round 1 incident and the Round 2 response says something about Niemann's competitive resilience. Players who absorb a misconduct penalty in the opening round of a U.S. Open and then post a 65 the next morning are not players suffering from a loss of mental composure. Whatever the frustration level was on hole 6 of Round 1, he cleared it entirely by the time he reached the first tee the following morning.

What's Next

The Niemann incident will be referenced as part of the 2026 U.S. Open's record — a footnote in how the event was reported. Whether it prompts any discussion at Tour level about conduct standards, or whether it settles into the historical record as an isolated incident handled correctly in the moment, will be determined by how frequently the conversation resurfaces. For now, Niemann made the cut, played through the week, and Wyndham Clark won the tournament. The penalty mattered in the moment and not much beyond it.
