LIV Golf's Louisiana Cancellation Raises Fresh Questions About the Tour's 2026 Direction

By Ryan Calloway | Golf Fanrecap | June 20, 2026

LIV Golf was supposed to be in New Orleans next week. The Louisiana event at City Park, scheduled for June 25-28, had been part of the 2026 calendar since the season was announced. Now it's gone — cancelled, postponed, removed from the schedule — and the circumstances around its departure add to a growing list of questions about where LIV Golf goes from here.

Louisiana paid $3.2 million to host the event under the terms of the contract. LIV Golf is returning most of that money, but approximately $2 million invested in City Park infrastructure upgrades will not be refunded — the improvements were made, the work is done, and LIV considers that portion spent. It's an unusual arrangement that leaves Louisiana officials absorbing costs for an event that never happened.

The Official Reason — and the Questions Behind It

The publicly cited reason for the cancellation is a scheduling conflict with the FIFA World Cup, which dominates the global sports calendar in late June. International audiences are watching soccer, advertisers are focused elsewhere, and the media coverage that LIV depends on to build its profile would be diminished in a World Cup window.

That explanation makes surface-level sense. But the more troubling element was LIV CEO Scott O'Neil's response this week when asked about the stability of remaining 2026 tour stops. Rather than giving a clear answer — "yes, the remaining events are secure" — O'Neil gave what multiple outlets described as an unclear, non-committal response. That's the kind of language that invites speculation about structural issues beyond scheduling conflicts.

Where the Tour Stands Competitively

Setting aside the structural questions, LIV's 2026 competitive season has been compelling. Jon Rahm leads the individual standings with 928.58 points — a significant margin over Bryson DeChambeau in second (644.40) and Joaquin Niemann in third (344.66). Tyrrell Hatton, who won the Andalucía event in early June at 11-under par, sits fourth at 332.93 points.

Rahm's dominance is the season's primary storyline in LIV. He has performed at the level that justified his move from the PGA Tour — whatever criticism accompanied that decision has been answered by the scoreboard. The competitive product is legitimate.

The strategic product — the business model, the broadcast deals, the stability of the schedule — is where the questions remain.

The Broader Context

LIV Golf entered 2026 after a period of significant turbulence: fractured negotiations with the PGA Tour, executive leadership changes, and an ongoing debate about its long-term viability. The 72-hole format with 57 players and no cut represents an evolution from the 54-hole model of previous seasons, an adjustment designed to make the events feel more like traditional golf tournaments.

Those changes were real improvements. But a cancelled event — regardless of the reason — reinforces the perception that LIV's calendar is less stable than its rivals. The PGA Tour cancelled events for the wrong reasons once, and it's a scar the organization still carries. LIV can't afford to accumulate similar perceptions if it wants major sponsors and broadcasters to commit long-term.

What's Next

LIV has indicated the Louisiana event will be rescheduled, potentially in the fall. Whether that materializes depends on negotiations with New Orleans officials, who now know the financial exposure of hosting an event that can be cancelled six days before it begins.

The remaining 2026 tour stops are officially on the calendar. Whether they stay there is a question that gets harder to wave away as the season progresses.
