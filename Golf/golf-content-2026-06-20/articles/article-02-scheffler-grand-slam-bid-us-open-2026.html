The Missing Major: Scottie Scheffler's Grand Slam Bid at Shinnecock Hills

By Jake Torres | Golf Fanrecap | June 20, 2026

The career Grand Slam belongs to five men: Gene Sarazen, Ben Hogan, Gary Player, Jack Nicklaus, and Tiger Woods. Scottie Scheffler is trying to add his name to that list. He has three of the four majors. The US Open is the one still missing from his résumé. And this week, the US Open is being played at Shinnecock Hills — one of the most demanding major championship venues in the world.

Through 36 holes, Scheffler sits at approximately one over par, eight shots behind leader Wyndham Clark. The math is difficult. Shinnecock is not a course that generates multiple 64s from the same player in consecutive rounds. But Scheffler is in the field, he made the cut, and he has Sunday left to play. At this point in his career, that's enough to hold the attention of everyone watching golf this weekend.

What He's Already Done

The case for Scheffler's place among the greatest players of his generation is not complicated. He won the Masters — the most prestigious major in golf — and confirmed it by winning it again. He captured the PGA Championship. He won The Open Championship at Royal Troon, hoisting the Claret Jug in the rain in a way that felt simultaneously inevitable and remarkable.

Three majors by age 30 puts Scheffler in a rarified category. The only major he hasn't won is the one whose scheduling has never quite aligned with his peak weeks this decade. The US Open, played on courses specifically selected for their difficulty and their capacity to expose weaknesses, has tested a game that doesn't have many.

How He Talks About It

One of the more interesting dimensions of Scheffler's pursuit is how carefully he avoids framing it as a pursuit. Before the tournament, he said: "The Grand Slam has never been a motivating factor for me. I always just wanted to be the best version of myself. That got me this far."

It's a statement that sounds like something a coach told him to believe — and also something he genuinely means. Scheffler's career has been built on process, on shot-by-shot focus, on removing himself from the noise of what a win would mean. The players who have completed the Grand Slam — Nicklaus, Tiger, Player — all understood that the only way to close the circle is to not think about the circle.

He's not thinking about the Grand Slam at Shinnecock Hills. He's thinking about getting the ball in the hole.

What Would Need to Happen

The gap to Clark entering Round 3 is eight shots. For Scheffler to win, he'd need roughly a four-shot swing per round — either Clark collapsing while Scheffler goes low, or a combination of factors that reshapes the leaderboard by Sunday afternoon. It happens in golf. It happened to Phil Mickelson at Winged Foot. It has happened at Shinnecock before.

The more likely outcome is that Scheffler finishes in the top 10, earns a strong result in the third most prestigious major, and returns to the US Open next year with the same mission. But golf's history is full of players who won championships by staying patient and playing within themselves while the leaders above them made mistakes on the hardest day of the week.

What's Next

Round 3 is underway. Round 4 is Sunday. If by some sequence of events Scheffler is within striking distance on Sunday afternoon, the magnitude of the moment will be impossible to ignore — Grand Slam or not.

Shinnecock Hills has hosted six US Opens. It has produced some of golf's most memorable moments. There is time left for one more.
