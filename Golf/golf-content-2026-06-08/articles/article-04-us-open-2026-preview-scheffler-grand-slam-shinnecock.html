Ten Days to the U.S. Open: Scheffler's Grand Slam Bid, McIlroy's Momentum, and Shinnecock Hills

By Jake Torres | June 8, 2026 | Golf Fanrecap

The 126th U.S. Open begins June 18 at Shinnecock Hills Golf Club in Southampton, New York. Ten days away. And the storylines heading in are as rich as any major in recent memory.

Scottie Scheffler is chasing history. Rory McIlroy is chasing another title freed from his greatest burden. And Shinnecock Hills — a course that has humbled champions and celebrated underdogs across five previous U.S. Opens — is waiting for both of them.

Scheffler's Grand Slam on the Line

Scottie Scheffler has won the Masters twice, the PGA Championship, and the Open Championship. The U.S. Open is the only major championship he has not claimed. A victory at Shinnecock Hills would complete the career Grand Slam — making him only the seventh golfer in history to win all four major titles.

The symmetry of the moment goes further. The final round of the 2026 U.S. Open is scheduled for Sunday, June 21. That is Scheffler's 30th birthday. It is also Father's Day. If Scheffler is contending on Sunday afternoon at Shinnecock, the narrative will be inescapable: the world's No. 1 player, turning 30, celebrating Father's Day, potentially completing the Grand Slam on national television.

Scheffler visited Shinnecock Hills earlier this week for a scouting round. He said the course largely matched his expectations, though he was "a little surprised" at the width of the fairways. The fairways at Shinnecock will average 48 yards wide in 2026 — significantly more generous than previous U.S. Opens at the venue. Scheffler noted, however, that the generous fairways do not reduce the challenge: "Once you start missing fairways you have no chance," he said, "but the green complexes are extraordinarily difficult and allow them to put pins wherever they want."

McIlroy's New Freedom

For years, Rory McIlroy carried the Masters as a weight. He had won every other major, but the green jacket eluded him in the most visible way possible — late Sunday collapses, agonizing near-misses, the burden of expectations at Augusta National compounding with each passing year. It defined his narrative whether he wanted it to or not.

McIlroy won the Masters in 2026. The burden is gone.

Many analysts believe this is the version of McIlroy that is most dangerous — the one who shows up to every major without a specific narrative haunting him. He, too, scouted Shinnecock Hills and described the course in terms consistent with Scheffler's observations: wide fairways, punishing greens, the kind of setup that rewards complete ball-strikers who can scramble when needed.

The Course and the History

Shinnecock Hills is hosting the U.S. Open for the sixth time. Previous champions at this course include Ray Floyd in 1986, Corey Pavin in 1995, Retief Goosen in 2004, and Brooks Koepka in 2018. The most recent Shinnecock U.S. Open — 2018 — was defined by setup controversy as the USGA pushed the course to near-unplayable conditions on Sunday before backing off. The 2026 edition is the first U.S. Open at Shinnecock on William Flynn's original 1930s design without modifications, and the USGA has indicated a different approach to Sunday setup.

The USGA received 10,201 entries for the 2026 championship — nearly an all-time record. J.J. Spaun is the defending champion. Every player in the top 50 of the Official World Golf Ranking is exempt.

What's Next

The U.S. Open begins Thursday, June 18 at Shinnecock Hills Golf Club in Southampton, New York. Final round is Sunday, June 21. The field is the strongest in major golf — nearly 10,200 entries filtered to 156 starters. Scheffler, McIlroy, Poston (fresh off the Memorial win), and the full world ranking will compete for the national title and a slice of golf history.

Ten days out. The preparation is done. What remains is the golf itself — four rounds at one of the sport's most demanding venues, with the sport's most prestigious individual championship at stake.
