Nelly Korda and the Short List: What Three Consecutive LPGA Majors Would Mean

By Ryan Calloway

June 27, 2026

Golf Fanrecap

The list has two names on it. Babe Zaharias in 1950. Inbee Park in 2013. Those are the only players in LPGA history to win three consecutive major championships in a single calendar year. Nelly Korda is six shots back of the KPMG Women's PGA Championship lead entering Round 3 on Saturday, and she is trying to join them.

To understand what that would mean, it helps to understand who is already on the list.

Babe Zaharias, 1950

Mildred "Babe" Didrikson Zaharias is one of the greatest athletes the United States has produced. An Olympic gold medalist in track and field who pivoted to golf in her 30s, Zaharias became a dominant force in the women's game at a time when women's professional golf was still establishing itself as a legitimate competitive circuit.

In 1950, she won the Titleholders Championship, the U.S. Women's Open, and the Western Open — three of the most prestigious events available to women golfers at the time — in succession. The feat was accomplished against a backdrop where the tour itself was still being built and the calendar of available majors was different from what it would become in the decades that followed. That does not diminish the achievement. Zaharias was so far above her contemporaries in that stretch that the records she set have endured into an era of globalized professional golf.

Inbee Park, 2013

Inbee Park's 2013 major season is the modern benchmark for sustained major championship excellence in women's golf. The South Korean star won the Kraft Nabisco Championship, the LPGA Championship, and the U.S. Women's Open in succession — all three of the first three LPGA major championships of the season — before finishing the year as the world No. 1.

Park's game in 2013 was defined by a putting precision that made her nearly unbeatable under major championship pressure. She became only the second player in LPGA history to win three consecutive majors in a year and did it on courses that demanded different skills and exposed different weaknesses. The Kraft Nabisco is a target course in the California desert. The LPGA Championship of that era was a parkland test. The U.S. Women's Open is the most demanding examination of complete golf the women's game produces. She won all three.

Nelly Korda, 2026

Korda won the 2026 Chevron Championship — the rebranded version of the Kraft Nabisco, still held in the California desert, still ending with the tradition of the champion walking (or jumping) into the pond surrounding the 18th green. She won the 2026 U.S. Women's Open to give herself a second consecutive major. She arrived at Hazeltine National for the KPMG Women's PGA Championship with an opportunity to complete the first half of a historic three-major calendar year.

The season context makes the chase even more remarkable. Korda has four wins and three runner-up finishes in her first eight LPGA Tour starts of 2026. The runner-up results are not failures — they are evidence of sustained excellence across a full season that would stand as remarkable even without the major victories. She has been the best player in women's golf, and it has not been particularly close.

A Round 1 double bogey on hole 16 at Hazeltine put her in a difficult position early. She recovered to -6 through Round 2, which is a credit to her composure, but it leaves her six shots behind Ina Yoon's -12 lead entering the weekend. Six shots in two rounds is not a trajectory — it is a challenge that requires both a surge from Korda and some attrition from the leaders.

The Hall of Fame Dimension

Korda's KPMG chase carries a second layer of stakes that has nothing to do with calendar-year major streaks. She is at 25 of the 27 points required for LPGA Hall of Fame qualification. A major championship is worth 2 points. A win at Hazeltine does not just add to the legacy — it meets the threshold for one of the most exclusive honors in the sport.

LPGA Hall of Fame membership is genuinely difficult to achieve. The points system is designed to require sustained excellence across a full career, not a single dominant season. The fact that Korda would qualify at a relatively young age — her 2026 dominance accelerated a timeline that would have stretched longer under normal circumstances — speaks to the concentration of achievement she has packed into her most recent seasons.

What's Next

Rounds 3 and 4 at Hazeltine National will determine whether the list has two names on it or three. Round 3 is Saturday. Round 4 is Sunday, June 28. Korda has the game to do it. Yoon has the lead that prevents it, for now. The record has stood since 2013. Whether it stands through this weekend is the question the next 36 holes will answer.
