LIV Golf Status Report: Merger Talks, Norman's Shrug, and the 72-Hole Pivot

By Marcus Cole

June 27, 2026

Golf Fanrecap

LIV Golf's 2026 situation keeps producing new angles. The league's CEO and the PGA Tour's CEO have been talking. The man who built LIV says those talks do not matter. And the tour itself is switching from 54-hole to 72-hole events in a bid to finally secure Official World Golf Ranking points — the missing piece that has kept LIV players off the world rankings since the tour launched.

Three separate developments, three different directions. Welcome to another week in the ongoing story of professional golf's fractured landscape.

O'Neil and Rolapp: "Opportunity to Come Together"

LIV Golf CEO Scott O'Neil and PGA Tour CEO Brian Rolapp have held discussions, with O'Neil characterizing the exchange as an "opportunity to come together." The framing is deliberately vague — "opportunity to come together" is the language of early-stage conversations that might lead somewhere or might not — and no timeline or specific terms have been attached to what the talks involve.

The structural problem with any LIV-PGA Tour framework remains unchanged: what do both sides actually want? The PGA Tour's television agreements, its player contracts, and its sponsor relationships are built around a competitive ecosystem that LIV disrupted. LIV's Saudi Arabian backing gives it financial staying power that the PGA Tour cannot match outright. Any arrangement that satisfies both would require concessions that neither has historically been willing to make publicly.

O'Neil's characterization is nevertheless notable. His predecessor's relationship with the PGA Tour was adversarial from the first day. A CEO who frames conversations as opportunities rather than battles represents at minimum a different tone, if not a different direction.

Greg Norman: "Doesn't Matter Anymore"

For a counterpoint, there was Greg Norman, LIV's founder and the figure most associated with its aggressive launch into the professional golf landscape. Norman's assessment of the merger conversation: it "doesn't matter anymore." His argument is that both the PGA Tour and LIV Golf have now established their independent footing, making a unification less necessary than it might have seemed when the two were in direct conflict.

Norman went further, noting that LIV's future depends on Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund continuing to back the league beyond 2026 — an honest acknowledgment of the financial realities that remain the central question about LIV's long-term viability. PIF has invested more than $5 billion in the project. The returns, in terms of commercial revenue and sponsorship, have not matched the investment pace.

That Norman would acknowledge the PIF dependency so plainly suggests either confidence that the funding will continue or an acceptance that the uncertainty is no longer a secret worth protecting.

The 72-Hole Pivot

The most operationally significant LIV development this week is the announcement of a format change from 54-hole to 72-hole tournaments. The motivation is clear: Official World Golf Ranking points require 72 holes of stroke play under recognized conditions. LIV's 54-hole shotgun-start format has been ineligible for OWGR recognition since its launch, which has meant LIV players who do not have existing exemptions from major championships have seen their world rankings erode.

For players like Bryson DeChambeau and Joaquin Niemann — who left the PGA Tour for LIV with established world rankings — the ranking decline creates a competitive credential problem that extends beyond any single tour's business arrangements. A 72-hole LIV that qualifies for OWGR points would restore that credential, or at least stop the erosion.

The operational challenges of switching to 72 holes are real. LIV events have been structured around a three-round shotgun format with team-based entertainment elements. A 72-hole format requires a different scheduling architecture and changes the television product that LIV's broadcast partners have built expectations around. How the transition affects those contracts and the overall product remains to be worked through.

What's Next

LIV's Louisiana event has been cancelled, and the remaining four events on the 2026 schedule represent the league's near-term priority — completing a season while the format transition, funding questions, and merger discussions develop in parallel. The $250-350 million in outside investment that O'Neil is seeking would provide a runway independent of PIF's continued participation and give LIV the financial cushion to navigate a transition toward 72-hole formats and OWGR eligibility.

Whether the merger conversations with Rolapp lead anywhere, whether Norman's dismissal proves accurate, and whether the 72-hole format change produces the OWGR access LIV needs — those are the three threads that will define professional golf's off-course story across the rest of 2026.
