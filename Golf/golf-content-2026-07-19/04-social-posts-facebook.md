### STORY 2: Ryan Fox Historic 62 in R3

#### Long-Form Post — 10:30 AM ET

The 154th Open Championship has already produced two rounds of 62 — one from Lucas Herbert, one from Sam Burns. On Saturday, Ryan Fox added a third.

Fox, a New Zealander competing on the DP World Tour, went around Royal Birkdale in 62 strokes in Round 3, becoming the 8th golfer in history to shoot 62 in a men's major championship. He also became the third player to achieve the feat at this single Open — a statistical coincidence that is almost unprecedented in major golf history.

The round was a display of sustained precision. Fox went out in 5-under on Birkdale's front nine, traded a bogey on 12 for birdies on 13 and 14, and closed with birdies on two of his final three holes. He started the third round at even par — well outside realistic contention — and ended it at -8, tied for second place entering the final round.

The historical context: only seven players before Fox had shot 62 in a major. The major scoring record is 62. Three of the players who have ever matched that number did so at the same tournament, in the same week, at Royal Birkdale.

Fox enters Sunday's final round as the most dangerous challenger to Burns. He has momentum, a hot putter, and absolutely nothing to lose.

### STORY 5: LIV Golf Funding Crisis

#### Long-Form Post — 8:30 PM ET

While Lucas Herbert and Bryson DeChambeau are competing for the Claret Jug at Royal Birkdale, the tour they play for is fighting for its survival.

Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund — the government-backed entity that founded and financed LIV Golf — is ending its financial backing of the tour after the 2026 season. Four LIV events remain on this year's calendar. The tour's future beyond 2026 is, by multiple accounts, "still up in the air."

LIV Golf was launched in 2022 with an enormous injection of Saudi capital, promising a new era of professional golf through a different format, massive guaranteed contracts, and an alternative to the established PGA and DP World Tour ecosystem. The disruption it caused — splitting the professional ranks, generating legal battles, and forcing a formal framework agreement between LIV and the PGA Tour — reshaped the sport in fundamental ways.

But the funding model required continued PIF support, and with that support ending, LIV is searching for new investors and attempting to stand independently as a commercial property. The challenge is significant: the tour does not have the same broadcast deals, sponsor infrastructure, or player depth as the PGA Tour, and without Saudi backing, its financial model needs rebuilding from the ground up.

The irony of The Open Championship this week is unmistakable. Herbert and DeChambeau are among the best performers in the field. They are reminding the golf world that LIV's players can compete at the highest level. But the tour they play on — which gave them those opportunities — may look very different in 2027.
