Ryan Fox Shoots 62 in Round 3 — Becomes 8th Player in History to Reach the Major Scoring Limit

By Marcus Cole | Golf Fanrecap | July 19, 2026

Before Round 3 of the 154th Open Championship, Ryan Fox was at even par — eight shots off the lead, effectively a spectator in the title race. Eighteen holes later, he was tied for second place, had shot one of the lowest rounds in major championship history, and had joined a list of golfers so short it barely fills a lunch table.

Fox went around Royal Birkdale in 62 strokes on Saturday, becoming the 8th golfer in the history of men's major championship golf to reach that score.

What the Round Looked Like

Fox went out in 5-under on Birkdale's front nine — a start that turned heads in the scoring tent. He ran into a bogey on the 12th hole, which threatened to deflate the momentum, but birdied the 13th and 14th holes back-to-back to regain the pace. His finish was emphatic: birdies on two of his final three holes to close out the 62 and vault into a tie for second place at -8.

ESPN called it "making history." The PGA Tour described Fox as one of the round's standouts in a tournament that had already produced historic scoring. Bleacher Report confirmed the record: 8th golfer ever in a major, full stop.

The Third 62 of the Same Open

The truly staggering part of Fox's round is its context. He is not the first player to shoot 62 at the 154th Open. He is not even the second. He is the third.

Lucas Herbert shot 62 in Round 2, tying the major scoring record with an outward nine of 28 and briefly leading the championship. Sam Burns shot 62 in Round 2 as well, sharing the historic score on the same day. Fox's Round 3 62 makes this Open Championship the only time in major golf history that three players have shot 62 at the same tournament.

Prior to this week, only seven golfers had ever shot 62 in a major across the entire history of professional golf. This one week at Royal Birkdale added three of those eight names.

What It Means for Sunday

Fox enters the final round at -8, two shots behind leader Sam Burns. In a normal Open, trailing by two shots entering the final round would be a challenging but achievable position for any player. In this Open — where the scoring has been this low and this compressed — the gap feels smaller still.

Fox, a New Zealander competing on the DP World Tour, has not won a major. He has been a consistent presence in European Tour competition but has never stood this close to a Claret Jug. He enters Sunday with the unmistakable advantage of a player who has nothing to fear and everything to gain — the dangerous position to be in when you are chasing a first major.

He has already made history once this week. Two shots is not an obstacle for a player who shot 62 on a Saturday.

What's Next

Fox tees off in the final round as one of the premier challengers to Sam Burns's two-shot lead. Watch his scoring through the front nine at Birkdale — if he can repeat anything close to Saturday's outward pace, he will be in the thick of the title race through the afternoon. A Fox victory would be the first by a New Zealander at The Open in the modern era.
