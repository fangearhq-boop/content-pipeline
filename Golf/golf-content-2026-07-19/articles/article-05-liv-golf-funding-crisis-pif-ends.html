LIV Golf's Funding Is Ending. Its Players Are Winning at The Open.

By Marcus Cole | Golf Fanrecap | July 19, 2026

Lucas Herbert shot 62 in Round 2 of the 154th Open Championship, briefly held the outright lead, and reminded everyone who had written off LIV Golf's competitive credibility that its players can perform at the highest level. Bryson DeChambeau fought through a two-stroke penalty and a public war of words with Rory McIlroy to shoot 69 in Round 3 and stand at -6 entering Sunday — very much in contention for the Claret Jug.

Meanwhile, the tour they play for is running out of runway.

What Is Happening with LIV

Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund — the government-backed sovereign wealth entity that launched and funded LIV Golf — is ending its financial backing of the tour after the 2026 season. Four LIV events remain on this year's schedule. The tour's future beyond 2026 is, by multiple accounts, uncertain.

LIV Golf launched in 2022 with an injection of Saudi capital that reshaped professional golf in ways the sport is still processing. It offered guaranteed contracts worth hundreds of millions of dollars to players who defected from the PGA Tour. It triggered legal battles, a formal framework agreement between LIV and the PGA Tour, and years of tension across professional golf at every level — from locker rooms to boardrooms to broadcast deals.

The disruption was real and lasting. So, now, is the funding crisis.

The Commercial Challenge

Without PIF support, LIV Golf would need to stand as a commercially viable property on its own terms. That is a significantly harder problem than it might appear. The tour lacks the broadcast infrastructure of the PGA Tour, the deep sponsorship relationships that come with decades of establishment, and the player depth that makes a full schedule sustainable for advertisers and fans alike.

LIV has been searching for new investors and attempting to position itself as an independent entity that could attract outside capital. The format — team-based, shotgun starts, no cut — was always a departure from golf's traditions, and its long-term appeal to a broader audience beyond the initial novelty remains an open question.

The tour's best argument for its survival has always been its players. DeChambeau, Herbert, Phil Mickelson, Brooks Koepka, Dustin Johnson — names that carry real weight in the sport. But big names attached to uncertain infrastructure do not automatically generate the revenues needed to sustain a full global tour without a sovereign backer writing the checks.

The Irony This Week

The timing of all this is not lost on those paying attention. The 154th Open Championship is one of the most important showcases in professional golf. Major championships are the venues where players cement their legacies and where the sport's broader commercial ecosystem — television, sponsorship, global interest — is most visibly on display.

Two of the most prominent performers at this Open are LIV players. Herbert co-led after Round 2. DeChambeau is in contention entering the final round. They are making the case, on the biggest stage, that LIV's talent level is genuine. But the tour that gave them their platform may look fundamentally different in 2027 — if it exists in its current form at all.

That irony is a strange thing to sit with. The players are winning. The institution is struggling. The question of which one of those facts matters more for the tour's long-term future does not have a clear answer yet.

What's Next

Four LIV events remain in 2026. The search for alternative investors continues. DeChambeau and Herbert will finish their week at Royal Birkdale and return to a tour that is, by its own admission, figuring out what comes next. Watch for announcements regarding LIV's 2027 structure in the months following this season's conclusion. If new investors materialize, the tour could stabilize. If they don't, the player contracts, team structure, and format that defined LIV Golf's short history will need to be fundamentally renegotiated.
