The Hometown Dream: Tommy Fleetwood at Royal Birkdale, Six Miles from Home

By Jake Torres | Golf Fanrecap | July 19, 2026

There is a version of this story where Tommy Fleetwood wins The Open Championship on Sunday at Royal Birkdale — six miles from Southport, England, where he grew up. It is not the most likely outcome. But it is a genuinely plausible one, and that alone makes this one of the week's most compelling subplots.

Fleetwood enters the final round at -5, five shots behind leader Sam Burns. He shot a 69 on Saturday — steady, competent golf that kept him on the leaderboard without closing the gap to the front. He is the fifth name on a board that has Sam Burns, Ryan Fox, Si Woo Kim, and Lucas Herbert ahead of him. But Fleetwood is the name the crowd at Birkdale will be watching.

The Course He Grew Up Beside

Royal Birkdale is not merely a tournament venue for Fleetwood. He grew up in Southport. The links course was, by his own telling, the place he snuck onto as a young boy to play holes in the evening after the club had closed — a kid with a club and a course that felt like his, even when it technically wasn't.

He attended the 1998 Open Championship at Birkdale as a child, watching the professionals from behind the gallery ropes with his father. He was dreaming even then of being on the other side. That was 28 years ago. This week, he is on the other side, and his father is on the bag.

The symmetry of it is the kind of thing that makes for great sports storytelling — and potentially great sports history, if Sunday goes the right way.

What Five Shots Actually Means

Five shots in the final round of a major is not an insurmountable deficit. It is a significant one, but it is not a closed door. Birkdale's closing holes are demanding, and Sam Burns — who would be a first-time major champion if he holds on — has never navigated a Sunday final round with a lead at a major. The pressure of a crowd that desperately wants to see an English champion, on a course with a history of late leaderboard movement, has a way of compressing gaps.

No English player has won The Open on home soil since Nick Faldo in 1992. That is a 34-year drought, and the Birkdale gallery will be acutely aware of it. Whether that awareness helps or hurts Fleetwood is one of Sunday's central questions.

Fleetwood himself has not won a major. He has had chances — most notably at the 2018 U.S. Open at Shinnecock Hills, where he finished runner-up. He knows how to contend. What he does not know yet is how to close one.

The Path to a Miracle

For Fleetwood to win, he would need Burns to falter and would need to produce one of the great final rounds in Open history. A 64 or 65 — combined with Burns losing two or three shots to the field — would get him there. Given what has happened at this Open (three rounds of 62 in three days of play), spectacular golf is clearly possible at Birkdale this week.

The more realistic version of Sunday is that Fleetwood plays well, finishes in the top three or four, and walks away from his home Open with a result to be proud of — but without the Claret Jug. That version is also a meaningful one. This is his Open, his course, his crowd. The outcome does not determine the significance of the week.

What's Next

Fleetwood tees off in the final round Sunday. Watch his scoring through the early holes — if he can get to -7 or -8 quickly and the leaders have a slow start, the leaderboard could reshape itself in unpredictable ways. His father is on the bag, the crowd is behind him, and Royal Birkdale is as close to a home game as professional golf gets. Whatever happens, Sunday at Birkdale will be the defining moment of Tommy Fleetwood's relationship with this particular course.
