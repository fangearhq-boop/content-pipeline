"Not Particularly Fond of Him": McIlroy's Scorched-Earth Assessment of DeChambeau

By Ryan Calloway | Golf Fanrecap | July 19, 2026

Rory McIlroy has many talents. Diplomatic restraint, it turns out, is not at the top of the list.

Following his third round at Royal Birkdale — where he shot a 69 that left him at -2 and well outside realistic contention — McIlroy was asked about Bryson DeChambeau's reaction to a two-stroke penalty assessed on Saturday. His answer was pointed, sustained, and unlikely to be forgotten by either party.

"I'm not particularly fond of him," McIlroy said. "I think a lot of it's performative. I think a lot of it's for attention. To hold the tournament hostage like that... I didn't feel like it was a great look."

The Penalty That Started It

DeChambeau received a two-stroke penalty in Round 2 under Rule 8.1a of the Rules of Golf — assessed after he stepped on tall fescue grass near his ball before his second shot at the fifth hole, improving the area of his intended swing. R&A chief referee Grant Moir confirmed the ruling clearly: intent was irrelevant. The physical act of improving the swing area constituted a violation, regardless of what DeChambeau meant to do.

The penalty dropped DeChambeau from -7 to -5 at a stroke. He went to the practice range afterward and stayed there late into the evening — at one point reportedly distributing snacks to the media gathered at the facility. He eventually confirmed via social media that he would play the weekend, after a period in which his participation was considered uncertain.

McIlroy watched from the players' lounge as the situation unfolded. "As soon as he made the step into the ball," McIlroy said, "we all sort of looked at each other, and we were like, 'That didn't seem right.'"

The Bigger Context

The relationship between McIlroy and DeChambeau is complicated by more than personality. McIlroy has been the PGA Tour's most prominent champion during the years of conflict between the Tour and LIV Golf. DeChambeau is among LIV's most visible and vocal stars. The two have found themselves on opposite sides of professional golf's central political dispute, and their public interactions have generally reflected the tension of that positioning.

McIlroy's Saturday comments were notable not just for their directness but for their completeness. He did not hedge. He did not offer the standard "I'll let the rules speak for themselves" deflection. He named his view and attached it to a name.

DeChambeau's Response

DeChambeau played on in Round 3 and shot a 69, moving to -6 entering the final round. He is very much in contention for the Claret Jug. His on-course response to the penalty — showing up, playing solid golf, and climbing within four shots of the lead — was more persuasive than any statement he could have made at the range Friday evening.

Whether he responds publicly to McIlroy's Saturday comments is a separate question. Given DeChambeau's history of engaging directly with public criticism, it would be surprising if this week passed without further exchange between the two.

What's Next

Both players tee off Sunday in the final round. McIlroy at -2 is eight shots from the lead — an unlikely but not mathematically impossible position if the leaderboard collapses. DeChambeau at -6 is four shots back with 18 holes to play, genuinely in contention. Watch for any further public exchange between the two, and for how DeChambeau plays under the scrutiny of Sunday's final round.
