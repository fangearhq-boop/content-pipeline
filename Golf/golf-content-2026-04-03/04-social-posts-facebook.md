#### Long-Form Post — 8:00 AM ET

A second consecutive green jacket would make him only the second player in the 90-year history of the event to win back-to-back, following Tiger's 2001-02 consecutive victories.

McIlroy has spent the run-up to Augusta deliberately resting rather than playing. The approach is a calculated one: McIlroy knows his game well enough to know that peak Augusta form requires clarity and rest, not a crowded pre-major schedule.

At +1100, he is the second-highest on the betting board behind Scheffler. But the history he is chasing is more compelling than any number.

In the tournament's 90 years, only two players have managed three Masters titles within a similar five-year span: Jack Nicklaus during his dominant 1960s-70s run, and Tiger Woods, who won in 2001 and 2002 and extended his collection multiple times over the following decade.

Scheffler enters as the betting favorite at +550, despite being ranked third in the world rather than first. That apparent contradiction is explained by his Augusta-specific record: a course that rewards precise iron play, disciplined scrambling, and comfort off the tee in front of large crowds — skills that Scheffler has demonstrated under pressure better than almost anyone in the current generation.

He also recently joined Tiger Woods and Rory McIlroy in the $100M career earnings club on the PGA Tour — a financial milestone that reflects the sustained excellence that has defined his last four seasons.

There is also this: Scheffler has won the Masters only in even-numbered years — 2022 and 2024. The pattern gives Augusta observers something to watch. For Scheffler, the only number that matters is the one on the scorecard.
