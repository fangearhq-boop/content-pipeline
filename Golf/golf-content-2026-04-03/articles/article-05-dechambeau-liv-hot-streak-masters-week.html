DeChambeau's LIV Win Streak Enters Masters Week: Two Titles, Five Career, One Goal

By Jake Torres April 3, 2026

The Momentum Man

Of all the competitors arriving at Augusta National on April 9, Bryson DeChambeau may be the one carrying the most immediate momentum. His last two competitive results: a win at LIV Singapore and, most recently, a win at LIV South Africa, where he edged Jon Rahm in a playoff after both players finished 26-under through 72 holes. That South Africa victory was his fifth career LIV title and his second in back-to-back events — a run of form that places him among the hottest players in professional golf regardless of tour affiliation.

He enters the Masters at +1000 odds, tied with Rahm. For a player who competes exclusively on LIV Golf and therefore does not carry Official World Golf Rankings points, those odds are a significant expression of market confidence — based entirely on the quality of his recent play and his established Augusta track record.

The LIV South Africa Win in Context

The LIV South Africa playoff against Rahm deserves specific attention. At 26-under through 72 holes, both players had delivered rounds of sustained excellence that pushed the tournament's finishing score into elite territory. A playoff at that level, against Rahm — a former World No. 1 and Masters champion — is not a victory over a depleted field. It is a win in the most pressure-tested scenario possible: sudden death against one of the best players in the world.

DeChambeau's ability to close in high-pressure playoff situations has been one of the more consistent features of his recent game. The LIV format, with its 54-hole individual events and team scoring, demands specific qualities of focus and ball-striking that translate reasonably well to major week preparation. Whether the LIV schedule fully replicates the physical and mental demands of a 72-hole major is a debate that does not need resolution here — what matters is that DeChambeau is arriving at Augusta playing the best golf of his career, and that is a fact that leaderboard watchers should take seriously.

Augusta's Power Profile and DeChambeau

Augusta National rewards long, accurate driving above almost any other skill in its opening profile. The course has several par-5s that short hitters can reach in three but that long hitters can reach in two, creating scoring opportunities that compound over four rounds. Holes 13 and 15 — the two par-5s on the back nine — are routinely identified as the tournament's scoring corridors, and a player who can hit both greens in two with a long iron or hybrid while the rest of the field plays up and chips on is working from a structural advantage.

DeChambeau's driving is the most prodigious in professional golf. He has averaged distances off the tee that routinely place him at the top of any field he enters, and at Augusta — where the fairways are wide enough to absorb some deviation but the approach shot angles demand accuracy — his power is as useful as it is anywhere he plays. Add the precision that has developed in his iron game over the last three seasons, and the combination becomes genuinely dangerous.

His 2021 Augusta runner-up finish, when he contended throughout the week before Hideki Matsuyama held on for the victory, established his Augusta credentials. A runner-up and a week of sustained contention at 32 years old — with five LIV titles and demonstrably improved course management — suggests a player who has continued to develop rather than plateau.

The Missing Element: A Major

DeChambeau has won one major in his career — the 2020 U.S. Open at Winged Foot, where his combination of power and precise iron play overwhelmed a course that was supposed to punish the approach he brought to it. That win was one of the more dominant major performances in recent history, and it established DeChambeau as a legitimate candidate every time a major is contested.

The Masters remains on his list. He finished second in 2021. He has contended in other years. Augusta National, with its specific demands and its premium on long driving, is perhaps the major most structurally suited to his game. If the hot form from LIV Singapore and LIV South Africa carries into the first week of April, DeChambeau will be one of the most dangerous players on the Augusta leaderboard. The momentum is built. The course suits him. Six days to April 9.
