McIlroy's 67 Keeps Quail Hollow Record Bid Alive — Four Shots Back With Two Rounds to Play

By Marcus Cole | May 9, 2026 | Golf Fanrecap

You can forgive anyone who looked at Rory McIlroy's first-round 1-under 70 at Quail Hollow Club and concluded that his record bid was over before it truly began. Seven shots off the pace. The Masters winner looking like a player still finding his groove after a well-earned rest. The historic fifth Quail Hollow title — a record that no one else has come close to — seemed like it would have to wait at least another year.

And then McIlroy went out Friday and shot 4-under 67, trimmed the deficit from seven shots to four, and reminded everyone exactly why this man owns this golf course in a way no player owns any other course on tour.

What the 67 Meant

McIlroy's second round at the Truist Championship was not a spectacular scoreboard round — it wasn't the 62 he posted here in a previous edition or anything that will be highlighted for its sheer scoring brilliance. But it was exactly what a player in his position needed: consistent, quality golf that took advantage of scoring opportunities and made no catastrophic errors.

After making the turn 1-under in Round 1 and looking passive and disconnected, McIlroy's Friday was assertive. He attacked pins he'd previously played conservatively. He holed putts he'd left short. He played with the rhythm that comes from someone who has memorized every green complex, every fairway contour, and every likely wind direction at a course he has walked 100 times competitively.

That comfort level is Quail Hollow's gift to McIlroy. And at 5-under for the tournament, four behind 36-hole leader Sungjae Im, the record bid is alive.

The Record He's Chasing

McIlroy has won the Truist Championship and its predecessor events at Quail Hollow Club four times: in 2010 (his first PGA Tour win, with a then-course-record 62 in the final round), in 2015, in 2021, and in 2024. No player in the history of this event has won it five times.

The record matters to McIlroy in the way that records at specific courses tend to matter to players who have built a relationship with those venues over time. Augusta National is the most famous example — certain players return to certain places and find something in themselves that they can't access elsewhere. Quail Hollow is that place for McIlroy. Its tight, tree-lined layout, its Green Mile closing stretch, its demand for creativity and nerve — all of it brings out his best.

He would need to make up four shots on Sungjae Im over the final two rounds. That's less than he made up just between Round 1 and Round 2.

An Open Field Helps

The absence of Scottie Scheffler changes the calculation significantly. The World No. 1 elected to skip the Truist Championship to prepare for the PGA Championship next week — a decision that, while bold, removes from the field the one player most capable of winning from anywhere. With Scheffler absent, the tournament lacks a dominant favorite. Im is steady but not proven at this level. Fowler is exciting but historically inconsistent in late-tournament positions. McCarty has been excellent in his young career but has never won a signature event.

McIlroy is the most decorated player in this field. He's won here before — four times before. When the conditions of a tournament line up for a player with his profile, outcomes that seemed unlikely on Thursday begin looking very reasonable by Saturday.

What's Next

Round 3 is underway today (Saturday), with the final round on Sunday, May 10. For McIlroy, the weekend's approach is clear: he needs to shoot 65 or better in at least one round, keep Im under pressure, and let the historical familiarity with Quail Hollow's closing holes do the rest.

The record may come this weekend. If not, there is always next year — McIlroy has made very clear that Quail Hollow is a permanent fixture on his annual schedule for exactly this reason. But something about his Friday 67, about the controlled urgency with which he played, suggests that this year feels different. The record is within range. And Rory McIlroy has proven, many times over, that within range is close enough.
