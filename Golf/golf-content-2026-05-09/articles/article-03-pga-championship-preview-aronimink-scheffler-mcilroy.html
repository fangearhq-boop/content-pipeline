PGA Championship Preview — Scheffler's Bold Preparation, McIlroy's Momentum, and the Aronimink Test

By Jake Torres | May 9, 2026 | Golf Fanrecap

One week from today, the 2026 PGA Championship begins at Aronimink Golf Club in Newtown Square, Pennsylvania. It will be the first time the historic Donald Ross design has hosted the PGA Championship since Gary Player claimed the Wanamaker Trophy there in 1962 — 64 years ago, under conditions and with equipment that would be unrecognizable to the 154-player field gathering this May.

The storylines entering the week are rich with intrigue: a World No. 1 who deliberately removed himself from competition this week to prepare for one specific course; a defending major champion playing himself into form at the Truist Championship; and two of LIV Golf's biggest names arriving via the same Virginia tune-up event. Let's unpack them all.

Scheffler's Calculated Absence

Scottie Scheffler is not at the Truist Championship this week. He made the deliberate decision to skip the signature event — with its world ranking points, prize money, and competitive spotlight — specifically to spend the week preparing for Aronimink. It's the kind of choice that a champion makes when he has a particular major locked in his mind as the primary target.

Scheffler's motivation is clear. He has finished runner-up in his last three starts: behind McIlroy at the Masters, behind Matthew Fitzpatrick in a playoff at the RBC Heritage, and six strokes back at the Cadillac Championship. That streak of excellence without a win is remarkable by any objective measure — but Scheffler is not playing for "remarkable by any objective measure." He's playing for trophies, and his 2026 collection is empty.

Aronimink suits his strengths. Donald Ross courses require precision ball-striking above all else — not length, not power, but the ability to hit specific targets on approach shots and control trajectory through the complex bunkering that characterizes every Ross design. Scheffler's ball-striking is the best in the world. If he executes his week of preparation correctly, he will arrive at Aronimink's first tee with a meaningful advantage over players who have treated this as just another week.

McIlroy's Form Is Building

Rory McIlroy won the Masters on April 12, closing with 64 at Augusta National for his second consecutive green jacket. He took a brief break before returning to competition at the Truist Championship this week, and the initial Thursday signs were mixed — a 1-under 70 that left him seven shots off the pace.

But Friday he found the gear that makes him dangerous: a 4-under 67 that elevated him to 5-under and placed him four shots off the 36-hole lead. McIlroy arrives at the PGA Championship in a state of building form, having played competitive golf this week and fired a round that reminded observers of his capability. If he continues his upward trajectory through the Truist weekend, he'll reach Aronimink carrying real momentum.

McIlroy's relationship with the PGA Championship has been complex — he's won it once (2012 at Kiawah Island) and been in contention multiple other times. A second Wanamaker Trophy would make him a three-time consecutive major winner in 2026 and cement his status as the dominant player of this era.

The LIV Factor — Rahm and DeChambeau at Aronimink

Both Jon Rahm and Bryson DeChambeau are confirmed in the PGA Championship field, using this week's LIV Golf Virginia event as competitive preparation. Neither has been able to challenge Herbert's dominant lead in Virginia, but competitive rounds matter for sharpening touch and maintaining tournament instincts.

Whether LIV players can genuinely challenge at major championships in 2026 has been an ongoing question. The format differences — 54-hole stroke play on LIV versus 72-hole traditional stroke play at the majors — create adjustment challenges. But both Rahm and DeChambeau have major championship pedigrees and the game to compete if Aronimink sets up favorably for their strengths.

What's Next

The PGA Championship runs May 14-17 at Aronimink Golf Club, Newtown Square, Pennsylvania. The field of 154 includes 29 major champions. Two spots remain open for the winners of the Myrtle Beach Classic and the Truist Championship — meaning Sunday's final round at Quail Hollow could double as a PGA Championship qualifying performance.

With Scheffler preparing, McIlroy in form, and the world's best players converging on a course that hasn't seen a major in over six decades, this PGA Championship promises to be exceptional. The Wanamaker Trophy awaits.
