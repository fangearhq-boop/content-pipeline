Scheffler Bouncing Back: Defending Open Champion Finds His Game at Royal Birkdale

By Ryan Calloway | Golf Fanrecap | July 17, 2026

There are few things rarer in professional golf than Scottie Scheffler failing to make a cut. When it happened at the 2026 Genesis Scottish Open last week, the golf world paused — briefly, as it always does with anomalies — before turning its attention to what would happen next.

What happened next was a -2 opening round at the 154th Open Championship. Four birdies in his first six holes. A performance that said everything the world No. 1 needed to say about his form, his readiness, and his hunger to defend the Claret Jug at Royal Birkdale.

Scheffler did not arrive at Birkdale looking like a player who missed the cut seven days ago. He arrived looking like the defending champion.

The Round in Detail

Scheffler's opening round was built on an aggressive early start. He made four birdies across his first six holes — a stretch that suggested comfort, confidence, and command of a golf course that has eaten up lesser performances in major championships past.

The round was not without blemish. Bogeys at holes 7 and 17 pulled him back to -2, short of what the early burst threatened to deliver. But -2 through 18 at Royal Birkdale in an Open Championship is a solid platform, not a disappointment.

He finished three shots off Jackson Suber's lead and one behind Sungjae Im and Daniel Brown. The defending champion entered Round 2 in a position to make a genuine run.

The Scottish Open Context

Missing the cut at the Scottish Open was a genuine surprise. Scheffler had maintained an exceptional streak of consistency across the season — his ability to find the weekend at major and non-major events alike had become a defining trait of his dominance. When it ended at the Scottish Open, the conversation turned quickly to what it meant for his Open Championship form.

The answer from Thursday at Birkdale: not much. The missed cut may have represented an off week, a deliberate energy conservation, or simply the kind of statistical anomaly that eventually finds every player no matter how good. What it did not represent, based on what Scheffler showed in Round 1, was a collapse in form or confidence.

If anything, the missed cut may have sharpened him. Players who arrive at major venues with something to prove sometimes play their best golf precisely because of that edge.

The Pairing That Sets Up Friday

Scheffler's Round 2 pairing with Tyrrell Hatton and Bryson DeChambeau creates one of the marquee groups walking Royal Birkdale on Friday. Hatton is a European fan favorite with a game suited to links conditions. DeChambeau sits at -3 through R1, one shot ahead of Scheffler, adding competitive context to every hole they share.

The dynamic inside the group is compelling — a defending champion, a crowd-ready European, and one of the game's most powerful and unpredictable players, all in the same pairing while three shots off the lead. Gallery interest in this threesome will be significant all day.

The Title Defense

Open Championship title defenses carry their own weight. The course is the same. The trophy is the same. But the field has spent a year studying what the champion did, and the pressure of returning as the man everyone is chasing adds a layer of complexity that first-time contenders don't face.

Scheffler has navigated that pressure before. His ability to perform under scrutiny — in the biggest events, on the biggest stages — is among the most reliable qualities in modern professional golf. Nothing about his Thursday performance suggested he finds this week's version of that pressure any different from the others he has managed successfully.

What's Next

Scheffler needs a strong Round 2 to close the three-shot gap on Suber and position himself for a weekend run. At Royal Birkdale, three shots is recoverable in a single round — the course's par-5s and short par-4s create scoring opportunities that can move leaderboards quickly.

The defending Open Champion has found his game. Friday will tell us whether he has found enough of it to lead the tournament heading into Moving Day.
