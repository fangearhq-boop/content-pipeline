LIV Golf Financial Crisis: Four Events Hang in the Balance as PIF Funding Falls $400M Short

By Jake Torres | Golf Fanrecap | July 17, 2026

The 154th Open Championship is underway at Royal Birkdale, and for the golfers competing in it, the focus is entirely on the course in front of them. But for a significant number of professional golfers employed by LIV Golf, the week is carrying a different kind of weight — one measured not in shot totals but in financial reality.

LIV Golf's 2026 season is in serious jeopardy. According to reporting from Golf Digest, Field Level Media, and Sportico, the rival tour needs $600 million to complete its calendar year. Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund — the entity that has financed LIV since its launch in 2022 — has delivered approximately $200 million to date. That leaves a $400 million gap with four events remaining on the schedule.

From Investor to Lender

The financial strain is visible in the paperwork. In June 2026, PIF filed a debenture agreement in UK and Jersey company filings that formally shifted its relationship with LIV Golf from investor to lender. The implication is significant: PIF is no longer putting money into LIV Golf because it believes in the venture's long-term equity value. It is lending money to keep operations running.

LIV has been burning through roughly $100 million per month in operating costs — player salaries, event infrastructure, travel, and broadcast commitments all contributing to that figure. Even with the $200 million PIF has delivered in two tranches (approximately $66 million in early May and $130 million in early June), the math does not work without additional capital.

Four Events, Six Days

The next LIV Golf event — LIV Golf UK at JCB Golf and Country Club in Rocester, England — is scheduled for July 23 through 26. That is six days away. Three additional US events follow in August: LIV Golf New York, LIV Golf Indianapolis, and a $40 million LIV Team Championship in Michigan.

Multiple insiders told Sportico and other outlets that PIF could cut monthly payments before the tour completes those four events. LIV's chief executive has reportedly declined to guarantee that all four remaining events will take place.

PIF confirmed publicly in April that its financial commitment to LIV extends only through the end of the 2026 season. There is no publicly announced commitment for 2027 or beyond.

The Search for New Investment

LIV has reportedly approached hundreds of prospective investors since the funding shortfall became apparent. US investment bank Ducera Partners was engaged to lead the capital-raising effort. Two independent board directors were added, a move typically associated with governance improvements ahead of a formal investment process.

No new investment has been announced. PIF has invested more than $6 billion into LIV Golf since the tour launched in 2022, making it one of the largest sports investments in history. Whether any outside investor is prepared to match that commitment — or even provide enough capital to bridge the four-event gap — remains unknown.

The Players in the Middle

LIV Golf's roster includes some of the biggest names in professional golf. Bryson DeChambeau, Phil Mickelson, Jon Rahm, Tyrrell Hatton, and Brooks Koepka are among the players whose financial arrangements and competitive schedules are tied to LIV's ability to complete its calendar.

Many of those players are competing at The Open Championship this week, which remains a major championship open to LIV members. Their week at Royal Birkdale offers a temporary pause from the off-course uncertainty — but the question of what the second half of their 2026 season looks like is not going away.

What's Next

The most immediate answer to LIV Golf's financial crisis will come on July 23, when LIV Golf UK either begins play at JCB Golf and Country Club or it doesn't. An event cancellation at that scale — in England, with international attention already focused on the golf world through The Open — would be a significant moment for the sport's landscape.

LIV's search for outside investment continues. Whether the outcome comes in the form of a new financial partner, an agreement from PIF to extend its commitment, or something else entirely, the clock is running. Four events in six weeks is the schedule. The funding to execute it may not be there.
