McIlroy's Open Quest: A Seventh Major Would Make European History at Royal Birkdale

By Marcus Cole | Golf Fanrecap | July 17, 2026

Rory McIlroy does not need to explain what The Open Championship means to him. The affection is mutual and long-established — links golf is where his game was forged, where his accent fits the landscape, and where the game feels most like the sport he fell in love with as a child.

He arrives at Royal Birkdale for the 154th Open Championship in the best possible form. McIlroy won last week's Genesis Scottish Open, giving him momentum, confidence, and a game tuned specifically for the conditions that define British links golf in July.

A win at Birkdale this week would deliver something more than another trophy. It would give McIlroy seven career major championships — one more than Nick Faldo — and make him the most decorated European player in major championship history.

The Record in Context

McIlroy currently holds six major titles, tying him with Faldo for the European record. Faldo's six came across three Masters titles and three Open Championships — a haul that defined his era and established him as one of the five or six greatest players the sport has produced.

McIlroy has matched that number through a career that already includes back-to-back Masters victories in 2025 and 2026. He won his first Open at Hoylake in 2014, a round of championship golf that remains among the best Sunday performances in recent major history. That win began a stretch that cemented his status as one of the sport's defining players of his generation.

Now 12 years removed from that Hoylake victory, McIlroy returns to an Open Championship with the record in front of him and the form to reach it.

The Scottish Open as Foundation

Winning the Scottish Open the week before The Open Championship is rarely a coincidence. The event is designed, in part, to give players a final tuning opportunity on links terrain before the week that matters most.

McIlroy has historically treated the Scottish Open as preparation rather than diversion, and his win last week — which came as Scheffler was making his unexpected exit from the field — provided momentum at exactly the right moment. Arriving at a major on a winning streak carries its own psychological weight, and McIlroy will carry that confidence into every hole at Royal Birkdale this week.

The Open and the Wait

McIlroy's last Open Championship win came at Royal Liverpool, Hoylake in 2014. Twelve years is a long time between wins at golf's oldest major, particularly for a player who has demonstrated sustained excellence throughout that period. He has come close on multiple occasions and has consistently been among the field's top performers in links conditions.

Royal Birkdale offers a different kind of challenge — wide fairways that reward power off the tee, testing rough that punishes wayward drives, and greens that can be among the trickiest on the Open rota when the wind picks up. McIlroy's ball-striking has always made him suited to this kind of test.

He played in the afternoon wave during Thursday's first round and is in the field for Round 2 on Friday. His specific Round 1 score had not been officially confirmed through research sources at the time of writing — the focus, as always with McIlroy at an Open, is on what happens next.

What's Next

The path to the record is straightforward: four rounds of golf, 72 holes, on one of the Open rota's most celebrated courses. McIlroy has played these links before. He has won majors in more pressure-filled circumstances than this. The form is there, the venue is suited to his game, and the history is waiting.

Seven majors would not just break a record. It would permanently alter how Rory McIlroy is remembered — not just as one of the best of his generation, but as the best European player the game has ever produced.
