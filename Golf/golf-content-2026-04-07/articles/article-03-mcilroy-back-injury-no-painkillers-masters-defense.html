McIlroy Says Back 'Wasn't an Issue' — But Questions Linger Heading Into Masters Defense

By Marcus Cole April 7, 2026

The Update the Field Needed to Hear

Rory McIlroy delivered the message Masters fans were hoping for this week: he is off painkillers, his back is not bothering him, and he is ready to defend his green jacket. "Wasn't an issue," McIlroy said when asked about his back injury ahead of Thursday's first round. The three-week competitive layoff — the longest of his professional career — is behind him. He is here at Augusta. He is healthy enough to play. The rest is golf.

The questions surrounding McIlroy's back have been building since he withdrew from the Arnold Palmer Invitational in March and then finished T46 at The Players Championship on March 12 before not competing for nearly four weeks. That was his last tournament. Now, almost a month later, he tees it up at Augusta National — the most demanding course in major golf for a player managing lower back issues.

Why Augusta Demands Caution

Augusta National is not forgiving on the body. The course's dramatic elevation changes — the descent from the ninth green to the 10th tee, the long climb on 18, and the physically demanding walks through Amen Corner — put real stress on lower back health over four rounds of championship golf. Golf analyst Amanda Balionis summarized the concern directly: "Back issues at Augusta National are tough given the course's demanding terrain."

That concern is reflected in the betting markets. McIlroy's odds have drifted to the +1000-+1200 range as the week has progressed — down from where they opened. The market is making a probabilistic judgment: even if McIlroy can play, a player who has not competed in four weeks faces an uncertain path through four rounds of the most pressure-filled golf on earth.

The Counterargument: He Loves This Place

The case for McIlroy is simple and historically proven. He has won here. He knows how to navigate Augusta's angles and demands. He spent last year's final round in one of the most emotionally charged situations in recent golf memory — birdie on 18 to force a playoff, then winning the extra hole against Justin Rose to complete the career Grand Slam. Motivation is not something McIlroy will be searching for this week.

What happened in practice rounds Monday and Tuesday will tell Augusta's medical team and McIlroy's own body whether the three-week rest did its job. He says it has. He is off the medication. He says the back is fine. In golf, the truth of any injury assessment comes out on the first tee, not in the press tent.

What's Next

McIlroy tees off Thursday alongside U.S. Amateur champion Mason Howell. The first round will be the real test — whether his swing is uninhibited, whether he can handle the stress of Augusta's terrain through 18 holes, and whether the layoff has cost him any of the competitive sharpness he will need to defend against the deepest Masters field in years. His back, he says, is not the issue. Everything else about his game will answer the questions Thursday begins asking.
