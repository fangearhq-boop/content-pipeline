Par 3 Contest Tomorrow at Augusta: Golf's Most Charming Tradition Explained

By Jake Torres April 7, 2026

Before the Drama, There Is Joy

On Wednesday, April 8, Augusta National becomes something different — looser, warmer, and more joyful than it will be for any of the four competitive rounds that follow. The Masters Par 3 Contest takes place on the club's beautiful nine-hole Par 3 course beginning at noon, with the main ESPN broadcast running from 2-4 PM ET. It is also available on Masters.com and the Masters App.

The Par 3 Contest has been a Masters tradition since 1960. It is contested by the same players who will compete in the tournament proper, but with one crucial difference: the players use their families as caddies. Children carry the bags. Spouses read the putts. Grandchildren make the club selections. The scorecard matters far less than the smiles.

What Makes It Special

Augusta National is a place of ceremony and tradition. The Champions Dinner happens Tuesday. The first competitive round begins Thursday. And on Wednesday, the Par 3 Contest fills the space in between with something that no other major venue offers: a genuine glimpse of the players as people, not just professionals.

Players who might have spent Sunday night processing a difficult final round are, on Wednesday morning, watching a seven-year-old struggle to carry a bag on Augusta's immaculate grounds. The atmosphere is festive and informal. The azaleas are in bloom. Fans along the ropes are loose and conversational. It is the only two hours of Masters week when the word "leaderboard" doesn't particularly matter.

The Superstition

Then there is the superstition — the one that has held for 66 consecutive years. No player who has won the Par 3 Contest has gone on to win the Masters that same week. Not once, in the contest's entire history. The streak is so well-established that many players take it seriously. Some intentionally miss short putts at the end to avoid winning. Others lean into the narrative, saying they'll happily win the Par 3 if it means a good week.

Whether the superstition holds any statistical weight is irrelevant. It has become part of the lore of the week, which is exactly the right context for a contest that is more about tradition than competition. The Masters is full of rules, signals, and rituals. The Par 3 superstition is the one that is allowed to be fun.

What's Next

After Wednesday's Par 3 Contest wraps up, Masters week shifts. The practice rounds end. The scorecards become real. Thursday morning, April 9, the first major of 2026 begins at Augusta National. Between now and then, enjoy tomorrow's two hours on ESPN. For all the pressure and significance of what comes after, the Par 3 Contest is a reminder of what made so many of these players fall in love with the game in the first place.
