LIV Golf Financial Crisis: Saudi PIF Has Funded Just $200M of $600M, Final Events in Doubt

By Marcus Cole | Golf Fanrecap

June 15, 2026

LIV Golf's 2026 season may not make it to the finish line.

A Financial Times report cited by Golf Digest revealed that Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund (PIF) has provided approximately $200 million of the roughly $600 million it committed to fund LIV Golf through the end of the 2026 season — around one-third of the total promised. The season still has four events remaining, and the question of whether those events will happen is no longer theoretical.

A high-ranking executive from one of the league's partners put it bluntly: "every remaining tournament is on the fence."

The PIF's Exit Strategy

The immediate funding shortfall takes on greater significance given the PIF's longer-term decision: the sovereign wealth fund announced it will end its financial backing of LIV Golf after the conclusion of the 2026 season entirely. The PIF's stated reasoning — that "the substantial investment required by LIV Golf over a longer term is no longer consistent with the current phase of PIF's investment strategy" — signals a fundamental shift in the Saudi government's approach to the league it created and bankrolled.

LIV Golf was built on the premise of essentially unlimited Saudi financial support. Without it, the organization must either find alternative funding or face an uncertain future. One event — the Louisiana tournament — has already been postponed. More disruption may follow.

CEO O'Neil's Optimism vs. the Numbers

LIV Golf CEO Scott O'Neil has maintained a public posture of optimism. He told CNBC that he has held five formal meetings with potential investors and has 18 more planned, calling the response "positive." He aims to close a new fundraising round this summer — what some are calling "LIV 2.0" — that would replace PIF support with outside capital and restructure the league for long-term viability.

Whether that timeline is achievable before the final four events is the question. Player contracts, operating costs, and prize purses all require funding. If the investment round does not close in time, the league's ability to honor its commitments comes into question.

For the players involved — many of whom signed multi-year contracts worth enormous sums — the uncertainty is significant. Some may be quietly exploring what a return to the PGA Tour would look like. The Tour, for its part, is watching.

What's Next

O'Neil's fundraising timeline — closing this summer — aligns with LIV Golf's schedule through the end of 2026. If new investors are secured, the organization can restructure and move forward. If not, the league faces a future that looks very different from what its founders imagined.

For now, golf's attention is on Shinnecock Hills and the US Open this week. But LIV Golf's financial story will not wait for golf season to end.
