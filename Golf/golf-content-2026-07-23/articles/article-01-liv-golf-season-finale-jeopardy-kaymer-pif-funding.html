LIV Golf's $40 Million Season Finale Is "Highly Unlikely" to Happen

By Ryan Calloway | Golf Fanrecap | July 23, 2026

The most expensive event on LIV Golf's calendar may never take place.

Martin Kaymer, captain of Cleeks GC, stated publicly this week that LIV Golf's $40 million team championship season finale is "highly unlikely" to occur. The event was scheduled to be held at The Cardinal at Saint John's in Plymouth, Michigan. Kaymer spoke to the media on July 22 and 23, and his comments represent the clearest public acknowledgment yet that LIV's financial situation has reached a critical point.

The reason is straightforward: Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund, the sovereign wealth entity that has bankrolled LIV since its launch, is pulling its financial backing after the 2026 season. Over three years, PIF has invested more than £3.75 billion in the tour. That money is now stopping.

The CEO's September Deadline

LIV CEO Scott O'Neil, who took the role in January 2025, is now attempting to raise $350 million in outside investment to keep LIV operating beyond 2026. He has a September deadline. If that funding cannot be secured, the tour faces an uncertain future heading into 2027.

Players have reportedly been told directly that they do not know whether LIV will exist next year. For athletes who left the PGA Tour — in some cases accepting reduced competitive schedules and complicated eligibility situations — the uncertainty is real and immediate.

The next three events on the LIV schedule — the UK event currently underway at JCB Golf & Country Club, followed by New Jersey and Indianapolis — are expected to proceed as planned. But after those three events, the picture is deeply unclear. The season finale, the marquee team championship that was supposed to cap the year, is the first high-profile casualty.

Broader Infrastructure Losses

The financial withdrawal does not stand alone. Earlier this week, the Asian Tour announced it was joining the PGA Tour-DP World Tour alliance. LIV had invested approximately $300 million to use the Asian Tour as a development and feeder system. That investment has now been redirected. LIV is simultaneously losing its primary funding source and the infrastructure it built to support its competitive ecosystem.

A June event in New Orleans was already postponed this season, citing course conditions and heat. That postponement, viewed at the time as a scheduling inconvenience, now looks more like an early signal of the structural pressures the tour was under.

There are no active PGA Tour-LIV merger negotiations as of mid-2026. Two White House-facilitated meetings in 2024 and 2025 produced no agreement. Rory McIlroy has publicly called LIV "irrational." The relationship between the two tours has not moved meaningfully toward consolidation despite years of speculation.

What Happens Next

The immediate future is defined by two parallel realities. On the course, LIV Golf UK is underway and the competition is genuine — Round 1 saw Joaquin Niemann post a bogey-free 64, with Jon Rahm chasing a third consecutive individual title at JCB. The players are playing, the leaderboards are moving, and the sport continues.

Off the course, the September deadline is the number that matters. Scott O'Neil needs $350 million from private investors willing to back a tour that PIF no longer wants to subsidize indefinitely. Whether that investment materializes will determine whether LIV Golf competes in 2027 — and whether the season finale that Kaymer described as "highly unlikely" becomes a permanent cancellation rather than a postponement.

The players who built their careers around LIV's format and financial promises are now watching that foundation shift in real time.

What's Next

Watch the September deadline for any investor announcement or funding resolution. LIV UK Round 2 takes place Friday at JCB Golf & Country Club. The New Jersey and Indianapolis events follow in the coming weeks. Any announcement about the 2027 schedule — or the absence of one — will be the defining signal of where this story ends.
