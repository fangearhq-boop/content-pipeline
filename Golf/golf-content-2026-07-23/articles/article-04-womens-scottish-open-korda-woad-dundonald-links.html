Women's Scottish Open: Korda and Woad Headline Final Major Tune-Up at Dundonald Links

By Ryan Calloway | Golf Fanrecap | July 23, 2026

The last event before the final major of 2026 is now underway.

The ISPS Handa Women's Scottish Open opened Thursday at Dundonald Links in Gailes, North Ayrshire, Scotland. The tournament runs July 23-26, carries a $2 million purse, and features a field that includes 19 major champions representing 35 combined titles. It is also the final competitive event before the AIG Women's British Open — the last major of the 2026 women's season.

For the players in this field, the week is as much about preparation as it is about prize money. The courses, the conditions, and the links-style demands of Scottish golf provide a direct rehearsal for what lies ahead.

Korda Leads the Name Value

Nelly Korda is the headliner. The world No. 1 has been the dominant force in women's golf for two-plus years, and her presence at Dundonald Links reflects her approach to major preparation — she wants competitive rounds in conditions similar to what the British Open will demand.

Korda has not won in Scotland before, but she has used European events effectively as preparation for major runs. A strong week at Dundonald Links would send her into the AIG Women's Open with momentum. A difficult week would provide information she needs to adjust.

Defending champion Lauren Coughlin is also in the field, as is Hyo Joo Kim, who currently sits at world No. 3. Charley Hull, one of the most popular players in Scottish galleries, brings home-crowd energy to the event. Minjee Lee, Jin Young Ko, and Linn Grant round out a field that represents essentially the full depth of the women's game.

The Lottie Woad Factor

The most watched player beyond Korda this week may be Lottie Woad. The English golfer — an amateur-turned-professional who has moved rapidly through the world rankings to No. 4 — is playing in her home region. Dundonald Links sits less than an hour from the English coastline she grew up near, and the Scottish galleries are familiar with her game.

Woad's rise through 2026 has been one of the genuinely surprising stories of the women's game. Her ascent to world No. 4 represents one of the fastest rises from amateur status to elite professional standing in recent LPGA history. How she performs this week, and then at the AIG Women's Open, will be one of the defining questions of the end of the women's golf season.

The Bigger Picture

The AIG Women's British Open is the last major of 2026. Heading into this week, Haeran Ryu has already won two LPGA majors this season — the KPMG Women's PGA Championship in late June and the Amundi Evian Championship in early July. No other player has won more than one. The player who wins the British Open will join Ryu at the top of the 2026 major count, or, if Ryu herself wins, she will stand alone with three.

That context gives the Women's Scottish Open an additional layer of meaning. Every stroke at Dundonald Links is preparation for what comes next. The player who figures out links golf this week will be positioned to contend at the British Open. The player who struggles may arrive there uncertain.
