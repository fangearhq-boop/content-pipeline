Scottie Scheffler Makes His 3M Open Debut — And the World's Best Player Is on a Mission

By Jake Torres | Golf Fanrecap | July 23, 2026

The 3M Open has its biggest name ever.

Scottie Scheffler — world No. 1, FedEx Cup leader, and the most dominant player in golf over the last two-plus years — is making his debut at TPC Twin Cities in Blaine, Minnesota. He flew directly from Royal Birkdale to Minnesota after The Open Championship concluded last week. He had never played this tournament before. He is here now because he wants to be, and because he has something to prove.

The context matters. Scheffler missed the cut at The Open Championship at Royal Birkdale. It was his first missed cut in four years. For a player who has defined consistency at the top level of professional golf, it was a jarring moment. The 3M Open is where he goes to re-establish what everyone already knows about him.

The Tournament Itself

The 3M Open runs July 23-26 at TPC Twin Cities. The purse is $8.8 million, with the winner taking home approximately $1.58 million. The course is a par-71 Arnold Palmer design measuring 7,431 yards — not a layout that typically rewards power alone. The event offers 500 FedEx Cup points.

Those 500 points are significant right now. There are only three events remaining before the FedEx Cup Playoffs begin at the FedEx St. Jude Championship in Memphis. Scheffler leads the FedEx Cup standings with 3,823 points, but the battle for playoff positioning has intensified. Every position on the leaderboard this week has real implications for the 70 players who make the postseason.

Defending champion Kurt Kitayama is in the field, looking to repeat his 2025 victory. Tom Kim, who sits 35th in the FedEx Cup standings on the strength of a strong recent run, is here. Bubble players like Jason Day, Brian Harman, Tony Finau, and Brandt Snedeker are all competing with their seasons potentially on the line.

Why This Week Is Different

Scheffler's presence changes the event. The 3M Open has historically been a solid mid-tier PGA Tour stop — a course that produces legitimate winners without necessarily drawing the sport's elite names. Scheffler's decision to come here directly from a major changes the competitive calculus and the storyline.

A missed cut at a major is not a crisis for a player of Scheffler's caliber. He is still the best player in the world by most objective measures. But professional golf is a sport built on momentum, and after Royal Birkdale, Scheffler will want a strong week in Minnesota before the stretch run toward Memphis.

His FedEx Cup lead is substantial, but that lead was built on consistent performance across the full season. If Scheffler contends this week — or wins — he will enter the Playoffs as the clear favorite in every meaningful sense. If he struggles again, the conversation will shift.
