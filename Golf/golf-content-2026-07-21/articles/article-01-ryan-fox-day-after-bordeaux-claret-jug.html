Ryan Fox's Day After: Bordeaux in the Claret Jug, a Flight Home With the Man He Beat

By Marcus Cole | Golf Fanrecap | July 21, 2026

Ryan Fox still cannot quite believe what happened on the 18th hole at Royal Birkdale.

"I still don't know what to think," Fox said on Monday. "My nerves are all over the place. I'm not quite sure how I hit that putt on 18."

The putt he is referring to — a 12-foot make that gave him the 2026 Open Championship outright — had not fully processed even 24 hours after the fact. Fox, 39 years old, ranked No. 56 in the world, and listed at 200-1 odds before the week began, is the Champion Golfer of the Year. He described the feeling as "surreal." Then he described it again. "To be an Open champion, to be the Champion Golfer of the Year," he said, "it's unbelievable, surreal, crazy."

He celebrated the way he chose to: by pouring Bordeaux red wine into the Claret Jug. He had to empty it for a round of interviews, then filled it again. He spent most of the night at Royal Birkdale. Sleep was minimal.

A Flight to Remember

The next morning, Fox boarded a flight from Manchester to London. The Claret Jug came with him — in its protective case, traveling as any rightful piece of carry-on should. And when he settled into his seat, he found himself sitting next to Cameron Young.

Young, who finished one stroke behind Fox at the 72nd hole, was now Fox's travel companion on the post-championship journey south. The moment had a certain absurdity to it — the champion and the runner-up, separated by a single stroke and the width of an armrest, heading in the same direction. Fox took it in stride. The trophy was there. The reality was slowly settling.

The Long Road to Royal Birkdale

Only the third New Zealander to win The Open Championship, Fox grew up watching the tournament in the middle of the night — the time difference between New Zealand and the UK made live viewing a late-night endeavor. The idea of becoming the Champion Golfer of the Year was something he watched others become, not something he could have mapped onto his own career trajectory with confidence.

Eight international victories. No major titles. A world ranking that hovered in the 50s. Fox arrived at Royal Birkdale as a respectable professional golfer, not as a major champion in waiting. The oddsmakers had him at 200-1. When he holed the decisive putt on the final hole of the final round, he became one of the more improbable Open champions in recent memory.

His winner's share — $3.2 million — is the largest ever paid by the R&A, reflecting the $17.75 million total purse for the 2026 Open Championship. Fox did not dwell much on the money. He dwelled on the trophy and where it was going.

Going Home

Fox plans to bring the Claret Jug back to New Zealand. The country has now produced three Open champions, and Fox intends to give his homeland a proper look at the prize.

He will also return to the PGA Tour schedule in due course, with a calendar now permanently bookmarked by the memory of Royal Birkdale. But for now, the story is simpler: a 39-year-old who had never won a major championship holed the most important putt of his life, poured red wine into the oldest trophy in professional golf, and boarded a plane home with the man he narrowly beat.

Surreal. Unbelievable. Crazy. His words. All of them accurate.

What's Next

Ryan Fox will return to New Zealand to celebrate the Open Championship win before rejoining the PGA Tour schedule. Cameron Young, who earned $1,842,000 as runner-up, will look to convert his form from Royal Birkdale into a win in the coming weeks. Sam Burns finished T3 at $1,181,000 and will process one of the more emotional near-misses in recent major history. The PGA Tour resumes immediately with the 3M Open at TPC Twin Cities, July 23-26.
