Sam Burns Nearly Won The Open With a Two-Week-Old Daughter at Home

By Jake Torres | Golf Fanrecap | July 21, 2026

Two weeks before The Open Championship, Sam Burns became a father. His daughter Belle was born in Louisiana, and Burns made what seemed like the obvious choice: he would stay home. He would not play at Royal Birkdale.

Then he changed his mind. He boarded a flight to England.

What followed was one of the most extraordinary — and ultimately heartbreaking — performances in recent Open Championship history. Burns shot 62 in Round 2 and 65 in Round 3, producing the lowest two-round combination (Rounds 2 and 3) in men's major championship history. He entered Sunday with a two-shot lead at 10-under par. The Claret Jug was within reach. He almost certainly would have held it.

Then three consecutive bogeys changed everything.

The Collapse That Wasn't Coming

The damage came fast and early. Burns made bogey on the 4th hole. Then bogey on the 5th. Then bogey on the 6th. Three consecutive holes, three strokes dropped, and the two-shot cushion he carried into the final round was gone.

Burns made only one birdie all day. He could not recapture the form that had been almost preternatural through the first three rounds. Ryan Fox and Cameron Young were pressing, and Burns had nothing left to push back with.

On the 72nd hole — the 18th green at Royal Birkdale — Burns had a lengthy birdie putt that, if made, would have forced a tie with Young at nine-under and extended the championship to a playoff. He hit the putt. It grazed the edge of the cup. It stayed out.

The Numbers That Defined a Week

It is worth dwelling for a moment on what Burns accomplished before Sunday. His Round 2 score of 62 was extraordinary on its own. His Round 3 of 65 sustained it. Together, a 62 and a 65 across Rounds 2 and 3 represent the lowest such two-round combination in the history of men's major championship golf. That record now belongs to Sam Burns, even if the Claret Jug does not.

He finished T3 at 8-under par, earning $1,181,000. Ryan Fox, the 200-1 longshot who made the decisive putt on 18, claimed the Claret Jug and $3.2 million.

The Context That Amplifies Everything

Burns almost did not come. He had a newborn daughter at home — Belle, two weeks old. He had made a decision not to play, and then reversed it. He flew from Louisiana to England, which is not a simple journey, and arrived at Royal Birkdale with every reason to be distracted.

Instead, he was dominant for three rounds. He broke records. He led. And then, in the span of three holes on Sunday morning, the tournament that was nearly his became someone else's.

Few near-misses in recent major history carry the emotional weight of this one. The new baby. The last-minute decision to fly. The historic 36-hole stretch. The three-bogey stretch. The putt on 18 that grazed the cup. It is the kind of golf story that gets told for years.

What's Next

Sam Burns will return home to Louisiana following The Open Championship and to his daughter Belle, who at two weeks old now has a father who nearly made history — and, in the records category, actually did. Burns will continue on the PGA Tour schedule and will almost certainly carry the memory of Royal Birkdale into every major he plays from here forward. The PGA Tour resumes with the 3M Open at TPC Twin Cities, beginning July 23.
