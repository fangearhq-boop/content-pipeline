Aspen Acres Fire Update: 93,916 Acres, Partial Re-Entry Near Colorado City, Rain Expected Today

By Sarah Morales | COS Parenting | July 8, 2026

Colorado Springs, CO

The Aspen Acres Fire has burned nearly 94,000 acres across Custer and Pueblo counties — making it the 7th largest wildfire in Colorado history — and today brings two significant developments for families following the situation: partial re-entry has begun for some evacuated areas, and the first meaningful rain since the fire ignited is expected Wednesday.

As of Tuesday evening, July 7, the fire had burned 93,916 acres with containment at 16%. More than 1,600 firefighters are on the ground, with nearly 1,900 expected as resources continue to be assigned. At least 263 homes have been destroyed across Custer and Pueblo counties.

Partial Re-Entry Has Started

Some areas around Colorado City in Pueblo County were cleared for partial re-entry beginning Sunday, July 6. A Disaster Assistance Center opened Monday, July 7, to issue re-entry cards, and a public meeting was held Monday evening to brief affected residents.

Mandatory evacuations remain in effect across portions of Custer and Pueblo counties, including Colorado City, Beulah, Rye, San Isabel, Wetmore, Red Creek, and Apache City. A mandatory nighttime curfew from 9 PM to 7 AM is in place for impacted areas. A Level 2 "prepare to leave" warning remains in parts of Fremont County. If you know families affected by the fire, advise them to contact local emergency management before assuming their area is cleared for return.

Rain Is Expected Today — With Caveats

Wednesday's weather forecast is carrying the best news the fire has seen since it ignited June 29: monsoonal moisture is expected to push into southern Colorado, representing the first meaningful chance of rainfall since the fire began. Forecasters note this is genuine potential for precipitation — not just cloud cover.

The caveats are real, though. Thunderstorm development is also possible, which brings gusty outflow winds of up to 50 mph and lightning strikes that can start new ignitions in dry grass. Severely burned areas face elevated flash flood risk when rain falls on denuded soil that can no longer absorb water at normal rates. The weather could help — or it could introduce new problems alongside the benefit.

Colorado Springs Air Quality Today

For COS families making outdoor plans Wednesday, the air quality outlook is positive. As of Tuesday evening, the air quality index reading for Colorado Springs was 40 — in the Good range. The worst reading in the prior 24 hours was 53 (Moderate) on Monday afternoon. No Ozone Action Day advisory is in effect for the Front Range corridor through at least 4 PM Wednesday.

Wednesday's outdoor activities should be fine from an air quality standpoint. Check AirNow.gov before heading out to confirm conditions haven't shifted, particularly if fire behavior changes with the incoming weather system.

What's Next

Live fire status updates are available at InciWeb (inciweb.wildfire.gov — search "Aspen Acres Fire"). Air quality readings for your specific zip code are at AirNow.gov. For re-entry information and Disaster Assistance Center hours, check with Pueblo County Emergency Management and Custer County Emergency Management directly — conditions can change quickly as fire behavior and containment levels shift.
