### STORY 1: RECALL — GOPO Toys Pull String Teething Toys (Choking Hazard)
**Pillar:** National Parenting
**Tier:** 1
**Status:** NEW STORY (CPSC recall announced June 18, 2026)
**Sources:** cpsc.gov, yahoo.com, foxbusiness.com, dailyvoice.com
**Posting Window:** 8:00 AM MT
**Platforms:** X, Facebook, Article

The U.S. Consumer Product Safety Commission announced the recall of approximately 70,410 GOPO TOYS Pull String Teething Toys on June 18, 2026. The toys violate mandatory safety standards because the silicone strings are smaller and longer than permitted. The strings can reach the back of a baby's throat, posing a serious risk of respiratory distress and deadly choking. Three incidents have already been reported. The toys were sold on Amazon.com from August 2023 through March 2026 for $11–$15. They are described as an off-white disc with a grey ball in the center and six multi-colored silicone "tentacle" pull strings. To complete the recall, parents should stop use immediately, cut all silicone strings, write "DESTROYED" on the toy in permanent marker, photograph the destroyed toy, and email the photo to recalls@gopotoys.com for a full refund.

**Posting Window:** 3:00 PM MT
**Platforms:** X, Facebook, Article

Banning Lewis Academy (BLA), a District 49 charter school in Colorado Springs, is ending its management contract with ACCEL Schools effective June 30, 2026, and moving to direct local control. The change was triggered when a new management company was selected through a competitive process but then withdrew. BLA will now employ its staff directly, with additional support from partners in HR, finance, and operations. The WARN letter issued to 246 ACCEL employees (195 regular, 51 on-call/substitute) on April 30 reflects the end of their employment with ACCEL — not the closure of BLA. The school has confirmed there are no plans to close campuses, reduce programming, or impact student services. The 2026-2027 school year will open as planned.

### STORY 5: Summer Screen Time — What the Research Says and What COS Families Can Do
**Pillar:** National Parenting
**Tier:** 2
**Status:** NEW STORY (summer 2026 research synthesis)
**Sources:** blog.kodely.io, happydayplay.com, timily.app, chla.org
**Posting Window:** 5:00 PM MT
**Platforms:** X, Facebook, Article

New research shows 68% of children use screens significantly more during summer when school structure disappears. Children ages 8–10 average 6 hours of screen time per day; teens average over 8 hours. The American Academy of Pediatrics updated its approach in 2026, moving away from strict time limits toward a more individualized, content-focused framework. Passive, solo entertainment — especially social media for teens and fast-paced video for young children — carries the most concern. Creative, educational, and interactive screen use shows a different risk profile. Pediatricians recommend parents have proactive conversations about screen habits before summer gets underway, and establish consistent routines even when school is out.
