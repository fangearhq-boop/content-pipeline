Summer Screen Time 2026: What the Research Actually Says and What Families Can Do

By Jamie Rivera

June 25, 2026

Every summer, the same conversation comes up in parenting circles: how much screen time is too much, and what do we actually do about it? This year, there's a meaningful shift in the guidance — and it changes the frame of the question entirely.

In 2026, the American Academy of Pediatrics updated its approach to screen time, moving away from strict hour-based limits toward a more individualized, content-focused framework. Understanding that shift makes the summer conversation a lot more useful.

What the Numbers Look Like

Research consistently shows that screen time increases significantly when school structure disappears. Roughly 68% of children use screens meaningfully more during the summer months than during the school year.

Current averages by age group:

  • Children ages 8 to 10: average 6 hours of screen time per day
  • Teenagers: average more than 8 hours per day
  • Toddlers around age two: an average of 129 minutes per day (according to a UCL study)

Those numbers are not small. But the new guidance focuses less on the clock and more on what's happening on the screen — and why.

What the AAP Actually Recommends Now

The updated AAP framework asks parents to think about screen time in terms of content type and context rather than raw minutes. The categories that matter most:

Higher concern

Passive, fast-paced, solo screen use — especially social media for teenagers and fast-paced video content for children under age eight. This category is associated with disrupted attention, sleep interference, and social comparison in older kids. It's also the category that tends to increase most when summer removes the structure of a school day.

Lower concern

Creative, interactive, and educational screen use at any age. This includes video calls with family, creative apps (drawing, music, coding), co-viewing with parents, and educational content. The AAP's updated position is that not all screen time carries the same risk profile, and treating it as a single category leads parents to the wrong conclusions.

What Research Says Works

Pediatricians and researchers consistently point to two practical strategies that make a difference:

Proactive conversations before summer starts. Families that discuss expectations before the season begins — rather than reacting to problems mid-July — tend to maintain more consistent habits throughout. "We'll figure it out when school gets out" usually means figuring it out in September instead.

Consistent routines even without school structure. The research suggests that the structure of a school day does a lot of the heavy lifting during the year. When that disappears, screen time tends to fill the vacuum. Families who maintain some version of a consistent daily rhythm — not necessarily rigid scheduling, but predictable anchors — see smaller summer spikes in screen use.

A Different Way to Ask the Question

The most useful question isn't "how many hours is my kid using screens?" It's "what are they doing, and is there enough non-screen time in the day for the things that matter — sleep, physical activity, face-to-face connection, creative play?"

If the answer to those is yes, the screen time question becomes a lot easier to sit with. If the answer is no, the intervention isn't really about screens — it's about building in the other things first.

What's Next

The AAP's updated guidance is available through the American Academy of Pediatrics at aap.org. Children's Hospital Los Angeles also publishes clear, pediatrician-reviewed summaries at chla.org for parents who want to understand the research without wading through clinical language. If your family is navigating a specific concern about screen use — particularly around social media and older kids — starting with your child's pediatrician at their next well visit is the most direct path to personalized guidance.
