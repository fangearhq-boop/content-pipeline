PPLD Summer Adventure 2026 Starts June 1 — Free Reading Program for All Ages

By Jamie Rivera | COS Parenting | May 15, 2026

Summer break begins for most Colorado Springs families on May 22 when D11 schools close for the year. Two weeks later, on June 1, one of the best free summer activities in the region kicks off: the Pikes Peak Library District's Summer Adventure program, presented by the Friends of the Pikes Peak Library District.

The program is exactly what it sounds like. Read books, complete activities, earn prizes — and keep doing it all summer long. Sign-ups open June 1 at any of PPLD's 14 branches across the Pikes Peak region. There is no cost, but a library card is required to participate.

Getting a Library Card

If your kids don't already have a PPLD library card, getting one is free and takes just a few minutes at any branch location. Cards unlock physical books, e-books, and audiobooks through apps like Libby and Hoopla, plus access to PPLD's digital resources and museum pass lending program. Signing kids up for their own card — even at a young age — tends to increase engagement with the library and with reading generally.

Parents and guardians can register children for cards as well as themselves. The Summer Adventure program is open to adults, which makes it genuinely a whole-family activity if you want it to be.

Summer Events and Programs

Alongside the reading and activity challenge, PPLD runs summer programming throughout the season — storytimes, maker activities, teen events, and community programs at various branches. The full summer schedule is typically posted at ppld.org in late May or early June. Spots for some programs fill quickly, so checking the site when it goes live is worthwhile.

PPLD's events are another avenue for families looking for structured, free activities on summer weekdays when camps or childcare aren't in the picture.

Why It Matters: Summer Learning Loss

Research consistently shows that kids who read during summer months maintain stronger academic skills heading into the fall. The "summer slide" — the loss of skills that can occur over an extended break without any reading — is documented across all grade levels but hits hardest in earlier elementary years. A reading program with built-in rewards and community events is one of the most accessible tools families have to counteract it, especially at no cost.
