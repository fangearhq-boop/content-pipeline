Hot Car Danger: What Colorado Springs Families Need to Know This Summer

By Jamie Rivera | COS Parenting | June 26, 2026

Six children have died from vehicular heatstroke in the United States so far in 2026. Nationally, about 40 children die this way every year. As Colorado Springs heads into the hottest stretch of summer, this is the reminder that every parent and caregiver needs to hear — not because you'd intentionally leave your child in a car, but because the circumstances that lead to these deaths are far more ordinary than most parents believe.

How Fast a Car Heats Up

The numbers from NHTSA are stark. When the outside temperature is 90°F — a completely routine Colorado summer afternoon — the interior of a parked car can exceed 100°F in as little as five minutes. Interior temperatures can climb to 50°F above the outside air temperature. Cracking a window does not meaningfully reduce the rate of heat buildup.

A young child's body heats up three to five times faster than an adult's, according to the American Academy of Pediatrics. Children also dehydrate more quickly. The combination of rapid heat accumulation in a parked vehicle and a child's limited ability to regulate body temperature is what makes vehicular heatstroke so dangerous and so fast.

Heatstroke begins when the body temperature reaches 104°F. It becomes fatal at 107°F. For a child left in a hot car, that threshold can be reached in a matter of minutes on a warm day.

Who This Happens To

One of the most important things to understand about hot car deaths is that they are not limited to neglectful or inattentive parents. Research on these incidents has found that a significant portion involve loving, responsible caregivers who experienced a break in their routine — a forgotten appointment, a change in who was dropping off, cognitive autopilot on a familiar drive.

The scenario most often described in these cases: a caregiver intends to drop a child at daycare, but something disrupts the routine. The child falls asleep in the back seat and becomes quiet. The caregiver's brain — following the familiar pattern of the drive — completes the trip without the usual daycare stop. The child is still in the back seat when the caregiver parks at work.

This is not hypothetical. It's the documented pattern in a substantial portion of hot car deaths. Understanding that it can happen to attentive parents is the first step in building habits that prevent it.

Prevention Habits That Work

  • Put something in the back seat. Your phone, your bag, your left shoe — anything you need when you get out of the car. It creates a mandatory check of the back seat before you walk away.
  • Ask your child's caregiver to call if they don't arrive. A quick check-in system means a missed drop-off gets caught within minutes.
  • Learn your car's rear passenger alert. All new vehicles from the 2025 model year onward are federally required to include a rear passenger alert system. If your car has this feature, know how it works and make sure it's enabled.
  • Never leave a child in the car "just for a minute." Even a brief errand can take longer than expected. A car in direct Colorado sun heats dangerously fast.

If You See a Child Alone in a Hot Car

Call 911 immediately. Don't wait to see if someone returns. Don't walk into the store to look for the caregiver. Call 911 first, then stay with the car until help arrives. In Colorado, as in most states, good Samaritans who break a car window to rescue a child in danger are protected from civil liability. But get 911 on the line first — they can guide you.

What's Next

Colorado Springs will see continued heat through the summer. The habits above are worth building now and sharing with anyone who cares for your children — grandparents, babysitters, carpool drivers. For more information on hot car prevention, Kids and Car Safety at kidsandcars.org and NoHeatStroke.org are the leading national resources. The National Safety Council also tracks annual heatstroke data at nsc.org.
