Three Baby Bath Seats Recalled for Drowning and Entrapment Risk — Check Yours Now

By Sarah Morales | COS Parenting | June 26, 2026

If you have a baby bath seat at home, this is worth two minutes of your time. The Consumer Product Safety Commission has issued separate recalls for three different baby bath seat brands in 2026 — NFSVLB, YCXXKJ (sold by BenTalk), and Trankerloop — all for the same reason: a baby can slip through the leg opening and become trapped underwater. All three models violate the mandatory federal safety standard for infant bath seats.

These are not products that simply failed a minor compliance test. The hazard is real: the seats become unstable and tip over during use, and the leg openings are large enough that an infant's torso can slide down until the child is entrapped. In that position, a baby cannot free themselves. The risk of drowning is direct and immediate.

For the NFSVLB seat specifically: the model number ZY2025 is printed on a label on the side of the seat. If you have a blue and white bath seat with detachable arms and four suction cups, check that label. If you're not sure whether your seat is on the recalled list, go directly to cpsc.gov and search the product name — the full recall notices are posted there with detailed descriptions and contact information for refunds.

Bath Seats Are Not Safety Devices

This is worth understanding beyond the recalls themselves. Baby bath seats have never been designed to keep a baby safe if left unattended. The CPSC has warned about this for years, and the warning is unchanged: bath seats are a bath aid that helps position a baby during washing. They are not a substitute for a caregiver's hands or attention.

The recommendation is simple and absolute: stay within arm's reach of your baby any time they are in the tub. That means no phone, no answering the door, no running to get a towel from the next room. Infant drowning can happen in seconds and in very shallow water. A bath seat — any bath seat, recalled or not — does not change that equation.

If you have one of the recalled products, stop using it now. You don't need to wait to find out whether your specific unit is defective. All units in the recall are covered, and the remedy is a full refund from the seller.

How to Check for Recalls

The CPSC maintains a free database of all active recalls at cpsc.gov. You can search by brand name, product type, or hazard category. It's worth bookmarking and checking a few times a year — baby gear in particular has a high rate of recall because of the safety standards that apply to products for infants and young children.

Consumer Reports also maintains a running list of 2026 baby product recalls that's updated as new notices are issued. If you've bought baby gear on Amazon in the past year, it's worth a quick cross-check against both databases.

What's Next

If you have one of the recalled seats, contact the seller for a refund and dispose of the product so it can't be passed along or donated. If you're shopping for a bath seat replacement, look for products that carry current compliance certification and check cpsc.gov before purchasing. And regardless of what bath seat you use, the safest bath routine for any baby is one where a caregiver is present and within arm's reach the entire time.
