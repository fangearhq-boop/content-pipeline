### STORY 1: Baby Bath Seat Recalls — Drowning and Entrapment Risk

**Long-Form Post**

If you have a baby bath seat, please check it right now.

The CPSC has recalled three different baby bath seat models — NFSVLB, YCXXKJ (sold by BenTalk), and Trankerloop — all for the same terrifying reason: they're unstable, and a baby can slip through the leg opening and become entrapped underwater. All three violate the mandatory safety standard for infant bath seats.

The NFSVLB recall alone covers about 1,430 seats sold on Amazon between May and October of 2025. The seat is blue and white with detachable arms and four suction cups on the bottom — model number ZY2025 on the label. If you have it, stop using it and contact the seller for a full refund.

Here's the most important thing to know regardless of recalls: bath seats are a bath aid, not a safety device. They are not designed to hold a baby safely if left alone. The CPSC recommends that you stay within arm's reach of your baby any time they're in the tub — no exceptions, no phone breaks, no answering the door.

If you have one of the recalled seats, stop using it. Check cpsc.gov for the full list and remedy details.

Have you checked whether your baby gear is on the recall list lately? It's worth a few minutes.

### STORY 2: 4th of July COS Family Guide

**Long-Form Post**

The 4th of July is almost here, and COS families have two great options for fireworks this year.

Ford Amphitheater is hosting a full evening — gates open at 5:30 PM, the show kicks off at 6:30 PM, and fireworks light up the sky around 9 PM MT. It's a full summer night out, and one of the most popular spots in the area for the holiday.

If you're looking for something a bit more laid-back, Palmer Lake is worth the drive. The celebration runs from 4–10 PM MT, and the lake backdrop makes for a beautiful fireworks setting. It's about 20 miles north of Colorado Springs on I-25 — an easy trip for families.

A few tips from parents who've done this before: arrive early for parking (both spots get crowded), bring a blanket or lawn chairs, and pack earplugs for little ones who are still getting used to fireworks noise. A pair of kids' ear protection can make the difference between a meltdown and a magical night. 🎆

### STORY 3: D49 Student Success Center

**Long-Form Post**

D49 families — there's a new resource coming your way this August.

District 49 is opening a Student Success Center at the start of the 2026–2027 school year, housed in what was formerly Falcon Elementary School of Technology. It's designed to bring together specialized support programs under one roof, and it's a meaningful change for families who need those services.

The center will run two full-time preschool classrooms on a Monday–Thursday schedule, available to staff and families throughout the district. It'll also be home to the PEAK programs, which support students with disabilities who need higher-level intervention — with the goal of providing a more school-like environment as students work toward returning to their neighborhood school. And the Elevates 18–21 Transition Services program, which serves young adults in that age range, will relocate to the new center.

If your family is connected to any of these programs, more details are available at d49.org. The district's official announcement is posted on the district updates board.

D49 families — are you familiar with any of these programs? We'd love to hear from parents who've navigated the PEAK or Elevates programs.

### STORY 4: Hot Car Safety

**Long-Form Post**

This is a reminder that every parent needs, even if you're absolutely certain you'd never forget your child.

Six children in the United States have already died from being left in hot cars in 2026. About 40 die every year. And these are not all cases of negligent parents — many are loving, attentive caregivers who got distracted, changed a routine, or fell into a moment of cognitive autopilot.

Here's what happens inside a parked car: when it's 90°F outside — not even a record-breaking heat day, just a normal Colorado summer afternoon — the interior of a parked car can exceed 100°F in just five minutes. A child's body heats up three to five times faster than an adult's, and they dehydrate more quickly too. The risk escalates fast.

A few things worth knowing: all new vehicles from the 2025 model year onward are required to have a rear passenger alert system. If your car is newer, learn how yours works. If it's older, a visual reminder on your steering wheel or a bag left in the back seat can help break the autopilot that gets people into trouble. And if you ever see a child alone in a hot car, call 911 immediately — don't wait.

Colorado summers get hot. This week is a good time to review your habits and your car. Have you had this conversation with other caregivers in your family?

### STORY 5: Colorado State Fair 2026

**Long-Form Post**

Start making your plans — the Colorado State Fair is coming, and it's worth every minute of the drive from Colorado Springs.

This year's fair runs August 28 through September 7 at 1001 Beulah Avenue in Pueblo. That's about 60 miles south of COS on I-25, roughly 50–60 minutes depending on traffic. For families, it's a late-summer tradition that overlaps with the start of school — and a fantastic way to squeeze one last big outing into the season.

What's there for kids: pig racing, dog shows, a stilt circus, amusement park rides, and all the livestock barns you could ask for. The rodeo runs throughout the fair and includes bull riding and barrel racing. For music fans, the Big R Arena hosts three nights of country music on August 28, 29, and 30, with Ian Munsick, Dylan Scott, and Neal McCoy.

One highlight that's worth calling out specifically: Sensory Day is September 1 from 11 AM to 2 PM, designed for people with sensory sensitivities. If you have a child who benefits from a lower-stimulation window, that's the slot to mark.

Hours are Friday through Monday 11 AM–11 PM and Tuesday through Thursday 3–11 PM. Check coloradostatefair.com as the date gets closer for tickets and updated programming.
