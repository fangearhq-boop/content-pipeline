D11's Labor Dispute at 300 Days: What COS Families Need to Know

By Jamie Rivera | COS Parenting | May 16, 2026

Colorado Springs School District 11 wraps up the 2025-26 school year on May 22 — but for teachers, parents, and school board members in the district, the past year has involved a labor dispute that never really left the background. In early May, educators and parents rallied outside a D11 Board meeting to mark more than 300 days since the district ended its master agreement with the Colorado Springs Education Association (CSEA), the union that represents D11 teachers.

For families with kids in D11 schools, understanding what happened and what it might mean for next year is worth a few minutes of your time — especially with fall 2026 enrollment decisions and staffing questions on the horizon.

What the Master Agreement Was — and Why It Ended

The master agreement between D11 and CSEA was a collectively bargained contract that governed teacher pay structures, working conditions, grievance processes, and job protections. It had been in place for more than 50 years. In June 2025, the D11 Board of Education voted 7-1 to allow it to expire rather than renew it.

In its place, the district introduced an employee handbook that it says governs working conditions and compensation. The union argues that an employee handbook doesn't carry the same legal weight as a collectively bargained contract, and that teachers lost meaningful job protections in the transition. The district rejects that characterization.

The October Strike and Ongoing Tensions

The dispute came to a head in October 2025, when hundreds of D11 teachers went on strike — one of the first actions of its kind in the Colorado Springs region in recent memory. The strike drew significant attention and raised questions about teacher morale, retention, and the district's long-term staffing outlook.

The picket in early May 2026 — organized by educators and parents who wanted to keep the issue in front of the Board — reflects that tensions haven't fully resolved despite the school year continuing. From the picket organizers' perspective, 300 days without a binding contract is not a problem that resolves itself.

The D11 Board, for its part, has consistently stated that it's focused on moving the district forward and that it rejects what it describes as misinformation from union organizers. The district points to its current employee handbook as a functional replacement for the expired agreement.

For COS families, the practical questions center on teacher retention and school stability going into fall 2026. When teachers operate without a long-term collective bargaining agreement, some choose to transfer to other districts with stronger protections. That matters most in schools that were already managing turnover.

It's also worth watching what happens over the summer in terms of negotiations — whether D11 and CSEA reach any new agreements before the 2026-27 school year begins, or whether the dispute remains unresolved heading into a second year.

What's Next

D11's last day of school is May 22. D49's Sand Creek High graduation is May 23 at Broadmoor World Arena. Summer brings a pause in visible labor activity, but the underlying question — whether D11 will reach a new agreement with its teachers' union — remains open. Families in D11 can monitor updates at d11.org. For questions about individual schools, principals can speak to staffing plans for fall 2026. The Colorado Springs Education Association's communications are available at csea-cos.org.
