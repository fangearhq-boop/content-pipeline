CPSC Recall: 125,200 Cosyland Children's Tower Stools — Stop Use Now

By Sarah Morales | COS Parenting | May 16, 2026

If your family uses a Cosyland children's tower stool as a kitchen helper for your toddler or preschooler, stop using it immediately. The Consumer Product Safety Commission announced on May 14 that Cosyland has recalled 125,200 units due to fall and entrapment hazards that have already caused eight documented injuries, including a fractured arm.

The recalled models — CS0003 and CS0092-4, sold in natural bamboo and gray — can develop a serious problem over time: the interior platform loosens, becomes unstable, and can ultimately detach. That instability creates a fall hazard for young children standing on the step. Compounding the concern, openings on the front and back sides of the stool are large enough for a child's torso to fit through, creating an entrapment hazard if the unit tips.

The Hazard in Detail

Kitchen tower stools — also called learning towers or kitchen helpers — let young children stand safely at counter height to participate in cooking and food preparation. They're popular with families because they give toddlers a secure platform without relying on chairs or step stools that can slide.

The issue with the Cosyland models is structural. The platform inside the stool frame is designed to be fixed and stable, but the CPSC investigation found that it can loosen over time through normal use. Once the platform becomes loose, the risk compounds: a child who shifts their weight unexpectedly — which toddlers do constantly — can cause the unit to tip or collapse. If a tip occurs with the child still inside, the front or back opening poses an entrapment risk.

The CPSC reports 25 consumer complaints involving stability concerns and falls, with eight resulting in injuries. One child sustained a fractured arm. Others reported scrapes and contusions consistent with a fall from approximately counter height.

What to Do Right Now

Cosyland is providing a free repair kit that includes a protective net, stabilizing feet, and installation instructions. The kit addresses both the entrapment risk (net) and the stability concern (feet). Consumers need to contact Cosyland directly to receive the kit — it's mailed to you at no cost.

While you wait for the repair kit, the CPSC's instruction is clear: stop using the recalled stool and store it somewhere your children cannot access it. Do not try to continue using it with a temporary fix or additional bracing. The repair kit is specifically designed to address the structural issue.

To reach Cosyland, call 866-677-3889 (Monday through Friday, 9 AM to 5 PM PT) or visit cosyland.com/pages/recall. You can also email customerservice@cosyland.com. The company can be reached at cosyland.com — click "Product Recalls" at the top of the page.
