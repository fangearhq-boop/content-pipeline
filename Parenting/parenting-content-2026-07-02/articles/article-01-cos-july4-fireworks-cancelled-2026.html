All Colorado Springs July 4 Fireworks Shows Cancelled Due to Critical Fire Danger — What Families Need to Know

By Sarah Morales | July 2, 2026

City officials cancelled all professional July 4 fireworks displays on July 1, citing fuel moisture levels comparable to conditions before the 2012 Waldo Canyon Fire. Here's what that means for families this weekend.

Colorado Springs families planning around fireworks this July 4 should know: all professional, permitted fireworks displays in the city are cancelled. Mayor Yemi Mobolade, the Colorado Springs Fire Department, the Colorado Springs Police Department, and public safety partners announced the decision on July 1 after evaluating fire conditions across the region.

The reason officials gave is stark. Current fuel moisture levels in Colorado Springs are comparable to those measured in the days before the 2012 Waldo Canyon Fire — one of the most destructive wildfires in Colorado history. Officials also cited the ongoing strain on firefighting resources across the state, with 15 wildfires currently burning in Colorado at the time of the decision.

What Is Cancelled

All permitted, professional July 4 fireworks displays in Colorado Springs are cancelled. This includes the traditional fireworks finale at the Star-Spangled Symphony at Ford Amphitheater, which has been a centerpiece of COS July 4 celebrations. The symphony performance itself continues — just without the fireworks.

The cancellation covers organized shows across the city that require a permit. It does not cancel the non-fireworks programming at community events happening Saturday.

Personal Fireworks Rules — Know Where You Are

Consumer fireworks — including sparklers — are permanently banned within Colorado Springs city limits. This is not a 2026-specific rule. It applies every year, and it applies on July 4 regardless of whether there are professional shows or not. The fines for violating the ban can reach up to $2,650 per violation.

If you live in unincorporated El Paso County — outside city limits — the picture is more complicated. El Paso County is under Stage II fire restrictions, the highest restriction level in Colorado. However, a Colorado state law prevents counties from restricting the sale and use of consumer fireworks between May 31 and July 5 unless the Board of County Commissioners adopts a specific resolution. As of July 2, El Paso County had not adopted such a resolution.

Mayor Mobolade has publicly asked El Paso County residents not to use personal fireworks this weekend, noting that consumer fireworks launched in unincorporated areas pose risk not just to those areas but to the wider region.

What Is Still Happening

All non-fireworks July 4 programming continues as planned. Rock Ledge Ranch runs its Old-Fashioned July 4 celebration from 10 a.m. to 4 p.m. with historical interpreters, live music, and family activities. The Banning Lewis Ranch community event at Vista Park Pavilion runs from 3 p.m. with food trucks and live music. The Star-Spangled Symphony at Ford Amphitheater still performs — gates at 5:30 p.m., show at 6:30 p.m. The National Museum of WWII Aviation is open for July 4 programming from 10 a.m. to 5 p.m.

The city's emphasis is on the fire danger, not on cancelling July 4 itself. The decision reflects how serious current conditions are — not an attempt to dampen the holiday.

What's Next

The fire restrictions currently in place — Stage II for El Paso County, effective since June 29 — will remain in effect as long as conditions warrant. Families planning outdoor activities this weekend should check current conditions at the El Paso County Sheriff's Office fire information page and the City of Colorado Springs website.

For now: the fireworks are off, the day is still on, and staying safe is the priority. Colorado Springs has been through significant wildfires before, and the community knows what the risk looks like when officials take it seriously enough to make this call.
