America Turns 250 and Colorado Turns 150 This Year — Colorado Springs Has a Summer Full of Ways to Mark Both

By Sarah Morales | July 2, 2026

2026 is a milestone year for both the country and the state. Colorado Springs has events and historic sites all summer as part of its "Celebrate America 250 and Colorado 150" programming — and with July 4 fireworks cancelled, the daytime history-focused events are the main show.

This is an unusual July 4 in Colorado Springs. The professional fireworks are cancelled. The fire danger is real and serious. And the community is navigating what it means to celebrate a major national holiday in the middle of drought-level fire conditions.

But 2026 is also a genuinely remarkable year for families who want to engage with history. America turns 250 years old. Colorado turns 150. These are not trivial anniversaries — they're the kind of milestones that come around once in a lifetime, or less.

What Colorado Springs Has Designated

The City of Colorado Springs has organized "Celebrate America 250 and Colorado 150" programming running through the summer. The initiative connects families to the area's historic sites, community events, and patriotic programming as part of marking both anniversaries.

Colorado Springs has a considerable amount of history within a short drive. Garden of the Gods preserves land that people have inhabited for thousands of years and became a public park in 1909. Rock Ledge Ranch Historic Site — one of the July 4 options still open this weekend — brings to life how Colorado Springs families lived from the 1860s through the early 20th century. The Air Force Academy, the Cheyenne Mountain Zoo, the U.S. Olympic and Paralympic Museum: all of them connect to the story of Colorado and of the country in different ways.

Why This Summer Is Different

With the fireworks cancelled, families have more reason to look at what's available in daylight hours — and there is a lot available. The fire conditions that cancelled the fireworks are the same conditions that make outdoor exploration of places like Garden of the Gods, Cheyenne Mountain State Park, and Palmer Park feel especially vivid this time of year. The mountains, the rocks, the sky: Colorado is the backdrop, and it's a 250-year-old backdrop that looks the same this summer as it did in 1776.

Telling the story of where you live is one of the most durable things you can do with kids during a long summer. July 4, 2026 — without the fireworks but with the context of two major anniversaries — is one of the better prompts to start that conversation that families are likely to get for a while.

What's Next

The America 250 and Colorado 150 programming continues through the summer. For specific events and dates, visit coloradosprings.gov/America250Colorado150 and visitcos.com. Rock Ledge Ranch's Old-Fashioned July 4 celebration this Saturday (10 a.m.–4 p.m., $7–$13, ages 3 and under free) is the most direct example of the kind of programming the milestone year has generated — and it's happening even without fireworks.

Colorado Springs in 2026 has everything it always had: the mountain, the parks, the history, the community. The fireworks were always the smallest part of what made the place worth celebrating.
