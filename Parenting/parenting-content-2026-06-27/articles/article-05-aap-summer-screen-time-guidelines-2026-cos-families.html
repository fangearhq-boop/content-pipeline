AAP Screen Time Guidelines for Summer 2026: What COS Parents Need to Know

By Jamie Rivera | COS Parenting | June 27, 2026

Summer changes the screen time equation in almost every household. School structures disappear, routines relax, and the tablet or TV starts filling gaps that used to be filled by class time, homework, and afterschool schedules. Most parents feel it happening and aren't sure whether to intervene or let it ride.

The American Academy of Pediatrics updated its guidance in 2026 in a way that's actually useful for this exact situation. Here's what's changed, what's stayed the same, and what it means for Colorado Springs families right now.

What the AAP Says in 2026

The biggest shift in this year's guidance is that the AAP has moved away from treating screen time primarily as a time-limit problem. The updated framework focuses on quality, context, and what screen time is displacing — not just how many minutes are accumulating. That's a more honest reflection of how screens actually work in family life.

But the age-specific guardrails haven't gone away. They've gotten more nuanced, but they're still in place.

Quick Reference: AAP Screen Time Guidelines (2026)

Age Group Guideline
Under 18 months No screens except video calls
18–24 months High-quality content only; watch together with a parent or caregiver
Ages 2–5 Maximum one hour per day; co-view and discuss what's on screen
Ages 6 and up Consistent limits that don't displace sleep, physical activity, family time, or free play

The Summer Risk the AAP Is Watching

The specific summer concern the AAP flags isn't that kids are watching TV — it's that screens are filling the unstructured time that school used to structure. That matters because the activities screens tend to replace are the ones with the most developmental benefit: free play, outdoor time, face-to-face interaction with other kids, and adequate sleep.

For kids under six, the time limits still hold regardless of content quality. For older kids, the framework is more about what's being sacrificed. If a nine-year-old is watching an hour of YouTube before bed and it's cutting into their sleep, that's the issue — not the YouTube itself.

What Co-Viewing Actually Means

The AAP's push for co-viewing with kids under five isn't just about supervision. When a parent watches with a child and talks about what's happening on screen — asking questions, explaining things, connecting it to real life — the developmental benefit of that screen time goes up significantly. Content that's passively consumed without any adult engagement is the lowest-value version of screen time, even if it's technically "high-quality."

For families with toddlers, this is the most actionable piece of the updated guidance: it's not just about limiting minutes, it's about being present for the ones that happen.

Free COS Alternatives Right Now

Colorado Springs has a solid lineup of no-cost outdoor options this summer that make it easier to fill unstructured time without defaulting to screens.

America the Beautiful Park at 126 Cimino Drive has the Julie Penrose Fountain — a splash-pad-style water feature that's free and works well for younger kids on a warm afternoon. Garden of the Gods has shaded trails that are stroller-accessible and genuinely beautiful even on a short walk. Pikes Peak Library District's Summer Adventure is running through July 31 with free programming at 14 locations across Colorado Springs — the activities count toward the 30-day summer challenge, and there's no cost to participate.

None of these require reservations, advance planning, or money. Sometimes the alternative to screens is as simple as walking out the door.
