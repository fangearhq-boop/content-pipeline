### STORY 1: MedPride Baby Oil Recall

#### Long-Form Post — 8:00 AM MT

Quick heads-up for Colorado Springs parents who shop on Amazon: there's a new product recall you need to know about right now.

Shield Line recalled approximately 8,420 bottles of MedPride Baby Oil on June 25. The issue is the packaging — it's not child-resistant as required by federal law, specifically the Poison Prevention Packaging Act. That matters a lot with this product because MedPride Baby Oil contains low-viscosity hydrocarbons. If swallowed by a young child, the petroleum distillates can enter the lungs and cause chemical pneumonia or severe pulmonary damage. Both can be fatal.

The recalled bottles are clear, with a pink cap and white-and-pink labels with the "MedPride" logo in pink lettering. No incidents have been reported, which means taking action now — before anything happens — is exactly the right move.

What to do: secure the oil completely out of reach of your children right now, and contact Shield Line for a full refund. The company will ask you to send a photo of the product in the trash before issuing the refund. You can also visit cpsc.gov or call the CPSC hotline at 1-800-638-2772 for more details.

If you've already shared this bottle with friends, family members, or your childcare provider, please pass along the recall information.

### STORY 2: Colorado Springs 4th of July Family Guide

#### Long-Form Post — 10:00 AM MT

One week, Colorado Springs. July 4, 2026, is almost here — and this year it carries double weight: America's 250th birthday and Colorado's 150th.

Here's your quick guide to what's happening for families across COS:

The Star-Spangled Symphony and Grand Fireworks Show at Ford Amphitheater (95 Spectrum Loop) is the marquee ticketed event. Gates open at 5:30 PM, the Colorado Springs Philharmonic starts at 6:30 PM, and fireworks go up around 9:00 PM. The program features Dolly Parton, Tina Turner, Marvin Gaye hits, a Star Wars suite, the 1812 Overture, and Stars and Stripes Forever. It's a full evening.

Prefer something free? Banning Lewis Ranch is hosting a July 4 Celebration at Vista Park Pavilion — two live bands, 30 food trucks, and fireworks at dusk. Palmer Lake Centennial Park runs from 4 to 10 PM with fireworks around 9:15 PM, local vendors, and family-friendly activities. Both are no-cost options.

For families who want to skip the noise but still make a day of it, Rock Ledge Ranch Historic Site near Garden of the Gods hosts an old-fashioned Fourth from 10 AM to 4 PM — think carnival games, a pie-eating contest, historical interpreters, and patriotic music. No fireworks, but a genuinely fun daytime experience for younger kiddos.

### STORY 3: Rosemont Reservoir Update

#### Long-Form Post — 12:00 PM MT

If you've been watching the Rosemont Reservoir news, here's the update: it may be open for camping and boating as early as July 4 weekend.

Colorado Springs City Council approved expanded recreation at the reservoir earlier this year through a partnership between Colorado Springs Utilities and Colorado Parks and Wildlife. The target opening date was tied to the Colorado 150th and US 250th anniversary celebrations — meaning July 4, 2026. That's not a firm guarantee, but it's a week away and worth knowing about if you're planning outdoor adventures with your family.

Here's what to expect: Rosemont Reservoir sits about 12 miles southwest of Colorado Springs at more than 9,600 feet on the south side of Pikes Peak. The planned amenities include basic campsites, day-use parking, ADA-accessible restrooms, and non-motorized boating — kayaks, canoes, paddleboards, and electric watercraft are all welcome. No swimming or wading (the reservoir serves as a water source), but the elevation and views should make it a genuinely spectacular outing.

You'll need a Colorado State Parks Pass or Keep Colorado Wild pass, plus a camping fee. Reservations go through CPW's reservation system.

We'll post an update as soon as the official opening is confirmed. Follow COS Parenting so you don't miss it.

If you haven't signed up yet, you can start today. The 30-day window doesn't have to run consecutively — you work toward it through any combination of activities. There's genuinely no reason not to.

### STORY 5: Summer Screen Time and What the AAP Says

#### Long-Form Post — 5:00 PM MT

Here's the summer pattern most of us know: school gets out, routines relax, and screen time quietly climbs.

The American Academy of Pediatrics updated its 2026 guidance on this, and the shift is worth knowing about. They've moved away from strict time-limit rules toward something more nuanced — quality, context, and what the screen time is replacing. But they've kept the core guardrails in place.

Here's the practical breakdown:
- Under 18 months: no screens at all, except video calls
- Ages 18–24 months: only high-quality content, and watch it together
- Ages 2–5: maximum one hour per day, co-viewed and discussed
- Ages 6 and up: consistent limits that protect sleep, physical activity, family time, and free play

The big summer risk is that screen time starts filling the gaps that school used to fill. That's not inherently a crisis, but it's worth being intentional about.

Colorado Springs actually has a lot of free options going right now. America the Beautiful Park has the Julie Penrose Fountain — free, splash-pad style, great for younger kids on a warm day. Garden of the Gods has shaded trails that work for strollers. PPLD has Summer Adventure programming happening at libraries through July 31.

None of these require a plan, a reservation, or money. Sometimes the alternative to screens is just walking out the door.
