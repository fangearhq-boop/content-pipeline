D11 Considers $775 Million Bond for November Ballot: What It Means for Colorado Springs Schools

By Jamie Rivera | COS Parenting | June 24, 2026

The Colorado Springs School District 11 board is weighing whether to place a $775 million bond measure on the November 2026 ballot — a decision that could reshape school buildings across the district over the next several years. The board must vote by the end of summer to put the measure before voters in November.

The bond is driven by a straightforward problem: D11's school buildings are old and in need of significant repair. The district's average building is 58 years old, and the backlog of identified repair needs already exceeds $422 million. That gap has been growing for years as deferred maintenance compounds.

What the Bond Would Fund

D11 has outlined the following priorities for bond spending:

  • Building repairs and system replacements — roofs, HVAC, plumbing, electrical
  • Air conditioning — many D11 schools currently lack AC, a significant quality-of-life and health issue in Colorado summers
  • Safety and security upgrades — building access controls, security improvements
  • School modernizations — renovating older buildings to support current learning environments
  • New construction — where population growth or building condition warrants

The district has committed that every school in D11 would receive at least $1 million in investment if the bond passes.

The Tax Impact

The bond would increase D11's mill levy from 37.7 to 46.3 mills — an increase of 8.6 mills. For a Colorado Springs homeowner, that translates to roughly $6.50 per month for every $100,000 of assessed property value.

If your home's assessed value is $300,000, you'd pay approximately $19.50 more per month. At $500,000 assessed value, that's approximately $32.50 per month.

Teacher Pay Included

One element that distinguishes this bond proposal from a standard facilities measure: it would also enable a $10,000 annual pay increase for every D11 teacher. That increase would apply to teachers at every career stage — from first-year educators to those with 30 or more years of experience.

D11 has faced teacher retention challenges alongside its facilities challenges. Pairing compensation improvement with building investment is designed to address both at once.

Community Support

Early polling shows approximately 54–58% community support for the bond — above 50%, but below the higher thresholds that give campaigns comfortable margins. Bond measures in Colorado require a simple majority to pass.

D11 has set up a community input page at d112026bondplanning.com where families can review detailed project lists by school, ask questions, and submit comments. If you have a specific school in mind, the site breaks down planned investments at the individual school level.

What's Next

The D11 board must vote to place the measure on the November ballot by the end of summer 2026. The board has been holding community meetings through the spring and summer; check d112026bondplanning.com for upcoming events and the timeline for the board vote.

If the board approves placing the bond on the ballot, D11 voters will decide in November 2026. The district's full FAQ is available at d11.org/bond/faq.
