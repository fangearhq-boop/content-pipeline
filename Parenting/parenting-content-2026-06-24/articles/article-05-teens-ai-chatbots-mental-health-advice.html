1 in 5 Teens Are Using AI Chatbots for Mental Health Advice — And Most Haven't Told Anyone

By Sarah Morales | COS Parenting | June 24, 2026

Approximately 1 in 5 American adolescents and young adults between the ages of 12 and 21 have turned to AI chatbots for mental health advice — and 63% of them haven't told anyone. That's the headline finding from a new study published in JAMA Pediatrics on June 10, 2026.

The numbers reflect a behavior that's grown quietly and quickly. Use of AI chatbots for mental health support jumped more than 40% in a single year, from 13.1% to 19.2% of young people in that age range. In total, that's roughly 8.2 million young Americans using platforms like ChatGPT, Gemini, Character.AI, or Meta AI when they're struggling emotionally.

Why Teens Are Turning to Chatbots

The study, which was conducted by researchers affiliated with the RAND Corporation, doesn't identify a single cause — but the pattern fits what researchers already know about adolescent help-seeking behavior. Teens often prefer to process difficult feelings privately before deciding whether to bring them to a parent, counselor, or peer. A chatbot offers that private space with zero social risk.

The fact that 43% of these teens are seeking chatbot mental health advice at least once a month suggests this isn't casual curiosity. For a significant share of young people, AI has become a regular part of how they manage their emotional lives.

The chatbots being used are general-purpose AI tools, not platforms designed for mental health support. ChatGPT, Gemini, and Meta AI are designed for broad informational assistance. Character.AI, which allows users to chat with fictional or custom AI personas, is particularly popular among teens.

What the Researchers Recommend

The study's authors are careful not to conclude that AI chatbot use is inherently harmful. But they do raise concerns about teens who are using chatbots as a primary or exclusive source of mental health support — particularly if that use is replacing or delaying connection to evidence-based care.

Their recommendations fall into two categories:

For clinicians: Pediatricians, therapists, and school counselors should proactively ask patients and students about their AI chatbot use — specifically whether they're turning to AI for emotional support or mental health advice. This conversation isn't currently standard in most clinical settings.

For parents: Start the conversation before your teen does. If you find out your teenager has been going to ChatGPT when they're anxious or depressed, that's useful information — but it's easier to have the conversation about AI and mental health when it's not attached to a crisis. Knowing how your teen uses AI overall, including for emotional processing, is part of understanding their world.

Starting the Conversation

If you're not sure how to open this topic with your teen, here are a few low-pressure approaches:

  • Ask what they use AI for in general — homework, entertainment, questions. That opens the door to a broader conversation about how they interact with these tools.
  • Mention the study directly. "I saw a study that said a lot of teens use ChatGPT for mental health stuff. Do you ever do that?" is a straightforward opener that doesn't feel accusatory.
  • Express curiosity rather than concern. "I'm curious what chatbots are actually like to talk to — have you ever tried?" takes the temperature down and can lead somewhere more honest.

The goal isn't to eliminate AI from a teenager's emotional toolkit. It's to make sure they know that real people — parents, counselors, therapists — are available and willing to have those same conversations, and that a chatbot is a starting point, not a substitute.
