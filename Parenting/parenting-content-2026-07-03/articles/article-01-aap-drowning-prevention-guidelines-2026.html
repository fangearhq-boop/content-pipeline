The AAP Just Updated Its Drowning Prevention Guidelines. Here's What Parents Need to Know.

By Jamie Rivera | July 3, 2026

The American Academy of Pediatrics has published updated drowning prevention guidelines in the July 2026 issue of Pediatrics — and the timing matters. With July 4 weekend here and Colorado Springs families heading to pools, lakes, and splash pads, this is exactly the right moment to review what the AAP is now recommending and why.

Drowning is the No. 1 cause of unintentional injury death for children ages 1–4 in the United States. It's also one of the most preventable. The updated AAP policy statement, titled "Prevention of Drowning," builds on previous guidance with specific language designed to close gaps in how families understand and practice water safety.

Touch Supervision: The New Standard Term

The most significant language change in the updated guidelines is the formal use of "touch supervision" to describe what the AAP considers the appropriate level of adult attention around infants, toddlers, and children who aren't strong swimmers.

Touch supervision means a designated adult is within arm's reach of the child at all times when in or near water — not watching from across the pool, not sitting nearby, and not managing a phone or conversation while the kids are in the water. The AAP is explicit: this is a single-task role. One adult, one job, full attention.

The word "designated" carries weight here. Touch supervision requires a pre-assigned adult who has agreed to watch, not whoever happens to be closest or least distracted at any given moment. At crowded July 4 gatherings, that kind of informal assumption — "someone's watching them" — is exactly where gaps form.

Swim Lessons: When to Start, When to Wait

The updated guidelines confirm that swim lessons are appropriate starting at age 1. The reasoning is directly tied to developmental risk: increased mobility after the first birthday means children can reach water hazards — pools, buckets, bathtubs — more quickly and independently. Starting lessons at age 1 directly addresses that elevated risk window.

For infants younger than 1, the AAP's position is unchanged: there isn't enough evidence that swim lessons reduce drowning risk in that age group. Water acclimation activities can still be appropriate for bonding and enjoyment, but parents shouldn't count them as a safety intervention.

The Four-Sided Pool Fence Rule

For families with home pools, the AAP recommends a four-sided fence with a self-closing, self-latching gate at a minimum height of 4 feet. Critically, the fence should isolate the pool from the house and the rest of the yard — not just from the street or neighboring properties.

A fence on three sides that still allows direct access from a door or patio doesn't meet the intent of this recommendation. The goal is to require a deliberate, child-resistant action to reach the pool from any direction.

Additional Key Points from the Updated Policy

Recommendation Detail
Bath supervision Children should not bathe unsupervised until at least age 6
CPR training Recommended for all caregivers — response time directly affects outcomes
Layered protection No single method is sufficient — fencing, supervision, swim skills, and CPR work together
Life jackets U.S. Coast Guard–approved life jackets for open water; swim floaties are not a substitute

The layered protection principle is worth emphasizing: a child who takes swim lessons still needs active supervision. A fenced pool still requires a designiated water watcher at gatherings. No single layer is a substitute for the others working together.

What This Means for July 4 Weekend

Holiday weekends are statistically higher-risk for water incidents. More families are gathered, more people are distracted, and supervision can diffuse across a crowd in ways that leave kids less protected, not more. This July 4, before anyone gets in the water — at a home pool, a community splash pad, or a lake — it's worth explicitly designating who is on touch supervision and making sure that person understands the job.

The AAP update doesn't change what parents already know on some level. It makes the expectations clearer and the language more specific — which is exactly what makes it easier to apply.

What's Next

The full updated policy statement is available through the AAP at HealthyChildren.org, the organization's consumer-facing site for families. CPR certification courses are available in Colorado Springs through the American Red Cross and several local hospitals. If your child hasn't started swim lessons yet, most Colorado Springs recreation centers and the YMCA offer programs for ages 6 months and up — with structured lessons for toddlers and preschoolers beginning around age 1.
