Your Kid Is Going to Ask About the Fireworks. Here's What to Tell Them (and Do Instead).

By Jamie Rivera | July 3, 2026

It's going to happen sometime tomorrow. You'll be out the door, the car will be packed with sunscreen and snacks, and a small voice from the backseat is going to ask the question every Colorado Springs parent already knows is coming: "But where are the fireworks?"

Fireworks are cancelled this July 4 across Colorado Springs. Stage II fire restrictions are in effect, and that means all public fireworks displays are off — including the Star-Spangled Symphony, which was postponed entirely. It's the right call given the conditions. It's also, let's be honest, a parenting logistics problem that nobody asked for the day before a holiday.

The Short Answer That Actually Works

"We live in Colorado Springs. We don't need fireworks. We have a 14,000-foot mountain."

Depending on the age of your kids, this answer lands somewhere between "reluctant acknowledgment" and "genuinely interested." Either way, it's accurate — because this year, Pikes Peak America's Mountain is doing something it has literally never done before: opening for a first-ever July 4 sunset viewing at 8:29 PM from the summit.

Watching the sun go down from 14,115 feet on the Fourth of July is not a consolation prize. It's the kind of thing that photographs terribly (it's always more spectacular in person) and that kids talk about for longer than any fireworks show.

The Full Menu of COS July 4 Options

For families still building their plan for tomorrow, here's what's actually available in Colorado Springs this year.

For the overachievers: Pikes Peak opens at 4 AM for a special sunrise event. Last timed entry is 6 AM. This is a significant commitment, but watching the sun come up from the summit is genuinely unlike anything else you can do as a family. Advance tickets required at DrivePikesPeak.com.

For the everyone-else crowd: The first-ever July 4 sunset event at Pikes Peak opens the gateway at 7 PM, with sunset at 8:29 PM. This one fits into a normal family day without requiring anyone to set a 3 AM alarm. Same ticket situation — DrivePikesPeak.com, advance purchase only, capacity limited.

For the soccer fans: The Colorado Springs Switchbacks are playing Phoenix Rising at Weidner Field at 7 PM. Live sports, stadium energy, and the kids will be on their feet — which is exactly what July 4 is supposed to feel like.

For the free option: Banning Lewis Ranch is hosting a free July 4 celebration at Vista Park Pavilion. There's genuine community appeal here, and "free" is always a strong argument in a holiday weekend budget.

For the daytime hours: Rock Ledge Ranch Historic Site is open 10 AM to 4 PM. The WWII Aviation Museum is open 10 AM to 5 PM. Both are reliably good options for kids who need a structured activity with something to look at and touch.

On the Fireworks Conversation Itself

Young kids — especially toddlers and preschoolers — often don't actually miss fireworks. They've been told fireworks are a July 4 tradition, which is different from having strong personal feelings about the fireworks themselves. If you reframe the day around something specific ("we're watching the sun set from the highest point in Colorado"), the absence of fireworks often just... doesn't come up again.

Older kids who genuinely love fireworks are a different matter. It's okay to name the tradeoff honestly: the fire danger is real, the restrictions make sense, and the mountain sunset is legitimately a better experience even if it's not what they expected. That's not a spin — it's just true.

And for parents who also miss the fireworks: same. But a July 4 spent at 14,115 feet, or at a Switchbacks game, or just outside on a blanket in the backyard watching the actual stars, is still July 4. COS does the holiday its own way, and this year is a good reminder of why that's fine.
