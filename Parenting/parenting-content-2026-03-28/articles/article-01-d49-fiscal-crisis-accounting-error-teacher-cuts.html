D49 Cut $1.8M in Teachers — Then a $2M Accounting Error Came to Light

By COS Parenting | March 28, 2026

District 49 families have had a lot to process over the past few months, and the latest development doesn't make it easier. A district that declared a fiscal emergency in January and cut nearly $1.8 million in teacher salaries and staff in February is now dealing with the fallout from a reported $2 million accounting error that preceded those decisions.

Here's a timeline of what's happened — and what it means for families in COS's largest district by geography.

January: Fiscal Exigency Declared

On January 8, 2026, the D49 board voted 4-1 to declare fiscal exigency — a formal state of financial emergency that gives school boards broader authority to make cuts. The district cited three factors: declining enrollment (the district fell to 26,423 students, its first enrollment drop in five years), a loss of state mill levy equalization funding, and rising healthcare and salary costs.

The declaration set the stage for what came next.

February: Teacher Cuts Approved

On February 12, the D49 board voted 3-2 to eliminate 39 staff positions and not renew the contracts of 12 teachers. Combined, the cuts total approximately $1.8 million in salary and benefits for the 2026-27 school year. Many of the affected teachers had been with the district for more than 10 years.

The meeting drew heavy community pushback. Parents, students, and teachers walked out of the board meeting in protest. The Falcon Education Association — the district's teachers union — had already been demanding a full financial audit, arguing that D49 had been systematically underestimating its annual revenue by more than $30 million per year for three consecutive years. The district has disputed those figures.

March: The Accounting Error Surfaces

In early March, KRDO reported that D49 administrators had made a $2 million accounting error — and that this error had occurred before the February layoff decisions were made. The report renewed calls from the teachers union and parents for an independent audit of district finances.

The board president stated at the time that at least two other Colorado school districts are expected to declare fiscal exigency for the 2026-27 school year, suggesting D49's situation reflects a broader statewide trend. That context, while real, hasn't quieted local concerns about whether the severity of D49's cuts was justified given the financial picture that was actually available at the time.

New Programs Still Planned

In a somewhat jarring contrast to the cuts, D49 is still planning to open a new Student Success Center and two new preschool classrooms for the August 2026 school year. District officials have said these programs are funded separately and serve different purposes than the positions that were eliminated — but for many families, the optics are difficult.

What's Next

The audit question remains unresolved. The Falcon Education Association has continued to press for an independent review of D49's finances. Affected teachers have until their contract nonrenewal deadlines to respond through the district's official process.

For D49 families, the most immediate concern is classroom stability for fall 2026. If your child's school has been affected by staff cuts, the district's main communication channel is d49.org. School principals and parent liaisons should have more specific information about what's changing at individual buildings.

COS Parenting will continue to follow this story as the audit situation develops and the district approaches the start of the 2026-27 year.
