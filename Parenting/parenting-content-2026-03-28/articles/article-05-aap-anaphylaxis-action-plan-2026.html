AAP Updated the Anaphylaxis Action Plan — Here's What Changed for 2026

By COS Parenting | March 28, 2026

If your child has a food allergy or anaphylaxis risk, spring is the ideal time to pull out their action plan and make sure it's current. This spring, there's a specific reason to do that: a newly validated North American Pediatric Anaphylaxis Action Plan has been released, and it includes meaningful updates that parents and school nurses should know about.

What's New in the 2026 Plan

The updated plan, published in the Annals of Allergy, Asthma and Immunology, is described as the first major anaphylaxis action plan revision to incorporate three important changes:

Nasal Epinephrine Is Now Included

Neffy, the nasal spray form of epinephrine, was approved by the FDA in 2023. The updated action plan now officially incorporates nasal epinephrine as a management option alongside the traditional auto-injector (EpiPen or equivalent). Some families and caregivers find the nasal spray easier to use quickly, especially with young children who may resist an injection.

If your child's current emergency plan only references auto-injectors, ask your allergist whether updating to include Neffy as an option makes sense for your family's situation.

Preference-Sensitive Post-Epinephrine Guidance

Previous versions of the action plan were more prescriptive about what happens after epinephrine is administered. The 2026 plan introduces preference-sensitive guidance — meaning it acknowledges that different families, caregivers, and care settings may make different decisions about transport, observation, and follow-up care, while still ensuring safety standards are met. This is a shift toward personalized care rather than one-size-fits-all instructions.

Age-Specific Symptom Guidance

The new plan includes clearer guidance on how anaphylaxis can present differently in children at different ages. Infants and toddlers, for example, may not be able to communicate symptoms the way older children can, and the physical signs may differ. The updated plan helps caregivers recognize anaphylaxis across age groups more accurately.

In a survey of healthcare stakeholders who reviewed the plan, 86% said they would recommend using it.

Why Spring Is the Right Time to Review

For Colorado Springs families, spring brings a specific uptick in allergy exposure risk:

  • School field trips and outdoor classroom days increase food-sharing situations outside the school building
  • End-of-year celebrations, parties, and potlucks are common from April through June
  • Outdoor events and festivals bring new food environments that are harder to control
  • New caregivers or summer programs may not have your child's current plan on file

The AAP also released updated Food Allergy Management in Schools guidelines in late 2025, which many COS schools are implementing in 2026. It's worth connecting with your child's school nurse this spring to confirm that their plan matches the current guidelines — and that anyone responsible for your child during activities has a current copy.

What's Next

The full updated action plan is available through the Annals of Allergy, Asthma and Immunology and will be adopted by many pediatric practices and school health offices over the coming months. If your child's provider hasn't yet mentioned the update, bring it up at your next appointment.

The AAP's HealthyChildren.org also maintains current food allergy and anaphylaxis resources at healthychildren.org — a useful bookmark for COS families navigating allergy season this spring and through the summer outdoor events ahead.
