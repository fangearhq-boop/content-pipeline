AAP's 2026 Screen Time Guidance: It's Not Just How Much — It's How You Watch Together

By Sarah Morales | COS Parenting | June 12, 2026

Summer break in Colorado Springs means long days, kids at home, and screens that somehow appear in every corner of the house. If you've been feeling guilty about how much your kids are watching this summer, the American Academy of Pediatrics' 2026 guidance offers something more useful than more guilt: a nuanced framework that shifts the focus from clock-watching to intentional use.

The research behind AAP's guidance has evolved considerably over the past decade, and the 2026 update reflects that evolution. For most school-age children, the question is no longer "how many hours" — it's "what are they watching, how, and with whom."

What the Research Actually Shows

For children under 18 months, the guidance remains firm: no screens other than video calling. Infants and very young toddlers do not learn effectively from screens the way they learn from in-person interaction, and there's no evidence of benefit to justify the trade-off. Video calls with grandparents or family are different — there's a real person on the other end providing back-and-forth engagement.

For ages 2 to 5, the one-hour-per-day recommendation focuses heavily on what that hour contains. A child watching an educational program that teaches vocabulary, emotional concepts, or problem-solving is using screen time very differently than one passively scrolling through algorithmically-served short-form video. The AAP's position is that high-quality, age-appropriate programming within time limits has demonstrable developmental value. Low-quality, high-stimulation content within the same time limits does not.

For school-age children, the research picture is more complex. Moderate screen use in appropriate contexts does not show the developmental harms that excessive or late-night use does. The strongest predictors of negative outcomes are screens in the bedroom, screens at the dinner table, and screens as the last activity before sleep — all of which affect sleep quality, family connection, and self-regulation in ways that accumulated screen hours alone do not.

The Two Habits That Matter Most

The AAP identifies two household practices that research consistently links to better outcomes across age groups:

Device-free bedrooms. Screens in children's bedrooms — even when the child is "just listening to music" or "using it as a clock" — are associated with shorter sleep duration, worse sleep quality, and more daytime fatigue. The effect is present even when the device is not actively in use. Getting the screen out of the bedroom is the single highest-impact structural change a family can make.

Device-free mealtimes. Family mealtimes where everyone is present and not distracted by a screen are associated with stronger communication skills, more family cohesion, and better emotional regulation in children. The benefit isn't about the screen itself — it's about the quality of the interaction that happens in the screen's absence.

Neither of these requires counting hours or setting app timers. They're structural changes to the environment rather than policing of behavior — which the research suggests is both more effective and less conflict-prone for families.

Co-Viewing: Why Watching Together Is Different

One of the more actionable pieces of the AAP's guidance for summer is the emphasis on co-viewing and co-engagement. When a parent or caregiver watches with a child and talks about what they're seeing — asking questions, pointing out how a character is feeling, naming ads as ads, discussing privacy when apps ask for permissions — it builds a form of digital literacy that time limits can't create.

Children who learn to think critically about media from an early age are better equipped to navigate it independently as they get older. That's not a skill that develops from restriction alone; it develops from practice with guidance. Summer, when routines are looser and there's more time to sit down and watch something together, is actually a good window for this.

Applying This in Colorado Springs This Summer

Colorado Springs has a lot working in families' favor for screen-free time: extensive trail systems, multiple parks, community pools, and summer programming through the Pikes Peak Library District and local rec centers. But the goal isn't to eliminate screens — it's to use them with intention. A family that watches a nature documentary together and talks about it is using screens in exactly the way the AAP describes as beneficial. A child who spends three hours on a tablet while a parent works from home is a very different scenario, and the difference isn't primarily about the three hours.

The practical reframe for summer: focus on the two structural rules (bedrooms and mealtimes), make space for co-viewing when you can, and worry less about the hour count. For ages 2 to 5, the one-hour limit with quality content remains the clearest guideline. For older kids, the context around the screen time matters more than the total.

What's Next

The full AAP Family Media Plan tool, which lets families customize their own household guidelines by age, is available at healthychildren.org. The Pikes Peak Library District's summer reading program includes both in-person and digital components that count as intentional, educational screen time for kids who want to stay engaged over the summer. For families looking to reduce reactive screen time, the AAP recommends starting with the bedroom and the dinner table before tackling everything else.
