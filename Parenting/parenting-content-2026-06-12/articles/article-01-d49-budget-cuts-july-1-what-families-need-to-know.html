D49 Budget Cuts Take Effect July 1: What Colorado Springs Families Need to Know

By Jamie Rivera | COS Parenting | June 12, 2026

The process that started with D49's fiscal exigency declaration in January 2026 reached its conclusion Wednesday evening when the D49 Board of Education voted to formally adopt the 2026-27 budget. For families in School District 49, this means one thing: the cuts are no longer hypothetical. They take effect July 1.

With 19 days remaining before the new school year budget begins, here's what Colorado Springs families with D49 students need to understand about the changes coming to district programs.

What's Being Cut

The February 2026 vote approved approximately $2 million in reductions across several programs. Those reductions are now part of the adopted budget.

D49 Programs Affected by 2026-27 Budget Cuts
Program What It Does Status
Base49 Before and after school care for D49 students Eliminated or significantly reduced
PEAK Extended learning and enrichment programs Reduced
Preschool District-run preschool programming Cut
Elevates 18-21 Transition services for young adults with disabilities Impacted

The district has also eliminated 12 teacher positions. Enrollment decline is the primary driver of D49's financial situation — a decline in student enrollment results in less per-pupil state funding, and the district projected losing approximately $400,000 from that factor alone in the 2026-27 school year.

What This Means for Your Family

If your family uses Base49 for before or after school child care, you'll need to arrange an alternative starting in August. Base49 has served as an accessible, affordable option for working parents in D49 — its elimination creates a real gap that private child care programs in Colorado Springs will partially fill, at higher cost.

Families who've enrolled young children in D49's preschool program should connect with their school's administrative team to understand what transition options exist. Private preschools in Colorado Springs vary widely in cost and availability, and summer is the time to get on waitlists.

Families of students with disabilities who use Elevates 18-21 — transition services that support young adults ages 18-21 — should contact their D49 case manager directly. These families have the least flexibility and the most to navigate when programs change.

Why This Happened

D49 declared fiscal exigency — a formal designation that allows a school district to take emergency budget measures — in January 2026. The district cited ongoing enrollment decline as the root cause. When fewer students attend D49 schools, the state allocates less per-pupil funding to the district, which reduces overall revenue.

The board voted 4-1 in February to approve the program reductions and staffing cuts. Wednesday's vote formally adopted the full 2026-27 budget, making those reductions official.

D49 is not alone in facing enrollment pressures. Colorado Springs continues to see demographic shifts across all three major school districts — D11, D20, and D49 — that complicate long-term budget planning. But D49's geographic footprint and the speed of its enrollment changes made the fiscal impact particularly sharp.

What's Next

The new budget takes effect July 1. Families with questions about specific programs should visit d49.org or contact their school directly. The school year for 2026-27 will begin in August with a leaner program structure than previous years. For families relying on Base49, PEAK, or preschool, the next three weeks are the window to find and secure alternatives before summer runs out.
