### STORY 1: Hammer Fire — Wildfire Preparedness

#### Long-Form Post -- 8:00 AM MT

**Long-Form Post (8:00 AM MT)**

The Hammer Fire is out — and COS families can breathe a little easier this morning.

The nearly 5,000-acre fire that broke out southeast of Colorado Springs on April 22 was declared 100% contained on April 23. Evacuation orders for the Hanover area were lifted that same day, and Hanover School District 28 — which closed all schools on April 23 due to fire activity — has since reopened.

No structures were lost. That's the good news.

But fire season in Colorado doesn't wait for summer. And this week's Hammer Fire is a reminder that families in Colorado Springs and El Paso County need wildfire plans — not just in the mountains, but in and around the city too.

Here's what you can do right now:

**Know your zone.** El Paso County's Wildfire Risk map is at elpasoco.com/emergency-management. Find out if your neighborhood is in a high-risk area.

**Build a go-bag.** For each family member, pack three days of water, medications, documents (birth certificates, insurance), phone chargers, and pet supplies. Keep it accessible.

**Talk with your kids.** Children handle emergencies better when they've heard the plan before. Walk through what to do if you need to leave quickly.

**Follow Watch Duty.** Download the Watch Duty app for real-time fire tracking in El Paso County.

The Colorado Springs Fire Department's Ready, Set, Go program has free guides at coloradosprings.gov. Spring and summer are unpredictable — it's never the wrong time to update your family's plan.

Are you prepared? What's one thing you've done to get your family wildfire-ready?

### STORY 3: Pew Research on Teens and Social Media

#### Long-Form Post -- 3:00 PM MT

**Long-Form Post (3:00 PM MT)**

Here's something that might surprise you: most teenagers say TikTok, Instagram, and Snapchat don't hurt their mental health.

That's one of the key findings from a major new Pew Research Center study released April 15, 2026. The researchers surveyed 1,458 teen-parent pairs and found that the majority of teens across all three platforms say the apps neither help nor hurt their mental health. Only 9%–11% of teen users reported that their platform had hurt their mental health.

But there's a significant wrinkle in the data: sleep.

37% of teen TikTok users say the app hurts how much sleep they get — compared to 26% for Snapchat and 24% for Instagram. And we all know what sleep-deprived kids look and feel like by Thursday morning.

Then there's the parent-teen gap. 44% of parents of teen TikTok users think their kid spends too much time on the app. Only 28% of teens agree. That's a pretty wide disagreement about what "too much" looks like.

The study's takeaway isn't that social media is harmless — it's that the relationship between platforms and wellbeing is more complicated than the headline "social media is bad for teens" suggests. Context matters. How long, when, and how a teen uses a platform shapes the impact.

For COS parents, the sleep finding is probably the most actionable. Charging devices outside the bedroom at night removes a lot of the temptation for late-night scrolling, without requiring a full household ban.

### STORY 4: CPSC Recalls — Youth ATVs + Toothbrush Packaging

#### Long-Form Post -- 10:00 AM MT

**Long-Form Post (10:00 AM MT)**

Two new CPSC recalls from April 23 — both involving products marketed to children.

**Lil Pick Up Youth All-Terrain Vehicles (ATVs)**

The Consumer Product Safety Commission is recalling Lil Pick Up Youth ATVs due to crash and burn hazards. The vehicles violate mandatory ATV safety standards. One death has been reported in connection with these vehicles. If your family owns one, stop use immediately and contact the manufacturer for refund or remedy information. These were sold on Amazon.

**Autobrush Sonic Pro Children's Toothbrush Boxes**

If you've bought your kids the Autobrush Sonic Pro electric toothbrush, check the packaging. The CPSC recalled these boxes because children can easily access a lithium coin battery inside the packaging — posing a serious ingestion hazard. The packaging also lacks required warning labels under Reese's Law, which specifically regulates products containing button cell and coin batteries because of their danger to young children if swallowed.

Both products were sold on Amazon. As always, before buying any children's product on Amazon, we recommend checking cpsc.gov for recall status — the platform makes it easy to list recalled items for sale.

A reminder: the CPSC recall hotline is 1-800-638-2772. You can also sign up for email recall alerts at cpsc.gov/Recalls.

### STORY 5: Colorado Preschool Supreme Court Case

#### Long-Form Post -- 5:00 PM MT

**Long-Form Post (5:00 PM MT)**

Colorado parents with preschoolers: a case affecting the state's Universal Preschool program is heading to the U.S. Supreme Court.

Here's the background: In 2023, two Catholic preschools in Colorado sued the state after being excluded from the Universal Preschool (UPK) program. The state's UPK initiative offers families 15 free hours per week of preschool — a significant benefit for families with 4-year-olds. But the program requires participating providers to serve all families without discriminating based on LGBTQ status.

The Catholic preschools argued that being excluded from UPK because of their religious admission policies violates their First Amendment right to religious freedom. The Supreme Court agreed to hear the case, meaning a ruling is coming — though no timeline has been announced.

What does this mean for COS families?

If the Supreme Court rules in favor of the schools, it could open the door for more faith-based programs to join UPK — which would expand options for families who prefer religious settings. It could also potentially limit the anti-discrimination requirements for UPK providers.

If the Court rules against the schools, it affirms Colorado's current approach: UPK access for families comes with the requirement that providers serve all families equally.

Either way, the decision will reshape how Colorado families — including those in Colorado Springs — access and choose publicly funded preschool programs. There are multiple UPK sites in COS, and the ruling could affect enrollment choices for the 2027-28 school year and beyond.

We'll keep tracking this for COS families. What questions do you have about UPK in Colorado Springs?
