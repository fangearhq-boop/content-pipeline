### STORY 3: New Pew Research — Most Teens Say Social Media Doesn't Hurt Their Mental Health (But 37% Say TikTok Hurts Their Sleep)
**Tier:** 2 | **Pillar:** National Parenting | **Platforms:** X, FB, Article
A Pew Research Center study released April 15, 2026, surveyed 1,458 teen-parent pairs on experiences with TikTok, Instagram, and Snapchat. Key findings: Most teens (around 80%+) say these platforms neither hurt nor help their mental health. But 37% of TikTok users say the platform hurts the amount of sleep they get — compared to 26% for Snapchat and 24% for Instagram. Parent-teen gap: 44% of parents of teen TikTok users think their teen spends too much time on the app, vs. 28% of teens who agree. Entertainment and connection with friends are the main reasons teens use each platform.

### STORY 4: CPSC April 23 Recalls — Youth ATVs (1 Death Reported) + Children's Toothbrush Packaging (Battery Hazard)
**Tier:** 1 | **Pillar:** National Parenting | **Platforms:** X, FB, Article
Two CPSC recalls from April 23 affect children's products: (1) Lil Pick Up Youth All-Terrain Vehicles recalled due to crash and burn hazards — one death has been reported. These violate mandatory ATV safety standards. (2) Autobrush Sonic Pro Children's Toothbrush Boxes recalled because the packaging can be easily opened by children to access a lithium coin battery, posing an ingestion hazard; packaging also lacks required Reese's Law warning labels. Both sold on Amazon. COS parents with either product should stop use immediately and contact manufacturers.

### STORY 5: Colorado Preschool Case Heading to U.S. Supreme Court — What COS Families Should Know
**Tier:** 2 | **Pillar:** Local News / National Parenting | **Platforms:** X, FB, Article
The U.S. Supreme Court has agreed to hear a case that could reshape publicly funded preschool access in Colorado. Two Catholic preschools sued the state of Colorado in 2023 after the state's Universal Preschool (UPK) program excluded programs that discriminate based on LGBTQ status in family admission policies. The schools argue this violates their religious freedom under the First Amendment. The case could affect how state-funded UPK operates for Colorado families — both those seeking inclusive programs and those who prefer faith-based environments. Colorado Springs has multiple UPK sites and faith-based preschools, making this decision locally relevant.
