If your family has a 4-year-old or will have one in the next few years, a case heading to the U.S. Supreme Court could affect how you access Colorado's Universal Preschool program.

The Supreme Court agreed to hear a case in which two Catholic preschools in Colorado argue that the state violated their First Amendment religious freedom rights by excluding them from the Universal Preschool (UPK) program. The exclusion came because the schools' admission policies restrict enrollment based on LGBTQ family status — policies that conflict with UPK's non-discrimination requirements.

Colorado's UPK program, which launched in 2023, provides 15 free hours per week of preschool to all Colorado 4-year-olds. It's a significant benefit — roughly $3,000–$5,000 in annual value depending on the provider and location — and thousands of Colorado Springs families have enrolled their children.

What Are the Two Sides Arguing?

The Catholic preschools' argument rests on a line of recent Supreme Court precedents that have generally held that the government cannot exclude religious organizations from broadly available public benefit programs solely because of their religious nature. The schools argue that their admission policies are an expression of sincerely held religious beliefs, and that being excluded from UPK because of those beliefs amounts to religious discrimination by the state.

Colorado's position is that UPK is a program to benefit children and families — and that the program's integrity requires all participating providers to welcome all families equally. The state argues that faith-based schools aren't being excluded because of their religion but because they're choosing to discriminate against families in a program designed to serve everyone.

What a Supreme Court Ruling Could Change

If the Supreme Court rules in favor of the preschools, it could require Colorado (and potentially other states) to allow religiously motivated admission restrictions in publicly funded preschool programs. This would expand the roster of faith-based programs eligible to participate in UPK, giving families who prefer a religious school environment more publicly funded options.

It could also mean that some UPK-enrolled families — including same-sex parent households and other LGBTQ families — would face admission denial at some UPK providers despite the program being publicly funded with their tax dollars.

If the Court rules against the preschools, Colorado's current UPK framework remains in place: religious schools can participate, but they must serve all families without discrimination to do so.

What This Means for Colorado Springs Families Now

For the 2026-27 school year, Colorado Springs UPK enrollment is proceeding under current rules. Families can find UPK providers in Colorado Springs and El Paso County at coloradouniversalpreschool.com. The ruling — whenever it comes — would most directly affect future school years and the makeup of the UPK provider pool.

For families currently in or entering UPK, the best course of action is to enroll as planned under current rules, monitor the case if it's relevant to your family's situation, and stay tuned to COS Parenting and local news for updates as the case progresses through the Supreme Court.

For families of faith considering UPK, a ruling in the schools' favor could expand options at providers that share their values. For LGBTQ families, a ruling in the schools' favor could narrow the guarantee that all UPK providers will welcome them.

The U.S. Supreme Court will schedule oral arguments for the case and issue a ruling, likely in the 2026-27 term. CPR News will continue coverage of the case as it develops — coloradopublicradio.org.
