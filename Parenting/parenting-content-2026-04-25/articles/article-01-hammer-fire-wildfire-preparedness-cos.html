Colorado Springs families can breathe easier this weekend. The Hammer Fire — a nearly 5,000-acre wildfire that broke out southeast of the city on April 22 — is now 100% contained, evacuation orders have been lifted, and Hanover School District 28 schools are back open after closing on April 23 due to fire activity.

No structures were lost during the fire. The El Paso County Sheriff's Office issued evacuation orders for the area near Hammer Road east to South Peyton Highway and Myers Road north to Squirrel Creek at the height of the blaze. Emergency shelters were opened at Hanover Jr/Sr High School for people and small animals, and Norris Penrose Event Center for large animals. All shelters have since closed.

Why This Matters for All COS Families

The Hammer Fire was in a rural area southeast of Colorado Springs, but wildfires don't stay politely in the countryside. In recent years, COS-area communities including Broadmoor, Black Forest, and neighborhoods along the Pikes Peak corridor have experienced significant fire risk. April's dry winds and low humidity create conditions that move fires quickly.

Colorado Springs firefighters also responded to two grass fires in the Broadmoor Bluffs neighborhood on the overnight of April 24-25, a reminder that fire risk is active throughout the metro area, not just the rural perimeter.

This is a good week to update your family's wildfire preparedness plan — or create one if you haven't yet.

Wildfire Preparedness for Colorado Springs Families

Know Your Risk Zone

El Paso County has an interactive Wildfire Risk Map at elpasoco.com/emergency-management. Enter your address to see your neighborhood's risk level and recommended preparedness tier under the Ready, Set, Go program.

Build Your Go-Bag

For each family member (including pets), your emergency bag should include three days of water, prescription medications with copies of prescriptions, copies of vital documents (birth certificates, insurance cards, passports), phone chargers and a portable battery, a change of clothes, cash, and pet food and supplies. Keep it near your door.

Have the Conversation With Your Kids

Children handle emergencies better when they've heard the plan before the emergency happens. Walk through what to do if you need to leave quickly: where you'll meet if separated, where the go-bags are, and how to reach a trusted adult if parents aren't reachable. For young children, frame it as "our family's safety adventure plan" rather than focusing on the danger.

Download Watch Duty

The Watch Duty app provides real-time wildfire tracking for Colorado, including El Paso County. It's free and pushes notifications about fires in your area before local news can react. It's one of the most useful tools Colorado families can have on their phones during fire season.

Sign Up for Emergency Alerts

El Paso County uses a CodeRED emergency notification system. Register your cell phone at elpasoco.com so you receive automated alerts for evacuations, emergencies, and shelter-in-place orders. Without registration, cell phones in the area only receive Wireless Emergency Alerts — not the county's targeted CodeRED messages.

What's Next

Spring and early summer are historically the highest-risk fire months in El Paso County due to low humidity, wind events, and dry vegetation. With fire season beginning earlier each year in Colorado, the time to prepare is now — before the next fire starts, not after. The Colorado Springs Fire Department's Ready, Set, Go program at coloradosprings.gov has free downloadable guides for families at every risk level.

The Hammer Fire is contained. El Paso County families are safe. Use this moment to make sure your family is ready for next time.
