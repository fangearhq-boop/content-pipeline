The conversation around social media and kids' mental health is constantly evolving — and a major new Pew Research Center study released April 15, 2026, adds important nuance to a debate that often runs hotter than the evidence supports.

The study surveyed 1,458 teen-parent pairs about their experiences with TikTok, Instagram, and Snapchat. The headline finding may surprise parents who've spent years reading alarming stories about social media's toll on teenagers: the majority of teen users across all three platforms say the apps neither hurt nor help their mental health.

What Teens Actually Report

Only 9% of Snapchat and TikTok users and 11% of Instagram users said they believed the platform had hurt their mental health. That's a relatively small share of the teen population — and it suggests the relationship between social media and wellbeing is far more complex, and more varied by individual, than most headlines acknowledge.

Why do teens use these platforms? The study found that roughly nine in 10 or more teens across all three platforms cite entertainment as a reason they use it. Most also use social media to stay connected with friends and family, and to find others who share their interests. These are the same reasons adults use social media. The motivations, it turns out, are pretty human.

The Sleep Finding Is the One Parents Should Focus On

If there's one data point from this study that's worth a family conversation, it's this: 37% of teen TikTok users say the app hurts the amount of sleep they get. That compares to 26% for Snapchat and 24% for Instagram.

Sleep is where the research on teens and screens has consistently shown the clearest harm. Unlike the more complicated question of mental health — which depends on content, context, the individual teen, and how they use the platform — sleep disruption from late-night phone use is more direct. The phone is on, the teen is awake, the sleep is shorter and lower quality.

Teenagers between ages 13 and 17 need between 8 and 10 hours of sleep per night, according to guidance from the American Academy of Pediatrics. Sleep-deprived teens show measurable impacts on mood, attention, academic performance, and physical health. A TikTok habit that regularly pushes lights-out to midnight is doing real, documentable harm — even if the teen doesn't describe it as hurting their mental health.

Parents and Teens See "Too Much" Differently

The study surfaced a notable gap between how parents and teens evaluate social media use. Among parents of teen TikTok users, 44% said they believed their teen spent too much time on the app. Only 28% of those same teens agreed with their parents' assessment.

That 16-point gap isn't necessarily evidence that teens are in denial. It may also reflect genuinely different standards for what "too much" looks like — and a reminder that these conversations between parents and teens about phone use often talk past each other because each side is measuring different things.

The Pew researchers also found that Snapchat leads for posting frequency: 17% of Snapchat users posted several times a day, compared to 12% for TikTok and 8% for Instagram. Snapchat's streak-and-chat mechanics drive higher engagement frequency — something parents may not realize if they assume TikTok is always the heaviest platform.

What COS Parents Can Do With This Research

The most actionable step research consistently supports is device-free bedrooms. Charging phones in a shared family space (a kitchen counter, a charging station in the hallway) removes the late-night temptation that drives both sleep disruption and excessive use without requiring a complete ban. It's a structural change rather than a willpower test.

For families who want to go deeper, the Pew study is freely available at pewresearch.org and is worth reading together with your teen — not as a "see, the research says you're on your phone too much" conversation, but as a starting point for talking about what they actually get out of the platforms they use and what trade-offs they've noticed themselves.
