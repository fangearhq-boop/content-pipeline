### STORY 2: CPSC Recall — Crayola Pip-Cubes

#### Long-Form Post — 10:30 AM MT

COS parents, we need to talk about a toy recall that's on our radar this week — especially if you have kids under 14 at home 🚨

The Consumer Product Safety Commission has recalled approximately 9,400 Crayola-branded Pip-Cubes magnetic building cubes, sold under the CreateOn brand. These are colorful magnetic building cubes sold in Bold Colors (24-piece) and Glitter (24-piece) sets, as well as 27-piece sets.

Here's the concern: the magnets inside the cubes can come loose if the seams separate. When two or more high-powered magnets are swallowed, they can attract each other through intestinal walls — causing perforations, twisting, blockages, blood poisoning, and in the worst cases, death. No injuries have been reported so far, but the CPSC's language is clear: stop using these now.

**Where they were sold:**
- 24-piece sets at Michaels and Michaels.com for about $20
- 27-piece sets on Amazon.com for about $35
- Sold May 2025 through July 2025

**What to do:**
Stop using the cubes immediately. Contact CreateOn for a free replacement set. You'll receive a pre-paid return label.

- Phone: 1-800-333-0549 (Mon-Fri, 9:30 AM-4 PM CT)
- Email: pipcuberecall@CreateOn.com
- Website: CreateOn.com/recall

If you bought these as a gift, it's worth reaching out to whoever received them too. Magnetic toy hazards are among the CPSC's highest-priority categories — these are toys that look completely safe but can cause catastrophic internal injuries.

Does your family have these? Have you seen them at school or in other kids' homes?

### STORY 3: Blodgett Open Space New Trails

#### Long-Form Post — 12:30 PM MT

Summer hiking season is almost here — and Colorado Springs just made it a whole lot better 🌄

The City of Colorado Springs is wrapping up construction on approximately 14 miles of new trails at Blodgett Open Space, with completion expected by end of summer 2026. Located on the northwest side of COS, Blodgett has long been a favorite for local families — and this expansion takes it to another level.

Here's what's coming:
- **Hiking-only trails** in the northern portion (great news for parents who want a quieter, bike-free experience with younger kids)
- **Bike-only trails** in the southern portion (a dedicated zone for families with older kids who ride)
- Phased opening as sections are completed — so some trails may be accessible before the full 14 miles are done

The project is built on Blodgett's Master and Management Plan, which was approved by City Council in 2024, and focuses on sustainable trail design that protects the open space while expanding access for more families.

If you haven't visited Blodgett before, now's the time to put it on your radar. And if you're a regular, start scouting those trail maps — there's a lot more ground to cover this summer.

### STORY 4: D11 Labor Dispute — What It Means for Families This Fall

#### Long-Form Post — 3:30 PM MT

The D11 school year ends May 22 — but the contract dispute that's shaped this year for Colorado Springs families is far from over.

It's been more than 300 days since Colorado Springs School District 11 severed its 56-year master bargaining agreement with the Colorado Springs Education Association in December 2024. Since then, teachers held a one-day strike in October 2025, educators and parents picketed at the school board meeting earlier this month, and both sides remain without a new agreement as summer begins.

For families with kids in D11, here are some things worth thinking about as you head into summer:

**Class sizes.** Without a contract, there's no negotiated limit on class sizes. Teachers can be assigned more students than they were under the old agreement.

**Teacher retention.** When working conditions are uncertain and compensation isn't protected by contract, some teachers choose to leave. Turnover rates at D11 schools are worth monitoring.

**What's next.** The school board's position is that the district performs better without a union contract. The union disagrees. Bargaining updates, if any, would likely come through the CSEA or local news outlets like KRDO and the Colorado Springs Gazette.

If you have questions or concerns about what this means for your specific school, reaching out to your principal or attending an upcoming board meeting is the most direct path to information.

### STORY 5: Mental Health Awareness Month

#### Long-Form Post — 8:30 PM MT

May is Mental Health Awareness Month — and this year, the American Academy of Pediatrics is pushing for something Colorado Springs families may find reassuring: treating kids' mental health as a routine part of every pediatric checkup, not just a crisis response 💙

The AAP's proposed whole-health model says that mental and emotional development should be a standard part of the care your child receives at every visit — just like a growth chart or a hearing test. That means your pediatrician talking with you about your child's social skills, stress, sleep, and emotional development before there's a problem, not after.

For Colorado Springs families, this matters in some specific ways. This is a military community. Deployment, relocation, and the transitions that come with military life create unique stressors for kids that are easy to underestimate. Research consistently shows that military kids benefit from proactive — not just reactive — mental health support.

If you're wondering where to start:
- **Talk to your pediatrician** — ask them to address emotional wellness at your child's next visit
- **Colorado Crisis Services:** 1-844-493-8255, available 24/7 statewide
- **Military OneSource:** Free counseling for military families (militaryonesource.mil)
- **D11 and D20 school counselors** are a direct starting point for school-based concerns

You don't have to wait for a crisis to check in on your child's mental health. The AAP is saying: don't.
