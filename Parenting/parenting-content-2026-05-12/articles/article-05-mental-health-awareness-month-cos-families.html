Mental Health Awareness Month: What COS Families Need to Know — and Where to Turn

By Jamie Rivera | May 12, 2026 | COS Parenting

May is National Mental Health Awareness Month, and this year the American Academy of Pediatrics is making a case that Colorado Springs families would do well to take seriously: children's mental and emotional health should be a routine part of pediatric care, not something that only comes up in a crisis.

The AAP's proposed whole-health model would have pediatricians provide anticipatory guidance about healthy mental and emotional development at every checkup — talking with parents about social and emotional milestones, behavioral patterns, and positive parenting strategies the same way they'd discuss growth charts or immunizations. The goal is to catch warning signs earlier and build resilience before problems become serious.

For Colorado Springs specifically, that framing has extra relevance.

Why This Matters in a Military Community

Colorado Springs is home to some of the largest military installations in the country — USAFA, Fort Carson, Peterson Space Force Base, Schriever Space Force Base, and Cheyenne Mountain Space Force Station among them. A significant portion of D20 students have parents who serve, and military families across COS navigate a specific kind of stress that civilian family life doesn't always replicate.

Deployment separates families. Frequent relocations interrupt friendships, school routines, and support networks. The operational demands of military service can create emotional distance that shows up in children's behavior before it's easily named as stress. Research on military children consistently finds that this population benefits from proactive mental health support — not just access to services when things go wrong.

The same principle applies to any child navigating a high-stress environment — whether that's a military household, a family dealing with economic instability, or a kid who internalizes the tensions of the school year. If children's emotional health is assessed regularly rather than reactively, there's a better chance of catching problems early.

What a Proactive Approach Looks Like

The AAP's framework identifies several foundations for healthy mental and emotional development that parents can support at home:

  • Parent-child connection: Regular, predictable one-on-one time with a caregiver is protective. It doesn't need to be long — 10 to 15 minutes of focused, phone-free attention daily builds security.
  • Routines: Predictable daily structure helps children feel safe, particularly during transitional periods like the end of a school year.
  • Physical activity: Research consistently links regular physical activity to reduced anxiety and improved mood in children and adolescents. Colorado's outdoor culture gives families natural opportunities here.
  • Sleep: The AAP's sleep recommendations haven't changed — most children need more than parents typically estimate, and devices in bedrooms are one of the primary culprits disrupting it.
  • Community connection: Kids who feel connected to a community — a school, a sports team, a neighborhood — show better resilience outcomes.

If your child is seeing a pediatrician, it's worth asking at the next visit whether their emotional health is part of the assessment. More providers are incorporating brief screening tools into well-child visits. If your current provider isn't doing this and you'd like them to, it's a reasonable question to raise directly.

What's Next

Mental Health Awareness Month runs through May 31. The AAP's whole-health model is a framework, not a mandate — implementation varies by practice. The most direct action Colorado Springs families can take is to ask their child's pediatrician about mental health screening at the next well-child visit. For crisis situations, Colorado Crisis Services at 1-844-493-8255 is available around the clock. For military families, Military OneSource at militaryonesource.mil offers free counseling sessions with no referral required.
