D11's Unresolved Labor Dispute: What It Could Mean for Colorado Springs Families This Fall

By Sarah Morales | May 12, 2026 | COS Parenting

The D11 school year ends May 22. But the labor dispute that shaped this year for thousands of Colorado Springs families is unlikely to be resolved by the time students return in August.

It's been more than 300 days since Colorado Springs School District 11 ended its 56-year master bargaining agreement with the Colorado Springs Education Association in December 2024. As of early May 2026, no new agreement has been reached. Teachers held a one-day strike in October 2025. Educators and parents organized a picket at the D11 school board meeting on May 6, 2026. The school year has continued — students have been in classrooms, teachers have been teaching — but the underlying contract question is unresolved.

For families with children in D11, the end of the school year is a good time to think through what this ongoing situation could mean when fall arrives.

What Was in the Master Contract

The master bargaining agreement that D11 ended was not just about salaries — though compensation was part of it. It also covered:

  • Class size limits: Without a negotiated cap, the district can assign more students per teacher than the previous contract allowed.
  • Working conditions: Planning time, collaborative time, and workload expectations can shift without contractual protections.
  • Grievance processes: Teachers without a contract have fewer formal channels to raise concerns about assignments or working conditions.
  • Special education provisions: The union had historically negotiated specifics around support staffing and IEP implementation timelines.

The Colorado Springs Gazette editorial board published an op-ed on May 1, 2026 arguing that "teachers and schools do better without a union contract." District leaders have expressed a similar position. The union, and many educators, fundamentally disagree.

What It Means for Families

The practical implications for students can be harder to see from the outside — but they're real.

Teacher retention. When working conditions are uncertain and employment protections are weaker, some teachers choose to leave or transfer. If your child has a teacher they've connected with, it's worth knowing that turnover at D11 schools may be higher than in recent years as educators weigh their options over the summer.

Class sizes. Without a negotiated limit, class sizes at D11 schools in fall 2026 could be larger than they were under the previous contract. This is one of the primary concerns educators have raised through the CSEA.

School culture. Labor disputes take a toll on morale — for teachers and, in ways that are hard to quantify, for students who sense the tension. This isn't a reason to panic, but it's worth being aware of as a context for the fall.

Questions Worth Asking Now

If you have children in D11, summer is actually a reasonable time to get more information before the school year begins:

  • Contact your school's principal to ask about expected class sizes and staffing plans for fall 2026.
  • Follow D11 board meeting announcements — any update on negotiations would come through board meetings, the CSEA, or local outlets like KRDO, the Colorado Springs Gazette, and Colorado Public Radio.
  • If you're considering open enrollment options for next year, Colorado's public school open enrollment window typically opens in the fall. D49 and D20 are alternatives worth understanding if they're accessible to your family's location.

What's Next

D11 school year ends May 22. No new teacher contract is in place as the summer begins. Watch for any negotiation developments through the Colorado Springs Education Association (cseacolorado.org) and through D11's board meeting agendas at d11.org. Fall enrollment information, class placement notices, and supply lists should be available from individual schools beginning in July.
