CPSC Recall Alert: Three Children's Sleepwear Brands Pulled for Burn Hazard — What COS Families Need to Do Now

By Jenna Park | COS Parenting | April 26, 2026

Three children's sleepwear brands have been recalled by the U.S. Consumer Product Safety Commission for violating mandatory flammability standards — meaning the clothing can ignite faster than safety law allows, posing a burn hazard to children who wear them. If you've purchased any of these brands for your kids, the action required is the same for all three: stop using immediately, destroy the garments, and contact the company for a full refund.

None of the three brands has reported injuries, which means this is still a preventable situation. But the flammability standards exist for a reason — children move unpredictably, sleep near nightlights, and can be exposed to flame sources adults might not anticipate. Compliance with those standards is not optional under federal law.

The Three Recalled Brands

Blossom Blanks (dba Blossom)

Blossom Blanks LLC, based in Gadsden, Alabama, has recalled approximately 630 children's loungewear sets. The recalled products are two-piece sets in sizes 12 months through 12 years. They were sold at children's boutiques and online between September 2023 and May 2025.

To receive a refund, consumers should cut the loungewear set in half, photograph the destroyed garment, and email the photo to sales@blossomocc.com. More information is available at blossomocc.com/pages/recall.

Silks

Silks recalled children's two-piece loungewear sets — typically a short-sleeved shirt with matching shorts or pants — in sizes 2T through 13/14. The sets were sold in multiple colors with various prints at silksdesign.com from January 2024 through September 2025 for $40 to $50.

To receive a refund or store credit, consumers should cut the top and bottom in half, photograph the destroyed garments, and email the photo to silkssupport@silksdesign.com. More information is available at silksdesign.com/pages/recall.

Andy & Evan

Andy & Evan has recalled children's pajama sets for violating mandatory flammability standards for children's sleepwear. Consumers should contact Andy & Evan directly for remedy details and product information.

Why Flammability Standards Matter

The federal flammability standard for children's sleepwear exists because of a specific and well-documented risk: children's clothing that ignites easily can cause severe burns before a parent or caregiver can respond. The standard applies to sleepwear for children sizes 9 months through 14, and requires that garments either be made from flame-resistant fabric or be snug-fitting — which reduces the amount of fabric exposed to potential flame.

Loose-fitting loungewear that does not meet flame resistance requirements is a category the CPSC has been actively monitoring. The three recalls announced this year reflect that enforcement priority. Buying children's sleepwear from established retailers with clear labeling — whether treated with flame-resistant chemicals or labeled as snug-fitting — remains the safest approach.

How to Check Your Kids' Pajamas

Check the label inside the neck or waistband of any children's sleepwear you own. Compliant garments will be labeled either with "Flame Resistant" or "Wear Snug-Fitting, Not Flame Resistant." If a garment has no safety labeling and was purchased from a small boutique or online-only retailer, check the CPSC recall database at cpsc.gov/Recalls to confirm it hasn't been recalled.

You can also sign up for CPSC email alerts to receive future recall notices as soon as they're published.

What's Next

If you have any of these products in your home, stop using them today. Cut them in half before disposal to prevent them from being resold or passed on. Contact the brand via the emails listed above for your refund — all three companies are offering full refunds with no purchase receipt required.

The CPSC maintains a searchable recall database at cpsc.gov/Recalls where COS families can check any children's product for current recall status. No injuries have been reported for any of the three recalled brands — and that's the goal. These recalls work when families act quickly.
