Your Kid Probably Used AI This Week. You Might Not Know It.

By Jamie Rivera July 14, 2026

The Gap the Data Found

A new study asked parents and children the same simple question: has your child used generative AI in the past week? The answers didn't match. According to the Family Online Safety Institute's "Beyond Borders" report, published July 9, 2026, 38% of children said yes, they had used generative AI recently. Only 27% of their parents believed that was true. That 11-point gap is the widest finding in a survey that reached more than 4,000 families across the United States and Australia.

The pattern held across other digital behaviors too. Kids reported scrolling social media more than parents expected (54% vs. 46%) and posting on social media more than parents estimated (38% vs. 30%). But the AI gap stood out because it reflects something broader: AI has become embedded in everyday tools in ways that don't look like what most adults imagine when they think of "using AI."

Why the Gap Is Wider for AI Than for Social Media

When a teenager uses Instagram, there's a defined app to check. Parents can look at the phone, see the icon, see the account. With generative AI, the experience is more fragmented. A 12-year-old might ask an AI chatbot to help rephrase a paragraph. A 10-year-old might use an image generator to create art for a presentation. A teenager might use AI features built into their search engine or word processor without even identifying those features as "AI." The tool is there; the label often isn't.

Summer intensifies this dynamic. Without the structure of a school schedule, kids have more unmonitored time on devices, more space to explore, and less natural accountability. The study wasn't designed to be an alarm about summer specifically, but the timing of the release and the nature of the finding map onto a real summer challenge: parents who feel relatively confident about their awareness of their kid's digital life may be working from an outdated picture.

What "Using AI" Actually Looks Like at Different Ages

The FOSI study doesn't break down AI use by specific tool or age group in its July 9 summary, but the general pattern of how kids engage with AI is consistent with what child technology researchers have documented over the past two years. Younger children (ages 6-10) most commonly encounter AI through creative tools: image generators, voice assistants, or AI characters in games. Middle schoolers are more likely to use AI for homework assistance, writing help, or answering questions they'd rather not ask in a search bar. Teenagers use a wider range of tools and are more likely to use AI proactively rather than stumbling into it.

None of these uses are automatically good or bad. AI can support genuine learning, creative exploration, and problem-solving. It can also short-circuit the practice that makes learning stick, encourage reliance on tools that give incorrect information confidently, and expose kids to generated content that ranges from unhelpful to harmful. The question isn't whether kids should be allowed to use AI at all. It's whether families have had enough of a conversation to think through how and when.

Talking About It Without Making It a Lecture

The FOSI report's value for Colorado Springs families isn't in the specific numbers but in the prompt it provides. If there's an 11-point gap nationally between what kids are doing and what parents think, there's a reasonable chance there's a gap in your household too. The goal isn't to close that gap through surveillance. It's to close it through conversation.

A few starting points that tend to work better than direct interrogation: ask your kid to show you something they've used recently. Ask whether any of their friends are using AI tools. Ask whether they've ever gotten AI to give them wrong information. Curiosity opens conversations that permission-seeking or grilling usually closes. Most kids who use AI regularly have interesting and specific things to say about what works and what doesn't, and those conversations build the kind of shared awareness the FOSI data suggests many families are missing.

What's Next

FOSI plans to update the "Beyond Borders" research as more data becomes available. The full report is accessible through the Family Online Safety Institute at fosi.org. For Colorado Springs families looking for structured conversation starters, Common Sense Media (commonsense.org) offers age-specific guides on AI literacy that include discussion frameworks for families. The AAP's guidance on media use, available through healthychildren.org, is also being updated to address AI specifically, with new material expected later in 2026.
