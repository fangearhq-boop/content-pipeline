Researchers Tested 86 Social Media Safety Features for Kids. Fewer Than Half Worked.

By Sarah Morales July 14, 2026

The Study and What Researchers Found

Researchers from New York University, Northeastern University, the Heat Initiative, and the Cybersafety Research Center spent months testing child safety features on four major platforms: TikTok, Instagram, Snapchat, and YouTube. They created dummy accounts designed to represent both children and adults, then systematically worked through each advertised safety feature to see whether it performed as claimed. The study was published June 29, 2026.

The result: 86 features tested. Only 35 worked as the platforms said they would. That's 40%, or fewer than half. Every platform in the study failed more than 50% of its own advertised safety features. The researchers documented four recurring types of failure: features that were simply absent despite being advertised, tools that existed but didn't function as described, safety settings buried in menus that most users would never navigate to, and keyword filters that adults could bypass without difficulty.

What Specifically Failed

The research team published a detailed breakdown of failure types across platforms, but the summary tells the story clearly. Features meant to prevent adults from contacting child accounts still allowed that contact in practice. Filters set up to block harmful search terms missed obvious variations. Parental oversight tools that were supposed to give parents visibility into their teen's activity either didn't work consistently or weren't linked properly between parent and child accounts. In some cases, features that platforms promoted publicly in press materials didn't appear to exist in the actual product.

The research team's language was pointed: "We have found systemic issues with the design and implementation of many of these features," the report states. It went further, noting that harms to children "are not hypothetical, and when they do occur the consequences can be irreversible." The organizations involved in the study are not fringe critics. NYU and Northeastern University are research institutions, and the Cybersafety Research Center focuses specifically on technology's effects on vulnerable populations.

Why This Matters If Your Kid Is on Any of These Platforms

The instinct among parents who've thought about social media safety is often to turn on controls and consider the problem managed. This study suggests that instinct, while reasonable, may not be producing the protection parents assume. The platforms have every financial incentive to appear safe to parents while keeping young users engaged, and the gap between marketed safety features and functional safety features documented in this research reflects that tension.

For Colorado Springs families with kids in middle school or high school, this matters practically. If your teen is on TikTok or Instagram and you've set up parental controls or supervised mode, there's a meaningful chance that what you set up isn't working the way you think it is. That doesn't mean the accounts are uncontrolled, but it means the technical controls are a less reliable safety net than the platforms suggest.

This also reinforces what most child safety experts have said for years: platform controls are a layer, not a solution. The research is useful not because it tells parents to panic, but because it explains why relying exclusively on in-app controls hasn't worked as well as many families hoped.

What Actually Provides More Reliable Protection

Researchers who study child online safety consistently point to a few things that hold up better than technical controls. Knowing your child's username and being able to see their public profile is a baseline. Having ongoing, low-stakes conversations about what they see online, rather than waiting for a specific incident, tends to produce better disclosure when something goes wrong. Delaying access to public social media platforms, particularly for kids under 13, reduces exposure to the categories of harm these features were designed to address.

For families who want a more structured approach, resources like the family contracts available from Common Sense Media (commonsense.org) provide a starting point for setting expectations that work regardless of whether any specific platform feature is functioning. The goal isn't a platform-managed solution. It's a relationship-based one, with platform tools as a supplementary layer rather than the primary strategy.

What's Next

The full study from NYU and Northeastern is publicly available through the Cybersafety Research Center. Advocacy organizations that co-authored the report, including the Heat Initiative, have called on Congress and federal regulators to require platforms to demonstrate that safety features function before advertising them. Whether regulatory pressure produces changes is uncertain, but the research has already been cited in ongoing federal discussions about social media and child safety. The full report is available at cybersafety.center. For Colorado Springs families looking for guidance, the American Academy of Pediatrics' media use resources at healthychildren.org include updated guidance on supporting older kids on social platforms.
