## STORY 1: FOSI Study — Parents Underestimate Kids' AI Use

**Content Pillar:** National Parenting
**Tier:** T1
**Freshness:** NEW — Published July 9, 2026
**Byline:** Jamie Rivera

**Summary:** The Family Online Safety Institute surveyed 4,000+ families in the US and Australia. Key finding: 38% of kids say they used generative AI in the past week. Only 27% of their parents think that's true. The 11-point gap on AI is wider than the gap on social media scrolling (8 points) or posting (8 points). The study is called "Beyond Borders" and positions this as a cross-national pattern, not a US-specific phenomenon.

**Angles:**
1. Lead with the number gap — parents think they know, kids know more
2. Summer angle — more unstructured screen time means less parental visibility
3. What does "using AI" mean for a kid? (homework help, chatbots, image generators)
4. Practical angle — what to talk about at dinner tonight
5. Not a panic story — a "let's get curious" story

**Headlines:**
- "Your Kid Probably Used AI This Week. You Might Not Know It."
- "New Study: Parents Think 27% of Kids Used AI Recently. Kids Say 38%."
- "The AI Gap: What COS Families Need to Know About Kids and Generative AI"

**X hooks:**
- Lead with the number contrast
- End with a question or action

**FB hooks:**
- Conversational, warm, "we've all been there" opening
- Engagement question: What's your family's approach to AI tools?

**Audience sentiment:** Curiosity + mild concern. Not panic. Parents want to understand, not be alarmed.

## STORY 2: Social Media Safety Features — Fewer Than Half Work

**Content Pillar:** National Parenting
**Tier:** T1
**Freshness:** NEW — Published June 29, 2026
**Byline:** Sarah Morales

**Summary:** NYU and Northeastern University researchers tested 86 advertised child safety features across TikTok, Instagram, Snapchat, and YouTube. Only 35 worked as claimed — that's fewer than half. Every platform failed more than 50% of its own advertised features. Failures included missing tools, broken settings, and keyword filters easily bypassed by adults. The researchers say harms to children "are not hypothetical."

**Angles:**
1. The specific number (86 features, 35 worked) is the hook
2. The platforms' self-reported "safety" vs reality
3. What parents can actually do instead of relying on platform tools
4. This is relevant for older kids and teens (ages 10+)
5. Privacy settings ≠ safety — how to have an honest conversation with your teen

**Headlines:**
- "Researchers Tested 86 Social Media Safety Features for Kids. Fewer Than Half Worked."
- "TikTok, Instagram, Snapchat, YouTube: Platform Child Safety Features Are Mostly Broken"
- "Don't Trust the Safety Settings: What New Research Says About Kids on Social Media"

**X hooks:**
- Lead with the ratio (86 tested, 35 worked)
- Short, punchy delivery

**FB hooks:**
- Lead with the research finding
- What parents can do instead
- Engagement: Do you trust the platform parental controls?

**Audience sentiment:** Validation of existing distrust + desire for practical alternatives.
