CPSC Alert: Vevor Baby Swings + Zigjoy Sleep Sacks Recalled
NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post ``` A quick heads-up for Colorado Springs families with young children: the CPSC has issued recalls on two common infant products this week, and both are worth checking right now. First, Vevor baby swings are being recalled for violating the Safe Sleep for Babies Act. These swings exceed the regulated incline angle, which means a sleeping infant can slide into a position that blocks their airway. If you own a Vevor baby swing, stop using it immediately — even for brief naps or soothing sessions. Second, Zigjoy baby sleep sacks have been pulled for failing flammability standards. That's a burn hazard at bedtime, which is exactly when kids are in these for extended stretches. If you have one, take it out of rotation until you can verify it's not part of the recall. There are several other products flagged in the same window: Tuyedoqe travel bassinets (strangulation and fall risk), Bellabu Bear robes (burn hazard), HiQiLi wintergreen oil (poisoning risk for children), and 4our Kiddies anti-tip furniture straps (tip-over risk). All recall details, eligibility, and refund instructions are at cpsc.gov/recalls. Do you check cpsc.gov regularly, or do you find out about recalls another way? Curious what works for other families. ``` ### Image Post Caption #### Image Caption — 8:00 AM MT ``` CPSC recall: Vevor baby swings + Zigjoy sleep sacks. Check cpsc.gov now. ```

AAP Drops the 2-Hour Screen Time Cap — What Changed in 2026
NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post ``` If you've ever felt guilty about letting the 2-hour screen time limit slide on a tough day — the AAP just changed the rules. In 2026, the American Academy of Pediatrics updated its screen time guidance and removed the longstanding 2-hour daily cap for children over 2. The new framework isn't a free pass for unlimited device time, but it's a meaningful shift in how the AAP wants parents to think about the issue. Instead of tracking the clock, the focus is now on what your kids are watching, how they're engaging with it, and what's happening around it. The AAP's recommended tool is a "family media plan" — essentially a household agreement about when, where, and what kind of screen time is okay. Think: no phones at dinner, one show before bed, video calls with grandparents are different from passive YouTube scrolling. This is a more realistic framework for how most families actually live. A 30-minute FaceTime with cousins and an educational video during quiet time are not the same as two hours of unsupervised algorithm-driven content, and the old rule didn't distinguish between them. Has the 2-hour rule ever felt useful to your family, or has it mostly been a source of stress? Curious how COS parents are actually handling it. ``` ### Image Post Caption #### Image Caption — 12:00 PM MT ``` AAP 2026: the 2-hour screen time cap is gone.
