CPSC Warning: Stop Using Babypark Children's Carriers — Infant Can Slip Through Leg Openings

By Sarah Morales | COS Parenting | July 10, 2026

Colorado Springs, CO

The Consumer Product Safety Commission is warning parents to stop using Babypark Children's Carriers immediately. The leg openings on the carriers are large enough for an infant to slip through — a potentially fatal fall hazard. The carriers violate the mandatory federal standard for soft infant and toddler carriers. If you have one, stop using it now and check cpsc.gov for guidance on next steps.

What the Warning Covers

This is a CPSC Warning, not a formal recall. The distinction matters: with a formal recall, there is typically an established refund or replacement program administered by the manufacturer. With a warning, the CPSC is alerting consumers to a known hazard and directing them to stop using the product while further investigation or enforcement proceeds. Either way, the safety guidance is the same — stop using the product now.

The specific hazard with Babypark Children's Carriers is the leg opening size. Federal standard 16 CFR Part 1231 — the mandatory standard for soft infant and toddler carriers — sets requirements for leg opening dimensions, among other structural requirements including buckle strength, seam integrity, and load-bearing capacity. When leg openings are too large, a child can slip through them feet-first, even if they appear to be secured in the carrier. Falls from a standing height while a parent is wearing a carrier can cause severe injury or death.

How to Check Any Carrier for Safe Fit

The Babypark warning is a good prompt to evaluate every soft carrier in your household, not just that brand. Here's what proper fit looks like:

The leg openings should fit snugly around your child's thighs — not loosely. If your child's legs slide through the openings easily with room to spare, the carrier is either too large for your child or has a design issue. There should be no gap where a small body could slip through.

Also look at the "M-position" — your child's hips should be supported so that their knees are at or above the level of their hips, forming an M shape when viewed from the front. This is both the ergonomically correct position for infant hip development and a sign that the carrier is holding the child properly rather than letting them sag.

Check buckles and adjustment straps regularly. Buckles that don't click solidly into place or adjustment straps that have stretched significantly over time are safety concerns regardless of brand. Any carrier with fraying seams, worn stitching, or visible fabric thinning near stress points should be replaced.

What to Do If You Have a Babypark Carrier

Stop using it immediately. Visit cpsc.gov and search "Babypark" to find the official warning page with the most current information on next steps, including whether a refund or exchange program has been established. If you purchased the carrier through a retailer, contact that retailer directly about return or refund options — many retailers have policies for CPSC-flagged products regardless of whether a formal recall has been issued.

What's Next

For the latest information on the Babypark warning and any additional carrier safety notices, visit cpsc.gov. If you're shopping for a replacement carrier, look for products that meet 16 CFR Part 1231 and check for recent CPSC compliance history before purchasing. Consumer Reports and babywearing advocacy organizations also maintain updated lists of carriers with safety concerns.
