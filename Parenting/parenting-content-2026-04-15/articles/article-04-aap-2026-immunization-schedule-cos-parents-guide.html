AAP Holds Firm on 2026 Immunization Schedule — A Guide for Colorado Springs Families

By Jenna Park | April 15, 2026

The American Academy of Pediatrics has confirmed its 2026 childhood vaccination recommendations are unchanged. Here's what that means for COS families navigating a noisy national conversation about vaccines.

There's been a lot of noise at the national level about vaccines and federal health guidance this year. If you've found yourself uncertain about what the current recommendations actually are for your children, you're not alone — and you're right to seek clarity from a trusted source.

Here's what we know: The American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP), the professional organization representing U.S. pediatricians, has confirmed that its 2026 childhood immunization schedule is unchanged. All routine vaccine recommendations remain the same. Nothing has been removed, and no timing has been adjusted.

The AAP reviewed its schedule independently and found no basis for changes. This is the schedule your Colorado Springs pediatrician is following.

Why This Matters Right Now

The context matters here. Some changes have been made to the federal advisory process around vaccines at the national level, and those changes have generated news coverage and questions from parents across the country. The AAP's decision to hold firm on its own evidence-based recommendations provides a stable reference point for parents navigating that environment.

The AAP's schedule is based on decades of clinical research and ongoing review by its Committee on Infectious Diseases. It represents what pediatricians — the doctors who actually see your children — recommend for protecting kids from preventable diseases.

What's on the 2026 Schedule

The AAP 2026 childhood immunization schedule covers all routine vaccines, organized by age. For Colorado Springs families, the key vaccines on the schedule include:

  • DTaP (diphtheria, tetanus, whooping cough) — given at 2, 4, 6, and 15-18 months, and again at 4-6 years
  • MMR (measles, mumps, rubella) — first dose at 12-15 months, second at 4-6 years
  • Varicella (chickenpox) — same schedule as MMR
  • Polio (IPV) — at 2, 4, and 6-18 months, and 4-6 years
  • Hib (Haemophilus influenzae type b) — at 2, 4, 6, and 12-15 months
  • Hepatitis B — at birth, 1-2 months, and 6-18 months
  • Hepatitis A — two doses between ages 12-23 months
  • Influenza — annually for all children 6 months and older
  • HPV — two doses starting at age 11-12 (the AAP encourages starting at the recommended age for maximum effectiveness)
  • Meningococcal — at 11-12 years, with a booster at 16

The full schedule, including catch-up schedules for children who have missed doses, is available at aap.org.

What to Do If You Have Questions

The best next step if you have specific questions about your child's vaccines is a conversation with your pediatrician. They have access to your child's complete immunization history, can help identify any catch-up doses needed, and can explain the reasoning behind each recommendation in the context of your child's health.

If you're not sure whether your kids are up to date, many Colorado Springs pediatric practices offer a quick records review at any well visit. You can also request your child's immunization records through your pediatrician's patient portal or through El Paso County Public Health (elpasocountyhealth.org).

What's Next

The AAP publishes its full updated immunization schedule at aap.org/immunizations. For local guidance specific to Colorado, the Colorado Department of Public Health and Environment (cdphe.colorado.gov) maintains vaccination resources for families. If you're new to Colorado Springs and still finding a pediatrician, D11 and D20 both have resources for connecting families with healthcare providers in the community.
