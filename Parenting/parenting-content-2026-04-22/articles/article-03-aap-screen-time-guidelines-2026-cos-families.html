The AAP Just Changed the Screen Time Rules — Here's What It Means for Your Family

By Keisha Williams | COS Parenting

April 22, 2026

If you've ever looked up from dinner to find three separate screens glowing in the room and wondered whether you're doing this right — you're not alone. And now the American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) has updated its guidance to reflect the reality of how families actually live with technology in 2026.

The short version: for kids ages 6 and older, the old "2 hours per day" rule is gone. In its place is a framework based on quality, context, and what screens are replacing in a child's day.

The Age-by-Age Breakdown

Under 18 months: No screen time. The one exception is video chatting with family — grandparents on FaceTime, for example. Passive consumption (videos, shows, apps) should be avoided entirely during this window.

Ages 18-24 months: If you introduce screens at this stage, keep it to educational programming and watch it together. The "watch together" part matters because it allows you to narrate and interact — which is how toddlers actually learn from media.

Ages 2-5: One hour per day of high-quality content. Co-viewing is still recommended when possible. The AAP continues to emphasize that not all content is equal — a passive entertainment show and an interactive learning app are very different experiences.

Ages 6 and older: No set time limit. The focus shifts entirely to habits and quality. The AAP now asks three guiding questions:

  • Is the content high quality and age-appropriate?
  • Is it interactive or mostly passive?
  • Is screen time displacing sleep, physical activity, family time, or free play?

What Hasn't Changed

Device-free bedrooms and mealtimes are still strongly recommended. The AAP continues to flag that screens in bedrooms interfere with sleep, and that mealtime screens reduce family conversation — both of which have documented effects on child development and family connection.

Modeling also matters. Parents who use phones at the dinner table or scroll while spending time with their kids are sending a message — and kids notice. The AAP's framework explicitly includes parental behavior as part of the "digital ecosystem."

Why the Change?

The 2016 guidelines were written when most screen time meant TV. Today, kids are on phones, tablets, gaming consoles, educational apps, and video calls — all at once. A single blanket time limit doesn't capture the complexity of modern screen use. The 2026 update reflects what pediatricians were already telling parents in practice: it's less about how many hours and more about what kids are doing during that time.

What's Next

The AAP published its full policy statement — "Digital Ecosystems, Children, and Adolescents" — in the journal Pediatrics. It's available at publications.aap.org for parents who want to read the full research foundation behind these recommendations.
