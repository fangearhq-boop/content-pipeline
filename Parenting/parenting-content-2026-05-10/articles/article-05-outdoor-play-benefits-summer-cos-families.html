Why Outdoor Play Is the Best Summer Investment — Research for COS Families

By Jamie Rivera | May 10, 2026 | COS Parenting

Before you finalize the summer schedule, here's what pediatric research says about the most impactful thing kids can do for their health — and it doesn't cost a registration fee.

What the Research Actually Says

The American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) and major children's hospitals have built a consistent body of evidence around outdoor play and child development. The findings are not subtle. Unstructured time outdoors — kids playing outside without a coach or curriculum driving the experience — delivers benefits across physical health, mental health, cognitive development, and social skills simultaneously.

Here's what the research confirms:

Benefit What Research Shows
Physical health Lower rates of obesity, stronger bones and muscles; children burn more calories outdoors and engage in more vigorous movement
Vitamin D Sun exposure supports vitamin D production, which contributes to bone health and may reduce chronic disease risk
Stress reduction Stress levels for both kids and adults drop within minutes of being outside; nature exposure lowers cortisol
ADHD Children with ADHD who spend significant time outdoors exhibit fewer symptoms, according to multiple pediatric studies
Cognitive development Outdoor play improves focus, critical thinking, and creativity; schools with environmental education programs see higher standardized test scores
Social skills Unstructured outdoor play with other children builds sharing, cooperation, and conflict resolution in ways that structured activities don't replicate
Immune function Nature-based play has been associated with stronger immune system development, particularly in younger children

The Unstructured Part Matters

One distinction that comes up consistently in the research: unstructured outdoor play produces different — and in some ways greater — benefits than organized outdoor activities. Soccer practice, swim lessons, and organized camps all have value. But the kind of play where kids decide what to do, make up the rules, solve problems as they arise, and follow their own curiosity seems to be particularly powerful for cognitive and social development.

The challenge in modern parenting is that unstructured time has become rarer. Summers increasingly fill with back-to-back activities, screens, and structured programs. The research suggests that building in time that is explicitly unscheduled — just outside, just playing — is worth protecting as a family priority.

Colorado Springs Families Have a Genuine Advantage Here

Not every family has easy access to safe, quality outdoor spaces. Colorado Springs families do, and that advantage is worth using deliberately. Within the city and immediately surrounding it, families have access to:

  • Palmer Park — 730 acres of trails and open space within the city limits
  • Cheyenne Mountain State Park — hiking trails accessible from the south side of the city
  • Garden of the Gods — one of the most dramatically beautiful free natural spaces in the country
  • Monument Valley Park — green space and trails along Monument Creek running through midtown
  • Pikes Peak region trails — dozens of accessible options at varying difficulty levels

The goal doesn't need to be a summit. It can be an afternoon at a neighborhood trail, a creek, or even a backyard. The research is agnostic about altitude and scenery — it simply confirms that being outside, moving freely, matters.

What's Next

As D11 school ends May 22 and summer begins, families in Colorado Springs are in a good position to make outdoor time a deliberate part of the season. Summer camp schedules don't have to fill every hour. Screens don't have to fill the gaps. The research on outdoor play provides a clear and well-supported case for doing something that, honestly, most kids still prefer anyway — going outside.
