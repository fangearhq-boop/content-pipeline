### STORY 1: Vevor Baby Swing Recall

#### Long-Form Post — 8:00 AM MT

Attention, families with babies: if you have a Vevor baby swing at home, check the model number now.

The Consumer Product Safety Commission has recalled three models of Vevor baby swings — BB501K, BB702A, and BB005K — due to a serious suffocation risk. The swings were sold on Amazon.com and Vevor.com from January 2025 through August 2025 for between $65 and $80. 🚨

The problem: the swings are inclined at more than 10 degrees, which violates the federal mandatory standard for infant sleep products and the Safe Sleep for Babies Act ban on inclined sleepers. Inclined surfaces cause an infant's head to tip forward, which can block the airway. Between 2005 and 2019, inclined sleepers were linked to at least 94 infant deaths, according to the CPSC.

**If you have one of these swings:** Stop using it immediately. To get your full refund, remove the swing cover, cut it in half, write "Recalled" on the base with a permanent marker, and send a photo to recalling@vevor.com. Then dispose of the swing. Questions? Call Sanven Technology at 855-599-6320, Monday through Friday, 9 AM–5 PM PT.

Do you still have baby products with inclines at home? Drop a comment if you have questions — and share this so other parents see it.

### STORY 2: COS Parks Budget Cuts

#### Long-Form Post — 3:00 PM MT

Heads up for anyone planning summer playground trips around Colorado Springs.

The city's Parks, Recreation and Cultural Services Department is facing a $3.1 million budget cut this year, and the impacts on families are real. 🌳

Here's what to expect at your neighborhood parks this summer: if playground equipment is damaged or vandalized, it will be removed rather than repaired. The maintenance budget has been cut roughly in half, down to about $100,000. Playground surfacing materials — the wood fiber that cushions falls — have been cut 50%. And about 25% of neighborhood park permanent restrooms will remain closed for the 2026 season.

Before you write off summer parks entirely — there's some good news. Flanagan Park in central Colorado Springs is getting a major upgrade thanks to a $750,000 federal community development grant. That means a brand new playground, an ADA-accessible walking path with lighting and benches, and a resurfaced basketball court.

It's a mixed picture this summer. Some parks will have less than usual, and some will be better than ever. Checking coloradosprings.gov/PRCS before you head out is a smart move.

Which park do your kids love most? Tell us in the comments — we'll keep track of which ones are still in good shape this season.

### STORY 4: COS Childcare Desert

#### Long-Form Post — 10:00 AM MT

This is a number worth sitting with: El Paso County has approximately 46,800 children under the age of five. There are enough child care facility spots for around 17,500 of them.

That's a gap of more than 29,000 kids without a guaranteed child care option.

Eastern Colorado Springs — particularly the southeast and the Fountain-Fort Carson corridor — is where the shortage hits hardest. Child care advocates have described it as the "hottest part of the desert." When families there can find care, they're paying an average of $16,300 per year, per child. For many, that calculus means leaving the workforce entirely is actually the financially rational choice.

In January 2026, child care advocacy groups launched a media campaign to draw attention to the shortage. In February, a Colorado Springs Gazette guest column called it a serious economic and family stability problem for the city. The conversation is getting louder — but the number of available spots hasn't changed much.

What changes the math: more funding for providers, less red tape on building conversions that could create new facilities, and wages that allow child care workers to stay in the field.

Is the childcare shortage impacting your family? Have you found solutions that work? Share in the comments — this community has some great knowledge about local options.

### STORY 5: D11 Labor Dispute

#### Long-Form Post — 5:00 PM MT

If you have kids in Colorado Springs School District 11, this is worth knowing as you start thinking about the fall school year.

In December 2024, the D11 Board of Education voted 7-1 to dissolve the master agreement it had with educators — an agreement that had been in place for 56 years. The Colorado Springs Education Association pushed back hard. In October 2025, more than 1,000 teachers, students, and community members participated in a one-day strike outside Palmer High School and other D11 schools — about a third of the teaching staff. 91% of CSEA teachers had voted in favor of striking.

In May 2026 — more than 300 days after the master agreement was severed — educators and community members picketed again at the D11 Board of Education meeting.

As of June 2026, there is no new master agreement in place.

What this means for families heading into the 2026-27 school year: the labor dispute is ongoing and unresolved. Teacher morale and retention are factors to watch. The district has made some compensation adjustments but has not restored the formal collective bargaining structure. For the most current information, follow d11.org and csea-cos.org.
