Colorado Springs Childcare Desert: El Paso County Has 46,800 Kids Under 5 and Spots for 17,500

By Jamie Rivera | June 7, 2026

The numbers are stark. El Paso County has approximately 46,800 children under the age of five. Across all licensed child care facilities in the county, there are spots for roughly 17,500 of them. That's a gap of more than 29,000 children — more than half of the county's youngest kids — without guaranteed access to formal child care.

For families in Colorado Springs, particularly those in the eastern and southeastern parts of the city, this shortage isn't abstract. It's the waitlists that stretch months. It's the decision to reduce work hours or leave a job entirely because child care costs more than a salary can justify. It's a problem with real math behind it.

Where the Shortage Is Worst

Not all parts of Colorado Springs are equally affected. According to local reporting from KOAA, the childcare shortage in COS is geographic: eastern Colorado Springs, particularly the southeast and the Fountain-Fort Carson corridor, is described as the "hottest part of the desert." These areas are among the fastest-growing in the region, yet the childcare infrastructure has not kept pace with population growth.

The mismatch is compounded by the economics of running a child care business. Starting or expanding a child care facility requires complying with building renovation requirements that can be expensive and complex, which has slowed the growth of new providers in areas that need them most.

The Cost When You Can Find Care

When families in El Paso County do manage to secure a child care spot, they're paying for it. The average annual cost of child care in Colorado Springs is approximately $16,300 per child. For many working parents, particularly in lower- and middle-wage jobs, that figure approaches or exceeds what they earn.

This is the calculus that pushes families out of the workforce. When the cost of care exceeds the net income from a job — after taxes, transportation, and other work-related expenses — staying home becomes the financially rational choice, even if it's not the preferred one. The result is a dual crisis: families who can't afford care, and an economy that loses workforce participation as a result.

Child care in Colorado is sometimes compared, cost-for-cost, to in-state college tuition. That comparison has circulated in local media for good reason: it captures just how far the price of care has traveled from what the average family can reasonably absorb.

What Advocates and Officials Are Saying

The shortage has begun to generate sustained public attention in 2026. In January, child care advocacy groups in Colorado Springs launched a media campaign specifically aimed at drawing community and policy attention to the issue. In February, a guest column in the Colorado Springs Gazette called it a "serious lack of childcare problem" with consequences for the local economy and for working families across the city.

Proposed solutions generally fall into two categories: funding and regulation. On the funding side, advocates are calling for more state and local investment in child care subsidies and provider support. On the regulatory side, some argue that easing specific building renovation requirements would make it more feasible for new providers to open facilities in underserved areas. A new grant program from Rocky Mountain PBS noted in June 2026 is investing $140,000 to address childcare affordability across Colorado.

What Families Can Do Now

For Colorado Springs families navigating the shortage, a few practical resources are worth knowing. The Colorado Department of Early Childhood (cdec.colorado.gov) maintains information on licensed providers and subsidy programs. The Child Care Assistance Program (CCAP) provides subsidies for qualifying families — income and eligibility details are available through El Paso County Human Services. Starting waitlist applications early — often more than a year in advance for infant slots at popular centers — is one of the most effective strategies families can use in a constrained market.

What's Next

The childcare access conversation in Colorado Springs is expected to continue through 2026 as advocacy campaigns and state-level discussions about funding progress. Families seeking childcare resources can visit cdec.colorado.gov for the Colorado Department of Early Childhood, elpasohumanservices.org for subsidy program information, and coloradosprings.gov for local referral resources. For ongoing local coverage of the childcare issue, follow the Colorado Springs Gazette and KOAA.
