COS Parks Face $3.1 Million in Budget Cuts — What Families Need to Know This Summer

By Jamie Rivera | June 7, 2026

Before you pack up the stroller and head to your neighborhood park this summer, it's worth knowing that Colorado Springs parks are operating under a significant budget reduction in 2026. The city's Parks, Recreation and Cultural Services department is contending with $3.1 million in cuts — and the effects are being felt across playground equipment, restroom access, and general maintenance.

That doesn't mean every park in the city is in bad shape. But knowing what to expect helps families plan better — and there are specific parks where the impacts will be more visible than others.

What the Budget Cuts Mean for Playgrounds

The most direct impact for families with young children is to playground maintenance. The city's budget for playground repairs and upkeep has been reduced to approximately $100,000, down roughly 50% from previous funding levels. A key policy change that comes with that reduction: damaged or vandalized playground equipment will be removed rather than repaired.

That means families may find certain pieces of equipment at their local playground missing this summer — not because they were upgraded or relocated, but because the budget to fix them isn't there. Playground surfacing materials — specifically the engineered wood fiber that cushions falls under equipment — have also been cut by 50%, meaning some surfaces may receive less upkeep.

Before heading to a specific park with younger children, it's worth checking coloradosprings.gov/PRCS for any updates on specific locations.

Restroom Closures

Approximately 25% of neighborhood park permanent restrooms will remain closed throughout the 2026 season. Additionally, there will be fewer portable restroom units available across the park system. For families spending extended time at the parks — especially with younger children in the middle of potty training — this is worth knowing in advance. Planning for a restroom stop before you arrive, or identifying which parks in your area still have open facilities, will make for a smoother outing.

A Bright Spot: Flanagan Park Gets a Major Upgrade

While city-wide funding is tight, there is genuinely good news for one Colorado Springs park. Flanagan Park, located in central Colorado Springs, is receiving a $750,000 federal community development grant that will fund significant improvements.

The upgrade includes a brand new playground, an ADA-accessible walking path with lighting and benches, and a resurfaced basketball court. For families in the central part of the city, Flanagan Park is shaping up to be one of the best park options this summer.

Putting This in Context

The $3.1 million reduction represents a meaningful cut to a department that serves more than 300,000 Colorado Springs residents annually through its parks, community centers, sports complexes, and programming. The cuts are the result of broader city budget pressures and were announced earlier this year.

City officials have indicated that the goal is to manage reductions while preserving as much community access as possible. The Parks, Recreation and Cultural Services department operates more than 100 parks across Colorado Springs, so the impacts vary significantly by location. Some parks — particularly those that receive more use or are in areas with higher community investment — may see fewer visible effects than others.

What's Next

Families can check the status of specific Colorado Springs parks and facilities at coloradosprings.gov/PRCS. For questions about specific park conditions, contact the Parks, Recreation and Cultural Services department directly through the city website. The Flanagan Park improvements are expected to be completed during the 2026 season. To stay current on park updates throughout the summer, bookmark the PRCS page and check it before weekend outings.
