Aspen Acres Fire: Air Quality Alert for Colorado Springs Families

By Sarah Morales | July 13, 2026

The Aspen Acres Fire has burned more than 98,000 acres and is 35% contained as of July 12 — but the smoke it's generating is still a significant health concern for Colorado Springs families. IQAir is currently ranking Colorado's air quality among the worst in the world, and that's not just a problem for people with respiratory conditions.

Fine particulate matter — the type of pollution that dominates wildfire smoke — is small enough to penetrate deep into the lungs and enter the bloodstream. Health experts recommend that everyone, not just those with asthma, take precautions during periods of elevated wildfire smoke.

Current Fire Status

As of July 12, the Aspen Acres Fire stands at:

  • 98,099 acres burned
  • 35% contained (up from 34% the previous day)
  • 780+ structures destroyed — making it the 7th most destructive wildfire in Colorado's recorded history
  • 300+ additional firefighters deployed

Mandatory evacuation orders remain in effect across portions of Custer, Pueblo, and Huerfano counties, affecting communities including Colorado City, Beulah, Rye, San Isabel, Wetmore, Red Creek, and Apache City. If you have family in those areas, check in with them and direct them to inciweb.wildfire.gov for the most current information.

What the Smoke Means for Your Family

Wildfire smoke contains fine particulate matter (PM2.5) along with carbon monoxide and other toxic compounds. Children are particularly vulnerable because their lungs are still developing and they breathe more air per pound of body weight than adults do.

Kids with asthma, allergies, or other respiratory conditions face the highest risk during smoke events, but the health effects extend to otherwise healthy children, especially with prolonged exposure.

What to Do Right Now

Here's a practical list of steps for Colorado Springs families:

  • Keep kids inside. Especially during afternoon hours when smoke tends to concentrate at lower elevations.
  • Close windows and doors. Even when it doesn't look or smell smoky outside, fine particles can still enter your home.
  • Run air purifiers if you have one. HEPA filters are the most effective for capturing PM2.5 particles. If you don't have a filter, DIY box-fan filters using MERV 13+ furnace filters can help.
  • Skip outdoor exercise today. Exercise increases how much air — and therefore how many particles — your body takes in per minute.
  • Check the Air Quality Index. airnow.gov and IQAir provide current AQI readings by zip code. Anything above 100 (unhealthy for sensitive groups) warrants extra caution for children.
  • Watch for symptoms. Coughing, difficulty breathing, eye irritation, and headaches can all indicate smoke exposure. If your child's asthma symptoms flare, follow your doctor's action plan and don't hesitate to call.

When to Seek Medical Help

If your child is having difficulty breathing, is wheezing, or their rescue inhaler isn't providing relief, seek medical attention. Children with asthma should use their asthma action plans during smoke events — and if you don't have a written plan, this is a good time to ask your pediatrician for one.

What's Next

Containment on the Aspen Acres Fire is improving, but firefighters are still dealing with challenging weather — low humidity and strong outflow winds from afternoon thunderstorms. Meaningful smoke relief depends on both fire progress and wind patterns.

Check the CDPHE Air Quality Forecast at colorado.gov/cdphe for Colorado-specific forecasts, and follow inciweb.wildfire.gov for fire updates. We'll continue to cover air quality and evacuation status as conditions change.
