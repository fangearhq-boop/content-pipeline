AAP Updates Drowning Prevention Guidelines for Summer 2026: What Colorado Springs Parents Need to Know

By Jamie Rivera | July 13, 2026

Drowning is the leading cause of death for children between the ages of 1 and 4 in the United States. That number hasn't budged in years. This summer, the American Academy of Pediatrics is trying to change that — starting with updated guidance that pediatricians across the country are now using to counsel families.

The AAP published its updated "Prevention of Drowning" policy statement in the June 2026 issue of Pediatrics, revising guidance the organization last updated in 2019. The core message is one that many parents haven't fully internalized: no single safety measure is enough.

What Changed in the 2026 Guidelines

The most talked-about update involves swim lessons. The previous guidance was sometimes interpreted as holding off on formal lessons until age 4. The 2026 statement shifts that: the AAP now says swim lessons can be considered on an individualized basis after a child turns 1 year old.

That doesn't mean every 13-month-old should be enrolled in swimming instruction. The AAP's guidance is that the decision should be based on the child's emotional maturity, physical and cognitive readiness, and any relevant health considerations. Talk to your child's pediatrician if you're unsure when to start.

What the guidelines make crystal clear is that swim lessons — even good ones — don't eliminate drowning risk on their own. They're one layer in what the AAP describes as a multi-layer prevention system.

The Layered Prevention Approach

Think of water safety the way you think about car safety: a seatbelt is essential, but you also want airbags, traffic laws, and a safe speed. No single element is sufficient on its own.

The AAP's recommended layers for preventing drowning include:

  • Swim lessons — age-appropriate, taught by qualified instructors
  • Pool barriers and fencing — four-sided fencing with a self-latching gate significantly reduces drowning risk in residential pools
  • Life jackets — US Coast Guard-approved, worn correctly whenever children are near open water
  • Close, constant supervision — the guidelines are specific about what this means (see below)
  • CPR training — for caregivers and anyone who supervises children near water

The Water Watcher

The updated guidelines place significant emphasis on the concept of a "water watcher" — a designated adult whose only job while children are in the water is watching them.

The AAP is unambiguous about what that means: no phone. No conversations. No stepping away to grab a drink or check on something inside. The water watcher's sole responsibility is supervising the swimmers — and that person must be capable of recognizing when a child is in distress, performing a rescue, initiating CPR, and calling for help.

At backyard pools or any gathering where multiple adults are present, the assumption that "someone is watching" often means no one is truly watching. Designating a named water watcher — and rotating that responsibility on a timer — is one of the most effective things a group of adults can do.

Drowning Risk in Colorado

Colorado Springs has pools, splash pads, lakes, and rivers within easy reach. The Fountain Creek, Rampart Reservoir, and numerous HOA and recreation center pools are all within a short drive. The same hazards that make summer fun also require attention.

Adolescents have the second-highest drowning fatality rate in the US, per the AAP data — and many of those deaths occur in open water settings like rivers and reservoirs, where conditions change quickly and life jackets are often not worn.

What's Next

If you're looking for swimming instruction in Colorado Springs, check with the city's parks and recreation department, local YMCAs, and community pool programs. Most offer lessons for children starting around age 6 months for water familiarization and formal instruction starting at age 2 to 3.

For the full AAP policy statement, visit publications.aap.org or ask your child's pediatrician for the most current guidance at your next visit. And before your next pool day, talk with the other adults going about who the designated water watcher will be.
