Summer Sun Safety in Colorado Springs: What Parents Need to Know About High-Altitude UV Risk

By Jamie Rivera | June 5, 2026

Colorado Springs summers are long on sunshine and short on shade, and the city's elevation makes that combination genuinely different from most of the country. At 6,035 feet above sea level, families here are exposed to roughly 24% more UV radiation than at sea level — a difference that matters more than most people realize, especially for young children.

With summer break now underway and outdoor time increasing, here's a practical guide to protecting your kids in the Colorado sun.

Why Colorado Springs UV Is Different

Ultraviolet radiation increases with altitude because there's less atmosphere above you to filter it. For every 1,000 feet of elevation gain, UV exposure increases by roughly 4–5%. At 6,035 feet, that adds up fast. During June and July, the UV index in Colorado Springs regularly reaches 8 or higher — a level the EPA classifies as "Very High" — and can hit 10 or above on clear summer days, which is classified as "Extreme."

A UV index of 8 means unprotected skin can begin to burn in as little as 15 minutes. A level 10 means even less time. For children, whose skin is more sensitive than adults, exposure at these levels warrants real precautions — not just a quick spray of sunscreen on the way out the door.

Sunscreen: Getting It Right

The American Academy of Dermatology recommends SPF 30 or higher for children. SPF 30 blocks about 97% of UVB rays; SPF 50 blocks about 98%. The difference between SPF values is less important than consistent, correct application.

Apply sunscreen 15 minutes before sun exposure — it needs time to bind to the skin. Reapply every two hours, and immediately after swimming or heavy sweating. Spray sunscreens need to be rubbed in after spraying; spraying alone often leaves gaps in coverage. For children's faces, lotion or stick formulas are more reliable than sprays.

The American Academy of Pediatrics advises against using sunscreen on infants under six months old. For that age group, protective clothing, shade, and hats are the recommended tools. If sun exposure is unavoidable, a small amount of SPF 30+ on the face and back of hands is considered acceptable as a last resort.

Timing and Shade

The UV index in Colorado Springs peaks between 10 AM and 4 PM. Planning outdoor activities — park trips, hikes, sports practice — for early morning or late afternoon makes a meaningful difference. A family outing at 8 AM versus noon involves substantially less UV exposure, even on the same sunny day.

Shade is effective UV protection, but not complete protection. UV radiation reflects off concrete, sand, and water, meaning children can still burn in the shade near reflective surfaces. A shaded picnic table at a park is safer than direct sun but not zero-risk.

Heat and Hydration

Children, particularly toddlers, dehydrate more quickly than adults. On warm Colorado Springs summer days, offer water frequently even if children don't ask for it. Sugary drinks are not a substitute for water when it comes to hydration during heat exposure.

Signs of heat exhaustion in children include heavy sweating, pale or cool and clammy skin, weakness, dizziness, headache, and nausea. If your child shows these symptoms, move to shade or a cool indoor space immediately, give cool water in small sips, apply cool wet cloths to the skin, and contact your pediatrician if symptoms don't improve within 15 to 30 minutes or worsen at any point. Heat stroke — marked by hot dry skin, confusion, or loss of consciousness — is a medical emergency requiring 911.

Protective Clothing and Gear

Wide-brimmed hats (at least 3-inch brims) protect the face, neck, and ears — areas children frequently miss with sunscreen. UV-protective clothing with a UPF 50+ rating offers reliable protection on high-exposure days and reduces how much sunscreen a child needs overall. Sunglasses rated for UV400 protection protect children's eyes, which are more susceptible to UV damage than adult eyes.

What's Next

Check the UV index before outdoor plans using the National Weather Service forecast for Colorado Springs or a weather app with UV index display. On days forecast at 8 or above — which is most clear summer days here — use SPF 30+ sunscreen, schedule outdoor time before 10 AM or after 4 PM when possible, pack hats and water, and limit direct sun exposure for infants. For pediatric sun safety guidance specific to your child's age and skin type, your child's doctor is the best resource.
