72% of U.S. Parents Want More Time With Their Kids — New Survey Shows Money Is the Barrier

By Sarah Morales | June 5, 2026

A new national survey of more than 5,000 parents confirms what many Colorado Springs families already know from lived experience: the biggest obstacle to spending time with your children isn't desire — it's money.

The 2026 National Parent Survey, conducted by New America's New Practice Lab, surveyed 5,472 parents across all 50 states. The results, released this spring, paint a picture of families under real financial strain, with working arrangements that don't match what parents actually want.

The Financial Reality Behind the Numbers

Nearly three in four parents said they want more quality time with their children. But the survey found that financial pressure — not lack of desire — is what stands in the way. For families near the economic middle, child care alone can consume a quarter of household income. At an average of $13,184 per child per year, full-time care is simply out of reach for many families without significant workplace support or subsidy.

Three-quarters of parents said their current work arrangement is not what they want. That doesn't mean parents want to stop working — 91% said they do want to work. What they want is more flexibility, better pay, and enough support at home to be genuinely present when they're not on the clock.

On parental leave, the median for mothers was 10 weeks and just 2 weeks for fathers. Most parents in both groups said those windows weren't enough. More than half of fathers — 52% — said 2 weeks was too short. Nearly 6 in 10 mothers said the same about 10 weeks.

What This Means for Colorado Springs Families

The national numbers resonate locally. Colorado Springs has roughly 47,000 children under the age of 5. Licensed child care capacity in the area sits at fewer than 19,000 spots — meaning the community is structurally short on accessible care options even before cost enters the picture.

When child care is unavailable or unaffordable, parents face a narrower set of choices: reduce work hours, leave the workforce temporarily, rely on informal or unpaid care arrangements, or stretch financially to cover licensed care. Each of those paths carries its own costs, and the survey suggests most families are navigating real trade-offs rather than simple preferences.

The survey framing — more quality time with kids as a shared aspiration — is worth noting because it cuts across income levels and family structures. This isn't a niche concern. Among more than 5,000 parents surveyed nationwide, it was the most widely shared priority.

What Parents Actually Want

The survey's finding that 91% of parents want to work shifts the policy conversation. The question isn't whether parents value careers — they do. The question is whether working parents have the financial and logistical support to be present with their families outside of working hours.

The top items parents identified as solutions: higher wages, more flexible work schedules, affordable child care, and longer paid parental leave. These aren't abstract policy preferences — they're the specific things survey respondents said would change their daily experience of parenting.
