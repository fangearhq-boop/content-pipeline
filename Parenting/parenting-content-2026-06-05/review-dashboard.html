CPSC Recall — TOMY Boon NURSH 8 oz Baby Bottles (Choking Hazard, Walmart)
NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post If you have Boon NURSH baby bottles at home, check them right now. TOMY International, in cooperation with the CPSC, recalled about 40,000 Boon NURSH Silicone Bottle 8 oz 3-Packs in Pink Tie Dye on June 4. The hard plastic outer shell can bubble or partially peel off, releasing loose pieces of film-like plastic that pose a choking hazard to babies. The bottles were sold exclusively at Walmart and Walmart.com from November 2025 through May 2026 for about $20. To check if yours are affected, look at the bottom of the packaging for item number B11654 or UPC 669028116546. Only the Pink Tie Dye colorway is recalled — solid colors are not. Here's how to get your refund or replacement: write the word "RECALL" on each bottle, photograph them, and submit the photos to certify they've been destroyed. You'll receive either a replacement 3-pack in a solid pink color or a $22 store credit at Walmart.com. To start the process, visit recall.tomy.com/nursh or call 866-725-4407 (Monday–Friday, 8 AM–4 PM CT). You can also email cs@tomy.com. TOMY has received 135 reports of the peeling issue, and no injuries have been reported. If you've seen something similar with your bottles, that's the issue. Don't wait on this one. Do you use Boon bottles? Check yours today and share this with anyone who might have them. ---

NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post D20 families, two important dates to put on your calendar this month. First: transportation requests. If you'll need bus service for the 2026–27 school year, the deadline to submit your request is June 30. If you're a new D20 student, requests open June 8. New National Survey — 72% of Parents Want More Time With Their Kids But Money Gets in the Way
NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post New survey results just dropped — and if you've ever felt pulled in 10 directions at once, the data says you're not alone. The 2026 National Parent Survey from New America's New Practice Lab surveyed 5,472 parents from every state, and the results are striking. Nearly three in four parents — 72% — say they want more quality time with their kids. But here's the catch: money is the biggest thing in the way. The average cost of child care in the US is now $13,184 per child per year — that's about one-quarter of a family's income for households at 200% of the federal poverty level. At the same time, 75% of parents say their current work arrangement isn't what they want. It's not that families don't want to be home — they're navigating a financial reality that doesn't make it easy. On parental leave, the median is 10 weeks for mothers and 2 weeks for fathers. A majority of both said that wasn't enough time. What's interesting is that 91% of parents said they do want to work — they just want more flexibility, higher wages, and enough breathing room to actually be present when they're home. For Colorado Springs families, none of this is surprising. COS has about 47,000 children under 5 and fewer than 19,000 licensed childcare spots. The national survey puts data behind something local parents already feel. What would genuinely help your family find more quality time together? ---

COS Summer Sun Safety Guide — Altitude Means Higher UV Risk for Kids
NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post Now that summer is officially here, here's a gentle reminder that Colorado Springs isn't like most places when it comes to sun safety. At 6,035 feet above sea level, Colorado Springs receives significantly more UV radiation than coastal or lower-elevation cities — roughly 24% more than at sea level. During June and July, the UV index here regularly hits 8 or above, which the EPA classifies as "Very High," and can spike to 10 or higher ("Extreme") on clear summer days. What that means practically for COS families spending time outdoors: **Sunscreen:** Use SPF 30 or higher. Apply it 15 minutes before heading outside and reapply every two hours — more often after swimming or sweating. Spray sunscreens need to be rubbed in to work. **Timing:** The sun is strongest between 10 AM and 4 PM. If you're planning outdoor activities — a trip to the park, Get Outdoors Day at Memorial Park, hiking — early morning or late afternoon is significantly safer for kids' skin. **Babies:** The American Academy of Pediatrics recommends keeping infants under six months out of direct sunlight entirely. Shade, protective clothing, and hats are the primary protection for the youngest members of your family. Sunscreen is generally not recommended for infants under six months. **Heat and hydration:** Children, especially toddlers, dehydrate faster than adults. On warm days, offer water frequently. Signs of heat exhaustion include heavy sweating, pale or cool skin, weakness, dizziness, and nausea. If any of these appear, move to shade, give fluids, and call your pediatrician if symptoms don't improve quickly. **Protective clothing:** A wide-brimmed hat and UV-protective clothing (look for UPF 50+ labels) add meaningful protection on full-sun days. COS summers are beautiful. A few habits now make the whole season easier for your family. What are your go-to sun safety products for COS summer? ---
