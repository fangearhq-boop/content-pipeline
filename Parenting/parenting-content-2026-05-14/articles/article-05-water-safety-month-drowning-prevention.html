National Water Safety Month: What Colorado Springs Families Need to Know Before Pool Season

By Sarah Morales | COS Parenting | May 14, 2026

Colorado Springs, CO

May is National Water Safety Month. It lands at a useful time on the calendar — pools and splash pads are about to open, families are planning summer outdoor activities, and most of us haven't thought about water safety since last September. Colorado Springs outdoor pools open Memorial Day weekend, starting May 24. Before the season arrives, it's worth taking a few minutes to go over the basics that the American Academy of Pediatrics recommends for families with young children.

The statistic that frames everything: drowning is the leading cause of injury-related death in children ages one to four, according to the AAP. That's not a distant risk. It's the thing that kills more young children in that age range than any other preventable injury. Most of those incidents happen in familiar settings — home pools, bathtubs, buckets of water — not ocean beaches or unfamiliar waterways.

Touch Supervision: What It Actually Means

The AAP uses the term "touch supervision" for infants and toddlers near water. It means exactly what it sounds like: an adult should be within arm's reach any time a young child is in or near water. Not across the pool. Not inside the house watching through a window. Within arm's reach.

The reason the guideline is this specific is that children can drown in under two minutes, often silently. Drowning does not look like the movies — it typically involves no splashing, no yelling, no visible distress until it's too late. A child can slip underwater in a pool, bath, or even a shallow plastic tub while an adult is momentarily distracted. Touch supervision eliminates most of the window in which that can happen.

For older children who can swim, "active supervision" still applies: an adult watches them at all times and is not distracted by a phone, conversation, or book. Designating a Water Watcher — one adult whose sole responsibility is watching the water during pool time — is a practical tool that families and groups often find useful.

Swimming Lessons

The AAP recommends swim lessons as an important tool for drowning prevention. For most children, lessons can begin at age one. The right timing depends on your child's individual development, comfort in water, and physical readiness — your pediatrician is the best resource for guidance on your specific child.

In Colorado Springs, PPYMCA locations offer swim lesson programs for children starting in the early years. City recreation programs and private swim clubs also offer instruction through the summer. Checking ppymca.org or the Colorado Springs city recreation website before the season starts is the most reliable way to find current enrollment options.

One note: swim lessons reduce risk but do not eliminate it. A child who has completed swim lessons can still drown. Supervision remains essential at every level of swimming ability.

Pool Fencing

For families with backyard pools, the AAP recommends a four-sided isolation fence with a self-closing, self-latching gate. "Isolation" is the key word: the fence should surround the pool on all four sides and separate it from the house, rather than simply enclosing the yard. A fence that includes the back door of the house as one side of the barrier does not prevent a child from walking directly from the house to the pool.

Gates should latch at a height that children cannot reach and should close automatically after each use. Inspecting hinges and latches regularly ensures they function as intended. These are not expensive modifications for most families, and they represent one of the most effective physical barriers between young children and open water.

Life Jackets for Open Water

In open water settings — lakes, rivers, boats, kayaking — children should wear Coast Guard-approved life jackets at all times, not inflatable swim wings or floaties. Swim wings and floaties are pool toys; they are not designed to keep a child's head above water and should not be relied on for safety in moving or deep water.

A properly fitted life jacket keeps a child's face out of the water even if they are unconscious. That distinction matters in an emergency. Check the weight and size ratings on the jacket and test the fit before the trip — a life jacket that allows a child's chin to slip inside the neck hole is too large.

Sun Protection

Water and sun go together in Colorado Springs, where elevation increases UV exposure compared to lower-altitude locations. The AAP recommends sunscreen with SPF 15 or higher, applied 15 to 30 minutes before going outside. It should be reapplied every two hours and after swimming, sweating, or toweling off.

Look for "broad-spectrum" and "water-resistant" on the label. Broad-spectrum means the product covers both UVA and UVB rays. For infants under six months, the AAP recommends avoiding sun exposure when possible and covering with lightweight clothing and a broad-brimmed hat rather than relying on sunscreen.

What's Next

Colorado Springs outdoor pools and splash pads open Memorial Day weekend. Monument Valley Pool and Wilson Ranch Pool open May 24 (weather permitting) and are operated by the YMCA of the Pikes Peak Region. The Panorama Park Splash Pad opens May 25, runs through September 2, and is free. Check ppymca.org for pool hours, season pass pricing, and any weather-related closures before your first visit of the summer.

For the AAP's full drowning prevention and water safety guidance, visit HealthyChildren.org and search for "water safety." The resource covers every age group and includes specific guidance for bath time, backyard pools, public pools, and open water settings.
