D20 Reverses Course, Honors Rampart Senior Who Died of Cancer at Graduation

By Sarah Morales | COS Parenting | May 14, 2026

Colorado Springs, CO

When Rampart High School senior Cora Fogle died of cancer in December 2025, her classmates did what Colorado Springs families tend to do: they showed up. They shaved their heads. They raised money. And when the moment came to honor her at graduation, they fought for it.

On May 12, at Clune Arena on the U.S. Air Force Academy campus, Cora Fogle was honored at Rampart's Class of 2026 graduation ceremony. Her family walked across the stage on her behalf and received her diploma. A chair was left for her. The moment cost Academy District 20 nothing except the willingness to listen — which, to its credit, is exactly what the district ultimately chose to do.

The Community That Wouldn't Let This Go

The path to that moment was not straightforward. When students and family members first brought the idea to D20 — asking for an empty chair in Cora's honor and permission for her family to walk the stage — the district's initial response was that existing policy would not allow it. That answer, delivered while a community was still grieving, did not sit well.

Parents organized. Students organized. A petition circulated. KRDO13 reported on the situation, and public attention followed. The combination of sustained community advocacy and media attention prompted D20 to reconsider. By May 11, the district confirmed that Rampart's senior would be recognized at graduation.

In the weeks leading up to that moment, Cora's classmates had already done something remarkable. During a Bald for Bucks fundraiser, the Rampart community raised more than $50,000 for cancer research and patient care in her name. Students, staff, and families participated. The act of shaving heads together — visible, physical, public — was its own kind of honor before the diploma was ever placed in anyone's hands.

What This Means for Families

Stories like this one matter to the Colorado Springs parenting community for reasons that go beyond one graduation and one family. They are a reminder of what organized, persistent, compassionate advocacy can accomplish when families believe something is worth fighting for.

D20 made a policy exception. That does not happen automatically. It happened because people asked, pushed, and kept asking. Parents who have navigated school policies — whether around graduation, special education services, curriculum decisions, or anything else that required sustained effort to change — know how much energy that takes. The Rampart community did it while grieving.

For families currently navigating difficult situations with their own districts, the arc of this story offers something. It is not a guarantee that institutions change quickly or easily. But it is evidence that they can change, and that community voice is not without power.
