Wildfire Smoke Is Reaching Colorado Springs — Here's What Families Need to Do Right Now

By Jamie Rivera | COS Parenting | July 5, 2026

Colorado Springs, CO

If you've walked outside this week and noticed a haze on the horizon or caught the faint smell of smoke, it's coming from the Aspen Acres Fire — and if you have children at home, that smoke is a reason to pay attention.

The Aspen Acres Fire has burned more than 85,000 acres in the Wet Mountains of Pueblo and Custer counties since it started June 29. As of July 4, it remains at zero percent containment. Smoke from the fire has drifted north into Colorado Springs, Canon City, and the I-25 corridor, degrading air quality across the region. The Colorado Department of Public Health and Environment has issued warnings urging residents — especially those with children — to take precautions.

Why Children Are at Higher Risk

Wildfire smoke contains fine particles called PM2.5 — particulate matter small enough to bypass the nose and throat and reach deep into the lungs. Children are more vulnerable than adults because their lungs are still developing, they breathe more air relative to their body weight, and they spend more time being active outdoors. The smaller the child, the greater the concern.

Infants, toddlers, and children with asthma or other respiratory conditions are at the highest risk. Even in healthy children, exposure to elevated PM2.5 can cause coughing, throat irritation, wheezing, and eye watering. In children with asthma, smoke can trigger attacks.

What to Do at Home

The most protective step is to keep kids indoors as much as possible when air quality is poor. Here's what that looks like in practice:

  • Close windows and doors. Even if it feels warm inside, an open window during a smoke event brings polluted air in faster than any fan can push it out.
  • Run a HEPA air purifier. HEPA (High Efficiency Particulate Air) filters capture PM2.5 particles. If you have one, run it in the rooms where your children spend the most time. If you don't have one, a box fan with a furnace filter taped to the back — called a "Corsi-Rosenthal box" — is an inexpensive DIY alternative.
  • Limit strenuous outdoor activity. If kids need to go outside, early morning tends to have the best air quality. Keep sessions short and avoid anything that gets them breathing hard.
  • Hydrate well. Smoke dries out mucous membranes. Keeping kids well-hydrated helps their airways function better.
  • Use your AQI tracker. The AirNow.gov website and most phone weather apps show the current Air Quality Index (AQI) for your area. When the AQI is "Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups" (101 or higher), that's the threshold to keep children indoors. When it reaches "Unhealthy" (151+), everyone should limit outdoor exposure.

Symptoms to Watch

If your child develops any of the following, move them indoors and, if symptoms are severe, contact your pediatrician or seek medical care:

  • Persistent coughing or throat irritation
  • Difficulty breathing or shortness of breath
  • Wheezing (especially in children with asthma)
  • Watery or irritated eyes
  • Headache or unusual fatigue

If your child has asthma or another respiratory condition, follow your care plan and keep rescue medication accessible. Call your doctor if symptoms worsen or do not improve after moving indoors.

Quick Reference

AQI Level Category Action for Families
0–50 Good Normal outdoor activity
51–100 Moderate Sensitive children limit prolonged exertion
101–150 Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups Keep children indoors; limit all outdoor activity
151–200 Unhealthy All children should remain indoors
201+ Very Unhealthy / Hazardous Keep everyone indoors; no outdoor exposure

What's Next

The Aspen Acres Fire remains active and uncontained. Air quality conditions in Colorado Springs will fluctuate depending on wind direction, temperature, and fire behavior — which means some days may be acceptable for outdoor activity and others won't. Check AirNow.gov or your weather app before sending kids outside, and sign up for El Paso County emergency alerts at elpasoteller.org to receive updates if conditions change significantly.
