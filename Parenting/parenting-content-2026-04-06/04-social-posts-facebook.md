### STORY 1: CPSC Recall — Bellabu Bear + Blossom Sleepwear

#### Long-Form Post -- 8:00 AM MT

Two children's sleepwear products have been recalled due to serious burn hazard, and we want to make sure Colorado Springs families are aware right away.

The Consumer Product Safety Commission issued recalls for Bellabu Bear children's robes and Blossom children's loungewear — both for the same reason: they fail the mandatory flammability standard for children's sleepwear. This standard exists because children's nighttime clothing is worn in situations where they're asleep and less able to respond quickly if fabric catches fire.

These aren't products with minor defects. The CPSC characterizes the hazard as a serious risk of injury or death from burns.

Here's what to do: If you own either of these products, stop using them immediately. Check cpsc.gov/Recalls for the specific model numbers, lot codes, and instructions for each recall — including how to get a refund or replacement.

If you're unsure whether your child's sleepwear meets flammability requirements, look for the label. Children's sleepwear must be either flame-resistant or snug-fitting — that's the standard for a reason.

### STORY 2: CPSC Recall — Joyful Journeys Lounger + Vevor Baby Swing

#### Long-Form Post -- 10:00 AM MT

If you have a baby at home, please read this.

Two infant sleep products are under recall right now for serious safety violations, and both involve risks that have already caused infant deaths in similar products.

Joyful Journeys baby loungers have been recalled due to entrapment and fall hazards. The product violates the mandatory standard for infant sleep products — which means it doesn't meet the safety bar that the CPSC has set specifically to protect babies while they sleep.

Vevor-branded baby swings made by Sanven Technology have been recalled for an even more specific violation: the swing's incline angle exceeds 10 degrees, which violates the inclined sleepers ban. That ban exists because inclined sleep products have been linked to infant suffocation deaths — the incline causes babies' heads to fall forward in a way that can block their airway.

The CPSC describes both as posing a serious risk of injury or death.

If you own either product, stop using it now. Visit cpsc.gov/Recalls for the full recall details, including how to get a refund.

It can be hard to keep up with all the recalls — there have been quite a few in the infant sleep category this year. If you're ever unsure whether a product is safe, cpsc.gov lets you search by brand or product name.

### STORY 5: Spring Allergy Season Guide for COS Families

#### Long-Form Post -- 12:00 PM MT

It's April in Colorado Springs, which means we're officially in peak tree pollen season. If your kid has been sneezing, rubbing their eyes, or waking up stuffy, this might be why.

Here's what's in the air right now: juniper, oak, cottonwood, and pine pollen all peak from March through May. April sits right in the middle of that window — and because COS is known for variable spring winds, pollen gets spread widely and quickly. Warm, windy days make counts higher. Rain temporarily brings them down.

Pollen counts tend to be highest in the morning and midday hours. If your kids are sensitive, timing outdoor activities for mid-to-late afternoon can make a real difference.

A few local resources we actually use:
- Colorado ENT & Allergy (coloradoent.com/for-patients/pollen-count/) posts weekly local pollen counts specific to Colorado Springs. Not national estimates — actual local data.
- IQAir tracks COS pollen in real time at iqair.com.
- AccuWeather has an allergy forecast specific to Colorado Springs.

If your child's symptoms are new or seem severe, it's worth a conversation with your pediatrician — allergy testing for kids is more accessible than a lot of parents realize, and knowing exactly what you're dealing with makes management a lot easier.

Grass pollen season starts in May and runs through August, so this is only the beginning. Might as well get ahead of it now.
