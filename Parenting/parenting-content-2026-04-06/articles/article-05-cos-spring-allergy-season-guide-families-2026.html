If your kids have been sneezing more than usual, waking up stuffy, or rubbing their eyes throughout the day, April in Colorado Springs is a likely explanation.

We're in the middle of peak tree pollen season right now. Juniper, oak, cottonwood, and pine all pollinate from March through May, and April sits squarely at the top of that window. Add in the variable spring winds that Colorado Springs is known for, and pollen gets distributed widely and quickly.

When Pollen Is Highest

Pollen counts tend to be highest in the morning and midday hours. Plants release pollen in the morning as temperatures rise, and wind — which peaks in COS in the afternoon but can start building from mid-morning — carries it far.

If your child is sensitive, timing outdoor activities for late afternoon can make a real difference. After 3 p.m., counts often begin to drop. Rain temporarily reduces pollen significantly, so a day after rain is generally a lower-exposure day for outdoor play.

Warm, dry, windy days are the hardest. If the forecast shows that combination, consider adjusting your outdoor plans or making sure allergy medication is on board before heading out.

Where to Check Local Pollen Counts

National pollen forecasts aren't always accurate for Colorado Springs specifically — the city's elevation (6,035 feet) and local wind patterns create a different pollen environment than lower-elevation Front Range cities. Here are the local and regional resources that give you actual Colorado Springs data:

  • Colorado ENT and Allergy (coloradoent.com/for-patients/pollen-count/) publishes a weekly pollen count specific to the Colorado Springs area. This is the most locally specific count available.
  • IQAir (iqair.com/us/pollen/usa/colorado/colorado-springs) tracks real-time pollen levels for Colorado Springs.
  • AccuWeather includes a COS-specific allergy forecast alongside regular weather forecasts.
  • Pollen.com also provides zip-code-level extended pollen forecasts.

Bookmarking one of these makes it easier to build the pollen check into your daily morning routine the same way you'd check the weather before heading out.

What's Coming After Tree Pollen

Tree pollen season will begin to ease as May progresses, but grass pollen season picks up at the same time and runs through August. Colorado Springs isn't unique in this overlap — the Pikes Peak region has a fairly extended allergy season that transitions from trees to grasses to late-summer weeds like ragweed.

For families managing a child who's highly sensitive to multiple pollen types, this means the window between peak tree pollen and peak grass pollen is short. May is often the month when families first notice the pattern.

When to Talk to a Doctor

If your child's allergy symptoms are new this season, severe, or interfering with sleep and daily activities, it's worth a conversation with your pediatrician. Allergy testing for children is more accessible and less intimidating than many parents expect — a simple skin test or blood test can identify specific triggers and open the door to targeted treatment options including immunotherapy, which can reduce sensitivity over time.

Over-the-counter antihistamines are a common starting point, but the options for children with significant allergy burden have expanded. Your pediatrician can help identify what's appropriate for your child's age and symptom pattern.
