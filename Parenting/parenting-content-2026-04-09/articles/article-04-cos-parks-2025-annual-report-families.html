COS Parks Logged 22.5 Million Visits in 2025 — and a Budget Cut Looms for 2026

By Maria Chen | COS Parenting | April 9, 2026

The 2025 Annual Report from Colorado Springs Parks, Recreation and Cultural Services shows extraordinary family use of local parks, trails, and open spaces — and a proposed $2 million reduction in the 2026 budget.

Colorado Springs families used their parks a lot in 2025. A lot.

According to the annual report from Colorado Springs Parks, Recreation and Cultural Services, the city's parks, trails, and open spaces recorded 22.5 million visits last year. That number encompasses everything from a quick weekday walk at Memorial Park to a weekend hike up the Intemann Trail to a family round of golf at Patty Jewett.

The Trust for Public Land, which studies park economics nationally, estimates that system generates about $91.6 million in local economic activity annually. The organization also estimates $73.7 million in annual healthcare savings — a figure that attempts to put a dollar value on what green space, walking paths, and outdoor recreation do for physical and mental health outcomes in communities like Colorado Springs.

What Those Numbers Look Like on the Ground

Patty Jewett Golf Course — one of the oldest municipal golf courses in the country and a neighborhood institution on the east side of Colorado Springs — logged 119,000 rounds in 2025. Valley Hi Golf Course on the south side recorded 62,000 rounds.

At Garden of the Gods, the parks team installed 8,000 feet of new waterline last year — infrastructure work that keeps the park's facilities functional for the millions who visit each year.

Those are concrete, measurable outcomes from a parks system that, at first glance, might seem like a background amenity. For families in Colorado Springs — where outdoor access is one of the defining features of life here — these spaces are core infrastructure, not optional extras.

The 2026 Budget Picture

Here is where it gets complicated.

The 2026 general fund budget for Colorado Springs parks sits at $22.6 million. And a proposed $2 million reduction is on the table.

Budget cuts to parks departments don't always show up as dramatic closures. More often they look like deferred maintenance, reduced programming, shorter operating hours, and fewer staff. Those changes accumulate over time. A trail that doesn't get repaired. A playground that waits longer for replacement equipment. A recreation program that gets trimmed.

For parents who rely on the parks system as one of the primary free or low-cost ways to get their kids outdoors and active, those slow-moving changes are worth paying attention to before they become obvious.

What's Next

The 2026 budget process for Colorado Springs parks will play out through city council deliberations in the coming months. If you want to weigh in — as a park user, as a parent, as a neighborhood resident who relies on the system — public comment opportunities during budget discussions are the most direct channel.

Spring is here, and the trails, parks, and green spaces of Colorado Springs are open and waiting. The annual report is a reminder of how much use they get, and why the funding decisions that sustain them matter to families across the city.
