AAP Screen Time Guidelines: Why Quality and Context Matter More Than the Clock

By Maria Chen | COS Parenting | April 9, 2026

The American Academy of Pediatrics sets clear age-based limits for screen time in young children — but the guidance goes further, emphasizing that how families engage with media matters as much as how much of it they consume.

The American Academy of Pediatrics has clear guidelines on screen time for children, and they're worth knowing — especially in a world where screens are woven into virtually every part of family life.

For children under 18 months: no screen time, with one exception. Video chatting — FaceTime, Zoom, a call with grandparents across the country — is fine. The developmental concern at this age is passive, one-directional screen exposure. Two-way video communication is fundamentally different and is not included in the restriction.

For children ages 18 to 24 months: if parents want to introduce digital media, the AAP recommends limiting it to high-quality programming and watching alongside the child. At this stage, toddlers learn from human interaction first — the role of a caregiver watching together and talking about what's on the screen is significant.

For children ages 2 through 5: the AAP recommends a limit of 1 hour per day of high-quality programming. The same co-viewing guidance applies.

The Shift Toward Quality Over Quantity

Here is where the AAP's guidance gets more nuanced, and more useful for most families.

For children ages 6 and up, the AAP does not set a specific hourly limit. Instead, the guidance calls for consistent limits that ensure screens don't displace sleep, physical activity, or in-person social interaction. The emphasis is on what screen time is replacing, not just how much of it there is.

Across age groups, the AAP stresses that quality and context matter as much as quantity. There is a meaningful difference between a child passively scrolling through short-form video content and a child watching an age-appropriate documentary with a parent who's asking questions and making connections.

Co-viewing — watching together, discussing what you see, pausing to explain or ask — is one of the most consistent recommendations in the research on children's media use. The presence and engagement of a parent or caregiver changes the developmental experience of screen time substantially.

Modeling Matters Too

The AAP also addresses parental screen use — a piece of the guidance that's easy to overlook but hard to argue with. Children mirror what they observe. Parents who model thoughtful, intentional screen habits — putting phones away at mealtimes, not scrolling during conversations, being present when playing with kids — communicate those norms without saying a word.

That doesn't mean perfection. It means consistency and intention. The goal isn't a screen-free household; it's a household where screens serve the family rather than the other way around.

Quick Reference

AAP Screen Time Guidelines by Age
Age AAP Guidance
Under 18 months No screen time except video chatting (FaceTime, Zoom, etc.)
18–24 months High-quality programming only; parent watches alongside child
2–5 years Limit to 1 hour per day of high-quality content; co-view when possible
6 years and up Consistent limits; screens should not displace sleep, exercise, or face-to-face time
All ages Quality and context matter; co-view, discuss content, model healthy digital habits

What's Next

For families in Colorado Springs looking for concrete ways to think about their household screen habits, the AAP's Family Media Plan tool at healthychildren.org allows parents to set individualized guidelines by child and activity type. It's a practical way to move from general guidance to specific household agreements.
