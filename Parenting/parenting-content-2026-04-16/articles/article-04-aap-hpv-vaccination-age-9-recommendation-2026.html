The AAP Wants Kids to Start HPV Vaccination at Age 9 — Here's What Families Should Know

By Marta Kim | COS Parenting | April 16, 2026

The American Academy of Pediatrics published a blog post on April 7, 2026, titled "The Time Is Now — Starting HPV Protection Early," reinforcing its recommendation that pediatricians offer the HPV vaccine to children as early as age 9. If your child is approaching that age and you haven't had a conversation with your pediatrician about HPV vaccination yet, this is a good moment to start.

AAP vs. CDC: What's the Difference

There's currently a notable difference between how two leading medical authorities approach HPV vaccination timing. The AAP recommends a two-dose series beginning at ages 9 to 12. The CDC, in an updated recommendation, moved to a one-dose schedule for children vaccinated at ages 11 to 12.

Both organizations agree that HPV vaccination is important. The difference is in the approach: the AAP favors starting earlier and completing a two-dose series, while the CDC's updated guidance simplifies the schedule for adolescents who start the vaccine at the recommended age.

If a child starts the HPV series before age 15, two doses are typically sufficient. Children who begin at age 15 or older need three doses. This is why the age at initiation matters — starting earlier allows the series to be completed with fewer doses.

Why Starting at Age 9 Matters

The AAP's argument for starting at age 9 comes down to flexibility and completion rates. Research published in peer-reviewed journals has found that initiating the HPV series at age 9 or 10 significantly improves the likelihood that children complete the series by age 13. When the vaccine is introduced at puberty — around age 11 or 12 — it can get tied up in conversations about sexual health that some families find uncomfortable, reducing completion rates.

Starting at age 9 separates the vaccination from those discussions. It's framed simply as a cancer-prevention measure, which is accurate: the HPV vaccine protects against several cancers, including cervical, throat, anal, and penile cancers. Protection is strongest when the series is completed before any exposure to HPV, which is why early vaccination is medically preferred.

How to Approach This With Your Pediatrician

If your child is 9 or older, you can bring up HPV vaccination at any wellness visit. Pediatricians who follow AAP guidance will typically raise it proactively, but not all do — especially with younger patients in the 9-to-10 range. It's completely appropriate to ask.

You don't need to have a detailed conversation about HPV transmission at that age. The conversation can be as simple as: "Our pediatrician mentioned we could start the HPV series now. What should we know about timing?" From there, you and your child's provider can decide on the approach that fits your family's schedule and preferences.

What's Next

If your child is 9 or older, ask your pediatrician about the HPV vaccine at your next visit. If you haven't scheduled a wellness visit recently, this is a reasonable prompt to do so. For families who want to read the AAP's full guidance, the April 7 blog post is available at aap.org. Your child's pediatrician can review your family's specific situation and help determine the right timing and dose schedule.
