Colorado Lawmakers Protect School Funding in the 2026-27 Budget

By Keisha Williams | COS Parenting | April 16, 2026

Colorado lawmakers released the 2026-27 state budget proposal in early April, and despite a statewide revenue shortfall, education funding was largely protected from major cuts. The proposal now heads to the Governor for signature, and its passage would mean stable school funding for Colorado Springs families heading into next school year.

What Was at Stake

Colorado's state budget funds public education through a formula that distributes dollars to districts based on enrollment and other factors. When state revenues fall short of projections — as happened with the 2026-27 planning cycle — lawmakers face pressure to cut spending across agencies. Education, as one of the largest line items in the state budget, is often a focus of those discussions.

ChalkBeat Colorado reported on April 6 that the budget proposal avoids major education cuts. CPR News confirmed the same picture on April 7, noting that the budget addresses a broader state revenue shortfall without imposing significant reductions on school funding.

What It Means for Colorado Springs Families

For families in D11, D20, and D49 — the three main school districts serving Colorado Springs — state funding stability means local district budgets are less likely to face sudden gaps. Colorado's school finance formula ties district funding levels directly to state appropriations, so what happens in Denver affects classrooms here.

This doesn't mean district budgets are settled. Local factors, including enrollment trends, federal funding, and individual district priorities, still shape how resources are allocated at the school level. But a state budget that holds education funding steady removes a significant variable from the equation.

The Broader Budget Picture

Colorado is managing a revenue shortfall, which means other parts of the state budget face tighter constraints. Education's relatively protected status in the 2026-27 proposal reflects a bipartisan political consensus in Colorado around K-12 funding, as well as advocacy from districts and education organizations during the budget process.

The proposal must be signed by the Governor before it takes effect. Once signed, it sets the fiscal framework that local districts use to build their own 2026-27 budgets.

COS Parenting will continue tracking how state budget decisions translate into district-level impacts for D11, D20, and D49 families.
