COS Has a Childcare Crisis: 47,000 Kids, 18,400 Spots, and a City Trying to Fix What Zoning Broke

By Sarah Donnelly | COS Parenting | April 8, 2026

Colorado Springs has nearly 29,000 more children under age 5 than it has licensed childcare spots. A new city ordinance update targeting June 2026 aims to fix part of a problem that local zoning rules helped create.

The Numbers Behind the Shortage

Colorado Springs has approximately 47,000 children under the age of 5. The city has approximately 18,400 licensed childcare spots. That's a gap of nearly 29,000 seats — meaning the city would need to more than double its licensed childcare capacity to meet current demand.

A UCCS study found that 26 percent of parents who would otherwise be working are staying home because childcare isn't available to them. That's not a choice made from preference — it's a constraint imposed by a shortage. For families in the workforce, or trying to enter it, the math doesn't work.

The problem is most severe in the southeast area of Colorado Springs and the Fountain-Fort Carson corridor, where access to licensed care is most limited. But waitlists and shortfalls affect families across the city.

How the City Made It Worse

In 2023, Colorado Springs updated its Unified Development Code — the rulebook that governs what can be built where. The intent was to modernize how the city manages land use. The unintended consequence was that the new code effectively banned new in-home childcare facilities and church-based care centers in more zones than before.

In-home childcare — where a licensed provider cares for a small number of children in their home — is one of the most flexible and affordable options for families. Church-based care fills a similar gap, especially in neighborhoods without commercial childcare facilities nearby. Both were made harder to open under the 2023 code.

The city has acknowledged the problem and is working on a correction.

What the June 2026 Ordinance Could Change

The city's Childcare Ordinance Update is targeting a June 2026 implementation. The goal is to reverse the 2023 restrictions and allow more childcare providers — including in-home and faith-based facilities — to operate in more parts of Colorado Springs.

The Kids Strong Start awareness campaign, launched in January 2026 by Joint Initiatives for Youth and Families and the Family Friendly Initiative, has been building community input and visibility around the issue as the ordinance process moves forward.

If the ordinance passes on its current timeline, Colorado Springs families could start to see more licensed childcare options become available in the second half of 2026 — though closing a 29,000-seat gap takes more than a zoning change. It requires investment, training, and time.

What's Next

The Childcare Ordinance Update is targeting a June 2026 vote. Families in Colorado Springs who want to track or weigh in on the process can find information at coloradosprings.gov/ChildcareOrdinanceUpdate. The city has held public input sessions and may schedule additional community meetings before the June timeline. If childcare access is affecting your family's decisions about work or caregiving, this is the local policy process most directly tied to that situation.
