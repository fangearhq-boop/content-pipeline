COS Parks Budget Cuts: What Families Should Know Before Summer 2026

By Sarah Morales | May 13, 2026 | COS Parenting

Colorado Springs families who depend on neighborhood parks for summer outdoor time are facing some changes this season. Budget cuts to the city's Parks, Recreation and Cultural Services department have resulted in reduced restroom access, a pause on playground equipment repairs, and halted maintenance at some of the city's signature green spaces.

Understanding what to expect before your next park day can help families plan accordingly — and know where to find reliable alternatives when their nearest park falls short.

What Is Changing This Summer

According to reporting from KRDO based on a Parks, Recreation and Cultural Services Advisory Board meeting, three main changes are taking effect in 2026:

Restroom closures: Approximately 25 percent of neighborhood park permanent restroom facilities — roughly 10 locations — will remain closed for the entire 2026 season. For families with young children who cannot easily wait or walk long distances, knowing whether a specific park has open facilities before you go is more important than ever this summer.

Playground equipment: Damaged or vandalized playground equipment will be removed from parks rather than repaired or replaced. Over the course of the summer, this may mean fewer functioning play structures at certain locations. The specific parks affected were not announced publicly in advance, but parents should be aware that a park they visited in spring may look different by August.

Julie Penrose Fountain: The popular fountain at America the Beautiful Park will not receive its regular cleaning and restoration services in 2026. The same applies to 2027, 2029, and 2030. Families who visit America the Beautiful Park for splash-pad and fountain activities may notice a difference in the fountain's condition this summer.

What Stays the Same

The cuts target maintenance budgets, not operations. The Parks, Recreation and Cultural Services department continues to operate three community centers, seven sports complexes, and dozens of parks citywide. Community centers provide a reliable alternative for families seeking supervised, facility-supported recreation during the summer — particularly useful during Colorado's unpredictable afternoon thunderstorms.

The park trail system, including the Pikes Peak Greenway and the extensive multi-use trail network, is not affected by these cuts. Families looking for outdoor time without needing park facilities may find the trail system a more consistent option this season.

Tips for Families This Summer

A few practical steps can make park visits smoother this season. Calling or checking the city's parks page before visiting an unfamiliar neighborhood park can confirm whether restrooms are open. Arriving at parks earlier in the day generally means more equipment is available and less afternoon summer heat to contend with. For longer outings, community centers offer drinking fountains, restrooms, and shaded indoor areas that parks alone may not provide.

The Parks, Recreation and Cultural Services department can be reached through the City of Colorado Springs website at coloradosprings.gov for specific questions about individual park facilities.

What's Next

The Parks Advisory Board continues to review the budget situation. Families who want to advocate for park maintenance restoration can attend public advisory board meetings or contact their city council representative. The 2026 Park System Master Plan, also underway, provides a longer-term framework for how COS approaches its parks infrastructure — community input in that process helps shape future budget priorities.
