AAP 2026 Car Seat Update: Keep Children Rear-Facing Longer Than You Think

By Sarah Morales | May 13, 2026 | COS Parenting

If your mental model of car seat transitions is still built around a child's age — "turn them around at one year old" or "forward-facing by age two" — the American Academy of Pediatrics' updated 2026 guidance asks you to rethink that approach entirely.

The AAP updated its child passenger safety recommendations in February 2026, and the core message is a shift away from age as the primary trigger for transitioning car seats. Instead, the guidance focuses on manufacturer limits: a child should remain in a rear-facing car seat until they reach the maximum height or weight specified by the car seat manufacturer, regardless of how old they are.

Why Rear-Facing Matters Longer

The physics of a crash explain why rear-facing seats are more protective for younger children. In a frontal collision — by far the most common type — a rear-facing seat distributes the force of the impact across the child's entire back, neck, and head. The seat itself absorbs and redirects much of that force, rather than concentrating it on the child's body.

A forward-facing child, by contrast, is thrown forward against the harness straps in a frontal collision. For children whose musculoskeletal systems are still developing, particularly neck and spine structures, this difference in force distribution matters. Rear-facing protection is not about age — it is about size and the mechanics of how impact forces work.

Children can be safely rear-facing well past their first birthday. Many car seats today support rear-facing to 40–50 pounds. As long as a child fits within the manufacturer's rear-facing height and weight limits, rear-facing remains the safest option.

What to Check in Your Car Today

With summer road trips ahead — whether heading to Denver, up Pikes Peak, or farther across Colorado — now is a good time to verify your family's car seat setup against the manufacturer's specifications.

Pull out your car seat manual and check three things: the maximum rear-facing weight, the maximum rear-facing height, and whether your child currently fits within both of those limits. If they do, keep them rear-facing. If they have exceeded either limit, transition to a forward-facing seat with a harness and repeat the check with the new seat's maximums.

Car seat installation is the other key variable. The AAP and NHTSA both recommend getting a car seat checked at a certified Child Passenger Safety Technician location. In Colorado Springs, El Paso County health departments and some fire stations offer free car seat inspections. Calling ahead to confirm availability is recommended.

What's Next

The AAP publishes its child passenger safety guidance through HealthyChildren.org, which is the family-accessible version of AAP clinical recommendations. The full 2026 guidance is available there for parents who want the complete details.

As your children grow, revisiting car seat fit at least twice a year — or after any significant growth spurt — ensures the setup stays within current safety standards. The few minutes it takes to recheck can make a meaningful difference.
