AAP Changed Its Screen Time Guidance: Here's What It Means for COS Families

By Jamie Rivera | May 7, 2026 | COS Parenting

The American Academy of Pediatrics updated its screen time recommendations in January 2026, and for families with children ages 6 and older, the change is worth understanding. The short version: stop counting the minutes for older kids and start asking better questions about what they're watching.

The full guidance is more nuanced than a headline suggests, and the rules for the youngest children haven't changed. Here is what the updated guidance actually says.

What Changed — and What Didn't

The core change for older children reflects a shift in how the AAP thinks about screen time research. Rather than treating all screen time as equivalent, the updated guidance recognizes that an hour of co-watching an educational documentary with a parent is fundamentally different from an hour of unsupervised scroll-through social content. Time limits alone don't capture that distinction.

What "Quality, Context, and Conversation" Actually Means

The AAP's updated framework for children 6 and older asks families to think about three things:

Quality: What is the content? Is it age-appropriate, educational, or enriching? Is it content you'd be comfortable watching alongside your child?

Context: Where and when is screen time happening? Watching a nature documentary after dinner is different from devices at the dinner table or in the bedroom at bedtime. The setting shapes the experience.

Conversation: Are you engaged around the content? Asking a child what they just watched, talking through something confusing or concerning, or watching together makes the experience significantly different from passive consumption.

The guidance does not say "unlimited screen time is fine." It says that for older children, thoughtful engagement is more valuable than a strict daily count.

What Hasn't Changed: Social Media

The AAP's position on social media platforms has not softened. The updated guidance continues to recommend delaying children's access to social media as long as possible. The platforms' design — built around social comparison, algorithmic engagement maximization, and attention capture — poses distinct risks that don't improve with parental supervision the way other screen content does.

For Colorado Springs families with children approaching the ages when peers start using social platforms, the guidance is clear: later is better.

A COS Perspective

Colorado Springs sits at 6,035 feet above sea level with direct access to Pikes Peak, Garden of the Gods, and hundreds of miles of trails. The outdoor competition for screen time is real, and many COS families already operate with less screen dependency than the national average. The updated AAP guidance affirms that when screens are part of family life, how they're used matters more than how many minutes they accumulate.

What's Next

The full updated guidance from the American Academy of Pediatrics is available at aap.org. Families looking for specific recommendations for their child's age or situation can consult their pediatrician, who can apply the guidelines to the child's individual development. The AAP's Family Media Plan tool, available at healthychildren.org, helps families build a customized plan based on their children's ages and household values.
