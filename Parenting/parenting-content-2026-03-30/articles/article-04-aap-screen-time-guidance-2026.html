The AAP Rethought Screen Time for 2026 — Here's What Changed and What It Means

March 30, 2026 · COS Parenting

For years, the AAP's screen time guidance worked like a timer: zero minutes for children under 2 (except video calls), one hour per day for ages 2-5, and use your judgment after that. The message was simple, memorable, and — as families quickly discovered — extremely hard to follow in a world where screens are everywhere and some of the most educational content for kids lives on them.

The American Academy of Pediatrics has updated its guidance for 2026, and the central message has evolved. The framework is no longer primarily about the clock. It's about quality, context, and conversation.

What the New Guidance Actually Says

The core age-based rules are still in place, with some important nuance added:

  • Under 18 months: No screens except video calls with family members. This hasn't changed — and the research behind it is solid. Infants learn from human interaction, not from passive screen watching.
  • Ages 2-5: One hour of high-quality content daily is still the guideline, but the emphasis has shifted to what they're watching and whether a caregiver is present. The same hour of co-viewed educational content is meaningfully different from an hour of algorithmically recommended videos watched alone.
  • Older children: The focus is on creating consistent "screen-free" times — meals, before bed, family time — rather than trying to count cumulative daily minutes.

The research behind the update is clear: the risks associated with screen time — language delays, reduced literacy, trouble with self-regulation — are most strongly linked to passive consumption and unsupervised viewing. Co-viewing, talking about what kids see, asking questions, and connecting what's on screen to real-life experiences largely mitigates those risks.

What This Means in Practice

For families trying to apply this in daily life, the practical shift looks like this:

The 45-minute educational app your child uses independently while you cook dinner is worth paying closer attention to — not because 45 minutes is a problem, but because the content and the lack of interaction are the variables. What is it teaching? What's the engagement style? Passive or interactive?

The 30-minute Saturday morning cartoon watched together, where you're commenting on what the characters are doing and asking your child what they think will happen next, is not the same as 30 minutes of solo viewing. The minutes are the same. The developmental impact is different.

Video calls with grandparents, interactive learning apps that require input and problem-solving, and quality programming from trusted sources (PBS Kids, for example) are all different categories than ambient background TV or content that's been served to your child by a recommendation algorithm. The AAP is now explicitly asking families to think in those categories rather than tracking a clock.

A Note on Very Young Children

The guidance for infants and toddlers under 18 months hasn't changed. Screens — including background TV, even when the child isn't looking at it — affect the quality of caregiver-child interaction. Conversations slow down, joint attention decreases, and the rich verbal environment that drives language development gets disrupted. The one-year-old who doesn't appear to be watching the news doesn't mean the news isn't interfering with the conversation you'd otherwise be having.

For families with infants, the most impactful thing you can do is protect face-to-face interaction time as much as possible — talk, read, play, narrate your day. Screens for the adults are OK; background screens during feeding and play time are worth minimizing.

What's Next

The AAP's full updated guidance is available at healthychildren.org — their family-facing site is one of the most reliable resources for parenting guidance that's grounded in current pediatric research. If you have specific questions about your child's screen time habits, your pediatrician is the best starting point. The new framework gives families a more realistic and nuanced lens — use it to have a conversation with your household about what "intentional screen time" looks like in your specific context.
