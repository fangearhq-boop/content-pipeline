Cheyenne Mountain Zoo Turns 100 — and the New Giraffe Center Is the Best Birthday Present Yet

March 30, 2026 · COS Parenting

Colorado Springs has had Cheyenne Mountain Zoo for a century now — and it's a better zoo than ever.

CMZoo officially turns 100 in 2026, founded in 1926 by Spencer Penrose, the Colorado Springs entrepreneur behind The Broadmoor Hotel and the Pikes Peak Highway. What started as a personal animal collection has grown into one of the most respected zoological institutions in the country: ranked No. 2 in North America by USA TODAY Readers' Choice Awards in 2025, home to hundreds of animals, and sitting at 6,714 feet with some of the most dramatic views of any zoo anywhere.

The centennial year brings a milestone that's been in the works for years: the opening of the International Center for the Care and Conservation of Giraffe — a $40 million facility that will transform the zoo's giraffe experience.

What the New Giraffe Center Means for Families

The current giraffe exhibit has three feeding zones — meaning families need to time their visit and get positioned at one of those spots. The new center will have 11 feeding zones. That's a significant change in how giraffe interactions work: more locations, more access, less crowding at one spot.

The new 12,000-square-foot giraffe barn features an ETFE roof — the same lightweight, hail-resistant material used in some of the world's most innovative architectural projects — that floods the interior with natural light and allows the giraffes to be inside without the darkness of a traditional barn. The outdoor habitat expands by 25%, with more terrain variation including a new feeding tower that encourages giraffes to navigate hills they haven't accessed before.

The zoo is planning a weeklong celebration for the opening. Specific dates haven't been announced yet, but the timeline is summer 2026.

Free Right Now: The Penrose Heritage Museum Exhibit

You don't have to wait for summer to enjoy the centennial. The Penrose Heritage Museum is currently running a free exhibit celebrating CMZoo's 100-year history. It includes archival photographs, historic documents, a short documentary video, and hands-on activities designed for families and children. Admission to the museum is completely free — you don't need a zoo ticket to visit.

The museum is located near The Broadmoor resort, which Spencer Penrose also built. The connection between Penrose, the hotel, and the zoo is a piece of Colorado Springs history worth knowing — and the exhibit tells that story well.
