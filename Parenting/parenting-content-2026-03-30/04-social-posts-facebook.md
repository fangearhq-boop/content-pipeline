#### Long-Form Post -- 8:30 AM MT

**Long-Form Post (8:30 AM MT)**

```
Four product recalls to check this week — all involving products commonly found in homes with young children.

1. Vevor Baby Swings (by Sanven Technology): If you have one of these, stop using it now. These swings were marketed as infant sleep products, but the incline angle exceeds 10 degrees — a violation of the Safe Sleep for Babies Act and the federal ban on inclined sleepers. The hazard is suffocation.

2. YCXXKJ Baby Bath Seats (sold by BenTalk on Amazon): Nearly 9,000 of these bath seats have been recalled because they can tip over while in use, creating a drowning risk. Sold for $34-$40 in blue, gray, pink, and yellow.

3. CreateOn Pip-Cubes (Crayola-branded magnetic building toys): The magnets in these cubes can come loose if the seams separate. If a child swallows multiple magnets, they can attract each other inside the body, causing intestinal perforation, blood poisoning, or death. This is one of the most serious hazard types for young children.

4. 4our Kiddies Plastic Furniture Tip Restraint Kits: About 253,500 of these furniture straps were sold on Amazon from June 2019 through January 2026, priced between $6 and $21. The plastic components can break or degrade over time, meaning furniture can still tip over even when you think it's secured. There are 150+ reports of the straps breaking, including three incidents where furniture actually tipped.

For all of these: stop use immediately. Go to cpsc.gov/Recalls for the specific model numbers and to request a refund or replacement. If you're unsure, the CPSC hotline is 1-800-638-2772.

Which of these were you aware of?

### STORY 4: AAP Updates Screen Time Guidance for 2026

#### Long-Form Post -- 3:30 PM MT

**Long-Form Post (3:30 PM MT)**

```
If you've ever felt guilty about your kids' screen time, the American Academy of Pediatrics has some news that might reframe things.

The AAP updated its screen time guidance for 2026 — and the big shift is this: the focus is no longer primarily on how many minutes your child spends in front of a screen. It's about what they're watching and whether you're watching with them. The new framework centers on quality, context, and conversation.

The core age-based guidance remains: no screens for children under 18 months (except video calls with family), and for ages 2-5, one hour of high-quality content daily is still the target. But the emphasis has moved from policing the clock to being present and intentional. Research consistently links excessive, unsupervised screen time to language, literacy, and self-regulation delays — but the same research shows that co-viewing with a caregiver and talking about what kids see largely offsets those risks.

In practice, this means: the Saturday morning cartoon marathon isn't the end of the world if you're in the room commenting on it. The educational app they use alone for 45 minutes while you make dinner is worth paying more attention to. Context is everything.

The AAP also noted a shift specific to screen content: not all screen time is equal. Video calls with grandparents, interactive educational apps, and quality children's programming are very different from passive consumption of algorithmically generated content. Parents now have a more nuanced framework to work with.

How do you handle screen time with your kids?
