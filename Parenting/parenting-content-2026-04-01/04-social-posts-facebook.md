### STORY 3: AAP Alert — Vitamin K Refusal and VKDB

#### Long-Form Post -- 12:30 PM MT

**Long-Form Post (12:30 PM MT)**

```
If you're expecting or recently had a baby, this is worth reading. The April 2026 issue of *Pediatrics* — the American Academy of Pediatrics journal — published an article called "Vitamin K Deficiency Bleeding After Refusal: A Sentinel Event in a Misinformation Era."

Here's what it's about: When babies are born, standard care includes a Vitamin K injection — a quick shot that prevents something called Vitamin K Deficiency Bleeding (VKDB). VKDB is a rare but serious condition where a baby's blood can't clot properly, sometimes leading to internal bleeding in the brain. The Vitamin K shot has been standard practice for decades and is extremely safe.

In recent years, a growing number of parents have declined the Vitamin K injection at birth — often because of health misinformation circulating on social media. Some posts claim the shot is unnecessary, painful, or contains harmful additives. Medical evidence doesn't support those claims. What the evidence does show: babies who don't receive Vitamin K are at significantly higher risk for VKDB, including brain bleeds that can cause permanent neurological damage or death.

The AAP has also documented in a separate study that health misinformation is now a top concern among pediatricians nationwide. When parents arrive with information they've gotten from social media, pediatricians are increasingly spending appointment time correcting false claims that can affect newborn health decisions.

If you're pregnant or have recently had a baby and have questions about the Vitamin K shot, talk to your pediatrician — not the comment section. They can walk you through the evidence and answer your specific concerns.

### STORY 5: Drone Parenting and Velcro Parenting — Experts Speak

#### Long-Form Post -- 5:30 PM MT

**Long-Form Post (5:30 PM MT)**

```
Two parenting styles are getting a lot of attention from child development experts this year, and not for good reasons. "Drone parenting" and "velcro parenting" are being named as two of the approaches most likely to undermine kids' long-term resilience and independence.

Drone parenting is what it sounds like: parents who use location tracking apps, monitor every digital interaction, and maintain near-constant surveillance of their kids' physical and online lives. The concern isn't awareness — it's the inability to allow any unmonitored space for kids to figure things out.

Velcro parenting is the in-person version: hovering so closely that a child never experiences struggle, failure, or discomfort without an immediate adult intervention. While it comes from a loving place, experts say it prevents kids from building the coping skills they'll need when parents aren't around.

What child development research points toward instead: empathetic boundaries. That means being present, setting clear expectations, listening to kids' feelings — while still letting them experience age-appropriate challenges without rescuing them from every difficult moment.

Here's the honest truth: most parents recognize themselves in one (or both) of these descriptions. COS is a military-heavy community where many families have experienced prolonged deployment separations. The hypervigilance that can follow those experiences is understandable. But experts say the goal is to gradually expand kids' independence in a way that matches their developmental stage.

You don't have to be perfect. You just have to leave a little room for your kids to be.

Where do you fall — more drone/velcro, or more hands-off? No judgment here.
