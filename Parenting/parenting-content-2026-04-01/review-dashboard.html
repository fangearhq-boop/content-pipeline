### Long-Form Post ``` Another round of CPSC recalls that Colorado Springs parents need to know about — and this batch hits a few product categories that might be sitting in your baby's room right now. Little Pea Shop Crib Bumpers: These violate the federal crib bumper ban, which has been in effect since 2022. If you didn't know — crib bumpers of all kinds are now federally banned in the U.S. because of suffocation risk. Little Pea Shop was still selling them. If you picked these up at Hobby Lobby, check the legs. If they pull off, the toy is a hazard. Blossom Children's Loungewear: This line fails the mandatory sleepwear flammability standard. Loungewear that doesn't meet flammability rules is a burn hazard — full stop. Check your kids' pajamas and loungewear. Criblike Mattresses (sold by Mengna on Amazon): These mattresses don't fit play yards properly, creating gaps that can trap a child. Entrapment in sleep environments is one of the leading causes of infant death. The full recall list is at cpsc.gov/Recalls — it's worth bookmarking. Have you ever had to deal with a product recall? PowerSchool Data Breach — D11 and D49 Families: What Was (and Wasn't) At Risk [TIER 1]
NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post ``` If you have kids in D11 or D49, here's what you need to know about the PowerSchool data breach. PowerSchool is the student information system used by both districts to manage things like attendance, grades, course schedules, and family contact information. A data breach at PowerSchool has been confirmed to have included D11, D49, and Manitou Springs SD14 among affected districts. Here's the important reassurance, which came directly from the districts: neither D11 nor D49 stores student Social Security Numbers in PowerSchool. SSNs were not at risk in this breach. That's meaningful — SSN exposure is the highest-risk outcome of any school data breach because it enables identity theft that can affect your child for years. The confirmation that SSNs weren't stored means the most serious risk isn't present here. That said, other student data — names, contact information, enrollment records, and potentially academic information — may have been included. The districts haven't published a full list of every field that was exposed, and the specifics can vary by how each school configured their data. What to do: Check your email from D11 or D49 for any official notification. Call your school office if you haven't received one. Ask specifically: "What types of student data were included in the PowerSchool breach, and what steps is the district taking?" You deserve a clear answer. If you have questions, your child's school is the right first call. Have you heard from your district about this yet? ``` ### Image Post Caption #### Image Caption -- 10:30 AM MT ``` PowerSchool data breach confirmed in D11 + D49. ### Long-Form Post ``` If you're expecting or recently had a baby, this is worth reading. The April 2026 issue of *Pediatrics* — the American Academy of Pediatrics journal — published an article called "Vitamin K Deficiency Bleeding After Refusal: A Sentinel Event in a Misinformation Era." Here's what it's about: When babies are born, standard care includes a Vitamin K injection — a quick shot that prevents something called Vitamin K Deficiency Bleeding (VKDB). VKDB is a rare but serious condition where a baby's blood can't clot properly, sometimes leading to internal bleeding in the brain. The Vitamin K shot has been standard practice for decades and is extremely safe. In recent years, a growing number of parents have declined the Vitamin K injection at birth — often because of health misinformation circulating on social media. Some posts claim the shot is unnecessary, painful, or contains harmful additives. Medical evidence doesn't support those claims. What the evidence does show: babies who don't receive Vitamin K are at significantly higher risk for VKDB, including brain bleeds that can cause permanent neurological damage or death. The AAP has also documented in a separate study that health misinformation is now a top concern among pediatricians nationwide. When parents arrive with information they've gotten from social media, pediatricians are increasingly spending appointment time correcting false claims that can affect newborn health decisions. If you're pregnant or have recently had a baby and have questions about the Vitamin K shot, talk to your pediatrician — not the comment section. They can walk you through the evidence and answer your specific concerns. Was this helpful? Share it with any expecting families you know. ``` ### Image Post Caption #### Image Caption -- 12:30 PM MT ``` The AAP is raising alarms about Vitamin K refusal at birth. Drone Parenting and Velcro Parenting — Experts Say Hovering Is Hurting Kids [TIER 3]
NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post ``` Two parenting styles are getting a lot of attention from child development experts this year, and not for good reasons. "Drone parenting" and "velcro parenting" are being named as two of the approaches most likely to undermine kids' long-term resilience and independence. Drone parenting is what it sounds like: parents who use location tracking apps, monitor every digital interaction, and maintain near-constant surveillance of their kids' physical and online lives. The concern isn't awareness — it's the inability to allow any unmonitored space for kids to figure things out. Velcro parenting is the in-person version: hovering so closely that a child never experiences struggle, failure, or discomfort without an immediate adult intervention. While it comes from a loving place, experts say it prevents kids from building the coping skills they'll need when parents aren't around. What child development research points toward instead: empathetic boundaries. That means being present, setting clear expectations, listening to kids' feelings — while still letting them experience age-appropriate challenges without rescuing them from every difficult moment. Here's the honest truth: most parents recognize themselves in one (or both) of these descriptions. COS is a military-heavy community where many families have experienced prolonged deployment separations. The hypervigilance that can follow those experiences is understandable. But experts say the goal is to gradually expand kids' independence in a way that matches their developmental stage. You don't have to be perfect. You just have to leave a little room for your kids to be. Where do you fall — more drone/velcro, or more hands-off? No judgment here. ``` ### Image Post Caption #### Image Caption -- 5:30 PM MT ``` "Drone parenting" and "velcro parenting" are raising flags from experts. The alternative?
