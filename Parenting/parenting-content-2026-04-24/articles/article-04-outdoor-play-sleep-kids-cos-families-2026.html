Science Confirms It: Getting Kids Outside Helps Them Sleep — And COS Has the Space to Make It Easy

By Marcus Rivera | COS Parenting

April 24, 2026

If bedtime is a battle at your house, here's something worth considering: how much time did your child spend outside today? Research is increasingly clear that active outdoor play — particularly exposure to natural light — directly improves sleep quality and duration in young children. And a national poll suggests that many families aren't taking advantage of it.

What the Research Shows

A national poll conducted by the University of Michigan C.S. Mott Children's Hospital found that 1 in 10 toddlers and preschoolers plays outside just once a week or less. That's a low number, especially given how consistently research points to outdoor activity as a meaningful sleep support tool.

The connection works through several mechanisms:

Why Outdoor Play Improves Children's Sleep
MechanismWhat It Does
Natural light exposureRegulates circadian rhythms, helping children's bodies understand when it's time to sleep
Physical activityCreates healthy tiredness that makes falling asleep easier and improves sleep depth
Reduced screen timeTime outside is time not on screens — less blue light exposure before bed means better melatonin production
Stress reductionTime in nature lowers cortisol levels in children, reducing the anxiety that keeps some kids awake

A CNN report from March 2026 put a useful frame on it: when kids don't sleep, parents don't sleep either. Helping children build better sleep habits is one of the most direct things families can do for their own wellbeing too.

How Much Outside Time Is Enough?

Research doesn't prescribe a single magic number, but guidelines consistently suggest that 30 to 60 minutes of active outdoor time per day can make a measurable difference for sleep quality. Morning and midday outdoor exposure is particularly effective for circadian rhythm regulation. Evening outdoor play can also help, but very vigorous exercise right before bed can sometimes make it harder for some children to settle down — it varies by child.

The key is consistency. A daily outdoor routine — even a short one — tends to outperform sporadic long trips to the park once a week when it comes to sleep benefits.

Within city limits there are mountain parks, regional parks, natural areas, and neighborhood trails — many walkable from residential areas.

This weekend, the "3 Days 3 Parks" Earth Day volunteer event at Blodgett, Palmer Park, and Ute Valley makes getting outside easy and purposeful. Starting May 1, the free Trailability Program at Fox Run Regional Park expands outdoor access for families of all abilities. And across the city, hundreds of miles of trails are open and ready to use right now.

What's Next

If outdoor time isn't already a consistent part of your family's daily routine, spring in Colorado Springs is the best possible time to start. Temperatures are rising, trails are in good condition, and the city's park system has programming for all ages. Even a short daily walk — 20 to 30 minutes in natural light — is a meaningful investment in your children's sleep quality. The research supports it, and COS is full of places to make it happen.
