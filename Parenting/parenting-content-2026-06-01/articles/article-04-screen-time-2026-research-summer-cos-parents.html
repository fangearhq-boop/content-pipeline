Screen Time in 2026: New Research and What It Means for COS Families This Summer

By Jamie Rivera | June 1, 2026 | COS Parenting

School's out, the days are longer, and screens are suddenly everywhere. If your family's screen time has crept up since the last bell rang, you're in good company. But new research gives us reason to take a closer look — especially for our youngest kids.

A 2026 study from the University College London Centre for Longitudinal Studies found that the average two-year-old now spends 129 minutes per day on screens. That's more than double the World Health Organization's recommendation of 60 minutes or less per day for children ages two to five. And the effects aren't just about time logged — toddlers in the study with the highest screen exposure scored lower on vocabulary assessments and showed more emotional and behavioral difficulties than children with low screen use.

What the AAP Is Saying Now

The American Academy of Pediatrics updated its guidance in 2026 to focus more on the quality and context of screen use, rather than strict time limits. The shift reflects growing evidence that how kids use screens — and whether parents are engaged in that use — matters as much as how long they're on them.

The core guidelines still stand, however:

  • Under 18 months: No screens at all, except video calls with family.
  • Ages 18 to 24 months: Only high-quality programming, always co-viewed with a parent.
  • Ages 2 to 5: Limit to one hour per day of high-quality content; watch together and talk about what you're seeing.
  • Ages 6 and up: Set consistent limits on time and type; don't let screens displace sleep, physical activity, or family time.

The AAP also continues to recommend device-free zones at mealtimes and in bedrooms — for children and parents.

Why Summer Is the Hardest Season

School-year routines create natural limits on screen time: kids are in class, at practice, at activities. Summer blows those structures out. Longer days, bored afternoons, and parents juggling work with childcare make screens the easy solution.

That's not a moral failure — it's just reality. But it's worth being intentional about, especially for kids under five, where the research shows the impact is most significant.

What's Next

The AAP and UCL research teams continue to track the long-term effects of early screen exposure as part of ongoing longitudinal studies. For now, the takeaway is practical: you don't need to ban screens entirely to be doing right by your kids. Watch with them, talk about what they're seeing, and build in plenty of time away from devices. In Colorado Springs this summer, that time is well-spent outside. For the full AAP media guidance, visit healthychildren.org.
