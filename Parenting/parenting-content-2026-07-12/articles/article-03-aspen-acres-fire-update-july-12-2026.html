Aspen Acres Fire: 34% Contained, 780+ Structures Destroyed — Now 7th Most Destructive in Colorado History

By Jamie Rivera

July 12, 2026 · Colorado Springs

The Aspen Acres Fire in southern Colorado has made meaningful progress on containment this week, but new aerial analysis has revealed the full extent of the damage — and the numbers are sobering. As of July 11, the fire is 34% contained and has burned 97,504 acres. More than 780 structures have been damaged or destroyed, placing it among the most destructive wildfires in Colorado's recorded history.

Where Things Stand Today

Containment has jumped significantly from 15% earlier in the week, a sign that the more than 300 additional firefighters who joined suppression efforts have made real progress strengthening lines around threatened communities including Colorado City and Rye. At 97,504 acres, the fire's perimeter has grown only modestly from the 96,031 acres reported on July 9 — suggesting that while the fire isn't out, crews have largely held its spread in check.

That said, the structure count has climbed sharply. Aerial damage surveys from Pueblo and Custer counties now document more than 780 structures damaged or destroyed. That figure makes the Aspen Acres Fire the seventh most destructive wildfire in Colorado's recorded history — a grim milestone for communities that are still in the middle of active evacuations.

Infrastructure and Access

Three water treatment plants have been affected by the fire, creating additional logistical challenges for communities in the burn zone and surrounding areas. Highway closures remain in effect on Highways 165, 78, 67, and 96 — sections that connect communities in the region and serve as evacuation routes. Families with relatives in affected areas should verify current road status before attempting to travel south.

Evacuation Status

Mandatory evacuation orders remain in place for portions of Custer, Pueblo, and Huerfano counties. Level 2 evacuation warnings — meaning residents should be ready to leave on short notice — are active in parts of Fremont County. If you have family or friends in any of these areas, the most current evacuation zone maps and fire perimeter information are available at inciweb.nwcg.gov.

For COS families, the fire's core is roughly 60 miles to the south. It's not an immediate local threat, but air quality in Colorado Springs can be affected depending on wind direction and fire behavior. On days when smoke is visible or the air quality index is elevated, keeping kids indoors during peak afternoon hours is a sensible precaution, particularly for children with asthma or other respiratory conditions.

What's Next

Crews are working to extend and reinforce containment lines, with the goal of protecting communities that remain under evacuation orders. Weather conditions — particularly wind direction and relative humidity — will play a significant role in how quickly the fire's perimeter can be secured. Dry summer conditions have persisted across much of southern Colorado, and the National Weather Service is the best source for local fire weather forecasts.

For families who have been displaced by the fire or have relatives in the evacuation zone, the Red Cross typically operates shelter and assistance programs for active wildfire evacuations in Colorado — local Red Cross chapters can confirm what's currently available. The inciweb.nwcg.gov page for the Aspen Acres Fire is updated regularly with containment percentages, evacuation zone changes, and road closure updates.
