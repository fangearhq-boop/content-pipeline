CPSC Warning: Stop Using Babypark 3-in-1 Soft Carriers — Company Refused to Recall

By Sarah Morales

July 12, 2026 · Colorado Springs

If you have a Babypark 3-in-1 soft fabric baby carrier — the gray one with the colorful leaf design — stop using it now and dispose of it. Don't give it to another family, don't donate it, and don't sell it at a garage sale. The CPSC has issued a safety warning after finding that the leg openings on the carrier are large enough for an infant or toddler to slip through and fall.

What makes this situation more complicated than a typical recall: the manufacturer refused to cooperate with the CPSC's recall process. That means there's no refund program, no replacement, and no return process available to families who bought one.

Why This Is a Warning, Not a Recall

When the CPSC identifies a hazardous product, the agency typically works with the manufacturer to arrange a recall — a process that includes notifying consumers and providing a remedy like a refund or replacement. That's the standard path.

When a company refuses to participate, the CPSC's options become more limited. The agency can issue a public safety warning to alert consumers, but it can't compel an uncooperative overseas manufacturer to fund a remedy program. Beiyou Bless, the company behind this carrier, is based in China, which limits the CPSC's enforcement reach.

The practical result for families: there's no official mechanism to get your money back, and the CPSC's clear guidance is to dispose of the product rather than pass it along to someone else who might use it.

The Hazard Explained

The Babypark 3-in-1 carrier fails the mandatory federal safety standard for soft infant and toddler carriers. The leg openings on the carrier are wider than the standard allows — wide enough that a small child's body could slip through while the carrier is in use, potentially resulting in a fall from height.

This is a structural design flaw, not a manufacturing defect in a subset of units. All units of this product have the same leg opening dimensions. There's no way to modify or repair the carrier to make it safe — the only appropriate action is disposal.

The carrier was sold on Amazon from April 2024 through May 2026 at a price of approximately $27. If you purchased one during that window — or received one as a gift for a baby shower or new arrival — check whether you still have it in your home or car.

A Note on a Different Babypark Warning

COS parents who follow product safety news may remember a Babypark sling carrier warning from January 2025 — that was a different product (a sling-style carrier sold on Shein, with approximately 750 units affected). Today's warning covers a separate product: the 3-in-1 soft fabric carrier sold on Amazon. If you're uncertain which product you have, the Amazon listing, your order history, or the carrier's design (gray with multicolored leaf pattern vs. a sling style) can help you identify it.

What to Do Right Now

If you have this carrier, take these steps:

  1. Stop using it immediately.
  2. Dispose of it — cut the straps or otherwise render it unusable before putting it in the trash, so it can't be picked up and reused.
  3. Do not donate it, give it away, or sell it. Passing a hazardous product to another family doesn't eliminate the risk — it transfers it.

If you think a friend or family member may have received this carrier as a gift in the past two years, it's worth a quick message. Amazon purchase history can confirm whether someone ordered it, and the two-year sale window means it's likely still in use in some homes.

What's Next

Because the manufacturer refused to cooperate, there's no active remedy process to monitor. The CPSC can pursue legal action against non-compliant companies, but that process is slow and doesn't translate into consumer refunds in most cases. For affected families, the immediate action — dispose and stop using — is both the CPSC's guidance and the only available option right now.

If you want to report that you owned this product or have additional information about injuries or incidents, the CPSC's SaferProducts.gov portal accepts consumer reports, and those reports can inform future enforcement actions against the company.
