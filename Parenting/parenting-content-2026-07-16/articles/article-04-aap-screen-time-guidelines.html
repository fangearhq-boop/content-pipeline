The AAP Dropped the 2-Hour Screen Time Rule in 2026 — Here's What Replaced It

By Sarah Morales | COS Parenting

If you've been loosely following the "two hours of screen time per day" guideline for older kids, you're operating on outdated advice. The American Academy of Pediatrics released updated digital ecosystem guidelines in February 2026, and the 2-hour limit is no longer part of the framework.

What replaced it is actually more nuanced — and, for most families, more realistic.

What Changed

For more than a decade, the AAP's guidance for children ages 6 and up included a recommendation to limit screen time to no more than two hours per day. In February 2026, the AAP shifted to a framework that focuses on the quality of content, context, and family conversation rather than a hard clock limit.

The reasoning: how kids use screens matters more than how long they use them. Watching an educational documentary with a parent has a very different impact than scrolling through social media alone at 11 PM. A one-size-fits-all time cap doesn't capture that difference.

What the Updated Guidelines Actually Say

A few things from the updated AAP framework that every COS parent should know:

  • Under 18 months: No screens, with one exception — video calls with family members. This guideline hasn't changed.
  • Ages 2–5: Up to one hour per day of high-quality programming, with a parent watching alongside when possible.
  • Ages 6 and up: No hard time cap. The focus is on making sure screens don't crowd out sleep, physical activity, family time, or free play.

Device-free zones are now a centerpiece of the recommendations. The AAP specifically names bedrooms and mealtimes as spaces where screens should be off — for kids and parents alike. Co-viewing (watching or engaging with content together rather than handing over a device) is highlighted as more beneficial than solo screen time at any age.

A Good Summer Reset

July is a natural checkpoint. With a few weeks left before Colorado Springs schools return, summer screen habits are still in flux — and that's actually an opportunity. Patterns set now tend to carry into the school year.

A few practical moves that align with the updated AAP guidance:

  • Designate mealtimes as phone-free for everyone at the table, not just kids.
  • If screens are part of the evening wind-down, move devices out of bedrooms before bed.
  • Ask what kids are watching and occasionally sit down to watch with them — engagement beats monitoring.
  • Use screen time as a tool rather than a default: when the temperature or air quality makes outdoor play difficult, a quality show or educational game is a reasonable option.

What's Next

The full AAP guidance on children and digital ecosystems is available at healthychildren.org, the AAP's parent-facing resource. For families heading into the school year, now is a good time to talk with kids about what screen time will look like once schedules get busier. The conversation matters more than the clock.
