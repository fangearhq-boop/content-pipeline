78% of Parents Say Summer Wrecks Their Child's Sleep — Here's Why and What Helps

By Jamie Rivera | COS Parenting

July 4, 2026

If you're fighting your kids on bedtime more than usual this summer, you've got a lot of company. A June 2026 survey of parents and guardians of children ages 0–4, conducted by KeaBabies, found that 78% say summer negatively disrupts their child's sleep routine. More than half — 55% — say they're personally losing at least 1 hour of sleep per night because of it.

Here in Colorado Springs, summer sleep struggles come with an extra layer: the combination of high altitude, dry air, and intense daytime heat can affect hydration levels overnight in ways that compound the problem. The region averages 300 days of sunshine per year, which is great for outdoor activities and rough for getting kids to sleep before sunset.

Why Summer Is Harder on Sleep

The biology behind summer sleep disruption comes down to two main factors: temperature and light.

Your body falls asleep by cooling down. As the evening approaches, core body temperature naturally drops — that's one of the physical signals that triggers sleep onset. When ambient temperatures are high, the body has to work harder to achieve that cooling effect. For young children, who regulate temperature less efficiently than adults, this is particularly challenging.

The second factor is daylight. Human bodies produce melatonin — the hormone that regulates sleep — in response to darkness. In summer, sunset comes later, which pushes back melatonin production and effectively delays the body's "time to sleep" signal. A child whose body is used to falling asleep at 7:30 PM in March may find that genuinely hard to do at 7:30 PM in July, because it's still light outside.

What Actually Helps

The parents surveyed by KeaBabies cited breathability as their top priority when it comes to sleepwear — 77% ranked it highest. But sleepwear is just one piece. Here are evidence-informed strategies for helping children sleep better through Colorado Springs summers:

  • Keep the room cool and dark. Blackout curtains make a significant difference. Aim for a bedroom temperature between 65–70°F where possible. Fans help with air circulation even when they can't bring the temperature all the way down.
  • Dress kids in lightweight, breathable fabrics. Natural fibers like cotton or bamboo breathe better than synthetic materials and wick moisture away from the skin.
  • Stick to your regular bedtime routine. Even when it feels artificial, doing the same sequence of events — bath, book, lights out — at the same time sends consistent signals to a child's nervous system.
  • Consider starting the routine 15–30 minutes earlier. If light exposure is delaying melatonin production, building in extra wind-down time can help compensate.
  • Prioritize daytime hydration in COS. Colorado Springs' dry, high-altitude air accelerates fluid loss. Kids who are adequately hydrated during the day sleep more comfortably at night.
  • Limit screen time in the 60 minutes before bed. Screens emit blue light that suppresses melatonin production — a compounding problem in summer when natural light is already doing the same thing.

What's Next

Summer sleep disruption tends to peak in June and July and gradually improves as days shorten into August. If your child's sleep has been significantly disrupted for more than a few weeks despite consistent efforts, it may be worth a conversation with your pediatrician — particularly if you notice behavioral changes during the day that seem related to sleep quality. D11, D20, and D49 all return to school schedules in August, which naturally resets many families' routines.
