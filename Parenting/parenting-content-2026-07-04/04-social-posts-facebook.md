```
The U.S. Consumer Product Safety Commission has issued an emergency warning about Fasando Baby Loungers — and the situation is more serious than most recalls because the manufacturer has refused to act.

• Cut the cover in half
• Dispose of all pieces
• Do not resell or give away
```

### STORY 2: Target Gigglescape + GOPO Teething Toy Recalls

#### Long-Form Post — 10:00 AM MT

```
Two more children's product recalls to know about — both involving choking hazards. 🚨

First: Target's Gigglescape Under the Sea Popping Toy. About 49,000 of these were sold at Target for around $10 between August 2025 and January 2026. The clear plastic dome on top detaches, releasing small plastic balls — and yes, one child did begin to choke. If you have this toy, stop using it and return it to any Target store for a full refund. You can also request a prepaid return label by mail.

Second: GOPO Toys Pull String Teething Toy (sold on Amazon for $11–$15). About 70,410 of these off-white disc-shaped teethers with colorful silicone pull strings were sold from August 2023 through March 2026. The strings are smaller and longer than the mandatory safety standard allows — three incidents of choking or respiratory distress were reported. To get a refund: cut all the strings, write "DESTROYED" in permanent marker on the toy body, and email a photo to recalls@gopotoys.com before disposing.

Both of these were sold at major retailers or through Amazon — meaning they're easy to have in your home without realizing they're affected.

Have you done your product recall check lately? The CPSC has a free recall notification system at cpsc.gov if you want to stay up to date.
```

### STORY 4: Summer Sleep Survey

#### Long-Form Post — 5:00 PM MT

```
If your kids have been fighting bedtime all summer, you're not imagining it — and you're definitely not alone.

A new survey of parents of children ages 0–4, conducted by KeaBabies in June 2026, found 78% of parents say summer negatively disrupts their child's sleep routine. More than half said they're personally losing at least 1 hour of sleep per night because of it.

Here's why summer is harder: when the temperature goes up, it interferes with the body's natural cooling process that triggers sleep onset. And longer daylight hours delay the production of melatonin — the hormone that signals it's time to sleep. Your kid's body is literally being told "not yet" by both the heat and the light.

A few things that actually help:

• Keep the bedroom as dark as possible (blackout curtains are worth it)
• Aim for a room temperature between 65–70°F
• Dress kids in lightweight, breathable fabrics
• Stick to your regular bedtime routine even when it feels hard
• Start winding down 30–60 minutes earlier to compensate for delayed melatonin

Here in Colorado Springs, we've got the added factor of dry heat and high altitude — which can actually make dehydration worse, and that doesn't help kids sleep either. Make sure they're drinking enough water during the day.

What's your go-to strategy for keeping kids' sleep on track in the summer?
```

### STORY 5: AAP Screen Time 5 Cs

#### Long-Form Post — 8:00 PM MT

```
Good news for parents who've been stressed about screen time rules: the American Academy of Pediatrics updated its guidance in 2026, and the approach is a lot more realistic now.

The old rule was a hard 2-hour daily cap for kids ages 2–5. The new guidance replaces that with a "5 Cs" framework designed to be more practical for actual families:

Child: Every child is different. Consider your kid's temperament, age, and needs.
Content: What they're watching matters more than how long. Quality educational content is different from passive scrolling.
Calm: Can your child settle down without a screen? If not, that's a signal worth paying attention to.
Crowding Out: Is screen time displacing sleep, physical play, reading, or family interaction? That's the real concern.
Communication: Talk regularly with your kids about what they're watching and why.

Some rules that haven't changed: no screens at all under 18 months (except video calls with family). Ages 2–5, still aim for about 1 hour of quality content on weekdays. Bedrooms and mealtimes should still be device-free. No screens within 1 hour of bedtime.

The AAP also recommends making a written Family Media Plan — there's a tool for it at healthychildren.org that's worth bookmarking.

How does your family handle screen time? Has the old 2-hour rule ever actually worked for you?
```
