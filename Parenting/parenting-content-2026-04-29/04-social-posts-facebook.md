### STORY 1: Wiifo Tower Stool Recall

#### Long-Form Post -- 8:00 AM MT

**Long-Form Post (8:00 AM MT)**

If you have a Wiifo learning tower at home — the kind that lets toddlers stand at counter height so they can help in the kitchen — it's time to check the model number.

The CPSC has recalled Wiifo-branded children's tower stools, model LT005, after 22 reported incidents of the stools collapsing and tipping over. Six children were injured. The hazard isn't just the fall: the openings on the sides of the stool are large enough for a child's torso to fit through, creating an entrapment risk in addition to the fall danger. About 9,700 units were sold on Amazon between June 2022 and March 2026 for about $60.

To identify the recalled product: look for "Model LT005" on a label on the underside of the standing platform. It comes in white, natural, and light wood finish.

If you have one, stop using it now. The remedy is a full refund — contact Wiifo at support@wiifo.net and disassemble the stool, then send a photo of the destroyed stool as proof.

These stools have become a go-to in a lot of homes with toddlers who want to be part of mealtime prep. Worth the few minutes to check.

Did you have one of these stools at home? Or have you heard about this recall from another parent?

### STORY 2: Vevor Baby Swing Recall

#### Long-Form Post -- 10:00 AM MT

**Long-Form Post (10:00 AM MT)**

Parents who bought a Vevor baby swing in 2025 — it's worth checking whether yours is on the recall list.

Sanven Technology has recalled three models of Vevor baby swings: BB501K (white with white base), BB702A (white with gray base), and BB005K (black with black base). The issue is a federal one: these swings have an incline greater than 10 degrees and violate the Safe Sleep for Babies Act, which bans inclined sleep products for infants. The incline creates a suffocation risk — the angle can cause an infant's head to tilt forward and block the airway.

The swings were sold on Vevor.com and Amazon.com from January 2025 through August 2025 for $65 to $80.

If you have one, stop using it now. To get a refund, remove the swing cover, cut it in half, and send a photo to recalling@vevor.com. You can also call 855-599-6320, Monday through Friday, 9 AM to 5 PM PT.

The inclined sleeper issue has been on the CPSC's radar since the Fisher-Price Rock 'n Play recall years ago, and the agency continues to act against products that don't meet the standard. If a swing or sleeper has been sitting in a closet since your youngest outgrew it, now is a good time to check what it is.

### STORY 4: Screen Time Quality Research

#### Long-Form Post -- 3:00 PM MT

**Long-Form Post (3:00 PM MT)**

The American Academy of Pediatrics shifted its screen time guidance in 2026, and it's worth knowing what changed — because it's actually more practical than what came before.

For years, the conversation was about limits: under 18 months, no screens. Ages 2-5, one hour per day. And while those limits haven't gone away, the AAP's updated framework is now clear that limits alone aren't the full story. A new report covered by ABC News puts it plainly: screen time limits are no longer enough.

What matters more, according to current research: what your child is watching, and whether an adult is watching with them. Kids who watch alongside a parent show significantly better learning transfer than kids watching alone. Co-viewing turns passive content into an opportunity for conversation, connection, and comprehension. That is a meaningful difference.

The flip side also holds: low-quality content — the kind that autoplays one video after another with no educational value and maximum stimulation — can disrupt sleep and attention regardless of how short the session is.

So the practical takeaway for Colorado Springs families isn't "watch less." It's "watch better, and watch together." That's a lot more achievable than it sounds.

How does your family handle screen time — do you watch with your kids, or is it more of a solo activity for them?
