COS Is Under a Burn Ban and Stage II Fire Restrictions — What That Means for July 4

By Sarah Morales | July 1, 2026

Fire conditions in Colorado Springs have escalated since Monday. A burn ban is in effect, El Paso County is now under Stage II fire restrictions, and the penalties for fireworks violations are severe. Here's what families need to know before July 4.

The fire situation in Colorado Springs has changed significantly since the start of the week. A burn ban took effect at noon on Monday, June 29. El Paso County has moved from Stage I to Stage II fire restrictions — the highest level — as of late June. And with July 4 three days away, the Colorado Springs Police Department has assigned specific officers citywide to enforce the city's fireworks ordinance through the holiday weekend.

For Colorado Springs families, there are two separate sets of rules to understand: what applies within city limits, and what applies in unincorporated El Paso County. Both have changed, and both carry consequences for violations.

What Changed: Stage I to Stage II

As of the last COS Parenting pipeline on Monday, El Paso County was under Stage I fire restrictions. That has since escalated to Stage II, which is the highest level of restrictions available under the county system.

Stage II restrictions prohibit the use and sale of fireworks in unincorporated El Paso County. That means families in areas outside Colorado Springs city limits — in unincorporated communities throughout El Paso County — are now also subject to a fireworks ban under the county's Stage II declaration, in addition to the city's own permanent ban.

The burn ban, which took effect at noon June 29, covers Colorado Springs and prohibits open burning.

What's Permanently Banned in Colorado Springs

This is the part that surprises many families every year: consumer fireworks are not just banned this July 4 because of fire conditions. They're permanently banned within Colorado Springs city limits. That's true every year, regardless of weather or fire danger.

The ban covers all consumer fireworks — including sparklers. Sparklers burn at approximately 2,000 degrees Fahrenheit and are responsible for a significant portion of fireworks-related injuries each year, including burns and fires. They're not an exception to the ban.

What is allowed: attending one of the several professional fireworks displays authorized to operate in and around Colorado Springs during the July 4 holiday. Those shows use licensed professionals and proper safety protocols. A guide to public fireworks options in Colorado Springs was published in our Monday pipeline.

The Penalties

The Colorado Springs Police Department has increased its July 4 fireworks enforcement response this year, with specific officers assigned throughout the city for the holiday weekend.

Violations of the City Fireworks Ordinance carry:

  • A fine of up to $2,650
  • A mandatory appearance in municipal court
  • Up to 189 days in jail

These are not warnings or administrative citations — they're criminal violations. The mandatory court appearance applies even for a first offense.

What's Next

For July 4 plans, Colorado Springs families have several legitimate options for watching professional fireworks displays. The city of Colorado Springs, Banning Lewis Ranch, and Palmer Lake all have authorized shows. Our Monday pipeline covered those in detail.

For updates on fire restrictions throughout the July 4 weekend, check the El Paso County Sheriff's Office website at epcsheriffsoffice.com or the Colorado Springs city website. Conditions can change quickly during dry weather, and it's worth checking for any updates before heading outdoors.

If you see illegal fireworks being used, the Colorado Springs Police Department has a non-emergency line and accepts online reports. With specific enforcement officers assigned for the holiday weekend, the city is prepared to respond to reports.
