If your family spends time outside in Colorado Springs — on trails, in open spaces, or at park visitor centers — 2026 is a year worth paying attention to. The city's TOPS (Trails, Open Spaces and Parks) program is rolling out a substantial set of improvements across several popular areas, and several projects are either underway or set to complete this year.

Blodgett Open Space — Major Trail Expansion

Blodgett Open Space is one of the most ambitious trail projects in the 2026 plan. The city is implementing approximately 14 miles of new trail, including two new trailhead projects at the Quarry and North areas. The expansion will add 90 new parking stalls — a significant improvement for a site that fills quickly on weekends. Wildfire mitigation efforts are also part of the project.

For families who use Blodgett regularly, the additional parking and trail mileage will make a real difference in how crowded the area feels during peak weekend hours. The site offers wide, accessible trails that work well for families with younger children or strollers on the more gradual sections.

Rock Island Trail — New Pedestrian Bridge Over Sand Creek

A 210-foot pedestrian bridge over Sand Creek is under construction as part of the Rock Island Trail project. The bridge will connect segments of the trail network that currently require pedestrians to navigate road crossings. The project also includes a pedestrian-activated flashing crossing at Peterson Road. Construction began in spring 2026 and is expected to take approximately 12 months.

Once complete, the Rock Island Trail connection will be a meaningful improvement for families who bike or walk on the east side trail network and want continuous, traffic-separated routes.

Pikes Peak Greenway — Trail Resurfacing and Accessibility

The Pikes Peak Greenway corridor is getting approximately 0.5 miles of new concrete trail, replacing deteriorating asphalt that has been a notable hazard for cyclists, strollers, and wheelchair users. The project also improves ADA-accessible entry points from the Corporate Drive/Park and Ride area. The Greenway is one of the most family-friendly paved trail corridors in the city, connecting parks and open spaces along Fountain Creek.

Starsmore Visitor Center — North Cheyenne Canyon

The Starsmore Visitor Center at North Cheyenne Canyon is a popular starting point for families exploring the canyon. The 2026 improvements include expanded accessible parking, improved bus parking for school groups (good news if your child's class visits on a field trip), new ADA-accessible walkways, a new outdoor plaza, and renovated ADA-standard restrooms. The visitor center also oversees restoration of its garden, which is a kid-friendly educational feature of the site.

What's Next

The full 2026 TOPS plan — including all project details, timelines, and maps — is available at coloradosprings.gov/TOPS2026. As projects progress through the year, updates are posted to the city's parks website. If you're planning family hikes or bike rides this spring and summer, it's worth checking that resource before heading to a site where construction may be active. Colorado Springs' investment in its trail network is one of the best long-term gifts the city can give to families who call it home.
