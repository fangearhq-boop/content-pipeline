AAP 2026 Screen Time Guidelines: What Changed and What It Means for COS Families

By Keisha Williams | COS Parenting | May 1, 2026

AAP 2026 Screen Time at a Glance
Age GroupGuidance
Under 18 monthsNo screen time (video chatting with family is OK)
Ages 2-5Up to 1 hour per day of high-quality content
Ages 6 and upNo set limit, but balance with sleep, activity, and family time
All agesBedrooms screen-free at night; parents model good digital habits

The American Academy of Pediatrics updated its screen time guidance in 2026, and the biggest shift is one that many parents will find more realistic: the new framework focuses less on counting minutes and more on the quality of what kids watch, the context in which they watch it, and whether families are engaging in conversation about it.

What Changed

The previous AAP guidance, issued in 2016, called for a two-hour daily limit for children ages 6 and older. The 2026 update removes that specific cap for children over 5. This doesn't mean anything goes — the AAP is clear that screen time should never crowd out sleep, physical activity, or time connecting face-to-face as a family. But the shift acknowledges that how kids use screens matters as much as how long they use them.

The guidelines for younger children largely remain the same. No screens before 18 months, with the exception of video calls with family members. Children ages 2-5 should aim for no more than one hour of high-quality programming per day. The emphasis on "high-quality" is intentional — not all content is equal, and co-viewing with a parent or caregiver significantly increases the educational benefit.

The Shift in Approach

The phrase the AAP uses is "quality, context, and conversation." What are kids watching? Are they watching it alone or with a trusted adult who can discuss what's happening on screen? Are they watching it in a distraction-free setting, or is it on in the background while homework is being done?

The updated guidance also places renewed emphasis on bedrooms being screen-free at night for children of all ages, and encourages parents to model the kind of device behavior they want their kids to develop. If phones come out at the dinner table or during family conversations, children notice.

The AAP introduced what it calls the "5 C's" as a practical tool for families to reflect on their media habits without necessarily counting every minute. The framework is designed to be flexible enough to account for the wide variety of ways families use technology at different ages and stages.

What This Looks Like in Practice

For families in Colorado Springs with younger children, the most actionable takeaways from the 2026 update are: keep the under-18-months limit in place, watch with kids ages 2-5 rather than handing over a device and walking away, and make sure the bedroom is a screen-free zone at night for everyone in the household — including the adults.

For families with older kids and teenagers, the conversation shifts from a timer to a habit audit. Are they getting enough sleep? Are they physically active? Is there regular screen-free time together as a family? Those questions matter more than any specific number of hours per day.

What's Next

The full AAP guidance and the "5 C's" framework are available at aap.org. If you're looking for a practical starting point, the AAP's Family Media Plan — a customizable tool on their website — helps families set their own household screen use goals. The updated guidelines mark a meaningful evolution in how we think about technology and kids, and they're worth a read if you've been operating off the old two-hour rule.
