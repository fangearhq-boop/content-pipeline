### STORY 1: Cosyland Tower Stool Recall

**Long-Form Post**
Window: 8:00 AM MT

Colorado Springs parents — there is a safety recall that applies to a lot of families right now, and if you have a Cosyland children's tower stool, you need to act.

The Consumer Product Safety Commission announced on May 14 that 125,200 Cosyland tower stools (models CS0003 and CS0092-4) are recalled due to serious injury risks. The platform inside the stool can loosen over time, making the stool unstable. It can also collapse or tip over while in use — and the openings on the front and back are large enough for a child's torso to fit through, creating an entrapment hazard.

The CPSC has documented 25 reports of stability issues and falls, with eight injuries ranging from scrapes and bruises to a fractured arm.

These stools were sold on Amazon for about $70 from April 2021 through November 2025, in natural bamboo and gray finishes.

What to do: Stop using the stool immediately and contact Cosyland Official to receive a FREE repair kit, which includes protective nets, stabilizing feet, and installation instructions. The parts will be mailed to you directly.

You can also reach the CPSC at 800-638-2772 or visit CPSC.gov for more details.

### STORY 3: D11 CSEA Contract Dispute

**Long-Form Post**
Window: 12:00 PM MT

As District 11 families wrap up the school year, there is a labor dispute in the background that is worth understanding — not just for this spring, but for what it may mean next fall.

In December 2024, the D11 School Board voted 7-1 to dissolve the 56-year-old master agreement with the Colorado Springs Education Association (CSEA), the teachers union. That decision triggered more than 300 days (and counting) without a formal contract governing the relationship between educators and the district.

In October 2025, about a third of D11 teachers participated in a one-day strike — the first in recent district history — after 91% of CSEA members voted in favor of strike action. Educators returned to work, but the contract dispute was not resolved.

CSEA says the lack of a contract has led to "decisions that are destabilizing schools, driving educators out of the profession, and shortchanging students." Their key concerns: rising class sizes, insufficient school counselors and social workers, underfunded special education support, and restrictions on curriculum content.

In early May 2026, educators and parents organized a community picket — more than 300 days after the contract was severed.

For Colorado Springs parents planning for the 2026-27 school year: this is the backdrop to watch. Class sizes, teacher staffing levels, and school support resources are all on the table.

### STORY 5: COS Summer Parks: What to Know Before You Go

**Long-Form Post**
Window: 5:00 PM MT

School ends Friday. Summer is here. Before you pack up the stroller and head to your neighborhood park, there are a few things Colorado Springs families should know about the 2026 park season.

The city announced earlier this year that budget cuts will affect parks this summer in two specific ways:

First, approximately 10 neighborhood parks (about 25% of permanent park restrooms) will have their restrooms closed for the 2026 season due to budget constraints. The city will maintain portable restroom availability in some areas, but not at the same coverage level as prior years.

Second, the city has scaled back its approach to playground maintenance. When playground equipment is damaged or vandalized, the current plan is to remove it rather than repair or replace it. Over time, this could affect the playground equipment available at some neighborhood parks.

On a more positive note for trail-loving families: the Rock Island Trail pedestrian bridge project is underway, with a 210-foot bridge being constructed over Sand Creek to connect the east and west sides of the trail. The project will eventually add more than two miles of new trail access in the city, funded by PPRTA II, CDOT grants, and the TOPS program. The project is expected to take 10-12 months.

Practical tips for families this summer: Before a big park trip, check the city parks website at parks.coloradosprings.gov for current facility status. For nearby restroom alternatives, many parks have portable units; also, nearby recreation centers, libraries, and businesses can be backups.
