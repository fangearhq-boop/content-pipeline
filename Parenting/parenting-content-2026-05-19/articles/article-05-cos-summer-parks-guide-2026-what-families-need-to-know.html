Summer starts this Friday when D11 rings the last school bell of the year. Colorado Springs families will be heading to neighborhood parks, trails, and open spaces in the weeks ahead — and there are a few things worth knowing before you pack the bag and load the car.

The city made budget adjustments for 2026 that affect park facilities, and there is also a significant trail construction project underway that will eventually add new access for families but creates some temporary changes along the way.

Restroom Closures at Neighborhood Parks

The City of Colorado Springs announced earlier this year that budget constraints will result in approximately 25% of neighborhood park permanent restrooms — roughly 10 facilities — remaining closed for the 2026 season. The city stated it would maintain some portable restroom coverage, but at reduced levels compared to prior years.

For families with young children, this is practical information worth having before a long park visit. Before heading to a neighborhood park you have not visited recently, it is worth checking the city parks website at parks.coloradosprings.gov to see current facility status. Having a backup plan — a nearby library, recreation center, or coffee shop — is a good idea for longer outings.

Playground Equipment Changes

The city also announced a change to how it handles damaged or vandalized playground equipment: rather than repairing or replacing it, the current approach is to remove it. Over time, this could mean fewer play structures at some neighborhood parks compared to what families may remember from past summers.

This does not affect every park — it applies to equipment that is damaged or vandalized, which varies by location. But it is worth knowing if you visit a park and find that a piece of equipment you expected is no longer there.

Trail Improvements Coming for Families

The news is not all budget cuts. One of the most significant park improvements underway in Colorado Springs in 2026 is the Rock Island Trail project, which is adding meaningful new trail access to a part of the city that has been underserved.

The project includes a 210-foot pedestrian bridge over Sand Creek that will connect the east and west sides of the Rock Island Trail, making it possible to travel the corridor without interruption. The project will also add over two miles of new trail. A pedestrian-activated flashing crossing at Peterson Road is also part of the project, improving safety at a key intersection.

Funding comes from the Pikes Peak Rural Transportation Authority (PPRTA II), Colorado Department of Transportation grants, and the TOPS (Trails, Open Space, and Parks) sales tax program. The project began in Spring 2026 and is expected to take approximately 10 to 12 months.

When complete, the Rock Island Trail corridor will be one of the most connected family-friendly trail experiences in COS.

Other Great Options for Colorado Springs Families This Summer

Despite the restroom and playground limitations at some parks, Colorado Springs has an extensive network of outdoor spaces and programs worth exploring:

  • Trackchair Program: Free guided hikes on accessible all-terrain chairs at Ute Valley Park, Red Rock Canyon Open Space, Blodgett Open Space, and Bluestem Prairie. Wed-Sun; reserve at coloradosprings.gov.
  • Garden of the Gods: Free admission always; ranger programs and visitor center are family-friendly options throughout summer.
  • Monument Valley Park: A well-maintained city park along Fountain Creek with paved paths, good restroom access, and plenty of open space.
  • Cheyenne Mountain State Park: Requires a parks pass but offers excellent trails, archery, and outdoor programming for kids.
