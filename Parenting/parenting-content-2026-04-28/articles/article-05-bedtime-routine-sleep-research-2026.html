Consistent Bedtime Routine Gives Kids More Than One Extra Hour of Sleep Per Night, Research Shows

By Marta Kim | COS Parenting | April 28, 2026

Here is a finding from pediatric sleep research that parents who have been meaning to establish a bedtime routine — but haven't gotten around to it — may want to pay attention to: children with a consistent nightly bedtime routine sleep more than one hour longer per night than children with no routine at all.

That is not a minor difference. An extra hour of sleep, repeated night after night, adds up to meaningful changes in behavior, mood, attention, and health over weeks and months. And the research shows that the relationship is dose-dependent: the more consistently the routine happens, the bigger the benefit.

A multinational study published through the American Academy of Sleep Medicine found that children who had a bedtime routine every night slept for an average of more than an hour longer than those who never had one. Children who had the routine most nights (but not all) fell in between — demonstrating the dose-dependent relationship. This is not correlation from a single cultural context; the pattern held across multiple countries and family types.

The specific outcomes researchers tracked include earlier bedtimes, shorter sleep onset latency (the time between getting into bed and falling asleep), fewer nighttime wakings, and longer total sleep duration. These are the metrics that actually matter for child development and for family function.

What Counts as a Routine

A bedtime routine does not have to be complicated. What matters is predictability — a consistent sequence of activities that signals to the child's nervous system that sleep is coming. Researchers and pediatric sleep experts consistently point to the same basic elements: dim the lights in the hour before bed, close the blinds, and include a calming activity like reading.

The AAP's 2026 framework for family media use reinforces this. The updated guidance has shifted away from strict screen time limits and toward an emphasis on timing, context, and quality. One of the practical takeaways from that shift: a screen-free wind-down window before bed matters more than the total number of screen minutes during the day. Evening screen use — particularly television — disrupts the biological signals that prepare the brain for sleep. Building a screen-free routine into the hour before bed aligns with both the sleep research and the updated AAP guidance.

Starting Early and Staying Consistent

The research also shows that starting early matters. Families who establish bedtime routines during infancy tend to see better outcomes later — children who grew up with consistent routines maintain better sleep habits at school age than those who did not. That does not mean it is too late to start if you have older children. The evidence for benefit applies across ages. But if you have a baby or toddler at home, the investment now has a longer runway.

The practical challenge is consistency. Routines fall apart during travel, illness, school breaks, and schedule changes. Pediatric sleep experts generally say that the routine does not have to be perfect to be effective — what matters is returning to it after disruptions rather than abandoning it.

What's Next

If your family does not have a consistent bedtime routine, the research suggests starting with the basics: pick a target bedtime, dim the lights 30 to 45 minutes before that time, add a book or two, and keep it the same across the week. The American Academy of Sleep Medicine (aasm.org) and the AAP's HealthyChildren.org both have practical resources for families building sleep habits at different ages. The evidence is clear — the routine pays for itself in sleep.
