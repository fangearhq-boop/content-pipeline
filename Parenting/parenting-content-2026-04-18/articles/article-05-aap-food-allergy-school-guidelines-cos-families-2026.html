AAP Updates School Food Allergy Guidelines — What Colorado Springs Families Should Know

By Marcus Rivera | COS Parenting | April 18, 2026

If you have a child with a food allergy, there's a practical reason to review their school's policies before the end of the school year. The American Academy of Pediatrics published a major update to its clinical guidance on managing food allergies in schools in September 2025 — the first significant revision since 2010 — and the changes have implications for how parents, pediatricians, and schools work together.

The Numbers That Matter

The updated AAP clinical report puts the scope of the issue in clear terms. Food allergies affect up to 10% of children — meaning in a classroom of 25 kids, two or three of them may be managing an allergy. And the risk is not abstract: anaphylaxis, a severe and potentially life-threatening allergic reaction, is estimated to occur in one in 15 schools every year.

That means in a district the size of D11, D20, or D49 — each with dozens of schools — anaphylactic emergencies are not rare events. They're statistically expected, and schools need to be prepared for them.

What Changed in the 2025 Guidelines

The most notable practical update in the AAP's 2025 clinical report is a stronger recommendation for pediatricians to prescribe stock (unassigned) epinephrine auto-injectors (EAIs) for schools, in addition to individual student prescriptions. A stock EAI is one that's available for any student having an anaphylactic emergency — even a child whose allergy was unknown or undiagnosed.

Some states have already passed laws encouraging or requiring schools to keep stock epinephrine on hand. The AAP's updated guidance supports this approach and provides pediatricians with a clear framework for working with schools to implement it.

The report also reinforces the central role of individual emergency action plans for every child with a documented food allergy, and emphasizes that school nurses, teachers, and administrators all need clear training on how to recognize and respond to allergic reactions.

A Note on Summer Programs

The end of the school year is also a good moment to think ahead to summer programs, camps, and activities. The school nurse, classroom teacher, and staff who are familiar with your child's allergy and action plan won't be present at summer camps. Before registering your child for any summer program, ask directly: Does the program have staff trained in recognizing anaphylaxis? Is there an epinephrine auto-injector available on-site? What is the emergency protocol?

These are not difficult questions, and any reputable Colorado Springs summer program should be able to answer them. If they can't, that's important information too.

What's Next

The AAP's updated guidelines are available for parents and providers at publications.aap.org. Colorado Springs families with questions about their child's specific food allergy management plan should start with their pediatrician, who can help translate the new recommendations into a plan that works for your child's school. End-of-year is also an excellent time to make sure any EAI prescriptions are current — auto-injectors expire, and you want to start next school year with a fresh device on file.
