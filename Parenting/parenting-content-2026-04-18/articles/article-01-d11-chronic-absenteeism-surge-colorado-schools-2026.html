D11's Chronic Absenteeism Surged More Than Any Large Colorado District — Here's What Families Can Do

By Jenna Park | COS Parenting | April 18, 2026

New data shows that Colorado Springs District 11 recorded the largest surge in chronic absenteeism of any large Colorado school district in the 2025-26 school year. The district's rate jumped 16.6 percentage points in a single year — a dramatic shift that is drawing attention from education researchers across the state and raising urgent questions for families in the district.

More than half of D11's Black and Hispanic students are now chronically absent, meaning they're missing 10% or more of their school days. For a student in a 180-day school year, that's 18 or more school days — nearly four weeks of instruction.

What Chronic Absenteeism Means

The Colorado Department of Education defines chronic absenteeism as missing 10% or more of enrolled school days — whether excused, unexcused, or due to suspension. It's not just about truancy. A child who misses school regularly because of illness, transportation challenges, housing instability, or school anxiety counts as chronically absent.

Research consistently links chronic absenteeism to lower academic achievement, higher dropout rates, and long-term economic outcomes. When a student misses a significant portion of their school year, catching up becomes increasingly difficult — especially in the early grades where foundational literacy and numeracy skills are being built.

D11's Numbers Stand Out Statewide

Colorado's statewide chronic absenteeism numbers have remained largely unchanged from the 2023-24 school year, according to the Colorado Department of Education. That makes D11's 16.6 percentage point increase especially notable — and the largest recorded increase among any large Colorado school district.

The racial and ethnic disparities in D11's data are particularly significant. When more than half of the district's Black and Hispanic students are chronically absent, it points to systemic barriers — not just individual family decisions — that need coordinated district-level responses.

The Colorado Department of Education has set a statewide goal of reducing chronic absenteeism to 17.8% by the 2026-27 school year. D11's current trajectory makes that target particularly challenging to reach within the district.

What D11 Families Can Do

If you're concerned about... Start here
Your child's attendance record Contact your school's attendance office or check the family portal
Chronic illness or medical absences Ask your school about a medical excuse plan or 504 accommodations
School anxiety or avoidance Connect with your school counselor — D11 has counselors at every school
Transportation issues Contact D11's Transportation Department or ask about bus route changes
General attendance support Visit d11.org or call your school's main office

If your child is struggling with attendance for any reason, early communication with the school makes a significant difference. Most attendance issues that start small can be addressed before they become chronic — but schools can only help if they know what's happening.

What's Next

District 11 is expected to address chronic absenteeism as part of its 2026-27 school year planning. The Colorado Department of Education's ongoing campaign to reduce statewide absenteeism will bring additional resources and accountability measures to districts that show the largest gaps. For D11 families, the most important action right now is knowing where your child stands and reaching out to your school if you're concerned. Visit d11.org for attendance resources, or call your school's main office directly.
