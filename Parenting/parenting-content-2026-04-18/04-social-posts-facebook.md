### STORY 1: D11 Chronic Absenteeism Surge

#### Long-Form Post — 9:00 AM MT

Colorado Springs District 11 families — this is worth paying attention to.

New data shows D11's chronic absenteeism rate jumped 16.6 percentage points in just one year. That's the single largest increase recorded by any large Colorado school district. And the numbers are especially stark among D11's students of color: more than half of the district's Black and Hispanic students are now chronically absent — meaning they're missing 10% or more of school days.

Here's some context: Colorado's statewide absenteeism numbers have remained largely unchanged from the prior year, which means D11's spike really stands out. The Colorado Department of Education has set a goal of reducing statewide chronic absenteeism to 17.8% by the 2026-27 school year.

If you're a D11 family dealing with attendance challenges — whether it's illness, transportation, school anxiety, or anything else — the district has resources at d11.org. The attendance office at your school is also the first place to call if you're worried about where your child stands.

We want to hear from you: is attendance something your family has navigated this year? What's helped?

### STORY 2: CPSC Baby Bath Seat Recalls

#### Long-Form Post — 11:00 AM MT

Time to check your bathroom 🛁 — two baby bath seats sold on Amazon have been recalled by the CPSC.

**YCXXKJ Bath Seats (sold by BenTalk on Amazon)**
These seats are unstable and can tip over while your child is in the bath, posing a serious drowning risk. If you have one, stop using it immediately and contact the seller for a refund.

**NFSVLB Bath Seats (Model No.: ZY2025 — about 1,430 units)**
These seats were sold on Amazon from May 2025 through October 2025. They have two hazards: they can tip over, and the leg openings are too wide, which can allow a child to slip down and become entrapped. To get your refund, here's what to do:
1. Stop using the seat immediately
2. Disassemble it (remove backrest and arm restraints, discard the screws)
3. Cut all four suction cups on the bottom
4. Write "Recalled" on the front of the seat
5. Send a photo of the disassembled seat to amzmaihuayd@163.com

Both seats violate the federal mandatory standard for infant bath seats. Check cpsc.gov if you're not sure whether you own one.

These kinds of recalls are exactly why we keep an eye on CPSC updates every week. Share this so other Colorado Springs parents can check their bathrooms too.

### STORY 3: Vevor Baby Swings Recall

#### Long-Form Post — 1:00 PM MT

One more safety recall to know about this week.

Sanven Technology has recalled about 1,020 Vevor Brand Baby Swings 🍼 because they violate the Safe Sleep for Babies Act's ban on inclined infant sleepers. The swings were marketed for infant sleep but have an incline angle greater than 10 degrees — that's the threshold that makes them illegal under federal law because of the suffocation risk.

The three affected models are:
- BB501K (white/gray swing with a white base)
- BB702A (white/gray swing with a gray base)
- BB005K (black/gray swing with a black base)

These were sold on Vevor.com and Amazon.com from January 2025 through August 2025, priced at $65–$80. If you have one, here's how to get your full refund:

1. Stop using it immediately
2. Remove the swing cover and cut it in half
3. Write "Recalled" on the base in permanent marker
4. Email a photo of the destroyed cover to recalling@vevor.com
5. Dispose of the swing per your local guidelines

You can also call Sanven Technology at 855-599-6320, Monday through Friday, 9 AM–5 PM PT.

Have you seen any of these in your home or at a childcare center? This one is worth sharing widely.

### STORY 5: AAP Food Allergy School Guidelines

#### Long-Form Post — 6:00 PM MT

If your child has a food allergy, there's something worth knowing before the school year wraps up.

The American Academy of Pediatrics published updated school food allergy guidelines in September 2025 — the first major update in 15 years. Here are the numbers that jumped out at us:

📌 Food allergies affect up to 10% of children
📌 Anaphylaxis — a life-threatening allergic reaction — is estimated to happen in 1 in 15 schools every year

The biggest practical update? The AAP is now specifically recommending that pediatricians prescribe stock (unassigned) epinephrine auto-injectors for schools to keep on hand for emergencies, in addition to individual student prescriptions. Some states have already passed laws requiring this.

What Colorado Springs families can do right now:

✅ Ask your pediatrician at your next appointment whether your child should have a prescription for a stock EAI for their school
✅ Review your child's 504 plan or school emergency action plan before the end of the year
✅ Contact your district — D11 (d11.org), D20 (asd20.org), or D49 (d49.org) — to ask about their epinephrine policy

The full AAP clinical report is available at aap.org for parents who want to read the whole thing.
