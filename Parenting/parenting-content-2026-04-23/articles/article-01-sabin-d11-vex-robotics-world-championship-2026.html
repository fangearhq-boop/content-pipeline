Three Sabin Middle School Students Are Heading to the VEX Robotics World Championship

By Marta Kim | COS Parenting

April 23, 2026

Three eighth graders from Sabin Middle School in Colorado Springs District 11 have earned one of the most competitive spots in youth robotics. Declan Martin, Riley Snyder, and Jaxon Wright will compete this week at the VEX Robotics World Championship in St. Louis — representing D11 against roughly 600 middle school teams from 55 countries.

The championship runs April 25-27 at the America's Center Convention Complex. For students who've been building and programming robots since August, it's the finish line of a season-long process — and a pretty remarkable one at that.

What VEX Robotics Is and Why It Matters

VEX Robotics competitions are engineering challenges in which student teams design, build, and program autonomous and driver-controlled robots to compete on a game field. Each season introduces a new game with its own objectives, field layout, and scoring rules. Teams spend months strategizing, testing, and iterating — learning skills in mechanical engineering, programming, teamwork, and problem-solving along the way.

The VEX V5 Robotics Competition at the middle school level is one of the most widely participated robotics programs in the world. The World Championship is the culminating event: only teams that qualify through regional and state competitions make it. It's not a participation event — it's earned.

For Martin, Snyder, and Wright, earning a spot at Worlds capped a season in which they brought home 15 awards competing in regional tournaments. That's a remarkable run by any measure.

A Season Built on Consistency

Robotics teams that reach the World Championship level typically spend as many hours in a school lab as athletes do on a practice field. The work involves designing a robot that can execute the season's game tasks reliably — not just once, but consistently enough to compete at full speed in elimination rounds against teams from across the country and around the world.

Sabin Middle School's robotics program has been building toward this. The 15 awards this season suggest a team that didn't just peak at the right moment but performed across multiple events. That kind of consistency earns respect from judges and competing teams alike.

At Worlds, they'll face programs from countries with national-level investment in robotics education — South Korea, China, Australia, Brazil, and dozens more. It's the kind of global stage that's rare for any middle schooler to stand on, regardless of the subject.

What's Next

The VEX V5 Middle School division runs April 25-27 in St. Louis. Results will be posted on the RECF website (recf.org) as rounds progress. COS Parenting will follow up with results after the championship concludes. In the meantime, Sabin Middle School families and D11 supporters can follow along on the VEX Robotics social channels and the RECF event livestream.
