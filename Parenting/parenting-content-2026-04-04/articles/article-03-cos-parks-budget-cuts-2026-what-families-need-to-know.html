COS Parks Budget Cuts: 10 Restrooms Closing, Playgrounds Won't Get Repaired — What Families Need to Know for Summer 2026

By Sarah Donnelly | COS Parenting | April 4, 2026

If your family's summer involves regular park visits in Colorado Springs — and for most families with kids, it does — there are changes coming this season that are worth knowing about before you pack the diaper bag and head out the door.

Colorado Springs Parks, Recreation, and Cultural Services is operating with a $3.1 million reduced budget in 2026 after the city experienced lower-than-expected sales tax growth. The general fund for parks dropped from approximately $19 million to $17 million. The cuts are real, and for families, they will be visible at neighborhood parks across the city starting this summer.

What Is Changing This Summer

The Playground Repair Concern

The decision to reduce repair funding is the change that may have the longest-term impact on COS park quality. A restroom closure is inconvenient; removed playground equipment does not come back easily. When the city removes a piece of broken equipment without replacing it, that particular structure is likely gone for an extended period — or permanently — unless a specific capital investment is made to replace it.

For families who rely on neighborhood parks as free, accessible summer destinations, the cumulative effect of equipment removal over a season can meaningfully reduce the play value of those spaces. The CPSC requires that unsafe playground equipment be removed — so if something breaks, the city has legitimate safety reasons for taking it out. The problem is the funding gap that prevents replacement from following removal.

What Is Still Moving Forward

Not all park-related news is a cutback. The city's TOPS (Trails, Open Space, Parks) program is proceeding with its 2026 priorities, funded separately through the TOPS sales tax. These include construction of a 210-foot pedestrian bridge over Sand Creek that will connect the east and west segments of the Rock Island Trail — a project that will meaningfully expand the trail network used by COS families for biking, walking, and running. The bridge construction is expected to begin spring 2026 and take approximately 10 to 12 months.

Pikes Peak Greenway corridor trail improvements are also planned through TOPS funding. The trail infrastructure investments are continuing even as day-to-day park maintenance and amenity operations are reduced.

Practical Planning for COS Families

The city has not yet published a specific list of the 10 restrooms that are closing, which makes it difficult for families to plan in advance by park. Until that information is available, the practical advice is to plan for no restroom access at most neighborhood parks — particularly relevant for families with young kids and toddlers who need facilities reliably. America the Beautiful Park and other larger parks with multiple facilities may still have some access points open, but neighborhood parks should be treated as having uncertain restroom availability this summer.

If you visit a park and find playground equipment has been removed or restrooms are locked, you can report concerns to Colorado Springs Parks at coloradosprings.gov or call 311.

What's Next

Watch for the city to publish its specific list of which 10 neighborhood park restrooms are closing — that detail will help families plan park visits for the summer. The Parks Department website at coloradosprings.gov/parks is the best source for updates on facility availability. For trail and open space updates related to TOPS projects, visit coloradosprings.gov/TOPS2026. If park quality in your neighborhood is a priority, consider attending a City Council meeting or Parks advisory board meeting to make your voice heard — community feedback influences future budget decisions.
