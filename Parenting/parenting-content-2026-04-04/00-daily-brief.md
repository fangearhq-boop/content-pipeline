On April 2, 2026, the U.S. Consumer Product Safety Commission announced a recall of approximately 18,000 Sam's Club "Member's Mark Valentine SS Notch Collar" children's pajama sets. ### STORY 2: CPSC Urgent Warning — Fasando Baby Lounger Linked to Infant Death [TIER 1]
The CPSC has issued an urgent warning for Fasando brand baby loungers: stop using them immediately. A 2-month-old infant died after being placed to sleep in a Fasando baby lounger on an adult bed. The Chinese seller has not cooperated with a formal recall. Approximately 6,200 units were sold on Amazon between August 2024 and July 2025 for approximately $35. The CPSC urges parents to immediately stop use, remove the foam padding, cut the cover in half, and dispose — do not donate or resell.

**Why it matters:** This is a CPSC warning rather than a formal recall because the seller is uncooperative — which means the product may still be circulating on resale markets. Parents in Colorado Springs should check if they purchased a Fasando baby lounger from Amazon within the past 18 months and take immediate action.

### STORY 5: AAP Drops the '2-Hour Rule' — New Screen Time Policy Is a Big Shift [TIER 2]
In January 2026, the American Academy of Pediatrics published a landmark new policy statement in *Pediatrics* titled "Digital Ecosystems, Children, and Adolescents." The policy officially abandons the decade-old strict hourly screen time limit approach (the "no more than 2 hours/day" rule for ages 2–5) in favor of a nuanced framework called the "5 C's": Child (individual needs), Content (quality over quantity), Calming (are screens being used to soothe?), Crowding out (what healthy activity is being displaced?), and Communication (family conversation about media). The AAP now recommends a Family Media Plan rather than enforced time limits, while still maintaining device-free bedrooms and mealtimes.

**Why it matters:** Many COS parents have been guilt-counting screen time minutes under the old two-hour rule. This is official AAP guidance that changes the conversation entirely — from policing minutes to thinking about why, what, and when screens are used.
