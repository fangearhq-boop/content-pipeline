NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post ``` If you picked up children's pajamas from Sam's Club over the winter, take a minute to check the label. On April 2, the U.S. Consumer Product Safety Commission announced a recall of approximately 18,000 Sam's Club "Member's Mark Valentine SS Notch Collar" children's pajama sets. These are the short-sleeve button-up pajama tops with a front pocket — they came in pink solid, pink hearts, and cream bows prints, in sizes XS (4/5) through XL (14/16). The problem: they fail mandatory federal flammability standards for children's sleepwear, which means they create a burn risk that the law specifically requires sleepwear to prevent. They were sold on Samsclub.com between December 2025 and January 2026 for around $14. If you have them, stop using them now. Here's what to do: return them to any local Sam's Club for a full refund, or if you'd rather dispose of them, cut the pajamas in half and email a photo to Supersam@Samsclub.com for your refund. This recall is fresh — issued just two days ago — so there's a good chance these are still in rotation in a lot of families' pajama drawers. The holiday/Valentine color palette makes them recognizable. Check your kids' drawers tonight. Did you buy these? Share this post so other Sam's Club families can check too. ``` ### Image Post Caption #### Image Caption -- 10:00 AM MT ``` RECALL: Sam's Club Member's Mark children's pajama sets (Valentine-print, sizes 4/5–14/16) fail flammability standards. AAP Drops the '2-Hour Rule' — New Screen Time Policy Is a Big Shift [TIER 2]
NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post ``` If you've been counting iPad minutes and feeling guilty about your kids' screen time, the American Academy of Pediatrics has something to tell you: that approach is officially outdated. In January 2026, the AAP published a landmark new policy statement in the journal Pediatrics called "Digital Ecosystems, Children, and Adolescents." The headline change: they are moving away from strict hourly screen time limits (the old "no more than 2 hours per day for ages 2 to 5" rule) in favor of a much more nuanced framework. The new approach is built around the "5 C's": 📱 **Child** — your individual child's needs, temperament, and developmental stage 📺 **Content** — what are they actually watching or doing? 😴 **Calming** — are screens being used as a soothing tool? (The AAP wants families to be thoughtful about this) ⚽ **Crowding out** — is screen time displacing sleep, physical activity, or family connection? 💬 **Communication** — how does your family talk about and navigate media together? Instead of enforced time limits, the AAP now recommends a Family Media Plan — a household agreement that reflects your family's specific values, routines, and child's needs. Device-free bedrooms and mealtimes are still strongly recommended. Practically, this means: stop guilt-counting minutes and start asking better questions. Is what they're watching high quality? Is it displacing something important? Are we talking about it as a family? What does your family's screen time routine look like? Has this policy shift changed how you think about it? ``` ### Image Post Caption #### Image Caption -- 8:00 PM MT ``` AAP update (Jan 2026): the 2-hour screen time rule is out. New "Digital Ecosystems" policy introduces the 5 C's framework — focus on content quality and family culture, not clock-watching. ```
