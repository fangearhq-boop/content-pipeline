June in Colorado Springs means high temperatures, intense sunshine, and the kind of bright midday heat that the city's elevation makes especially powerful. It also means the return of one of the most preventable warm-weather dangers to children: hot cars.

A vehicle's interior temperature can rise approximately 19 degrees Fahrenheit in just 10 minutes — and this happens even on days when the outside air feels mild. On a warm June afternoon in Colorado Springs, a car left in direct sun can reach interior temperatures well above 120°F within 20 to 30 minutes. Children's bodies heat up three to five times faster than adult bodies, which means conditions that feel uncomfortable to an adult can become life-threatening for a child in a much shorter window of time.

Why Colorado Springs Presents a Particular Risk

Colorado Springs sits at 6,035 feet above sea level. At that altitude, the atmosphere is thinner and provides less filtering of solar radiation — meaning the sun's energy reaches surfaces (and vehicles) more intensely than at lower elevations. Families who moved to Colorado Springs from lower-altitude cities may underestimate how quickly conditions inside a parked car can become dangerous.

Heatstroke begins when a child's body temperature reaches 104°F. It becomes fatal at 107°F. At Colorado Springs temperatures and elevation levels during June, a child left in a parked vehicle can reach these thresholds faster than most parents would expect based on casual observation of the outdoor temperature.

The "Look Before You Lock" Habit

The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration promotes a simple technique called "Look Before You Lock." Before leaving any vehicle, look into the back seat — every single time, as a habit, regardless of whether you think a child is with you.

The technique exists because hot car incidents happen not only to negligent parents — they happen to attentive, caring parents and caregivers who simply forgot. A change in routine, a distracted phone call, an unusually quiet child who fell asleep in their car seat: these are the circumstances under which these tragedies occur. Building the habit of a back-seat check before exiting the car removes the reliance on memory in moments when attention is elsewhere.

Some families place an item — a shoe, a bag, a phone — in the back seat when their child is with them, so that retrieving it triggers a back-seat check on exit. Others set a phone reminder when heading out with children.

What to Do If You See a Child in a Hot Car

If you see a child alone in a vehicle — particularly on a warm, sunny day — call 911 immediately. Do not wait to see if someone returns. Do not assume the situation is fine because the child appears asleep.

If the child appears unresponsive or is showing signs of distress, and emergency services have been called but have not yet arrived, local law and common sense both support taking action to get the child out of the vehicle. Use whatever is available — a window-break tool, a rock — and move the child to a cooler location. Cool the child's skin with wet cloths or air conditioning and continue waiting for emergency responders.

In Colorado, it is not illegal to break into a car to rescue a child in danger. Bystander intervention has saved lives in documented hot car incidents. If the situation is urgent, act.

The American Academy of Pediatrics Position

The American Academy of Pediatrics is unequivocal: children should never be left unattended in a vehicle, even for a very brief errand. The organization does not carve out exceptions for quick stops, shade, slightly cooler days, or cracked windows. Cracked windows reduce interior temperature only marginally and do not eliminate the risk.

This guidance applies year-round in Colorado, where temperatures can spike unexpectedly even in spring and fall months — but it is most urgently relevant in June, July, and August, when Colorado Springs regularly sees highs in the 80s and the sun is at peak intensity.
