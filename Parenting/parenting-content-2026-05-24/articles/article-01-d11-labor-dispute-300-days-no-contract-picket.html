300 Days and No Contract: D11 Parents and Teachers Are Calling for a Picket This Month

By Sarah Morales | COS Parenting | May 24, 2026

The school year is ending, but for families in Colorado Springs School District 11, the labor dispute that dominated headlines last fall is far from over. More than 300 days after the D11 school board voted to eliminate its long-standing master agreement with the teachers union, the Colorado Springs Education Association is calling for a picket at an upcoming Board of Education meeting.

For parents tracking this situation, here's a clear recap of where things stand and what it means for D11 families.

How We Got Here

In December 2024, the District 11 Board of Education voted 6-1 to eliminate a master agreement that had been in place between the district and the Colorado Springs Education Association for 56 years. The board framed the move as a way to gain operational flexibility. The CSEA framed it as the board stripping teachers of collective bargaining rights that had governed the employment relationship for over five decades.

The dispute escalated in October 2025 when hundreds of D11 teachers went on strike. The strike drew significant community attention and eventually ended — but it did not produce a new contract. As of May 2026, the district and the CSEA remain without a new agreement.

The May 2026 Picket Call

On May 6, 2026, KRDO reported that educators and parents were organizing a picket at the D11 Board of Education meeting to mark what the CSEA described as more than 300 days since the master contract was severed. The message of the picket is straightforward: the board has not engaged in genuine collective bargaining since the December 2024 vote, and the CSEA wants the community to know it.

The significance of parents joining teachers at this kind of action is worth noting. These are not just union members showing up — these are families saying the labor instability at D11 affects their children's education.

What This Means for D11 Families

D11 is Colorado Springs' largest school district, serving tens of thousands of students across elementary, middle, and high schools. The ongoing dispute creates practical uncertainty: teachers navigating an unresolved employment situation, school administration operating without the structured framework the master agreement provided, and families wondering what the 2026-27 school year will look like.

There's no indication of another strike being planned for the fall — but without a new agreement, the conditions that produced last year's walkout remain in place.
