Two Amazon Toy Recalls Announced April 16: ATOYUS Activity Cubes and Sanlebi Pet Vet Playsets

By Sofia Reyes | COS Parenting | April 17, 2026

The U.S. Consumer Product Safety Commission announced two separate toy recalls on April 16, 2026 — both involving products sold through Amazon, and both posing serious risks to young children. If your family owns either an ATOYUS Children's Activity Cube or a Sanlebi Pet Vet Playset, stop using the product immediately and follow the refund steps below.

Recall 1: ATOYUS Children's Activity Cubes — Choking Hazard

ATOYUS Children's Activity Cubes were recalled because multiple components can detach and create a choking hazard for young children. The xylophone screws and clock hands can come loose, and the mallets that come with the cube have spherical ends that violate the federal small parts ban. These products are designed for children under three years old — the age group most at risk from small-part choking hazards.

The cubes were sold by Melofaver US through Amazon.com. The CPSC's recall notice does not specify how many units were sold, but the product appeared in Amazon's catalog in a category that typically reaches a broad audience of parents with toddlers.

How to Get a Refund — ATOYUS

To receive a refund for the ATOYUS Activity Cube, consumers must complete the following steps exactly as described by the CPSC:

  1. Write "recalled" on all sides of the activity cube.
  2. Cut the playmat that came with the cube in half.
  3. Take a photo showing the writing and the cut playmat.
  4. Email the photo to ATOYUSActivityCubeRecall@outlook.com with your purchase information.

These destruction steps are standard practice for toy recalls — they're designed to ensure that recalled units can't be resold or gifted to another child.

Recall 2: Sanlebi Pet Vet Playsets — Button Cell Battery Hazard

Sanlebi Pet Vet Playsets were recalled because young children can easily access the button cell batteries inside two of the playset's components: a nail grinder and an otoscope. Button cell batteries — the small, flat, coin-shaped batteries used in many toys and electronics — are one of the most dangerous ingestion hazards for children. When swallowed, they can cause severe chemical burns to the esophagus within two hours, leading to serious and sometimes fatal injuries.

The Sanlebi Pet Vet Playset was sold by Letokids through Amazon.com. The recall applies specifically to the versions containing the nail grinder and otoscope components with accessible battery compartments.

How to Get a Refund or Replacement — Sanlebi

For the Sanlebi Pet Vet Playset, consumers should:

  1. Remove the nail grinder and otoscope from the playset immediately.
  2. Remove and dispose of the batteries from both components.
  3. Take a photo of the removed components.
  4. Email the photo to letorecall@outlook.com for a $5 refund or replacement parts.

Note that unlike the ATOYUS recall, the Sanlebi recall does not require destroying the entire playset — only removing and disposing of the two components with accessible batteries.

A Note on Button Cell Battery Safety

Button cell batteries deserve specific attention because the hazard isn't always obvious. A battery that looks small and harmless can cause severe internal injuries within hours of being swallowed — and children often show no immediate symptoms, which can delay medical treatment. If you suspect a child has swallowed a button cell battery, go to the emergency room immediately without waiting to see whether symptoms develop.

The National Capital Poison Center maintains a 24-hour hotline at (800) 498-8666 for battery ingestion emergencies.

What's Next

If you own either of these products, stop using them now and follow the specific refund steps listed above. The CPSC's full recall notices for both products are available at cpsc.gov, where you can find additional product details and photographs. If you purchased either product as a gift for another family, let them know directly — Amazon order histories are searchable by date if you need to confirm what was purchased. For future toy purchases, the CPSC's recall database at recalls.gov is a useful resource for checking whether a product has been flagged before you buy.
