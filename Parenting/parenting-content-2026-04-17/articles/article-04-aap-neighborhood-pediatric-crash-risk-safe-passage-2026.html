New AAP Research: Where Your Child Lives Shapes Their Pedestrian Crash Risk

By Marta Kim | COS Parenting | April 17, 2026

A new research article published in Pediatrics — the journal of the American Academy of Pediatrics — is shifting the frame on how we think about keeping children safe near traffic. The article, titled "Safe Passage: How Neighborhoods Influence Pediatric Crash Risk," was published on April 16, 2026, and examines a question that parents often don't ask: does the neighborhood itself put children at risk, independent of how carefully the child behaves?

The short answer, according to the research, is yes.

What the Research Examines

The "Safe Passage" study takes a community-level approach to pediatric pedestrian safety. Rather than focusing on individual behavior — looking both ways, using crosswalks, avoiding distractions — the research examines the structural and environmental factors that create higher or lower crash risk for children depending on where they live.

The factors the research addresses include walkability (how well a neighborhood is designed for pedestrian use), traffic density and vehicle speed, infrastructure like crosswalks and traffic signals, street lighting, and the broader socioeconomic conditions of a neighborhood. The study finds that these factors interact to create meaningfully different risk environments for children — meaning a child in one part of a city faces a genuinely different level of danger than a child in another part of the same city, even if both children follow all the right safety rules.

Why This Matters Beyond Individual Behavior

Most pedestrian safety messaging directed at families is behavioral: teach your child to look both ways, use crosswalks, don't run into the street, put your phone away. That guidance is still sound and worth reinforcing. But the AAP-published research adds an important layer: individual behavior operates within a structural context that shapes risk in ways parents can't fully control by coaching their child.

A neighborhood with incomplete sidewalks, high-speed through-traffic, poor lighting, and infrequent crosswalks creates more pedestrian crash risk than a neighborhood with calm traffic, frequent crossings, and well-lit walking paths — regardless of how carefully children in both neighborhoods behave. This is a community infrastructure finding, not just a parenting one.

What This Means for Colorado Springs Families

Colorado Springs is a geographically diverse city, and pedestrian infrastructure varies considerably across its neighborhoods. Some parts of COS have well-developed sidewalk networks, school zone protections, and traffic-calmed residential streets. Others have gaps in sidewalk coverage, higher-speed arterial roads that cut through residential areas, and limited crossing infrastructure.

The "Safe Passage" research is a useful lens for thinking about where your child walks independently — not just to and from school, but to friends' houses, parks, transit stops, and anywhere else they're navigating on foot without direct supervision. The routes children take matter, and the safety of those routes isn't solely determined by the child's awareness.

For families, this can translate into practical decisions: choosing walking routes that prioritize sidewalks and marked crossings over shorter paths that cross busy arterials without infrastructure, and advocating at the neighborhood and district level for the kind of infrastructure improvements that reduce structural risk for children.

The Role of AAP Research in Parenting Conversations

The American Academy of Pediatrics publishes Pediatrics as its primary peer-reviewed journal. Research and commentary appearing there undergoes editorial review and carries significant weight in pediatric health and safety conversations. The "Safe Passage" article is a blog-format piece in the Pediatrics publication, which AAP uses for timely commentary on emerging research and policy-relevant topics.

This isn't the first time AAP research has pointed to community-level factors in child health outcomes — similar findings have emerged around air quality, food access, and green space. The pedestrian safety angle is a meaningful addition to that body of evidence.

The full "Safe Passage" article is available through aap.org and the Pediatrics journal website. For COS families, this research is a useful prompt to evaluate the specific routes your children are using when they're out on foot — and to consider whether those routes offer adequate infrastructure for safe pedestrian travel. Community-level pedestrian safety is also an issue that local governments and school districts respond to with input from parents and neighborhood groups, so families who have identified specific hazardous walking routes in their neighborhoods can raise those concerns with the City of Colorado Springs or with their school district's transportation department.
