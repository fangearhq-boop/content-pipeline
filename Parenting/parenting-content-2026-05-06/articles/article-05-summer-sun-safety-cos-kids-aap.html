Summer Sun Safety for Colorado Springs Kids: What the AAP Recommends at 6,000 Feet

By Jamie Rivera | May 6, 2026

Colorado Springs families spend a lot of time outdoors in summer — and at 6,000 feet, UV radiation hits harder than at sea level. Here's what the American Academy of Pediatrics recommends for protecting kids this season.

Colorado Springs summers are for being outside. Garden of the Gods in the morning, Cheyenne Mountain trails in the afternoon, and backyard evenings that stretch well past 8:00 PM. The outdoor lifestyle that draws families to COS is one of the city's greatest assets — but it comes with a UV consideration that families here need to take seriously.

At roughly 6,000 feet elevation, Colorado Springs receives measurably more ultraviolet radiation than cities at sea level. UV intensity increases approximately 4-5% for every 1,000 feet of elevation gain. That means what might feel like a mild afternoon in the shade at the Foro Italico, or in Tucson, is actually more intense sun exposure in Colorado Springs — even on days that feel cool or partly cloudy. The American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) provides clear, age-specific guidance for sun protection that every COS parent is worth knowing.

For Babies Under 6 Months: No Sunscreen

The AAP and FDA are aligned on this: sunscreen is not recommended for babies younger than 6 months. Their skin is more permeable and sensitive to sunscreen ingredients, and the potential for absorption of active ingredients is greater at this age. The recommendation instead is to keep infants under 6 months out of direct sunlight, use lightweight clothing that covers their arms and legs, and put them in a wide-brimmed hat that shades their face, ears, and neck.

If direct sun exposure cannot be avoided for a young infant, a small amount of zinc oxide or titanium dioxide sunscreen may be applied to limited exposed areas — but shade and clothing are the primary protection tools at this age.

For Children 6 Months and Older: Broad-Spectrum SPF 15-50

Once a child is 6 months old, sunscreen is appropriate and recommended. The AAP advises using a broad-spectrum product — meaning it protects against both UVA and UVB radiation — with a Sun Protection Factor (SPF) between 15 and 50. SPF above 50 offers only marginal additional protection while potentially increasing skin exposure to ingredient quantities that are higher than necessary.

Application matters as much as product choice. The AAP recommends applying about one ounce of sunscreen — roughly the amount that fills a shot glass — to all exposed skin. For most kids, that's more sunscreen than parents typically apply on a single outing. The difference between thorough coverage and a quick rub-on matters.

Reapplication should happen every two hours for standard sunscreen, and every 40 to 80 minutes after swimming or significant sweating. Water-resistant formulas help maintain protection during pool and splash pad visits, but no sunscreen is truly waterproof, and reapplication after water exposure is still necessary.

What to Look for (and Avoid) in Sunscreen

For families choosing a sunscreen for their kids, the AAP has a specific caution about one common ingredient: oxybenzone. This chemical UV filter has been shown to have mild hormonal properties in some studies, and the AAP recommends avoiding it in sunscreens used on children. When in doubt, look for products with zinc oxide or titanium dioxide as their active ingredients — these mineral sunscreens sit on top of the skin rather than absorbing into it, and they're well-tolerated by most children, including those with sensitive skin.

Beyond Sunscreen: The Multi-Layer Approach

Sunscreen is one layer of a multi-part sun safety strategy. The AAP recommends combining it with sun-protective clothing (UPF-rated gear is available for kids and is particularly useful for water activities), shade-seeking during peak UV hours, and timing adjustments for outdoor activities.

Peak UV intensity in Colorado Springs typically runs from 10:00 AM to 4:00 PM. Planning outdoor activities before 10:00 AM or after 4:00 PM reduces UV exposure without limiting time outside. This is especially practical for young children who are most vulnerable and least able to communicate when they're getting too much sun.

What's Next

Colorado Springs summer arrives fast, and the outdoor calendar fills up quickly — from park visits to hikes to pool days and outdoor festivals like MeadowGrass. Keeping a bottle of mineral-based broad-spectrum SPF 30 or 50 in your bag, reapplying consistently, and being thoughtful about midday sun exposure are all practical habits that don't require significant effort to implement. For more detail on age-specific sun protection guidance, the AAP's full recommendations are available at aap.org. At 6,000 feet with 300 days of sunshine, Colorado Springs is one of the most UV-intensive environments in the country — which is part of what makes it beautiful, and part of what makes sun protection non-negotiable for the whole family.
