The Research Is Clear: Your Kids Need More Outdoor Time This Spring

By Maria Chen | COS Parenting | April 10, 2026

Outdoor play isn't just a nice-to-have — research consistently shows it reduces anxiety, improves focus, and produces real physical and emotional health benefits for children. Colorado Springs families have exceptional access to make it happen.

Spring in Colorado Springs means something that's harder to find in most American cities: genuine, accessible wilderness right at the edge of a major urban area. Trails through the Garden of the Gods. Open meadows at Bear Creek. Towering red rocks at Palmer Park. Miles of paths at Fountain Creek Regional Park. Pikes Peak visible from almost anywhere you stand.

And research is increasingly clear that the ability to spend regular time outside — especially in green, natural settings — produces measurable benefits for children's mental health, focus, and emotional development that indoor environments simply cannot replicate.

What the Research Shows

The benefits are not vague or anecdotal. Children who spend regular time in outdoor, green environments show lower rates of anxiety and depression compared to children who primarily play indoors. Multiple studies have found reduced cortisol — the body's primary stress hormone — in children who spend time in natural settings.

Focus and attention also improve with outdoor time. Researchers have found that outdoor play tends to be more varied, self-directed, and unstructured than indoor play — and that variability has cognitive advantages. Children learn to regulate their emotions and behaviors through navigating constantly changing environments, and they carry those regulatory skills back inside with them.

Perhaps the most striking finding applies to children with ADHD. Research has found that children with ADHD who regularly play in green settings — parks, trails, wooded areas — show measurably milder ADHD symptoms compared to those who play primarily indoors or in built outdoor spaces like concrete playgrounds. The nature element specifically, not just the outdoor time, appears to matter.

Physical Benefits Add Up Too

The combination of physical activity and exposure to natural light increases serotonin production — a genuine mood regulator. Time outdoors in spring and summer also supports healthy Vitamin D levels in children, which plays a role in immune function, bone development, and mood.

Outdoor play tends to be more physically demanding and variable than screen time or indoor play. Climbing, running across uneven terrain, exploring creeks, and navigating rocks all engage gross motor skills in ways that are developmentally appropriate and genuinely challenging for kids across age groups.

COS Families Are Well Positioned

Colorado Springs residents live in an unusually fortunate situation when it comes to outdoor access. The city's parks system has more than 100 parks and open spaces, 200+ miles of trails, and several nature centers. Major green spaces like Garden of the Gods, Palmer Park, and Ute Valley Park are within easy driving distance of almost any neighborhood in the city.

Bear Creek Nature Center, run by El Paso County Parks, offers structured nature programs for children throughout spring — including programs this month designed specifically for toddlers, homeschool families, and deaf and hard-of-hearing children. (See our related story for April program details.)

The Pikes Peak region's elevation also makes spring particularly beautiful — wildflowers, wildlife activity, and clear views before the summer crowds arrive.

A Simple Place to Start

None of this requires a major expedition. Research suggests that even short, regular outdoor breaks — 20 to 30 minutes in a park or on a trail — produce measurable benefits over time. It doesn't need to be a destination hike. A neighborhood park works. A backyard works. The key is regularity and genuine outdoor exposure, not a perfectly curated outdoor adventure.

With spring underway in Colorado Springs and temperatures climbing into comfortable territory, the barrier to outdoor time is low. The case for prioritizing it is strong.
