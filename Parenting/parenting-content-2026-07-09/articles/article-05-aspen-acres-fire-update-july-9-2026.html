Aspen Acres Fire Reaches 96,031 Acres as Flash Flood Watch Goes Into Effect

By Jamie Rivera

July 9, 2026 · Colorado Springs

The Aspen Acres Fire has grown to 96,031 acres as of Wednesday, July 8, with 15% containment — a clear jump from the 93,916 acres reported in yesterday's update. The fire has now destroyed 275 total structures: 192 in Pueblo County and 83 in Custer County.

Today's update carries a new development that families in the region need to know about: a flash flood watch is in effect through Wednesday evening as monsoonal moisture arrives in southern Colorado.

Why the Rain Creates New Risks

The arrival of the monsoon is a double-edged development. Firefighters welcome the moisture, but the rain introduces serious new hazards for communities near the burn area.

When a wildfire burns through terrain, it can create what's known as hydrophobic soil — ground that has been heated to the point where it actively repels water rather than absorbing it. Rain that would normally soak in instead runs off quickly across the surface, and it picks up ash, debris, and loose soil as it goes. The result is fast-moving flash flooding that can travel far from the original fire footprint.

The flash flood watch through Wednesday evening is a direct response to this risk. On top of the flooding concern, gusty outflow winds from the incoming storm system could reach up to 50 mph, which can shift fire behavior rapidly even as overall moisture increases.

If you're in or near the burn scar or downstream of fire-affected drainages, treat tonight's flash flood watch as seriously as the fire itself. Flooding on burned terrain can happen with very little warning and very little time to react.

Evacuation and Re-Entry Status

There's been a meaningful change in Fremont County. The communities of Rockvale, Coal Creek, and Williamsburg — which were previously under mandatory evacuation orders — have been downgraded to pre-evacuation status. This means residents in those areas are no longer required to leave but should remain prepared to do so quickly if conditions change.

Pre-evacuation status is not an all-clear. It means evacuation is no longer immediate but remains a real possibility. Families in those communities should keep go-bags ready, monitor local emergency management communications, and know their routes out.

The Disaster Assistance Center is now issuing re-entry permits for some areas where conditions allow residents to return. If you've been displaced and believe you may be eligible for re-entry, contact the DAC directly through Pueblo County or Custer County emergency management for current permit information.

How to Stay Informed

Fire and evacuation status can change quickly, especially with weather systems moving through. The most reliable places to track current conditions:

Pueblo County Emergency Management and Custer County Emergency Management both post current evacuation orders and status changes on their official channels. The Colorado Division of Fire Prevention and Control maintains incident information at inciweb.nwcg.gov — search for Aspen Acres Fire.

For weather-related hazards, the National Weather Service office in Pueblo covers southern Colorado and posts flash flood watch updates and alerts. Sign up for Wireless Emergency Alerts on your phone to receive automatic notifications if a flash flood warning is issued in your area.

What's Next

With monsoon moisture now arriving, conditions on and around the fire will continue to evolve in the coming days. Rain can accelerate containment efforts, but the flash flood risk on fire-scarred terrain will remain a concern throughout the monsoon season — not just tonight. Residents in affected areas should plan for an extended period of weather-related hazards even as the fire itself comes under greater control.

We'll continue to provide updates as significant changes occur. If you have family or friends near the fire or in the flash flood watch zone, please share this information with them.
