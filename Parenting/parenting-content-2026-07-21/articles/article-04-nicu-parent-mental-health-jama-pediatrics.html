Study Confirms Mental Health Support Works for NICU Parents — and Tells Us What Works Best

By Sarah Morales | COS Parenting

July 21, 2026 — Colorado Springs, Colorado

When a baby is admitted to the NICU, the focus understandably goes to the infant. But a major new meta-analysis published in JAMA Pediatrics confirms what many NICU families already know from experience: the mental health of parents during that stay matters enormously, and the interventions designed to help actually work.

The study, published July 6, 2026, and led by Claire E. Niehaus Milligan, PhD, of the Heart Mind Wellbeing Center at Cincinnati Children's Hospital Medical Center, reviewed 160 studies involving 16,639 parents. It is one of the largest analyses of NICU parental mental health interventions ever conducted.

What the Research Found

Researchers grouped the interventions they studied into six categories: bonding approaches, expressive and creative therapies, psychotherapy and emotional support, family-centered care and education, meditation and holistic approaches, and spiritual interventions.

Across categories, the effect sizes ranged from large to extremely large. That's an unusually strong signal in mental health research, where modest effects are more common. Three categories stood out as having the most consistent evidence of reducing parental stress and anxiety during a NICU stay: psychotherapy and emotional support, family-centered care and education, and meditation and holistic approaches.

Psychotherapy showed the most consistent pattern of benefit across all outcomes measured in the study, including depression, anxiety, and parenting stress. Family-centered care — the approach in which parents are treated as active participants in their child's NICU care rather than visitors — also showed significant effects. So did meditation-based interventions.

The study included 85 of the 160 reviewed studies in its formal meta-analysis. The mean age of parents in the research was 30.2 years, and 93.5% of participants were women — a reflection of who most often participates in parenting studies, not a claim about who experiences NICU stress.

What This Means for NICU Families

For any family currently in a NICU — including families at Children's Hospital Colorado at Memorial Central or UCHealth Memorial Hospital in Colorado Springs — the practical takeaway is direct: ask your care team what mental health support is available to you. It's not a luxury or an afterthought. This research puts support in the same category as medical care during a NICU stay.

If your NICU stay is in the past and you're still carrying the weight of it, that's also something the research speaks to. Parental stress from NICU hospitalization doesn't automatically resolve when the baby comes home. If it hasn't resolved for you, therapy and support groups designed specifically for NICU graduates and their families exist, and they work.

Local resources in the Colorado Springs area include behavioral health services through UCHealth, Centura (Penrose), and community mental health providers. NICU parent peer support groups may be available through the hospitals where COS families deliver or through national organizations like Hand to Hold (handtohold.org) or the March of Dimes.

What's Next

The JAMA Pediatrics study is a meta-analysis, meaning it synthesizes existing research rather than presenting new clinical trials. Its value is in the breadth of the evidence base it assembled — 160 studies across different settings and intervention types — and the strength of the signal it found. Future research will likely focus on which specific approaches work best for which parents and at which points in the NICU journey.

For COS families, the immediate next step is knowing that support is available and that asking for it is both appropriate and likely to help. The full study abstract is available at jamanetwork.com for those who want to read the research directly.
