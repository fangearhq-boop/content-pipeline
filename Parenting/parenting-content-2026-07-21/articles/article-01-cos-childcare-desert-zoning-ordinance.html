Colorado Springs Approves Zoning Fix for Childcare Desert

By Jamie Rivera | COS Parenting

July 21, 2026 — Colorado Springs, Colorado

El Paso County has 47,000 children under the age of five. It has 18,400 licensed childcare spots. That gap — 28,600 missing seats — is what city officials and advocates have been calling a childcare desert, and it's been a defining frustration for Colorado Springs families trying to piece together reliable care.

This month, the Colorado Springs City Council approved a zoning ordinance designed to address the root cause. For years, the city's Unified Development Code prevented individuals from opening new childcare facilities in homes, churches, or businesses where care would serve a secondary purpose. The new ordinance changes that. Home-based childcare, church-based care, and employer-provided facilities are now permitted uses in residential zones.

What the Ordinance Changes

Before this ordinance passed, opening a home daycare or starting childcare in a church fellowship hall required navigating a zoning process that treated childcare as an exception rather than a permitted activity. That friction discouraged providers from trying, particularly in neighborhoods that already had few options.

Under the revised code, those barriers are removed. A provider who wants to open a home-based center in a residential zone can now do so as a permitted use. A church that wants to offer childcare during the week can proceed without a special zoning approval. An employer who wants to set up on-site care for employees can do the same.

The change doesn't mandate that new spots open. It removes the legal obstacle that prevented them. Whether providers respond to that opening is the next chapter.

Where the Need Is Greatest

Colorado Springs is not uniformly short on childcare — the shortage is concentrated. Advocates for the change have consistently pointed to the southeast quadrant as the area with the fewest available spots relative to the children who live there. Families on the east and southeast sides of the city have had the fewest options and the longest waitlists.

That geographic reality matters for what comes next. If the ordinance change prompts new providers to open, the question becomes whether they open where families need them most, or in neighborhoods that are already better served. The policy doesn't control for that. Advocacy groups and city staff will likely track where new providers emerge in the coming months.

What Families Should Know Now

The ordinance is approved, but new childcare spots don't appear overnight. Providers still need to obtain appropriate licensing through the Colorado Office of Early Childhood, meet safety requirements, and secure physical space. That process typically takes months.

For families currently on waitlists or trying to patch together care from week to week, the practical relief won't be immediate. What's changed is the environment for providers who want to open. The zoning wall is down. The licensing and setup process is still a real hurdle, but it's one that motivated providers can work through.

If you're interested in becoming a home childcare provider in Colorado Springs, the Colorado Office of Early Childhood (coloradooec.gov) is the starting point for licensing information. The City of Colorado Springs childcare ordinance update page at coloradosprings.gov/ChildcareOrdinanceUpdate has additional detail on the zoning changes.

What's Next

The ordinance is now in effect. The work of closing a 28,600-spot gap doesn't end with a zoning change — it continues with providers deciding to open, families finding and affording those spots, and city and county programs supporting the infrastructure that makes care financially viable. Colorado Shines, the state's childcare quality rating system, and state subsidy programs through Early Achievers are part of the ecosystem that determines whether new spots are accessible to families across income levels.

We'll continue following where new childcare providers open in COS and how the landscape shifts for families in the southeast quadrant and across the city in the months ahead.
