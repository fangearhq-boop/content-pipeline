### STORY 1: COS Childcare Desert — Zoning Ordinance Approved

**Long-Form Post (8:00 AM MT)**

If you've ever tried to find childcare in Colorado Springs and come up empty, there's a reason for that — and it has a name. A childcare desert is what happens when the number of kids who need care far outstrips the number of licensed spots available. In El Paso County, that gap is 28,600 spots wide. The county has 47,000 children under the age of five and only 18,400 licensed childcare spots to go around. 🏫

The Colorado Springs City Council has approved a zoning ordinance that changes how childcare facilities can operate in the city. Under the previous rules, opening a childcare center in a home, church, or business where it served a secondary purpose wasn't allowed. The new ordinance makes home-based care, church-based care, and employer-provided childcare permitted uses in residential zones. That removes the biggest policy-level barrier that's kept new spots from opening.

The southeast quadrant of Colorado Springs has the fewest available spots relative to the children who live there. If you're on the east side or the southeast side, you've already felt this firsthand. The ordinance won't immediately fill 28,600 spots — that's not how zoning works — but it removes the legal wall that was blocking providers from opening where families need them most.

This is a real step. We'll keep watching to see how many new providers take advantage of the change and where they set up.

What's your experience been finding childcare in COS? Drop a comment — we're curious whether families have found it harder in certain parts of the city.

### STORY 4: NICU Parent Mental Health — JAMA Pediatrics Study

**Long-Form Post (2:00 PM MT)**

If your family spent time in the NICU, you already know what the research has now confirmed: it's one of the hardest things a parent can go through. 💙

A meta-analysis published in JAMA Pediatrics on July 6, 2026 pulled together 160 studies involving 16,639 parents to look at what kinds of support actually help during a NICU stay. The findings are clear: psychotherapy and emotional support, family-centered care and education, and meditation and holistic approaches all produced significant reductions in parental stress and anxiety. Psychotherapy showed the most consistent pattern of benefit across all mental health outcomes. Effect sizes across the study ranged from large to extremely large — which means these weren't marginal benefits.

The study was led by Claire E. Niehaus Milligan, PhD, of the Heart Mind Wellbeing Center at Cincinnati Children's Hospital Medical Center. The six categories of intervention included bonding approaches, expressive and creative therapies, psychotherapy, family-centered care, meditation and holistic approaches, and spiritual interventions.

What this means practically: if you're in a NICU right now, or you know someone who is, asking the care team about mental health support isn't optional — it's part of the care. And if your NICU stay is in the past and the stress stayed with you after discharge, that's worth naming and treating too.
