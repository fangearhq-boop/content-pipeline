D11 Raises Starting Pay After Month-Long Negotiations: What It Means for COS Families

By Sarah Morales | June 2, 2026 | COS Parenting

Colorado Springs School District 11 has ratified a new pay agreement that raises starting teacher and staff compensation to approximately $50,000 — a roughly 20% increase that gives D11 the highest starting salary among the region's three major school districts. The agreement follows a difficult stretch that included a staff picket and month-long negotiations, and it comes as the district heads into the 2026-27 school year.

What the New Agreement Includes

The ratified agreement raises D11's starting pay to approximately $50,000 per year. By comparison, D20 starts around $49,000 and D49 around $47,000, according to regional comparisons reported by KOAA. D11's new figure represents the highest starting salary in the Pikes Peak region under those comparisons.

The roughly 20% increase reflects movement from a lower base — one of the persistent criticisms D11 staff raised during negotiations. Staff voted to ratify the agreement after the month-long process concluded.

How the Negotiations Got Here

The context matters. In 2025, the D11 board voted to eliminate the Classified School Employees Association collective bargaining agreement — a decision that removed the formal negotiating structure that had governed staff pay discussions. That move drew significant criticism from D11 employees and set the stage for the extended negotiations that followed.

Staff responded with a picket. The negotiations that produced this new agreement ran approximately one month before both sides reached a ratifiable result.

Even with the agreement in hand, some D11 employees told KOAA that the raise isn't enough. That sentiment reflects a broader tension: compensation increases that bring a district to the regional top don't necessarily close the gap between what teachers and staff feel they're worth and what school budgets can deliver.

Why This Matters for D11 Families

Teacher compensation and morale have a direct effect on what happens in classrooms. Districts that struggle to attract and retain experienced educators face higher turnover, and families with kids in those schools feel the consequences — new teachers each year, gaps in program continuity, and staff who are distracted by financial stress.

D11 serves a large portion of Colorado Springs families, and the district has faced documented staffing challenges in recent years. Whether this agreement stabilizes D11's workforce going into 2026-27 is something that will become clearer over the summer as hiring decisions are made and fall enrollment settles.

The fact that staff ratified the agreement — while also saying publicly it isn't enough — suggests a pragmatic acceptance rather than enthusiasm. That's worth keeping in mind as D11 families evaluate what the start of next school year looks like for their kids' schools.

What's Next

D11 families can follow budget and staffing updates through the district's official communications at d11.org. As the 2026-27 school year approaches, watching for information about staffing levels and any remaining open positions at your child's school will give you the most accurate picture of what this agreement means on the ground.
