### STORY 1: Build-A-Bear Heartwarming Hugs Bear Recall

**Long-Form Post (8:00 AM MT)**

If you or your kids received a Build-A-Bear Heartwarming Hugs Bear this year, you'll want to stop using it and return it now.

The CPSC has officially recalled the Heartwarming Hugs Bear (model 034464) — a weighted plush bear with a side pouch holding about 2.5 pounds of ceramic beads that can be heated or cooled for comfort. The problem: the zipper slider on that side pouch can detach. If a young child gets hold of it, it's a choking hazard. The agency is treating this as a risk of serious injury or death.

About 36,000 of these bears were sold in the US from January through March 2026 at Build-A-Bear Workshop stores and on buildabear.com — at around $48. The recall was announced April 30, 2026.

The fix is simple: stop using the bear and return it to any Build-A-Bear Workshop store. The company will refund you in your original form of payment or with a gift card for the purchase price, redeemable on any merchandise. You can also call Build-A-Bear at 844-541-0144 (Mon–Sat 8 a.m.–5 p.m. CT; Sun 7 a.m.–4:30 p.m. CT) or visit buildabear.com/recalls for details.

Do your kids have one of these? Let other COS families know in the comments. ⚠️

### STORY 2: AAP New Screen Time Guidelines 2026

**Long-Form Post (10:00 AM MT)**

If you've been counting screen minutes and feeling guilty about it, the American Academy of Pediatrics just gave you permission to stop clock-watching.

In January 2026, the AAP released updated digital media guidelines that officially moved away from the old strict daily time limits — like "no more than one hour per day" — and shifted toward a new framework. Their reasoning: what kids are watching matters more than how long, and what screens are displacing (like sleep or family time) matters more than any raw number.

The new framework is built around five questions, which the AAP calls the "5 Cs":
• Child — What's this child's age, temperament, and current needs?
• Content — Is what they're watching or playing high-quality and age-appropriate?
• Calm — Does screen time help them relax, or is it overstimulating?
• Crowding out — Is it replacing sleep, physical activity, or real-world connection?
• Communication — Are you talking about what they're doing online?

A few things didn't change: under-18-month-olds should still have no screens except video calls. Device-free bedrooms and mealtimes are still recommended. And turning screens off at least an hour before bed remains solid guidance.

The shift is meaningful. Instead of a guilt-producing number, you're asking whether screen time is fitting into your family's life well — and if it's getting in the way of the things that matter most.

### STORY 5: SDADI Kitchen Step Stool Recall

**Long-Form Post (4:00 PM MT)**

If you bought a SDADI kitchen tower step stool in the last year or two, stop using it and move it away from your kids.

The CPSC issued a formal recall on July 16 — just four days ago — for SDADI kitchen helper tower stools, models LT01 and LT05, imported by Yiwushi Bihe Trading. The problem: the stools can collapse or tip over while being used, and the openings on the front and back sides are large enough for a child's torso to pass through. That's an entrapment, fall, and tip-over hazard — the CPSC rates the risk as potential serious injury or death.

About 5,952 of these were sold from April 2024 through September 2025 on Amazon, Walmart.com, Target.com, and BestBuy.com. They came in gray, white, espresso, pink, and natural wood finishes at prices ranging from $45 to $155. The CPSC received 8 reports of instability or tip-overs; 4 of those resulted in injuries including scrapes, cuts, and bruises.

To get a refund: stop using the stool, store it somewhere your kids can't get to it, and email john@SDADI.net. You'll receive a full refund for the purchase price.

Worth double-checking if you have step stools from Amazon in your home — this brand was widely sold and may not have come with much packaging that would make the brand name obvious.
