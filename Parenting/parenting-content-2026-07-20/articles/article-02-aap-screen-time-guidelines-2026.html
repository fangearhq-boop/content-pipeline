The AAP Just Changed Its Screen Time Guidance. Here's What's Actually Different.

By Sarah Morales | July 20, 2026

National Parenting

If you've spent years feeling like you're losing the battle against screen time — counting minutes, setting timers, arguing about one more episode — the American Academy of Pediatrics has some news that might actually help.

In January 2026, the AAP released updated digital media guidelines that officially dropped the strict daily time limits that have shaped parenting conversations for years. No more "no more than one hour per day" as a hard rule. Instead, the organization is asking parents to think differently about what screens are actually doing in their children's lives.

Why the Change

The AAP's reasoning is straightforward: the old time-limit approach was too blunt. Research increasingly shows that what kids watch matters more than how many minutes they spend watching it. A child doing an interactive creative activity online for 90 minutes isn't in the same situation as a child passively consuming low-quality video for 30. Duration alone was never the right metric.

The new guidance, announced January 20, 2026, encourages parents to focus on context instead of clocks — and gives them a practical framework for doing that.

The 5 Cs Framework

The AAP's new framework is built around five questions, which the organization calls the "5 Cs." Each one is designed to help parents evaluate whether screen time is working for their child and family in a given moment.

  • Child — What's your child's age, temperament, and current state? A tired four-year-old and a rested nine-year-old aren't having the same experience with the same show.
  • Content — Is what they're watching or playing high-quality and age-appropriate? Are they learning something, using creativity, or passively absorbing?
  • Calm — Does screen time help them regulate and decompress, or does it wind them up? The effect on their mood afterward matters.
  • Crowding out — Is screen time displacing sleep, physical activity, outdoor time, or real-world connection? That displacement is where harm tends to occur.
  • Communication — Are you maintaining an ongoing conversation with your kids about what they're doing online, who they're interacting with, and what they're watching?

What Didn't Change

The 5 Cs framework gives parents more flexibility, but a few rules stayed firm. Children under 18 months should still have no screen time except video calls. Families are still encouraged to keep bedrooms and mealtimes device-free. And the AAP still recommends turning screens off at least one hour before bed — the sleep-disruption link is well-established and isn't going away.

For kids under five, the guidance still encourages parents to co-view content and watch alongside their children, since young kids learn significantly better with an engaged adult present than from a screen alone.

What This Means in Practice

The shift isn't permission to put screens on indefinitely. It's an invitation to stop managing parenting by a number and start paying attention to outcomes. Is your child sleeping well? Are they staying active? Do they still choose face-to-face activities when given the option? Is their device use bringing them closer to people or pulling them away?

Those questions are harder than checking a clock — but they're also more useful.

What's Next

The full updated guidance is available at aap.org. If you want a starting point for applying the 5 Cs with your family, the AAP also publishes a Family Media Plan tool at healthychildren.org that lets you set customized agreements based on your child's age and your family's priorities — and has been updated to reflect the 2026 framework.
