AAP 2026 Vaccine Schedule: What Changed, and Why It's Now Separate From the CDC's

By COS Parenting | March 29, 2026

If you have a well-child visit coming up in the next few months, there are some things worth knowing about the 2026 childhood immunization landscape — including a significant and public disagreement between two of the country's most authoritative voices on pediatric health.

The American Academy of Pediatrics released its 2026 Recommended Childhood and Adolescent Immunization Schedule earlier this month. For the first time, the AAP schedule is formally separate from the federal CDC schedule. The AAP schedule is endorsed by 12 major medical organizations, including the American Medical Association and the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists.

What's New in the 2026 AAP Schedule

Several updates in the 2026 schedule are directly relevant to families with infants and young children in Colorado Springs:

Update Who It Affects Key Detail
Clesrovimab (RSV prevention) Infants New monoclonal antibody option; dosing guidance depends on whether the mother received an RSV vaccine during pregnancy
HPV vaccination Children ages 9-12 Recommended starting at age 9; earlier start is clinically equivalent and may improve uptake
COVID-19 vaccination Children ages 6-23 months Universally recommended for this age group in the 2026 schedule

If your child was born in late 2024 or 2025 and you're navigating the infant vaccine schedule, ask your pediatrician specifically about clesrovimab. The guidance on RSV prevention has evolved as more data has become available about maternal RSV vaccination, and the 2026 update reflects that.

The CDC Controversy: What Parents Should Know

The reason the AAP schedule is now formally separate from the CDC's comes down to a policy disagreement with real implications for how vaccines are presented to families.

The CDC announced plans to reclassify childhood vaccines into three new categories: vaccines recommended for all children; vaccines recommended only for high-risk children; and vaccines placed in a "shared clinical decision-making" category, where the recommendation is neither clearly for nor against. This represents a significant departure from how the vaccine schedule has worked for decades, where recommendations go through the CDC's Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices and are typically binary — recommended or not recommended.

The AAP publicly called this plan "dangerous and unnecessary" and stated that it "upends the longstanding expert review process" that has produced some of the safest and most effective public health tools in history. The concern is that reclassifying vaccines into a "shared decision-making" category reduces the clarity of public health guidance and could lead to decreased uptake of important vaccines — including among children who are at elevated risk and need them most.

What This Means for COS Families

For most families in Colorado Springs, the most practical takeaway is this: ask your pediatrician which schedule their practice follows. Most pediatric practices in Colorado follow the AAP's clinical guidelines — but with the CDC and AAP now diverging formally, it's worth asking directly. Your pediatrician can clarify which recommendations they're applying and explain any specific changes that affect your child's age group.

If your child is behind on vaccines for any reason — illness, schedule gaps, or hesitancy — this is a good moment to have that conversation without the framing of controversy. The AAP's schedule represents the consensus of 12 major medical organizations based on the available clinical evidence.

What's Next

The AAP schedule is published at aap.org/en/policy and is updated annually. Your child's pediatric practice should have the 2026 version available. If you have specific questions about the RSV prevention update or the COVID-19 recommendation for young children, the AAP's HealthyChildren.org resource is written directly for parents and covers both topics in plain language.

The CDC-AAP disagreement is likely to continue through 2026 as vaccine policy evolves at the federal level. The AAP has committed to maintaining its own evidence-based schedule regardless of federal policy changes, which means COS families have a consistent clinical reference point in their pediatricians' offices.
