COS Parks 2026: What's Changing for Families This Spring

By COS Parenting | March 29, 2026

With spring arriving and the city's parks and open spaces back in full swing, Colorado Springs Parks, Recreation and Cultural Services has released its priorities for 2026. Several updates are directly relevant to families — including a new neighborhood park, upgrades at a Cheyenne Canyon favorite, and an opportunity to shape the city's park system for the next decade.

One important note first: Burn Restriction Order 2026-R1 is currently in effect across Colorado Springs. All outdoor burning is prohibited — including campfires and charcoal grills. Parks are fully open for hiking, picnicking, wildlife watching, and outdoor recreation, but families planning a cookout should hold off until the restriction is lifted.

Stratton Neighborhood Park: Could Open This Year

Colorado Springs Parks, Recreation and Cultural Services is working with the surrounding community in 2026 to develop a Master and Management Plan for Stratton Neighborhood Park. The goal is to open the site to the public in 2026 — making it a new outdoor option for families in the area.

The community planning process is currently underway. If you live in the Stratton neighborhood or near the park's planned location, watch the city's parks page (coloradosprings.gov/parks-trails-open-space) for meeting dates and public comment opportunities. These planning processes are most useful when the surrounding community is actively engaged — your input on what the park should include can make a genuine difference.

Starsmore Visitor Center: Accessibility Upgrades in 2026

The Starsmore Discovery Center in Lower Cheyenne Canyon is one of the most family-friendly natural attractions in Colorado Springs — a free (or donation-based) facility with natural history exhibits, wildlife discovery, and access to the canyon trail system. In 2026, the city is completing accessibility upgrades and expanding programming space at the facility.

For families with children who use mobility aids or strollers, or for families with members who benefit from enhanced accessibility, these upgrades should make Starsmore more welcoming. The expanded programming space may also enable more educational programs for school groups and community events. The center is worth adding to your spring outdoor rotation if you haven't been recently.

Park System Master Plan: Your Input Counts

Colorado Springs is developing a new 10-to-15 year Park System Master Plan that will guide decisions about parks, trails, open spaces, community centers, and recreation programming through the late 2030s. The current plan dates from 2014, and the city is in the visioning and strategic planning phase of its replacement.

A draft plan is expected to be presented to the community in spring 2026 — which means community input is being gathered right now. This is one of those planning processes where parent voices can directly shape outcomes that affect your family for the next decade: trail maintenance priorities, playground locations and standards, programming at community centers, accessibility improvements, and more.

The city's official page for the master plan is at coloradosprings.gov/ParkSystemMasterPlan. Information on how to provide input and upcoming community meetings is posted there.

What's Next

The full TOPS (Trails, Open Space, Parks) 2026 priority list is available at coloradosprings.gov/TOPS2026. Earlier this week, the city confirmed trail improvements on the Pikes Peak Greenway corridor and a pedestrian bridge connection on the Rock Island Trail — both projects that will benefit families who use COS trail networks for outdoor recreation.

As fire conditions ease this spring, outdoor opportunities in Colorado Springs will expand. Families who want to get outside now can hike, bike, and visit open spaces without restriction — just without lighting fires. Check the city's fire restriction status regularly at coloradosprings.gov before planning any outdoor activities that involve a flame.
