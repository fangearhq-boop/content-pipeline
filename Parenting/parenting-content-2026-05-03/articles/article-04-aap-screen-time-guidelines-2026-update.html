AAP Changes Screen Time Guidelines for 2026: What COS Parents Need to Know

By Marta Kim | COS Parenting | May 3, 2026

The American Academy of Pediatrics updated its screen time recommendations in early 2026, making a significant shift in how pediatricians think about children and digital media. The headline change: for kids ages six and older, the AAP has dropped the strict "no more than two hours per day" rule that had been the standard since 2016.

That doesn't mean screens are now fine without limit. What it means is that pediatricians are acknowledging something most parents already know: not all screen time is the same thing.

What Changed — and What Didn't

Age Group Old Guidance (2016) New Guidance (2026)
Under 18 months No screens No screens (unchanged)
Ages 2–5 1 hour/day of high-quality content 1 hour/day of high-quality content (unchanged)
Ages 6 and older No more than 2 hours/day No strict time limit — focus on quality, context, and habits

The "5 Cs" Framework

The new AAP approach centers on a framework called the "5 Cs," designed to help parents evaluate screen time beyond minutes counted. The framework shifts the question from "how long?" to "how is this being used, and what is it replacing?"

The 5 Cs focus on: the child's individual development and temperament; the content being consumed (educational, passive, interactive, or mindless); the context in which screens are being used; the absence of co-use when a parent isn't present; and the child's overall balance of activity including sleep, physical play, and family time.

Practically, the AAP is saying that a 10-year-old who builds things online, learns an instrument through video tutorials, or connects with distant family members via video call is using screen time very differently than one who is scrolling social media without any context or conversation.

What the AAP Still Recommends

Several recommendations carry over strongly from the 2016 guidelines and are now framed as family habits rather than hard rules. Device-free bedrooms and mealtimes are emphasized — these two habits protect sleep and family connection in ways that have consistent research support. Co-viewing and talking about what children are watching is described as the gold standard, particularly for younger children. And parents modeling good digital habits is a recurring theme, because kids watch what adults do at least as closely as they listen to what adults say.

The AAP also maintains that screens should not displace sleep, physical activity, free play, or face-to-face family time. Those are the real competitors for children's time and development, and if screen time is crowding them out, that's worth addressing regardless of the hour count.

What This Means for COS Families

For families in Colorado Springs, where outdoor options are plentiful year-round, the new framework reinforces what many parents here already practice: the goal is balance, not an arbitrary number. A summer afternoon on a trail at Garden of the Gods or a morning at a COS park counts for a lot. So does intentional, co-viewed screen time that starts a conversation.

The practical upshot: if your eight-year-old is over the two-hour mark on a rainy Saturday but spent the morning hiking and the evening at the dinner table without a device, you don't need to panic. But if screens have quietly replaced sleep, family meals, or outdoor time, that's worth looking at — not because of a rule, but because of what your child is and isn't getting.

What's Next

The full AAP policy is available at HealthyChildren.org and aap.org. Families who want a guided approach to the 5 Cs framework or digital media planning can use the AAP's Family Media Plan tool, which allows families to set customized goals by child and age group. If you have questions about screen time as it relates to your child's specific development, a conversation with your pediatrician is the best next step.
