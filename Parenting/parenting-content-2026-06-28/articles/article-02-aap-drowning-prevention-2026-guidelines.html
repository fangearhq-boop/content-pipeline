AAP 2026 Drowning Prevention Guidelines: What COS Families Need to Know This Summer

By Jamie Rivera | June 28, 2026

Drowning is the leading cause of death for children ages 1 through 4 in the United States. After years of progress — drowning rates fell steadily from 1999 through 2019 — those rates have been rising again. Racial, ethnic, and geographic disparities in drowning fatalities have also widened in recent years.

Against that backdrop, the American Academy of Pediatrics published updated drowning prevention guidelines in the June 2026 issue of Pediatrics. The updated policy statement carries a clear central message: no single precaution prevents all drowning deaths. Families need a layered approach.

With COS neighborhood pools now open and peak summer swim season underway, here's what the AAP's 2026 guidelines say — and what they mean for Colorado Springs families specifically.

The Layered Approach

The AAP frames drowning prevention as a system of overlapping protections, not a checklist you complete once. Each layer reduces risk. Together, they create meaningful safety for children in and around water.

AAP 2026 Drowning Prevention Layers
Layer What It Means
Touch supervision Stay within arm's length whenever a child is in or near water — pool, bathtub, or splash area
Swim lessons Can begin after a child turns 1 year old; reduces drowning risk in young children
Four-sided pool fencing Surrounds pool on all four sides with a self-closing, self-latching gate; one of the most effective structural interventions
Life jackets Coast Guard-approved life jackets for open water — lakes, rivers, and reservoirs
Adult CPR training Knowing CPR can be lifesaving in the critical seconds before emergency responders arrive

Touch Supervision: What It Actually Means

The AAP uses the phrase "touch supervision" deliberately. It means a designated adult — not distracted by a phone or conversation — stays within arm's length of a young child at all times around water. That applies to bathtubs, not just pools. It also applies to any standing water: buckets, fountains, irrigation ditches.

The supervision rule doesn't diminish once a child knows how to swim. Children who have completed swim lessons still need active supervision. Lessons reduce risk; they don't eliminate it.

When Can Swim Lessons Start?

The AAP updated its guidance on swim lesson timing in recent years and its 2026 statement maintains that swim lessons can begin after a child turns 1 year old. Earlier versions of AAP guidance were more cautious about recommending lessons before age 4. The current position reflects research showing that swim instruction beginning after age 1 can reduce drowning risk in the 1–4 age group.

For COS families, the YMCA of the Pikes Peak Region offers swim lessons for a range of ages and skill levels this summer.

Children With Elevated Risk

The 2026 AAP statement specifically calls attention to children with epilepsy, autism spectrum disorder, and other conditions that may increase drowning risk. The AAP encourages pediatricians to raise water safety during well-child visits for these families and help develop targeted precautions.

If your child has a condition your pediatrician has connected to water safety concerns, ask about individualized guidance at your next visit.

A Note for COS Families: Rosemont Reservoir

Rosemont Reservoir, which is opening for the season, is open for non-motorized boating only. Swimming and wading are not permitted. Families visiting the reservoir should plan accordingly — life jackets remain appropriate for any boating activity, regardless of the setting.

What's Next

The single highest-impact action most COS families can take right now is enrolling children who haven't started swim lessons. The YMCA of the Pikes Peak Region has programs available throughout the summer. Check their current schedule for availability and registration.

Beyond that, the AAP's layered approach is something families can build out over time: add the second supervision layer, evaluate your pool fence if you have one, take a CPR refresher if it's been a few years. None of these steps requires a single large commitment — each one independently reduces risk while you work toward the full picture.

The full updated policy statement is available through healthychildren.org and aap.org.
