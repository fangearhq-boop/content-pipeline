High-Altitude Sun Safety for COS Families: What Parents Need to Know at 6,000+ Feet

By Sarah Morales | June 28, 2026

Colorado Springs sits at 6,035 feet above sea level. That elevation is part of what makes this place remarkable — the views, the air quality, the proximity to mountains and trails. It also means COS families face meaningfully higher UV exposure than most of the country, every single day.

UV radiation increases approximately 4% for every 1,000 feet of elevation gain, according to the Skin Cancer Foundation. At 6,035 feet, that translates to roughly 24% more UV intensity than families experience at sea level. That's not a marginal difference. And it's the baseline for a typical summer afternoon in COS before anyone starts hiking.

AAP Sun Safety Recommendations for Children

The American Academy of Pediatrics provides age-specific sun protection guidance that applies to all children, and that guidance matters more at elevation.

AAP Sun Safety by Age
Age Group AAP Recommendation
Under 6 months No sunscreen — use protective clothing, wide-brimmed hats, and shade. Avoid direct sun.
6 months and older SPF 15–30 on exposed skin; avoid peak sun hours when possible.
All ages SPF 30+ broad-spectrum sunscreen applied 30 minutes before going outside; reapply every 2 hours. UPF 50+ sun-protective clothing recommended. Seek shade between 10 AM and 4 PM.

Two COS-Specific Facts That Often Get Overlooked

Clouds don't block UV at elevation

Mountain weather can shift quickly. A sunny morning can turn overcast by noon, and it's tempting to skip the sunscreen reapplication when the sky turns gray. Don't. UV radiation penetrates cloud cover effectively — especially at higher elevations where you're already above some of the atmosphere that would otherwise filter it. Bring sunscreen on every outdoor outing, overcast or not.

Car windows block UVB but not UVA

Standard automotive glass filters UVB radiation, but UVA — the wavelength associated with long-term skin damage and melanoma risk — passes through most car windows. On mountain drives and long highway stretches, your back-seat passengers are accumulating UVA exposure. Window shades for back-seat passengers are a practical solution, especially for infants in rear-facing seats who can't move away from direct window sun.

Practical Checklist for Mountain Days

  • Apply SPF 30+ broad-spectrum sunscreen 30 minutes before leaving the house or car
  • Reapply every 2 hours, or immediately after swimming or heavy sweating
  • Keep infants under 6 months in shade and protective clothing — no sunscreen needed
  • Plan outdoor activities before 10 AM or after 4 PM to avoid peak UV hours
  • Pack sunscreen even if the forecast shows clouds
  • Use rear window shades for back-seat passengers on mountain drives
  • Add wide-brimmed hats and UPF 50+ clothing for long-exposure days at elevation

What's Next

For families heading to Garden of the Gods, Pikes Peak, or any trail above the city, the checklist above is the starting point. A full-spectrum SPF 30+ sunscreen for adults and children, a wide-brimmed hat, and shade planning around midday covers most situations.

Families with infants under 6 months face the strictest constraint: no sunscreen, full shade required. That means planning mountain outings for early morning or late afternoon when shade and angle of the sun reduce UV intensity, and keeping infants covered with clothing and in shade at all times.

For specific guidance on sunscreen ingredients, photosensitive medications, or children with sensitive skin, the AAP's Healthy Children resource at healthychildren.org and the Skin Cancer Foundation at skincancer.org both offer detailed guidance that goes beyond the basics covered here.
