### STORY 2: AAP Releases Updated Drowning Prevention Guidelines — Layered Approach for Summer Safety
**Tier:** 1
**Status:** NEW STORY (published in Pediatrics, June 2026)
**Pillar:** National Parenting
**Posting Window:** 10:00 AM MT
**Platforms:** X, Facebook, Article
**Sources:** healthychildren.org, contemporarypediatrics.com, hngn.com, usswimschools.org, aap.org

The American Academy of Pediatrics published updated drowning prevention guidance in the June 2026 issue of Pediatrics. Key points: drowning is the leading cause of death for children ages 1–4; pediatric drowning rates that declined from 1999–2019 have been rising again; racial, ethnic, and geographic disparities are widening. Core recommendation: no single method prevents all drowning — use layered protection. Layers include: touch supervision (within arm's length) at bath and swim time; swim lessons (can begin after age 1); four-sided pool fencing; Coast Guard-approved life jacket use; adult CPR training. Special attention for children with epilepsy, autism, or other conditions that elevate drowning risk — speak with pediatrician about targeted precautions. AAP urges pediatricians to integrate water safety counseling into well-child visits year-round.

COS angle: America the Beautiful Park has the Julie Penrose Fountain. Neighborhood pools are opening across COS. YMCA of the Pikes Peak Region offers swim lessons. Rosemont Reservoir (opening soon) is non-motorized boating only — no swimming/wading.

### STORY 5: High-Altitude Sun Safety for COS Families — Summer UV Risk at Pikes Peak Country
**Tier:** 2
**Status:** NEW STORY
**Pillar:** National Parenting (COS angle)
**Posting Window:** 5:00 PM MT
**Platforms:** X, Facebook, Article
**Sources:** aap.org, skincancer.org, cancer.org, coloradosprings.gov

Colorado Springs sits at 6,035 feet elevation — 14% higher UV intensity than sea level. Every 1,000 feet of altitude increases UV exposure by about 4%. Families heading to Garden of the Gods (6,400 ft), Pikes Peak (14,115 ft), or any mountain trail face dramatically higher UV than the national average. AAP sun safety recommendations: SPF 30+ broad-spectrum sunscreen applied 30 minutes before going outside and reapplied every 2 hours; UPF 50+ sun-protective clothing for infants and toddlers; seek shade 10 AM–4 PM; no sunscreen needed for infants under 6 months — use clothing, shade, and hats instead; babies 6 months+ can use SPF 15–30 on exposed skin.

COS-specific tip: Mountain weather changes fast. Bring sunscreen even on cloudy days — UV penetrates clouds. Car windows block UVB but not UVA rays — use window shades for back-seat passengers.
