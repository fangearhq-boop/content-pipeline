### STORY 2: AAP Drowning Prevention 2026 Guidelines

**LONG-FORM POST**

With summer in full swing and COS pools opening everywhere, this is worth a careful read. 🌊

The American Academy of Pediatrics just published updated drowning prevention guidelines — and the message is urgent. Drowning is the leading cause of death for children ages 1–4 in the United States. After years of declining rates, pediatric drowning deaths have been rising again since 2019. Racial, ethnic, and geographic disparities have widened too.

The AAP's core message: no single precaution prevents all drowning. You need layers.

Here's what the updated 2026 guidelines recommend:

✔️ **Touch supervision** — Stay within arm's length any time your child is in or near water, whether it's a pool, bathtub, or splash pad.

✔️ **Swim lessons** — Can begin after a child turns 1 year old.

✔️ **Four-sided pool fencing** — Surrounds the pool on all four sides, with a self-closing, self-latching gate.

✔️ **Coast Guard-approved life jackets** — For any open water, including lakes and reservoirs.

✔️ **Adult CPR training** — Knowing CPR can make the difference in the seconds before help arrives.

Special note: If your child has epilepsy, autism, or any condition that may elevate drowning risk, ask your pediatrician about targeted precautions at your next well-child visit.

COS families: The YMCA of the Pikes Peak Region offers swim lessons this summer. And just a reminder — Rosemont Reservoir is non-motorized boating only. No swimming or wading.

### STORY 5: COS High-Altitude Sun Safety

**LONG-FORM POST**

Before you head to Garden of the Gods, Pikes Peak, or any mountain trail this summer, here's something every COS parent should know. ☀️

Colorado Springs sits at 6,035 feet elevation — and that altitude comes with significantly higher UV exposure. UV radiation increases approximately 4% for every 1,000 feet of elevation gain. That means COS families are already dealing with roughly 24% more UV intensity than families at sea level, before you even start climbing.

Heading up to Pikes Peak at 14,115 feet? That's dramatically more UV than anywhere along the Front Range.

**Here's what the AAP recommends:**

**Infants under 6 months:** Skip the sunscreen. Keep them covered with clothing, wide-brimmed hats, and shade. Avoid direct sun entirely.

**Babies 6 months and older:** SPF 15–30 on exposed skin.

**Everyone:** SPF 30+ broad-spectrum sunscreen, applied 30 minutes before going outside and reapplied every 2 hours. UPF 50+ sun-protective clothing for infants and toddlers. Seek shade between 10 AM and 4 PM.

**Two COS-specific tips that often get overlooked:**

Mountain weather changes fast. Clouds don't block UV — bring sunscreen even on overcast days at elevation.

Car windows block UVB but not UVA. Use window shades for your back-seat passengers on mountain drives.
