### STORY 1: AAP vs. CDC 2026 Vaccine Schedule

**Long-Form Post (10:00 AM MT)**

There's something important happening in children's healthcare right now that a lot of COS parents haven't heard about yet — and it's worth understanding before your child's next well-visit.

The American Academy of Pediatrics published its 2026 immunization schedule this year recommending vaccines against 18 diseases. The CDC's revised schedule covers only 11. That's not a small difference — and it's the first time in recent memory that the two have issued meaningfully different guidance for childhood vaccines.

The vaccines the AAP kept that the CDC dropped include hepatitis A, hepatitis B, meningococcal disease, rotavirus, influenza, and RSV. The CDC's changes were made without convening the Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices, the body of experts that typically reviews this kind of evidence, and without citing new supporting data. Twelve major medical organizations — including the American Medical Association and the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists — have backed the AAP's schedule.

What this means practically: if your child's pediatrician follows AAP guidance (and most do), they may recommend a broader set of vaccines than the federal schedule now reflects. It's worth asking your child's doctor directly which schedule their practice follows — and why.

The full AAP schedule is available at HealthyChildren.org. Your pediatrician is still your best resource for questions about your individual child.

### STORY 2: Kith Retail Children's Loungewear Recall

**Long-Form Post (12:00 PM MT)**

Heads up for any COS families who picked up Kith loungewear sets for their kids around the holidays.

The U.S. Consumer Product Safety Commission has recalled Kith Retail's "Baby Kithmas" and "Kids Kithmas" plaid lounge sets due to a burn hazard. The woven fabric in these sets fails mandatory federal flammability standards for children's sleepwear — meaning it can ignite too easily, creating a risk of serious burn injuries. No injuries have been reported, but the CPSC's guidance is to stop using these immediately.

The recalled items are matching sets: a long-sleeved button-front shirt with a front pocket and matching pants, in a black and red plaid pattern with "Kith" embroidered on the pocket and pant leg. They were sold in sizes ranging from 9–12 months all the way through XL (14/16), at Kith stores and on kith.com from December 2025 through January 2026 for approximately $75.

If you have one: stop using it. The recall remedy requires you to destroy the set by cutting it in half, take a photo of the destroyed product, and email that photo to recalls@kithnyc.com. Kith will then issue a full refund.

About 130 sets were sold, so the number of affected families is relatively small — but if your kids got these as gifts and you haven't heard about the recall yet, now you know.

### STORY 3: D49 Student Success Center Opens August 2026

**Long-Form Post (8:00 AM MT)**

D49 families, here's something worth knowing heading into the new school year.

District 49 is opening a new Student Success Center this fall — it's housed in the former Falcon Elementary School of Technology building, and it opens when the school year starts on August 3–4. The center brings several specialized programs under one roof: two new full-time preschool classrooms (available Monday through Thursday to D49 families districtwide), PEAK programs for students needing higher-level support and intervention, Elevates 18–21 Transition Services, and Base49 before and after school care.

The preschool piece is a meaningful expansion — previously, families had limited options within the district for early childhood programs, and the Student Success Center makes two full-time classrooms available to any D49 family, not just those in a specific neighborhood school.

For families with students who receive PEAK services, the new center is designed to feel more like a traditional school environment while still providing the specialized support those kids need as they work toward eventually returning to their home schools.

If any of these programs apply to your family, it's worth reaching out to your school or D49 directly at d49.org to get more information before the year starts.

### STORY 4: CMZoo Dog Days Fridays

**Long-Form Post (3:00 PM MT)**

This one's for the families with four-legged members of the household.

Cheyenne Mountain Zoo is hosting Dog Days Fridays as part of its centennial year programming — and there are two chances to bring your well-behaved dog to the zoo: this Friday, July 24, and the following Friday, July 31. Both are open from 9 a.m. to 5 p.m.

Here's the important part: dog admission is limited. You can't just show up at the gate with your dog and buy a ticket. Dog tickets need to be booked in advance at cmzoo.org, and they're allocated by entry time and date. If you want to bring your dog this Friday, you'd want to lock in tickets today.

The zoo was ranked the No. 2 zoo in the United States for 2026, and this is one of the more unusual programming additions they've made during the centennial year. It's a fun outing — the zoo experience plus having your dog along for it.

Standard admission still applies for humans: adults are $34.75 and kids ages 3 to 11 are $29.75 at peak pricing. Advance timed tickets are required for people as well.

### STORY 5: Rosemont Reservoir Fishing

**Long-Form Post (5:00 PM MT)**

If your family has been looking for a high-altitude fishing spot close to Colorado Springs, Rosemont Reservoir is worth knowing about.

The Penrose-Rosemont Reservoir State Wildlife Area sits about 12 miles southwest of Colorado Springs along Gold Camp Road — on the south side of Pikes Peak at over 9,600 feet. It's open for fishing from mid-May through October, 5 a.m. to 9 p.m. The access is flies and lures only (no bait), and anyone 16 and older needs a valid Colorado hunting/fishing license or a State Wildlife Area pass.

Getting there takes a short hike — about half a mile from the parking lot to the water's edge. It's manageable for kids who are comfortable on unpaved terrain, but worth planning for.

A few things to know before you go: swimming and wading are not permitted at Rosemont, which is a watershed area managed by Colorado Springs Utilities. No water contact of any kind. It's a fishing and walking destination, not a beach day.

On the expansion front: Colorado Springs City Council approved a plan earlier this year to add camping, non-motorized boating, and better restrooms to Rosemont in partnership with Colorado Parks and Wildlife. The target was sometime this summer, but the actual status of the campground is still uncertain — it was in a permitting phase as of earlier this year. Before loading up the car with camping gear, we'd recommend checking cpw.state.co.us or calling CPW directly to confirm what's currently available.
