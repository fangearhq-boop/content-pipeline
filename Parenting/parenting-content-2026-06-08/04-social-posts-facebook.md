### STORY 1: TOMY Boon NURSH Baby Bottle Recall

#### Long-Form Post — 8:00 AM MT

COS parents, if you have Boon NURSH 8 oz baby bottles that came from Walmart, stop using them today.

TOMY International recalled approximately 40,000 of these bottles on June 4 after 135 reports of the outer hard plastic shell bubbling and peeling off. Those loose film-like pieces are a choking hazard for infants. The recalled bottles are the Boon NURSH 8 oz 3-Pack in pink tie-dye, item number B11654, sold exclusively at Walmart and Walmart.com from November 2025 through May 2026 for around $20.

If you have them: stop use immediately. You can get a $22 store credit to booninc.com or a replacement set in a different color. Submit a photo of the bottles with "RECALL" written on them and certify that you've disposed of them. Reach TOMY at 866-725-4407 (Monday–Friday, 8 AM–4 PM CT), by email at cs@tomy.com, or online at recall.tomy.com/nursh.

No injuries have been reported, but this is worth acting on now if you have these bottles in your cabinet.

### STORY 2: Blodgett Open Space Trail Expansion

#### Long-Form Post — 10:00 AM MT

Great news for COS families who love getting outside: Blodgett Open Space is getting a major upgrade this summer.

The City of Colorado Springs is completing 14 miles of new and improved trails at the 384-acre open space near the Rockrimmon area. The expansion includes dedicated mountain bike trails in the southern section and hiking and multi-use trails in the north, along with a new trailhead and parking area at the base of the old quarry. Trail construction is expected to be finished by summer 2026.

Even before the new trails open, Blodgett is worth the trip. The lower Blodgett Trail offers great city views with manageable elevation gain, and the Chickadee Trail loops for about half a mile — perfect for younger kids. The best part: it's free, and it borders Pike National Forest.

The city's Trackchair program is also expanding this season, with two accessible guided hikes offered daily (Wednesday through Sunday) at Blodgett and other locations. Colorado families who need accessible outdoor options should look into it.

### STORY 3: Summer Slide Prevention

#### Long-Form Post — 12:00 PM MT

Schools just let out. Here's what the research says about summer learning loss — and what COS families can actually do about it.

The summer slide is real: studies consistently show that children lose an average of 25–30% of their school-year learning over the summer. Math takes the hardest hit, and younger children are more affected than older ones. The good news is that recovery is possible — but it helps to build in some structure before September arrives.

The research-backed tips are simpler than they sound. Reading for 20 minutes a day — anything the child chooses, even below grade level — makes a measurable difference. Adding math to everyday activities like cooking, grocery shopping, or measuring things around the house works for younger kids without feeling like homework. And setting aside 20–30 minutes for structured review three to four times per week keeps skills sharp without making summer feel like school.

For Colorado Springs families who want more structured options, there are good local resources right now. PPYMCA's Summer Day Camp runs through June (ppymca.org for current weeks and registration). The Pikes Peak Library District's Summer Adventure program runs free through July 31 at all 14 locations — no fee, no registration, and the Beanstack app makes tracking easy. Rock Ledge Ranch is running hands-on historical activities through Saturday, June 14 (this week is the last chance).

The summer is long. A little consistency makes a big difference.

### STORY 4: Rock Ledge Ranch Last Week

#### Long-Form Post — 3:00 PM MT

If you've been meaning to take the kids to Rock Ledge Ranch this summer, this is the week to make it happen. The historic site closes for the season this Saturday, June 14.

Rock Ledge Ranch Historic Site is open through June 14, Tuesday through Saturday, 10 AM–5 PM. Located at Garden of the Gods Park in Colorado Springs, the site brings four eras of Colorado history to life through hands-on activities. Families can learn about Native American trade, try making a cornhusk doll at the Homestead Cabin, explore the 1880s Garden and see what pioneer life looked like, play Victorian-era games, tour the 1870s Rock Ledge House, and watch live blacksmith demonstrations.

It's the kind of place where kids don't realize they're learning anything — they're just trying to figure out how to use a drop spindle or watching a blacksmith shape metal. For families looking for something to do this week that isn't a screen, it's worth a visit.

Check admission and details at coloradosprings.gov/rock-ledge-ranch before you go.

### STORY 5: D49 Budget Vote Reminder

#### Long-Form Post — 5:00 PM MT

D49 families: your school board votes on next year's budget this Thursday, June 11.

The District 49 Board of Education meets at Sand Creek High School on June 11 to vote on the 2026–27 fiscal year budget. The budget includes funding for the Student Success Center, which is set to open in August 2026 at the former Falcon Elementary of Technology campus in Falcon. The center will offer preschool (Monday–Thursday), Base49 before and after-school care, PEAK programs, and Elevates 18–21 services for young adults with disabilities.

It's a public meeting, which means families who want to attend or submit comments ahead of the vote can do so. The agenda is available at d49.org, and the meeting details — including address and start time — are listed on the district calendar.

If you have kids in D49 or plans to enroll in any of the new programs at the Student Success Center, this is a meeting worth knowing about.
