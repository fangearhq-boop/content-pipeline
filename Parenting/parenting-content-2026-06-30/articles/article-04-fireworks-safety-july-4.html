Before July 4: What Colorado Springs Parents Should Know About Fireworks and Kids

By Jamie Rivera | June 30, 2026

Consumer fireworks are permanently banned in the City of Colorado Springs — and Stage I fire restrictions extend those rules into unincorporated El Paso County. Here's what's legal, what protects little ears, and how to make a public show safer for your kids.

Before July 4 weekend gets underway, COS families need to know two things: backyard fireworks aren't an option here, and attending a professional show comes with its own safety considerations — especially for young kids. Here's the full picture.

What's Legal in Colorado Springs

Consumer fireworks are permanently banned in the City of Colorado Springs — not just during fire season, but always. This isn't new, and it doesn't change year to year based on conditions.

Stage I fire restrictions currently in effect in unincorporated El Paso County also prohibit fireworks use in all unincorporated areas of the county. That means families in the county outside city limits are also covered by active restrictions, on top of Colorado's statewide rules.

Colorado's statewide fireworks law already prohibits anything that explodes or leaves the ground — firecrackers, bottle rockets, roman candles, aerial shells, and similar products are illegal statewide. What's technically legal as "permissible" fireworks in Colorado includes sparklers, snake and glow worm devices, fountains, smoke bombs, and ground spinners. But in COS and unincorporated El Paso County, even permissible fireworks are banned under current local and county rules.

The practical result: no backyard fireworks of any kind for COS families this July 4. The three professional shows covered earlier today — Star-Spangled Symphony at Ford Amphitheater, Banning Lewis Ranch, and Palmer Lake — are exactly where your family should be if you want fireworks this weekend.

Protecting Little Ears at a Public Show

Professional fireworks shows are significantly safer than personal fireworks for families. But proximity to professional displays still carries a real risk for young children's hearing.

Fireworks at a typical public show can reach 150 decibels or higher. The safe hearing threshold — the level at which sustained exposure begins causing damage — is 85 decibels. That gap matters. Even a brief exposure at 150 dB can cause immediate, permanent hearing damage.

The AAP and pediatric hearing specialists recommend earmuffs designed for children and infants at any public fireworks event. Look for a noise reduction rating (NRR) on the packaging — higher is better for loud events. Infant ear canals are smaller, which actually amplifies sound pressure, making them more vulnerable than older children.

The AAP's position is that infants ideally should not attend fireworks shows at all. If you do bring an infant, quality earmuffs and positioning away from the loudest areas are essential.

Distance and Other Safety Considerations

At a public show, the AAP and healthychildren.org recommend staying at least 500 feet from the fireworks launch site. This distance protects hearing, reduces the risk of debris from misfired shells, and keeps kids away from the area where shells and sparks land after the burst.

A note on sparklers: even though sparklers fall into the "permissible" category under Colorado law (and are widely sold in stores outside COS), they burn at approximately 2,000 degrees Fahrenheit. That's hotter than glass melts. They're not an appropriate activity for young children despite being commonly available and perceived as "safe." If sparklers are part of your extended family's July 4 celebration outside COS, keep young kids well away and have water immediately on hand.

What's Next

For families in COS this July 4: find a professional show (see our 8:00 AM post for options), bring earmuffs for babies and toddlers, stay 500 feet back from the launch area, and consider skipping the show entirely for infants. More information on pediatric hearing safety at shows is available through the AAP at healthychildren.org.
