Water Safety for COS Families: What the Stats Show and What Actually Prevents Drowning

By Sarah Morales | June 30, 2026

Drowning is the leading cause of accidental death for children under 4 — and it doesn't look like it does in the movies. Here's what research shows actually reduces the risk, plus Colorado-specific context heading into the July 4 weekend.

With July 4 weekend ahead and water activities on a lot of family agendas, this is the right moment to talk about water safety — not to alarm anyone, but because the numbers are serious and the prevention toolkit is well-established.

Drowning is the leading cause of unintentional injury death for children ages 1 to 4, and the second leading cause for kids ages 5 to 14. Colorado had its deadliest year on record for drownings in 2023, and that total includes rivers, pools, and backyard situations across the state. These aren't freak accidents — they're preventable events, and most happen during brief lapses in supervision.

One thing parents often don't know: drowning is silent. A child in distress in the water typically can't call out for help. They can go under in under a minute, without any of the dramatic splashing depicted in movies or TV. That's why active supervision isn't just a recommendation — it's the single most important variable in whether a child survives a water emergency.

What Research Shows Actually Works

Swim Lessons

The American Academy of Pediatrics recommends starting swim lessons at age 1. Research shows that formal swim instruction reduces the risk of drowning by 88% for children ages 1 to 4. That's not a marginal improvement — it's a dramatic reduction in risk from a single intervention. If your child hasn't started swim lessons yet, summer is a natural time to enroll. In COS, SafeSplash Swim School and the Pikes Peak YMCA both offer year-round instruction.

Active Supervision

When children are in or near water, a supervising adult should be within arm's length and undivided in attention — not on a phone, not in conversation, not looking away. At pool gatherings with multiple adults, designating a specific "water watcher" — someone whose only job for a defined time period is watching the water — removes the ambiguity of assuming someone else is watching. The designation rotates, but it's always explicit.

Four-Sided Pool Fencing

A proper four-sided fence that completely surrounds a pool — not just a fence that separates the yard from the street — reduces a child's drowning risk by 83%, per CDC research. The key is that a four-sided fence with a self-latching gate prevents a child from wandering to the pool unsupervised, which is how most pool drownings begin. A fence on three sides with a house wall as the fourth side is significantly less effective because it doesn't prevent access from inside the home.

Life Jackets — Not Water Wings

US Coast Guard-approved life jackets are safety devices. Water wings, arm floaties, pool noodles, and inflatable swim vests are toys. They are not certified safety equipment, they can deflate, they can slip off, and they give children a false sense of security in water they haven't learned to navigate. For open water — rivers, lakes, reservoirs — a properly fitted, USCG-approved life jacket is required. For pools, it doesn't replace supervision.

Colorado-Specific Risks

Colorado's water hazards carry some additional context worth knowing heading into summer weekend activities.

Mountain water is cold even in peak summer. Streams, rivers, and reservoirs fed by snowmelt run significantly colder than a backyard pool, and cold water causes the body to react quickly — muscle cramps, shock, and involuntary gasping can happen within seconds of submersion. Rivers and streams in early summer are particularly dangerous because snowmelt from the Front Range keeps water levels high and current speeds fast through June and into July. What looks like a calm stream at the edge can have a powerful current a few feet out.

Colorado Parks and Wildlife (CPW) maintains water safety resources specific to Colorado's conditions at cpw.state.co.us/water-safety. If your family is planning any river or reservoir activities this weekend, it's worth a quick read before you go.

What's Next

If you're heading to a pool, lake, or river this weekend: have a dedicated water watcher, keep young kids within arm's reach, put real life jackets on anyone on open water, and step away from the phone when kids are in the water.

For swim lesson enrollment in COS, check SafeSplash Swim School or the Pikes Peak YMCA. For Colorado-specific water safety information, visit cpw.state.co.us/water-safety.
