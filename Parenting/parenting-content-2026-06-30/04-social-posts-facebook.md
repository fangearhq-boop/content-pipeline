### STORY 2: CooCooBaby Recall

#### Long-Form Post — 10:00 AM MT

Heads up, COS parents: if you have a CooCooBaby baby lounger at home, the CPSC has issued a recall and the action to take is straightforward.

The recall covers 2,355 CooCooBaby "Classic" and "Deluxe" Baby Loungers sold on Amazon and CooCooBabyOfficial.com from December 2024 through March 2026 for $35 to $70. The loungers have three overlapping safety problems: the sides are shorter than the mandatory minimum height, the sleeping pad is thicker than the maximum allowed, and the open foot of the lounger can allow an infant to slip down until entrapped. All three issues relate to suffocation and fall risk.

Here's how to get your refund:

1. Stop using the lounger immediately.
2. Cut the sides of the lounger and cut the sleeping pad.
3. Take a photograph of the destroyed product.
4. Email the photo to support@coocoobabyofficial.com.
5. CPSC says a full refund will be issued to your original payment method.

No phone number or physical return is needed — the remedy is handled entirely by email with a photo of the destroyed lounger.

The Classic model has a ribbon tie at the foot opening; the Deluxe has buttons. Both are recalled.

If you have one of these loungers, or you know someone who might — please share this.

### STORY 3: Water Safety

#### Long-Form Post — 12:00 PM MT

With a big outdoor weekend ahead, this feels like the right time to talk about water safety.

Drowning is the leading cause of accidental death for children ages 1 to 4 — and the second leading cause for kids ages 5 to 14. Colorado had its deadliest year on record for drownings in 2023, and that number includes rivers, pools, and backyard situations. Drowning is also silent: it doesn't look like the movies. A child in distress can go under in under a minute, without crying out.

Here's what the research actually shows works:

**Swim lessons:** The American Academy of Pediatrics recommends starting swim lessons at age 1. Research shows they reduce drowning risk by 88%.

**Active supervision:** When children are in or near water, a supervising adult should be within arm's length — not on a phone, not distracted. The designation of a "water watcher" at any pool gathering is a simple, effective practice.

**Four-sided pool fencing:** A proper four-sided fence around a pool reduces a child's drowning risk by 83%, per CDC research. A fence with a latch that kids can't open changes the risk calculation dramatically.

**Life jackets — not water wings:** US Coast Guard-approved life jackets are a safety device. Water wings and arm floaties are toys, not safety equipment, and should never be treated as substitutes.

**Colorado-specific**: Mountain water is cold even in summer. Cold water can cause shock and muscle cramps quickly. Fast-moving rivers and streams in early summer carry snowmelt that makes them significantly more dangerous than they look.

If you're planning any water activities this weekend, enjoy them — just keep eyes on your kids and keep the watcher's job undivided.

### STORY 4: Fireworks Safety

#### Long-Form Post — 3:00 PM MT

Before July 4 weekend gets underway, a couple of things Colorado Springs families should know.

First: consumer fireworks are banned in the City of Colorado Springs — permanently, not just this year. Stage I fire restrictions currently in effect in unincorporated El Paso County also prohibit fireworks use in all unincorporated areas. The statewide Colorado ban already covers anything that explodes or leaves the ground — firecrackers, bottle rockets, roman candles, and similar products. For families in COS, the message is simple: no backyard fireworks.

The good news is there are professional shows happening (covered earlier today), and professional shows are significantly safer for kids.

If you're attending a public fireworks event, here's what the AAP and hearing specialists recommend:

**For little ears:** Fireworks can reach 150+ decibels. Anything above 85 dB can cause hearing damage. Earmuffs designed for children (check the noise reduction rating, or NRR) are recommended for babies and toddlers. Infants are especially vulnerable because their smaller ear canals amplify sound pressure. The AAP advises that infants ideally not attend fireworks shows at all.

**Distance matters:** Stay at least 500 feet from the launch site. Not only does this protect hearing — it reduces the risk of falling debris.

**Sparklers:** Even "permissible" fireworks like sparklers burn at approximately 2,000°F. They're not a safe activity for young children despite being widely sold. If sparklers are part of your celebration, keep young kids away and have water nearby.

### STORY 5: PPLD Summer Adventure

#### Long-Form Post — 5:00 PM MT

Today is June 30 — the exact halfway point of the Pikes Peak Library District's Summer Adventure program, which runs through July 31. If you haven't signed your kids up yet, there are still 31 days to go, and the whole thing is free.

The program is open to all ages — kids, teens, and adults. You don't need to register in advance to participate. Just head to any of PPLD's 14 library branches or catch one of the 2 bookmobiles, or download the Beanstack app to track your family's progress and qualify for prizes.

What's in it: 30 unique activities, a book log, and grand prizes that include a Broadmoor Cloud Camp stay and a Pikes Peak Attractions VIP pass. The activities range from things you can do at home to in-library programs — it's genuinely flexible for summer schedules.

The research behind programs like this is consistent: kids who read and stay engaged over summer enter the fall in better academic shape. Studies estimate that unstructured summers lead to 25–30% learning loss between school years. Summer Adventure is one of the simplest tools available to COS families to push back against that.

If you've been meaning to sign up — now is a good time. ppld.org/summer.
