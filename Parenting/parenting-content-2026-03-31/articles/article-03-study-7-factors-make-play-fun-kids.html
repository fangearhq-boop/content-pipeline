New Research: 7 Factors That Make Play Fun for Kids — And Why Forcing It Backfires

March 31, 2026 · COS Parenting

What actually makes it fun for kids to play together? Researchers set out to answer that question, and a new study published March 30, 2026 in the journal Frontiers in Psychology came back with a concrete answer: seven factors, in a specific combination, determine whether children experience group play as genuinely fun or as something they'd rather not be doing.

The findings are relevant to any parent heading into Easter weekend, spring break play dates, or the upcoming summer with kids who need to figure out how to share space with other kids.

The Seven Factors

The researchers identified the following seven elements that children associate with fun group play:

  1. Social inclusion — feeling genuinely part of the group, not on the outside looking in
  2. Imagination — room to pretend, invent, and use creative thinking in the play scenario
  3. Wild and exciting atmosphere — a sense of energy and aliveness; the feeling that something could happen
  4. Having something to do — a purpose, activity, or structure that gives the play its shape
  5. Silliness and transgression — the fun of being a little silly or mildly bending rules, without real consequences
  6. Achievable challenges — goals that feel real but not impossible; success should be possible
  7. Play feeling — a subjective but recognizable sense that this is genuinely play and not something else masquerading as play

That seventh factor, the "play feeling," had the strongest association with whether children described an activity as fun. It's hard to define precisely — researchers acknowledged that — but children know it when they feel it, and its absence turns something that looks like play into something that feels like work.

The Finding That Will Resonate Most

The study's most counterintuitive finding is also its most immediately useful: forcing children to play together actively undermines the first factor on the list — social inclusion.

When adults direct children to play with a specific peer, or intervene to insert a child into a play group, the children in the group don't experience the same sense of organic belonging. They know the inclusion was externally imposed rather than socially earned, and that changes the dynamic. According to the researchers, adult-directed group formation runs counter to the social inclusion factor and makes the experience feel less fun, not more.

This is a finding that feels true to anyone who has watched a play date fall flat because both families tried to engineer it too hard, or seen a birthday party's free play turn chaotic the moment well-meaning adults stepped in to organize it.

What This Means for COS Families

The practical implication for Colorado Springs parents is straightforward: the best way to support children's play is to create conditions for the seven factors rather than trying to produce the outcome directly.

That means providing a setting with something to do (whether it's playground equipment, art supplies, a yard, or a simple game structure), keeping challenges appropriate to the age group, and letting the social structure of the group find its own shape. If a child is struggling to enter a play group, coach from the sideline — offer approaches, encourage observation — rather than physically engineering their inclusion. The distinction matters to how children experience what follows.

Easter weekend and spring break create natural opportunities for this kind of supported-but-not-controlled play. COS families have access to outdoor environments — parks, trails, the Garden of the Gods visitor area, even a simple backyard — that provide the space for all seven factors without requiring careful orchestration. The research suggests the most important thing parents can do is get out of the way once the conditions are set.

What's Next

The Frontiers in Psychology study joins a growing body of research emphasizing that children's play outcomes are improved by adult support, not adult direction. Related work from recent years has found that co-viewing media with children improves its developmental impact (similar to the AAP's 2026 screen time guidance update), and that physical play environments influence children's willingness to take appropriate risks during play.

For COS families, the simplest application of this research is also the easiest one: find a park, let them run, and resist the urge to engineer it. The seven factors tend to emerge on their own when the space and permission are there.
