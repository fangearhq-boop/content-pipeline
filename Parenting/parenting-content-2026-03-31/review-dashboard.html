CPSC Safety Alert — Infant Walkers, Strollers, Travel Bassinets & Baby Monitors Recalled [TIER 1]
NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post ``` Four more children's products have been flagged by the CPSC with serious hazard warnings — and COS parents should check their homes now. The first is the Uuoeebb Infant Walker, sold on Amazon by BaoD. The CPSC found these walkers violate the mandatory safety standard for infant walkers in three ways: they fit through a standard doorway (meaning they can reach the top of stairs before stopping), they fail to stop at step edges, and their leg openings are large enough for a child's head to slip through — creating entrapment and strangulation risk. If you bought a baby walker on Amazon in the past year, check the brand. The second is a line of Convertible Strollers sold on AliExpress, priced between $215 and $415. The restraint system on these strollers can fail, creating a fall hazard. These were sold from September 2024 through October 2025. Third is the situation parents most need to know about: the Tuyedoqe Travel Bassinet. The CPSC issued a Warning — not a formal recall — because the company refused to cooperate with the recall process. That means there is no official refund program and no replacement option. CPSC's guidance is direct: stop using the bassinet immediately, remove the foam padding, cut the cover in half, and dispose of both. About 250 units were sold on Amazon between April and October 2025 for around $40. Fourth: the Babysense Max View Baby Monitor. The parent unit — the display screen — can overheat or spark when charging. This is a fire hazard, especially for parents who charge the unit near the crib overnight. For the full recall details and remedies, visit cpsc.gov/Recalls. If you're unsure whether your product is affected, err on the side of stopping use until you can verify. Which of these products do you have at home? New Study — Science Just Identified the 7 Factors That Make Play Fun for Kids [TIER 2]
NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post ``` A study published yesterday in Frontiers in Psychology asked a simple question: what actually makes it fun for kids to play together? The researchers identified seven factors. Kids find group play most enjoyable when they feel genuinely included in the group, when there's room for imagination, when the atmosphere is a little wild and exciting, when there's something specific to do, when there's an element of silliness or mild rule-bending, when the challenge is achievable rather than impossible, and what researchers called a "play feeling" — a hard-to-define but highly recognizable sense that this is actually play and not something else. That seventh factor, "play feeling," had the most to do with whether children described something as fun. It's the difference between a game that feels spontaneous and alive, and an activity that feels scheduled. The finding that will probably resonate with parents the most: forcing kids to play together actually undermines the social inclusion factor. Children who feel directed or pressured to interact with a specific peer don't experience the same sense of genuine belonging — and that, according to the study, is what makes it feel less fun. What this means practically: this Easter weekend, if you've got a group of kids and they're finding their own thing, that's probably working. The structured egg hunt is fine. But if you find yourself insisting two kids play together and it's not clicking, the research suggests stepping back is the more effective move. Does this match what you've noticed about your own kids' play? ``` ### Image Post Caption #### Image Caption -- 12:30 PM MT ``` New research: 7 things make play fun for kids.
