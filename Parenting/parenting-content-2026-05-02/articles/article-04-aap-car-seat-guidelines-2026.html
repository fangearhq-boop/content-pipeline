The American Academy of Pediatrics updated its child passenger safety recommendations in February 2026, and one change in particular is worth knowing about. The AAP removed the age-based guideline for rear-facing car seats — and for good reason. Here's what changed, why it matters, and how to apply the new guidance.

What Changed

The previous AAP recommendation stated that children should remain in a rear-facing car seat until age 2 as a baseline. The 2026 update removes that age guideline entirely. The new standard: keep children rear-facing until they reach the maximum height or weight allowed by their specific car seat, as specified by the manufacturer.

The change reflects updated crash test data and field research. The reason rear-facing is safer for young children is that the seat's shell absorbs and distributes crash forces across the child's entire head, neck, and torso rather than concentrating them at the harness points. That physics doesn't change at the child's second birthday — it depends on the child's size and how well they fit in the seat. Age 2 was always an approximation, and the data now supports removing it as a guideline.

What This Means in Practice

Check your car seat. Every rear-facing seat has a maximum rear-facing weight and height listed on the label or in the manual. That's the number to follow. If your child is under that limit and still fits comfortably rear-facing, they should stay rear-facing — regardless of whether they've turned 2 or 3 or are approaching 4.

Many convertible car seats today have rear-facing weight limits of 40-50 pounds, which means most children can and should stay rear-facing considerably longer than age 2. Families who were following the old age-2 guideline may have moved their kids forward-facing earlier than necessary.

The Rest of the Progression

Once a child outgrows the rear-facing limits of their seat, the progression moves to a forward-facing seat with a five-point harness. Keep children in a harness-equipped forward-facing seat for as long as the seat's weight and height limits allow.

After outgrowing forward-facing, children move to a belt-positioning booster seat. The booster stays until the vehicle's lap and shoulder seat belt fits the child properly without the booster — meaning the shoulder belt crosses the chest (not the neck), and the lap belt rests across the hips (not the belly). That proper fit typically happens when a child is at least 4 feet 9 inches tall, which for most kids is somewhere between ages 8 and 12.

What's Next

For the full AAP child passenger safety guidelines, visit aap.org and search "child passenger safety." If you're unsure whether your current car seat setup is correct, Colorado Springs families can also reach out to a certified Child Passenger Safety Technician (CPST) for a free seat check. The Safe Kids Worldwide network maintains a database of inspection stations at safekids.org. Car seat fits and installation checks are one of the most underused free safety resources available to parents.
