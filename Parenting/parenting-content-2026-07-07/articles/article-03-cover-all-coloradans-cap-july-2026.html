Colorado's Cover All Coloradans Program Has Hit Its 25,000-Child Enrollment Cap — What It Means for Families

By Jamie Rivera | COS Parenting | July 7, 2026

Colorado Springs, CO

A Colorado program that provides healthcare coverage for immigrant children and pregnant women hit a new enrollment cap on July 1, 2026 — meaning it is now closed to new applicants.

Cover All Coloradans, which launched January 1, 2025, extends healthcare access to immigrant children and pregnant women who don't qualify for traditional Medicaid. The program filled a significant gap, serving tens of thousands of children statewide. As of July 1, the legislature has capped enrollment at 25,000 participants. The age limit for children has also been reduced — from under 19 to under 18.

What Changed on July 1

The 25,000-enrollment cap is a hard limit. Once enrollment reaches that number, the program stops accepting new applicants until space becomes available. Children who were already enrolled before July 1 are not immediately affected — their coverage continues.

The age limit reduction is a separate change. Children who are 18 years old are no longer eligible going forward. Children currently enrolled who turn 18 will age out under the new rules.

These changes apply statewide, including families in the Colorado Springs and El Paso County area who have relied on Cover All Coloradans for their children's healthcare needs.

Why the Cap Was Implemented

The program's costs have grown far beyond initial projections. When Cover All Coloradans launched, the estimated cost was $14.7 million. For the 2026–27 fiscal year, that projection has grown to $104.5 million — more than seven times the original estimate. The enrollment cap was the legislature's response to bring costs under control.

Advocates for the program argue that the math on the alternative isn't straightforward. When children lack healthcare access, they are more likely to rely on emergency rooms for care, which is more expensive per encounter and often goes uncompensated — a cost that ends up absorbed by hospitals and ultimately by the broader healthcare system.

What This Means Practically for COS Families

For families already enrolled: continue using the program as you have. Your coverage has not been terminated.

For families who needed to enroll a child who isn't currently covered: the program is now closed to new applicants. Advocates are working to connect families with alternative resources. Options may include federally qualified health centers, which provide sliding-scale care regardless of immigration status, and other community health programs in El Paso County.

The Colorado Department of Health Care Policy and Financing (HCPF), which administers Cover All Coloradans, maintains information about the program and alternative resources at hcpf.colorado.gov.

What's Next

Advocates are continuing to push for a budget adjustment that would allow the cap to be raised or eliminated in a future legislative session. The outcome will depend on how the state budget picture evolves and what priority the legislature places on children's healthcare access. For now, families who need coverage for newly eligible children will need to explore other options. Community organizations in Colorado Springs and El Paso County can help navigate those resources — contact local federally qualified health centers or advocacy organizations for current guidance.
