Wildfire Smoke and an Ozone Alert Hit Colorado Springs at the Same Time — Here's What Parents Need to Know Today

By Jamie Rivera | COS Parenting | July 7, 2026

Colorado Springs, CO

Colorado Springs is dealing with two air quality concerns at once today, and if you have kids with outdoor plans, it's worth pausing before heading out.

The Colorado Department of Public Health and Environment (CDPHE) has issued an Ozone Action Day for the Front Range Urban Corridor through at least 4 PM today. At the same time, a Wildfire Smoke Advisory is in effect for El Paso County. The smoke is coming from the Aspen Acres Fire, which has burned more than 91,000 acres in Pueblo County since it started June 29 and is now being considered the seventh largest wildfire in Colorado history.

Those two factors combined put today's air quality in the Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups category — which in practice means children, elderly adults, and anyone with asthma, heart disease, or lung conditions should take precautions.

Why Children Are More Vulnerable

Kids are not just small adults when it comes to air quality. Children breathe more air relative to their body weight than adults do, which means they inhale proportionally more particulate matter. Their lungs are also still developing, making them more susceptible to damage from PM2.5 — the fine particles in wildfire smoke that are too small to see but small enough to penetrate deep into lung tissue.

On days like today, the recommendation from CDPHE is to limit time outdoors, especially during the peak heat of the day when ozone levels are highest. Morning hours are generally better than afternoon if you need to be outside. If your kids have outdoor sports, camps, or activities scheduled, it's worth checking air quality before you go and being willing to cut things short if conditions worsen.

How to Check Air Quality in Your Area

The easiest tool is AirNow.gov, where you can enter your zip code to get a real-time reading for your specific neighborhood. The site uses a color-coded AQI (Air Quality Index) scale:

AQI Range Category What It Means for Kids
0–50 Good No restrictions
51–100 Moderate Unusually sensitive children may want to reduce prolonged outdoor activity
101–150 Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups Children, people with asthma, and elderly should limit prolonged outdoor activity
151–200 Unhealthy Everyone should reduce outdoor activity; children should avoid it
201+ Very Unhealthy / Hazardous Avoid all outdoor activity; stay indoors with windows closed

Practical Steps for Today

If you need to be outside, morning before 10 AM is the better window — ozone levels typically build through the afternoon as sunlight drives chemical reactions in the air. Wildfire smoke can fluctuate throughout the day depending on wind direction.

If you're staying indoors, keep windows and doors closed to limit smoke infiltration. If you have a HEPA air purifier, run it. Avoid activities that add indoor air pollution — candles, frying, or vacuuming — on high smoke days.

Watch for symptoms in your children: coughing, eye or throat irritation, shortness of breath, or unusual fatigue during activity. Children with asthma should have their rescue inhaler accessible and follow their asthma action plan. If your child is having difficulty breathing, contact your pediatrician or seek care.

What's Next

The Ozone Action Day is in effect through at least 4 PM today. Wildfire smoke conditions depend heavily on wind direction and the ongoing progression of the Aspen Acres Fire in Pueblo County. For updated conditions, check CDPHE's air quality page at colorado.gov/airquality or AirNow.gov throughout the day. Sign up for air quality alerts through CDPHE's notification system to get updates directly to your phone when conditions change.
