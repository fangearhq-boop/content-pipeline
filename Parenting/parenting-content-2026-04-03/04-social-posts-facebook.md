#### Long-Form Post -- 10:30 AM MT

**Long-Form Post (10:30 AM MT)**

```
If you've been hearing confusing things about childhood vaccines lately — you're not imagining it. Here's what's actually going on, in plain language.

The American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) has published its own 2026 Recommended Childhood and Adolescent Immunization Schedule in the journal Pediatrics. It recommends vaccination against 18 diseases. That puts it at odds with the revised CDC schedule (shaped by RFK Jr.'s restructuring of the vaccine advisory committee), which moved to recommending only 11 routine vaccinations. Six vaccines — Hepatitis A, Hepatitis B, rotavirus, flu/influenza, RSV, and meningococcal disease — were moved from "routine" recommendations to something called "shared clinical decision-making." The AAP has called those changes "dangerous and unnecessary."

Here's where it stands legally: on March 16, a U.S. District Court judge blocked the CDC's new vaccine schedule, ruling that the restructured advisory committee was "unlawfully constituted" and that its decisions were "arbitrary and capricious." The June 2024 CDC schedule has been restored while litigation continues. The AAP was one of the plaintiffs in that case.

What does this mean for your family? The most practical thing you can do is ask your pediatrician which schedule they're following and whether any of your child's upcoming vaccines might be affected. Most AAP-member pediatricians will follow the AAP's broader schedule. Insurance coverage for some vaccines may also be in flux — worth a direct call to your provider.

This is genuinely confusing, and we know it. We're here to help cut through the noise.
```
