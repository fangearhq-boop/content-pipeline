AAP vs. CDC: The 2026 Childhood Vaccine Schedule, Explained for Colorado Springs Parents

By Maria Chen | COS Parenting | April 3, 2026

If you've been confused by recent news about childhood vaccines — which schedule is in effect, what happened to the recommendations, and what you should tell your pediatrician — you're not alone. Here's what's actually going on.

What Happened

For decades, the American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) and the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) jointly recommended a unified childhood immunization schedule. That collaboration broke down in 2026.

The CDC, under the direction of Health and Human Services Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr., restructured its Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices (ACIP) — the expert panel that informs the CDC's vaccine recommendations. The restructured committee produced a revised 2026 schedule that reduced the number of routinely recommended vaccinations from 18 diseases to 11. Six vaccines were moved from "routine" recommendations to a category called "shared clinical decision-making," meaning they became optional rather than standard of care: Hepatitis A, Hepatitis B, rotavirus, flu/influenza, RSV, and meningococcal disease.

The AAP disagreed strongly. The organization published its own 2026 Recommended Childhood and Adolescent Immunization Schedule in the journal Pediatrics, maintaining all 18 disease recommendations and calling the CDC's reductions "dangerous and unnecessary."

What the Court Said

On March 16, 2026, U.S. District Judge Brian E. Murphy issued a court injunction blocking the CDC's new vaccine schedule from taking effect. The judge found that the restructured ACIP committee was "unlawfully constituted" and that its decisions were "arbitrary and capricious." As a result of the injunction, the June 2024 CDC vaccine schedule — the one in effect before the RFK Jr. changes — has been restored and remains in effect while the legal case continues through the courts.

The AAP was among the plaintiffs in the lawsuit. Other medical organizations joined the case, arguing that the changes to the CDC's advisory committee structure violated federal law.

What This Means for COS Families Right Now

Here is the practical situation as of April 2026:

  • The June 2024 CDC schedule is legally in effect, pending litigation.
  • Most AAP-member pediatricians are following the AAP's own 2026 schedule, which recommends the broader set of vaccinations.
  • Insurance coverage for some vaccines may be uncertain — particularly for those moved to "shared clinical decision-making" under the now-blocked CDC changes.

The most useful thing a COS parent can do right now is call their pediatrician's office and ask two questions: which immunization schedule do they follow, and whether any of the child's upcoming vaccines might be affected by the current policy uncertainty. Most pediatricians who are AAP members will maintain the broader schedule and can explain their position clearly.

What We're Not Saying

This article is not an opinion on vaccine policy or politics. It is a factual summary of what happened, what was ruled, and what the current state of childhood vaccine recommendations is in the United States as of April 2026. Our goal is to give Colorado Springs parents accurate, complete information so they can have informed conversations with their children's medical providers.

The AAP's position — and the court's ruling — means that the standard of care your child's pediatrician follows has not changed in practice for most families. But the policy landscape is in flux, and staying informed is worth the effort.

What's Next

The legal case challenging the CDC's restructured advisory committee will continue through the federal courts. The AAP has said it will continue publishing its own schedule to give pediatricians and families clear, evidence-based guidance regardless of the outcome.

For the most current information, the AAP's HealthyChildren.org website (healthychildren.org) is the most reliable parent-facing source. For any specific questions about your child's vaccination schedule, your pediatrician is your best resource.
