Flanagan Park in central Colorado Springs is about to look very different — and very much better.

The City of Colorado Springs has secured a $750,000 federal community development grant to fund a comprehensive renovation of the park at 3132 N. Prospect Street. For families in the neighborhood, the changes are significant: a brand-new playground, an ADA-accessible walking path with pedestrian lighting and benches, new screen fencing, a resurfaced basketball court, and upgrades to the existing landscaping, irrigation system, and picnic pavilion.

The park was originally built in the mid-1970s. It has not received a major refresh since, and maintenance needs have accumulated over decades due to budget constraints — particularly after a significant city budget cut in 2008.

A Project Shaped by the Community

This renovation did not happen by accident. The City gathered community input over several years to understand what residents actually wanted from their neighborhood park. The priorities that emerged — accessible paths, updated playground equipment, better lighting — directly shaped the final project scope.

Flanagan Park serves a diverse, multi-generational neighborhood that includes families with young children, older adults, and people in assisted living facilities. The ADA-accessible path is not an afterthought — it is one of the centerpieces of the project, specifically designed to make the park usable for people who previously could not access it.

"Community input gathered over several years has directly shaped the priorities for this project," the city's project page notes, "and the project supports equitable access to neighborhood park space."

Why This Matters for COS Families

Neighborhood parks are often the most used green spaces in a city — and the ones most likely to be overlooked when budgets are tight. Flanagan Park is a clear example of what happens when a park does not receive consistent investment: the equipment ages, the surfaces deteriorate, and access for people with disabilities remains an afterthought.

The $750,000 grant changes that picture. New playground equipment means children in the neighborhood have a safe, modern structure to play on. The ADA path means parents with strollers, grandparents with walkers, and residents who use wheelchairs can all move through the park comfortably. The lighting improves safety and extends usability into the evening.

This is the kind of infrastructure that makes a neighborhood livable for families.

What's Next

Construction and renovation timelines will be posted on the City of Colorado Springs parks website at coloradosprings.gov/FlanaganPark. Families in the surrounding neighborhoods should check there for project updates and anticipated completion dates. Once the project is complete, Flanagan Park will offer one of the most accessible and complete neighborhood park experiences in central Colorado Springs.
