CPSC Renews "Do Not Use" Warning for Otteroo Infant Neck Floats as New Federal Safety Standard Takes Effect

The company has refused a recall — if your baby uses an Otteroo LUMI or MINI, stop now

By Jamie Rivera

June 16, 2026

On June 15, 2026, the U.S. Consumer Product Safety Commission renewed its "Do Not Use" warning for Otteroo LUMI and MINI infant neck floats — the same day the first-ever federal safety standard for these products officially took effect. For Colorado Springs families heading into a summer of pool visits, splash pads, and water play, the timing makes this warning urgent.

If you have an Otteroo infant neck float, the CPSC's guidance is clear: stop using it immediately.

What Is the Hazard

Infant neck floats are inflatable rings worn around a baby's neck to support them in water. The Otteroo devices were marketed as a way for infants to experience movement and buoyancy before they could independently hold their heads up.

The hazard is deflation. If an Otteroo LUMI or MINI loses air — whether through a leak, a valve failure, or simply not being inflated fully — an infant can slip through the neck opening and become submerged in water. Because the product is designed for use with very young infants who cannot lift their own heads, even a brief submersion can be fatal.

The CPSC's original warning, issued in 2022, followed one reported infant drowning death, one serious injury, and dozens of incidents in which caregivers had to pull infants from the water when the device failed or the infant slipped through.

Why the Warning Was Renewed Now

The CPSC renewed its warning on June 15, 2026 specifically because a new mandatory federal safety standard for infant neck floats took effect that same day. The standard establishes the first enforceable design and performance requirements for these products in the United States.

Otteroo's existing LUMI and MINI products do not meet those new standards. The company has not agreed to a recall and continues to manufacture and sell the floats. Because the company has declined to take corrective action, the CPSC is relying on public awareness — and parental decisions — to prevent further incidents.

This is a meaningful distinction from a standard product recall. When a company voluntarily recalls a product, it typically stops selling it and offers refunds or replacements. Otteroo has done neither. The warning remains a warning, not a recall, because the company has refused to cooperate with the CPSC's request for corrective action.

What to Do

If you own an Otteroo LUMI or MINI — or any other infant neck float that does not meet the new federal safety standard — the CPSC recommends the following steps:

  • Stop using the product immediately
  • Do not donate or resell it
  • Report incidents or injuries to CPSC.gov or by calling 1-800-638-2772

For general water safety with infants and young children this summer, the American Academy of Pediatrics recommends touch supervision — keeping a hand on or within arm's reach of a child any time they are in or near water, without any distractions.

What's Next

The new federal safety standard for infant neck floats applies to products manufactured after June 15, 2026. Families looking for alternatives to the Otteroo should check that any water support product they purchase meets the new CPSC standard. For questions about specific products or to check the CPSC's full list of recalled and warned products, visit cpsc.gov. The CPSC continues to monitor the infant neck float market for compliance with the new standard.
