Hot Car Safety 2026: What Colorado Springs Parents Need to Know This Summer

By Sarah Morales | COS Parenting | June 23, 2026

Nine children have died from vehicular heatstroke in the United States so far in 2026. Summer is in full swing, Colorado Springs temperatures are climbing, and parked cars become heat chambers faster than most caregivers realize.

This is a reminder worth reading now, before the hottest weeks arrive.

Why Cars Get So Dangerous So Fast

The interior of a parked car can rise by 20 degrees Fahrenheit in just 20 minutes — even on a mild day. On a 70°F day, a car interior can reach 110°F within an hour. Children's bodies heat 3 to 5 times faster than an adult's, and a child faces serious risk when their internal body temperature reaches 107°F.

Colorado Springs regularly sees afternoon temperatures in the upper 80s to low 90s during July and August. On those days, a parked car in direct sun can reach 140°F or higher. Cracking windows does not meaningfully reduce interior temperature in direct sunlight.

Who Is at Risk

Children under age 3 are most vulnerable to heatstroke because their bodies are less efficient at regulating temperature, they cannot communicate distress clearly, and they're often rear-facing in back seats where they're out of a driver's natural line of sight.

The 2026 fatalities span California, Florida, Louisiana, Maryland, New Mexico, New Jersey, and North Carolina — distributed across the country in different climate types, which underscores that this is not a "hot climate" problem exclusively. Any day with warm temperatures and a parked car is a risk factor.

It Happens to Attentive Parents

Safety experts consistently report that the overwhelming majority of hot car deaths are not caused by neglect. They happen when a loving, attentive caregiver has a routine disruption — a schedule change, a distraction, an unusual drop-off — and the child in the back seat temporarily falls outside their awareness. This fact is often uncomfortable to discuss, but it's critical: the solution is not better parenting. It's better habits and systems.

Prevention Strategies That Work

The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) recommends these practices:

  • Always check the back seat before locking the car — make it a ritual, every single time, whether or not you think a child is in the car
  • Place something you need in the back seat — phone, work bag, left shoe — so you're physically drawn to check before leaving the car
  • Use the "ACT" reminder: Avoid heatstroke; Create reminders; Take action if you see a child alone in a car
  • If you see a child alone in a hot car, call 911 immediately — do not wait to locate the parent or caregiver first

Some newer vehicles include rear seat reminders that alert drivers when rear doors were used before the trip. If your vehicle has this feature, make sure it's enabled in your settings.

What's Next

The peak risk period for hot car incidents is June through September. If you have young children, review your car routine today — especially if school is out and drop-off schedules have changed. Resources on prevention, including reminders and community awareness tools, are available at nhtsa.gov and kidsandcars.org.
