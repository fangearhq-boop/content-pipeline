Colorado Springs Adds 22 New School Zones as Part of Safe Streets COS Safety Initiative

By Sarah Morales | COS Parenting | May 23, 2026

The City of Colorado Springs launched Safe Streets COS on May 14, 2026 — a traffic safety initiative that includes 22 new school zones installed across the city, new flashing pedestrian beacons at two schools, and expanded mobile speed enforcement in areas near schools. The initiative is part of a broader city goal to reduce serious and deadly crashes by 35 percent before 2035.

For families who drive or walk near schools in Colorado Springs, the changes are already in effect. Enforcement around school zones has increased significantly, and two school locations are in line for new beacon infrastructure before the next school year begins.

What Changed

The city installed 22 new designated school zones citywide as part of the Safe Streets COS launch. These zones carry reduced speed limits during school hours and, under the expanded enforcement push, are receiving increased attention from mobile speed enforcement units.

Two schools are also getting new flashing beacon infrastructure:

  • Sierra High School — New flashing pedestrian beacons are expected to be operational for the 2026-27 school year.
  • Panorama Middle School — New flashing signs are expected to be in place by the end of summer 2026.

Flashing beacons increase driver awareness of pedestrian crossings, particularly during arrival and dismissal times when foot traffic near schools is highest. Both schools serve families in areas of the city where pedestrian and bicycle commuting to school is common.

The Enforcement Numbers

The city reported that total traffic enforcement has increased by 50 percent since 2023 citywide. Enforcement specifically around school zones has increased by 200 percent over the same period — a figure that reflects a deliberate shift in where police and traffic resources are concentrated.

Mobile speed enforcement — camera-equipped units that can be deployed to specific locations — is a key part of the school zone strategy. These units can be repositioned based on complaint patterns, incident data, or scheduled enforcement sweeps, which makes them more flexible than fixed radar signs or camera installations.

Families should expect to see mobile enforcement units near school zones during drop-off and pick-up hours. The city has not published a fixed schedule for these deployments, which is intentional — unpredictable enforcement tends to produce more consistent driver behavior than enforcement on a known timetable.

The Broader Goal

Safe Streets COS is Colorado Springs' formal commitment to reducing traffic fatalities and serious injuries on city streets. The 35 percent reduction target by 2035 aligns with Vision Zero frameworks that many U.S. cities have adopted over the past decade — the underlying principle being that traffic deaths and serious injuries are preventable, not inevitable.

School zones are a specific priority within that framework because they concentrate vulnerable road users — children on foot, on bikes, or being dropped off by parents — at predictable times and locations. The combination of new zone designations, flashing beacon infrastructure, and increased enforcement is meant to address the three main factors in school-area crashes: speed, driver inattention, and unclear pedestrian crossing points.

The city has directed residents with questions about specific school zone locations, the new beacon installations, or the broader Safe Streets COS plan to coloradosprings.gov/SafeStreetsCOS.

What This Means for Families

For parents who drive their children to school or pick them up during dismissal, the practical implication is straightforward: school zone speed limits are being enforced more aggressively than they were in previous years. The 200 percent increase in enforcement near school zones is not a background statistic — it means more citations are being issued in those areas.

For families who walk or bike with children to school, the infrastructure improvements at Sierra High and Panorama Middle represent a tangible safety improvement at crossings that previously lacked active warning signals. When the beacons are active, drivers approaching those crossings will receive a visual alert that pedestrians are present or crossing.

For families at schools not yet on the beacon list, the 22 new school zone designations still carry enforcement weight. A school zone designation without a beacon still reduces the legal speed limit during school hours and signals increased patrol attention to that area.

What's Next

The flashing beacon installations at Sierra High School and Panorama Middle School are the next visible milestones in the Safe Streets COS rollout. Families near those schools can expect the Panorama beacons before fall 2026 and the Sierra beacons active when school resumes for the 2026-27 year. The city has not announced a next phase of school zone additions beyond the initial 22, but the broader Safe Streets COS initiative is ongoing through 2035. For updates on new school zone designations, enforcement patterns, or infrastructure projects, visit coloradosprings.gov/SafeStreetsCOS or follow the City of Colorado Springs on its official communications channels.
