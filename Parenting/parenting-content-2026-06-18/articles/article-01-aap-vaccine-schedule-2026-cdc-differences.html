AAP vs. CDC Vaccine Schedules 2026: What Colorado Springs Families Need to Know

The two schedules differ on 7 vaccines — and your pediatrician is most likely following the AAP

By Sarah Morales

June 18, 2026

If you've seen headlines about the CDC cutting its childhood vaccine recommendations and you're wondering what it means at your child's next checkup, here's the important context: most pediatricians follow the American Academy of Pediatrics schedule, not the CDC's. And the AAP has maintained its full schedule for 2026, covering 18 diseases — even as the CDC's updated list was reduced to 11.

For COS families with children in regular pediatric care, this divergence is worth understanding. It affects what vaccines are recommended at well-child visits, when some vaccines begin, and how your doctor approaches a few specific decisions.

What Changed

The CDC updated its childhood immunization schedule in 2026, narrowing its routine recommendations significantly. The AAP reviewed the same landscape and reached a different conclusion — maintaining its existing recommendations for all 18 diseases it has historically covered. The AAP's schedule is co-authored and endorsed by 12 medical associations, including the American Medical Association and the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists.

The AAP explicitly stated that it no longer endorses the CDC's childhood immunization schedule as of 2026 — which is the first time the two organizations have published divergent recommendations in recent memory. Pediatricians who are AAP members, which represents the large majority of practicing pediatricians, are expected to follow the AAP schedule.

Which Vaccines Are Different

The AAP continues to recommend six vaccines that the CDC removed from its routine recommendation list in 2026: Hepatitis A, Hepatitis B, rotavirus, meningococcal disease, RSV, and influenza (flu) for all children. On the CDC's updated schedule, these are no longer routine recommendations.

Two additional differences involve timing rather than inclusion:

On COVID-19 vaccines, the AAP recommends vaccination for all children ages 6 to 23 months, plus older children who are in higher-risk groups. The CDC has moved COVID-19 to a shared clinical decision-making category, meaning it leaves the choice to patients and their doctors rather than recommending it routinely for any age group.

On HPV, the AAP recommends starting the two-dose series between ages 9 and 12. The CDC recommends one dose at ages 11 to 12. This reflects both a dosing difference and a timing difference that can affect when a child's HPV protection begins.

What This Means at Your Child's Next Appointment

For COS families, the practical answer is straightforward: if your child sees a pediatrician who is an AAP member — which is the case for most practicing pediatricians across El Paso County — that doctor is likely using the AAP schedule. Your child's recommended vaccines are unlikely to have changed based on the CDC's update alone.

That said, this is exactly the kind of change worth raising at your next well-child visit. You can ask your pediatrician directly which schedule they follow and what, if anything, has changed for your child's upcoming recommendations. Most pediatricians will welcome the question.

The AAP's parent-facing resource, healthychildren.org, has the full 2026 immunization schedule and a tool to look up what vaccines are recommended by your child's age. It's a useful reference to bring to appointments.

Why the AAP and CDC Diverged

The AAP's position is that maintaining the broader schedule reflects the best available evidence for preventing serious childhood illness. The organization has not signaled any intention to align with the CDC's reduced list. The AAP schedule carries the weight of 12 co-endorsing medical associations, which gives pediatricians a strong clinical basis for continuing to follow it.

The CDC's changes reflect a narrowing of what the federal health agency includes in its routine recommendation category, though the specific policy reasoning behind each removal has not been uniformly explained in public-facing materials. Parents who want to understand the CDC's rationale for any specific vaccine can find its published documentation at cdc.gov.

What's Next

Before your child's next well-child visit, review the AAP's current schedule at healthychildren.org and note which vaccines are due for your child's age. Write down any questions you have about the AAP-CDC differences — your pediatrician is the right person to address them in the context of your child's health history. If you've recently changed providers or your child is new to a pediatric practice, it's worth confirming early which schedule that practice follows. The AAP and its member pediatricians have been clear: the 2026 AAP schedule stands, and children in regular care should continue on it.
