Joyin Sloosh Dive Sticks Recalled: Impalement Risk, Violates Federal Pool Toy Ban

Check your pool bag now — these should not have been sold in the first place

By Jamie Rivera

June 18, 2026

If you have Joyin Sloosh brand dive sticks in your pool bag, stop using them. The Consumer Product Safety Commission has issued a recall of approximately 113,000 units due to an impalement hazard — and the reason goes beyond a manufacturing defect. Dive sticks as a category are federally banned, and Joyin sold these anyway.

With pool season fully underway in Colorado Springs — public pools, HOA pools, and backyard pools all in use — families who picked these up at a major retailer this spring may have them ready to go for the summer. Now is the time to check.

Why Dive Sticks Are Dangerous

The federal ban on dive sticks exists for a specific reason. Hard plastic sticks thrown into a pool sink to the bottom, and children dive or reach to retrieve them. In shallow water, the combination of a pointed or blunt-ended rigid stick and a child moving toward it at speed creates real risk of piercing injuries. The CPSC also cites eye and facial injuries that can occur during retrieval.

This is not a new concern — the federal ban on this product category was established precisely because the injury pattern was well-documented. Joyin's Sloosh-brand dive sticks violated that existing ban. These should never have been on store shelves.

Where These Were Sold

The Joyin Sloosh dive sticks were available at Amazon, Walmart, and Target — three of the most common sources for summer pool toy purchases. At roughly $7 to $15 per set, they were in a price range where families might grab them without much second thought alongside other pool essentials.

If you purchased dive sticks at any of those retailers in the past year or two and aren't certain of the brand, check the packaging or the product itself for the Joyin or Sloosh name. You can also check your order history in your Amazon, Walmart, or Target accounts.

What to Do

Stop using the sticks immediately. Do not bring them to the pool again, and do not give them to another family. Remove them from your pool bag now so they don't accidentally get packed this week.

To get a refund, contact Joyin directly. The full recall page at cpsc.gov has the current contact information and any updates to the remedy process. Do not attempt to use or modify the sticks in any way — the hazard is inherent to the product design, not something that can be corrected at home.

If you want to report an incident or near-miss with this product, the CPSC accepts consumer reports at cpsc.gov or by phone at 1-800-638-2772.

Alternatives for Pool Play

The goal of dive sticks — giving kids something to retrieve from the bottom of the pool — is a popular one. There are alternatives that don't carry the same impalement risk. Flat, disc-shaped dive toys or rings that settle flush to the pool bottom are generally considered safer options, as they don't create the same pointed-impact scenario. Check the product category and any CPSC guidance before purchasing replacements.

Colorado Springs public pools and most HOA-managed pools post their own rules around pool toys. If you're unsure whether a toy is permitted or appropriate, pool staff are usually a good first resource.

What's Next

Remove the Joyin Sloosh dive sticks from your pool bag today and contact Joyin for a refund. The full recall details, including current contact information for Joyin, are at cpsc.gov — search "Joyin Sloosh" in the recall database. If you're looking for safe alternatives, focus on dive toys that are flat, flexible, or ring-shaped rather than rigid stick-style products. Share this information with other families you know who have pools this summer — with approximately 113,000 units sold, these are likely in a number of COS pool bags right now.
