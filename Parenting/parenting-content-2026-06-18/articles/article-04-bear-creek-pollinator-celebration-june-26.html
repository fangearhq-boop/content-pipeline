If you're looking for something genuinely educational to do with kids next Thursday, Bear Creek Nature Center has it. The annual Pollinator Celebration is coming up June 26, and at $5 per person it's one of the more affordable family science experiences Colorado Springs offers in the summer months.

What Happens at a Pollinator Celebration

The focus is on native pollinators — bees, butterflies, moths, and beetles that play essential roles in the ecosystems surrounding Colorado Springs. The event includes bug sweeps, which are hands-on activities where participants use nets to collect insects from vegetation and then examine what they've caught. For kids who learn by doing rather than by watching, bug sweeps are the kind of activity that makes nature education stick.

The event also includes educational programming about native pollinator species, their habitats, and why they matter. Bear Creek Nature Center staff and naturalists lead the activities, so families with questions get answers from people who know the local ecology well.

This isn't a sit-and-listen event — it's structured for active participation, which makes it well-suited for kids roughly ages 4 and up who can follow organized activities and engage with hands-on materials.

About Bear Creek Nature Center

Bear Creek Nature Center is part of Bear Creek Regional Park, which spans the west side of Colorado Springs along the Bear Creek corridor. The nature center operates as an environmental education facility run by El Paso County Parks and Recreation, offering programs for school groups, families, and individuals throughout the year.

The park itself is a well-used family destination — it has trail access into the foothills, picnic areas, and the creek corridor that runs through it. The nature center sits at the hub of the park's educational programming, and the Pollinator Celebration is one of its signature summer events.

The west-side location makes Bear Creek accessible from most of Colorado Springs without a long drive, including from Manitou Springs, the 21st Street corridor, and neighborhoods on the south and central parts of the city.

Why Pollinator Education Matters Right Now

Native pollinator populations have been under pressure in Colorado and across the country, with habitat loss and climate shifts affecting the bee and butterfly species that plants — including food crops — depend on. Events like the Pollinator Celebration connect that broader picture to something tangible and local: here are the pollinators that live in the Bear Creek watershed, here's how they interact with native plants, here's what a healthy pollinator environment looks like up close.

For children, the experience of actually catching and examining insects — rather than just seeing them on a screen — creates a different kind of engagement with the natural world. It's the kind of memory that can shape how a kid thinks about outdoor spaces for years.

What to Bring

Wear clothes you don't mind getting grass stains on, since bug sweeps involve moving through vegetation. Closed-toe shoes are recommended for outdoor activities at the nature center. Sunscreen and water are standard summer hiking essentials and apply here too. The $5 admission is per person, so plan accordingly for the full group. Check with Bear Creek Nature Center directly to confirm whether registration is required in advance or if this is a walk-in event.
