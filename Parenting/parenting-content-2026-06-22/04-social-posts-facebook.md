### STORY 1: GOPO Toys Teething Toy Recall

#### Long-Form Post — 8:00 AM MT

If you have a GOPO Toys teething toy with silicone pull strings, please check it right away.

The Consumer Product Safety Commission announced a recall on June 18 covering more than 70,000 of these toys. The hazard is serious: the toy's silicone strings are smaller and longer than the federal safety standard allows, and they can reach the back of a child's throat and become lodged — creating a choking risk. Three families have already reported incidents involving choking or respiratory distress.

The toy is off-white and disc-shaped with a gray ball in the center and six multi-colored silicone pull strings that look like tentacles. It was sold on Amazon between August 2023 and March 2026 for about $11–$15.

Here's what to do if you have one: stop using it immediately, cut and discard all of the silicone string tentacles, write "DESTROYED" on the toy body with permanent marker, and email a photo of the destroyed toy to recalls@gopotoys.com to begin the refund process.

Batch numbers to check on the packaging: 250905, 250530, 250120, 240315, 231005, or 230610. If you're unsure, cpsc.gov has the full recall details, or you can call 1-800-638-2772.

### STORY 2: PPLD Summer Adventure

#### Long-Form Post — 10:00 AM MT

If you haven't signed your family up for PPLD's Summer Adventure yet, this is your reminder.

The Pikes Peak Library District's free summer program runs through July 31 and it's designed for everyone — babies, kids, teens, and adults. The theme this year is "Unearth a Story." To participate, you complete 30 days of activities over the summer: reading books, listening to audiobooks, attending library programs, making things, trying new activities. You track everything through the free Beanstack app.

The prizes are worth it. Grand prizes include a Pikes Peak Region Attractions VIP Access Pass (free and discounted admission to local attractions) and a one-night stay at The Broadmoor Cloud Camp for two.

There's more: PPLD East Library at 5550 N. Union Blvd. is partnering with D11 to offer free lunches for children and teens, Monday through Friday through July 31. Free books are also available to take home.

To sign up: go to ppld.org/summer, download the Beanstack app, or walk into any library branch and register in person. It's free, it takes five minutes, and it's running right now.

### STORY 3: D11 Free Summer Meals

#### Long-Form Post — 12:00 PM MT

This is one of those things that's worth sharing widely: D11 is offering free summer meals to every child ages 1–18 in Colorado Springs — and you don't need to be a D11 student to access them.

Free breakfast and lunch are available at schools, community centers, and playgrounds across the city. The program is part of the federally funded Summer Food Service Program, and it's available all summer.

One standout site is PPLD East Library at 5550 N. Union Blvd., where lunches are served Monday through Friday through July 31 — and while you're there, kids can also pick up free books to take home.

To find the nearest meal site, go to d11.org or visit kidsfoodfinder.org, which maps free summer food sites across Colorado Springs and the surrounding area. No paperwork, no income verification — just show up.

### STORY 4: COS Free Splash Pads Guide

#### Long-Form Post — 3:00 PM MT

Summer water fun in Colorado Springs doesn't have to cost anything.

All of the city's spray grounds and interactive fountains are free to use. Here's where to find them this summer:

The Watering Hole at John Venezia Community Park is one of the most popular spots — it sits right next to a universally accessible playground and is open 10 AM to 6 PM daily.

Panorama Park Splash Pad in southeast Colorado Springs is also open 10 AM to 6 PM daily.

Deerfield Hills Spray Ground features more than 50 spray nozzles and 16 water features, open 10 AM to 5 PM daily.

Uncle Wilber Fountain at Acacia Park downtown runs noon to 6 PM daily — and it's right in the heart of the city if you're already downtown.

One heads-up: the Julie Penrose Fountain at America the Beautiful Park will not operate this season due to 2026 budget adjustments, so if you've taken kids there before, plan accordingly.

For those who prefer a pool with lifeguards, the YMCA outdoor pools are available for $10 per youth, $15 per adult, or $45 for a family day pass.

### STORY 5: Screen Time and Kids' Mental Health

#### Long-Form Post — 5:00 PM MT

A new 2026 study adds to the growing body of research on kids and screen time — and the findings are specific enough to be actionable.

The study, published in the journal Humanities and Social Sciences Communications, analyzed data from more than 50,000 children and teens ages 6–17 across the United States. The research found that excessive screen time is associated with higher rates of anxiety, depression, behavior and conduct problems, and ADHD symptoms. That part isn't surprising. What's notable is what buffers those effects: physical activity and adequate sleep. Kids who get enough movement and rest show fewer negative outcomes even when screen time is higher.

The American Academy of Pediatrics updated its guidance in 2026 to reflect this nuance. The AAP now emphasizes quality, context, and conversation over strict time limits. The guidelines still recommend no screens before 18 months, and limiting to one hour per day of high-quality content for ages 2–5 — but the overall message is less about the clock and more about what children are watching, whether parents are watching with them, and whether screens are crowding out sleep and movement.

Practical takeaways: device-free bedrooms, screen-free mealtimes, and co-viewing with conversation are the three habits the AAP consistently points to.
