Summer Swim Safety: What Colorado Springs Families Need to Know Before Memorial Day Weekend

By Keisha Williams | COS Parenting | May 5, 2026

Memorial Day weekend is less than three weeks away, and water season in Colorado Springs is nearly here. This is the moment to get ahead of swim safety — not the first hot day when kids are already at the pool, lake, or splash pad.

The stakes are not abstract. Drowning is the leading cause of unintentional death among children ages 1 to 4 in the United States. It's also different from what most people imagine: drowning is quiet. A child in distress in the water often cannot call for help, wave their arms, or make the splashing sounds that people associate with an emergency. It can happen in the presence of nearby adults who are momentarily distracted.

The American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) and the YMCA have both renewed calls this week for parents to establish water safety habits now, before pools open and summer travel begins. The good news: the steps that reduce drowning risk significantly are practical and accessible for most Colorado Springs families.

AAP Water Safety Guidelines

The AAP recommends a layered approach to drowning prevention — no single step is enough on its own, but together they provide meaningful protection.

Swim lessons are the first layer. The AAP says swim lessons can begin for many children starting at age 1. This is an update from older guidance that suggested waiting until age 4; research has shown that earlier instruction provides a meaningful protective benefit. Lessons at this age focus on basic water safety and breath control, not competitive strokes. If your child is under 3 and hasn't started lessons yet, many programs have spots available before summer.

Pool fencing is the second critical layer. If you have a backyard pool or live in a home with one, 4-sided fencing is the standard — not a 3-sided fence that uses the house as the fourth wall. The fence should have a self-closing, self-latching gate that opens away from the pool. A door with a lock is not a substitute for proper pool fencing.

"Touch supervision" means an adult is within arm's length of any young child in or near water — at all times. For pools, lakes, rivers, or even backyard water tables, the adult designated as the watcher should not be doing anything else: no phone, no book, no conversation that takes their eyes off the water. When supervision responsibility transfers from one adult to another, the handoff should be explicit and verbal.

Life jackets for open water are non-negotiable. Any time children are near rivers, lakes, or reservoirs — including Cheyenne Mountain State Park's lake or reservoir day trips — they should wear a properly fitted, U.S. Coast Guard-approved life jacket. Arm floaties and puddle jumpers are not substitutes for life jackets and are not approved for open-water use.

Colorado Springs Water Safety Resources

The YMCA of the Pikes Peak Region offers swimming lessons for children at multiple ages and ability levels. Their programs serve the COS metro area and fill up quickly heading into summer — families interested in spring or early summer sessions should check ymcapikes.org now for availability and registration.

Colorado Springs Parks and Recreation operates several outdoor pools that open for the summer season in late May and June. Schedules and session information are available at coloradosprings.gov. Colorado Parks and Wildlife also has programs at some state park locations that include basic water safety components for younger visitors.

If your family is planning any summer trips to Cheyenne Mountain State Park, Mueller State Park, or mountain lakes outside of COS, bring life jackets and don't assume that natural water settings have lifeguards present — most do not.

What's Next

Memorial Day weekend is May 23–25 in 2026. Before then, this is the checklist: enroll your child in swim lessons if they haven't started; check your pool fencing if you have one; designate a water watcher when hosting gatherings near water; and confirm you have properly-sized life jackets ready for any open-water outings. The YMCA of the Pikes Peak Region can be reached at ymcapikes.org, and the AAP's full drowning prevention guidance is available at healthychildren.org.
