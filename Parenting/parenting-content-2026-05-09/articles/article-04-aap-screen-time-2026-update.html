The AAP Just Changed Screen Time Guidelines — Here's What COS Families Need to Know

By Sarah Morales | May 9, 2026 | COS Parenting

The American Academy of Pediatrics updated its screen time guidance in 2026, dropping the rigid two-hours-a-day cap for older children and replacing it with a more flexible, family-centered approach.

What Changed

For the past decade, many parents operated under a simple mental rule borrowed from the American Academy of Pediatrics: two hours of screen time per day is the limit for school-age kids. That number was easy to remember, easy to cite, and equally easy to feel guilty about ignoring.

In 2026, the AAP updated its guidance — and the two-hour ceiling for older children is no longer part of the official recommendation. For school-age kids and teens, the new framework replaces a hard number with a quality-first, family-centered approach.

What the New Guidelines Say

Age Group New AAP Guidance
Under 18 months Avoid screen media other than video chat (unchanged)
Ages 2-5 Limit to 1 hour per day of high-quality content (unchanged)
Ages 6 and up No hard time limit; focus on quality, context, and balance (changed)

The AAP's shift reflects a pragmatic acknowledgment: screens are now a fundamental part of modern life, education, and social connection. A blanket time rule designed before smartphones were ubiquitous was increasingly difficult for families to apply meaningfully.

What Stays the Same — The Core Principles

While the time limit is gone for older kids, the underlying concerns that drove the original guidance haven't changed. The AAP still wants families to ensure that screens don't displace the things that matter most for children's development:

  • Sleep — devices in bedrooms at night are a known sleep disruptor
  • Physical activity — kids still need daily movement
  • Family time — connection without a screen in the room
  • Free play — unstructured time away from structured content

The new guidance also emphasizes co-viewing — watching content with your child and discussing it — as one of the most protective practices available. A parent watching a show with their child and asking questions about what they're seeing is doing something fundamentally different than a child passively absorbing content alone for hours.

Practical Habits for COS Families

The AAP's updated framework suggests a few specific boundaries that research supports as genuinely protective:

  • Device-free bedrooms: Remove screens from kids' rooms at night; charge phones outside the bedroom
  • Device-free mealtimes: No phones at the dinner table — for parents and kids alike
  • Intentional use: Ask whether a given screen activity serves a real purpose — learning, creativity, connection — or is just filling time
  • Model what you want to see: Kids are watching how adults use screens, not just what adults say about them

What's Next

The AAP's full updated guidance is available at HealthyChildren.org, the Academy's family-facing resource hub. If you have questions specific to your child's screen use — particularly around age-appropriate content, gaming habits, or social media — your child's pediatrician is the best resource for a conversation tailored to your family.
