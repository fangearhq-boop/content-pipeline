CPSC Recalls 124,560 Light-Up Toys — Button Battery Risk in $1 Discount Store Items

By Jamie Rivera | May 9, 2026 | COS Parenting

The Consumer Product Safety Commission recalled 124,560 battery-operated light-up toys last week. Here's what was recalled, what the danger is, and exactly how to get a refund.

What Was Recalled

On May 7, 2026, the Consumer Product Safety Commission issued a recall of 124,560 battery-operated light-up toys manufactured and distributed by ZMC Group. The recalled products are small novelty toys that were sold at discount stores across the United States from May 2023 through April 2026 for approximately $1 each.

The recalled items include:

  • Light-up yo-yos
  • Light-up maracas
  • Halloween-style light-up wands
  • Light-up glow sticks

If you've picked up any of these kinds of small, cheap, battery-powered novelty toys from dollar-type discount stores in the past few years, it's worth checking whether they're covered by this recall.

What the Danger Is

The safety hazard is serious: the toys violate mandatory federal safety standards because button cell batteries inside them are easily accessible to children.

Button cell batteries — the small, round, coin-shaped batteries used in many small electronics — are among the most dangerous household objects for young children if swallowed. When ingested, these batteries generate an electrical current that can cause severe chemical burns to internal tissue within minutes. The damage can be irreversible and, in some cases, fatal.

Children don't always show immediate symptoms after swallowing a battery, which can delay diagnosis and treatment. This is why any suspected battery ingestion should be treated as a medical emergency.

As of the recall date, no injuries from these specific toys had been reported — but the CPSC acts when the hazard is present, regardless of incident history.

What to Do Right Now

Step Action
1 Stop using the recalled toys immediately
2 Take them away from children now
3 Destroy the toy: break it in half, or write "RECALLED" or a large "X" on it with a permanent marker
4 Take a photo of the destroyed toy
5 Email the photo to recallzmctoy@gmail.com to receive a full refund

You can also contact ZMC Group directly by phone at 888-407-3225, Monday through Friday, 9:30 AM to 5:30 PM Pacific Time. Additional information is available at zmcgroupinc.com — look for the "Recall" button at the top of the page.

Why These Toys Are Likely in Many Homes

These aren't expensive specialty toys. At $1, they're the kind of item that ends up in birthday goody bags, Halloween treat bags, stocking stuffers, and kids' backpacks without much planning or thought. Many families may not even remember acquiring them. A quick scan of toy bins, drawers, and bags is worth the two minutes it takes.

For any questions about this or other product recalls, visit cpsc.gov or call the CPSC Consumer Hotline at 800-638-2772.

What's Next

If you have these toys, act now: stop use, destroy, and email recallzmctoy@gmail.com for a refund. If you suspect your child has swallowed a battery — from any device, not just these toys — call Poison Control at 800-222-1222 or go to the emergency room immediately.
