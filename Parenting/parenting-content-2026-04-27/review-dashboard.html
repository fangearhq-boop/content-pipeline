CPSC Recall — SEGMART Mini Toddler Trampolines (Strangulation Hazard)
NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post Heads up: if you bought a SEGMART mini toddler trampoline from Walmart.com or TikTok Shop in the last few months, the CPSC has issued a recall you need to know about. The recall covers 55" SEGMART mini round trampolines — model numbers SOSTT051BR, SOSTT051CR, and SOSTT051YP — sold from December 2025 through February 2026 for about $140. These specific models came with a promotional accessory kit that included a hammock, punching bag, hand rings, and a basketball board. Those accessories are the problem. The hazard: young children can become entangled in the straps attached to the hammock, punching bag, and hand ring accessories, posing a strangulation hazard. SEGMART has already received one report of a child whose neck became entangled in hammock straps, resulting in a rope burn injury. Here's the good news: the trampoline itself is not being recalled. You can continue using it — just without the accessories. What to do right now: Remove the hammock, punching bag, hand rings, and basketball board. Cut the straps on those accessories. Photograph them. Submit the photos to SEGMART to receive a $30 refund for the accessories. If you're unsure whether your trampoline is one of the recalled models, check the model number on the trampoline frame or original packaging. Has your family used a SEGMART trampoline with those accessory kits? --- #### Image Caption -- 8:00 AM MT ### Image Post Caption CPSC recall: SEGMART 55" mini toddler trampolines (models SOSTT051BR/CR/YP). ### Long-Form Post If your kids play high school sports in Colorado Springs — or will one day — this is worth knowing about. On April 14, a letter signed by more than 60 Colorado school board members formally demanded that the Colorado High School Activities Association (CHSAA) adopt rules banning transgender athletes from participating in girls' sports. The letter was signed by board members from Colorado Springs D11, Academy District 20, D49, Widefield D-3, and Woodland Park RE-2, as well as D49 Superintendent Peter Hilts. The letter cites President Trump's executive order "Keeping Men out of Women's Sports" and references Title IX, framing the request as both a safety and fairness issue for female athletes. CHSAA responded by maintaining its neutral stance — the association said the ultimate resolution of these issues will come from the courts, not from CHSAA itself. This is the most explicit and coordinated statement yet from COS-area school districts on this issue. For families, it means your districts have publicly committed to a position on how girls' sports teams should be structured — and that CHSAA's response (or lack of one) is likely to be addressed through legal channels. New Research — When Kids Watch TV Matters More Than How Long
NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post Most conversations about kids and screen time focus on the total number — how many hours, how many minutes. New research is shifting that conversation. A 2026 study published in Frontiers in Sleep looked specifically at when preschool-aged children watch TV, and found that evening viewing disrupts sleep more than screen time at other times of day. It's not just the amount; the timing matters. The numbers on current habits are striking: children ages 8 and under average 2 hours and 27 minutes of screen media per day, with 2- to 4-year-olds averaging 2 hours and 8 minutes daily. The AAP's recommendation for that age group is 1 hour of high-quality content per day. Earlier this year, the AAP updated its digital guidance to shift focus away from strict time limits toward quality and context — looking at what kids are watching, what the viewing experience is like (interactive vs. passive, social vs. solitary), and when in the day it happens. For families, the practical takeaway is simple: creating a screen-free window in the hour or two before bedtime may do more for your preschooler's sleep than worrying about the total daily count. Calm, quiet activities — books, puzzles, a bath — give little brains time to wind down in a way that autoplay videos don't. What's your family's screen-before-bed situation like right now? --- #### Image Caption -- 5:00 PM MT ### Image Post Caption New research: evening TV viewing disrupts preschooler sleep more than daytime screen time.
