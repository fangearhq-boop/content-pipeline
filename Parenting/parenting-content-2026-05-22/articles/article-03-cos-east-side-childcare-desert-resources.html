Nearly 3 Children Per Spot: East COS Childcare Crisis and How to Find Help

By Sarah Morales | COS Parenting | May 22, 2026

If you are a Colorado Springs parent who has spent weeks on waitlists, called provider after provider, and still cannot secure childcare, the numbers explain why. A KOAA investigation confirmed that El Paso County has nearly three children aged zero to six competing for every one available childcare spot. The shortage is most severe in eastern Colorado Springs, the southeast corridor, and the Fountain-Fort Carson area — exactly where much of the county's population growth has been concentrated.

The cost compounds the access problem. Full-time childcare in the Colorado Springs region costs approximately $20,000 per year. For families on the east side, where median incomes tend to be lower, that price point makes care effectively inaccessible without assistance. KOAA found that some parents are leaving the workforce entirely rather than pay it.

Why the East Side Is Hardest Hit

Colorado Springs' east side has grown significantly over the past decade, driven by military expansion, new residential development, and population movement from higher-cost areas. The childcare infrastructure has not kept pace. Licensed childcare providers are expensive to open and operate, and many opt to locate in areas with higher concentrations of dual-income households who can pay market rates. That dynamic has left east-side and southeast families with the fewest options and the longest waitlists.

The result is what researchers call a "childcare desert" — a geographic area where the supply of childcare falls far short of demand. Colorado Springs' east side meets the criteria, and the gap is not narrowing on its own.

Resources for COS Families

Several programs exist to help families navigate the shortage and reduce costs. Here is where to start:

Colorado Child Care Assistance Program (CCCAP) provides subsidies to low-income families for licensed childcare when parents are working, in school, or in job training. El Paso County administers the program locally. Contact: 719-444-8178.

Pikes Peak Region Family Child Care Association helps families find licensed childcare providers in El Paso County. They can point families toward providers who have openings or who participate in subsidy programs. Contact: 719-475-8828.

Colorado Shines is a state-run online tool that rates and lists licensed early learning programs across Colorado. Families can search by location, age range, and program type. The search is free.

Colorado Enterprise Fund (CEF) operates a Child Care Loan Fund to help childcare businesses access financing when they do not qualify for traditional bank loans. This program is aimed at providers — but more funded providers means more spots for families over time.

PPLD LibGuide on Child Care: Pikes Peak Library District maintains a curated resource guide called "Child Care and Early Learning in Colorado" at research.ppld.org. It includes local contacts, state program links, and practical steps for finding and evaluating providers.

What's Next

The childcare shortage in east Colorado Springs is not a problem with a quick fix. It requires investment in new providers, expansion of subsidy eligibility, and sustained attention from the city and county. In the meantime, the programs above represent the most direct path for families who need care now. If you are on a waitlist, start with CCCAP eligibility and the Pikes Peak Region Family Child Care Association — both can help you understand what options exist and what financial support you may qualify for.
