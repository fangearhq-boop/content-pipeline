Heat Advisory in Effect for Colorado Springs Through Monday — What Families Need to Know

By Sarah Morales

July 11, 2026 · Colorado Springs

The National Weather Service has issued a heat advisory for Colorado Springs and El Paso County running from noon Saturday, July 11 through midnight Monday night, July 14. Forecast highs range from 97 to 102 degrees Fahrenheit — well above the July average high of approximately 87 degrees for Colorado Springs. If your family has outdoor plans this weekend, here's what to know and how to adjust.

What's Driving the Heat

High pressure parked over the region is trapping heat at the surface and suppressing cloud development that would normally provide afternoon cooling. The result is several consecutive days of extreme heat with limited overnight recovery. In Colorado, dry air means the sun's radiation reaches ground level with little atmospheric filtering — which is why Colorado's summer heat can feel more intense than the temperature alone suggests, even at an elevation where you might expect cooler conditions.

Red Flag conditions are also in effect for El Paso County alongside the heat advisory. Low relative humidity and gusty winds create elevated wildfire risk during this advisory window. Families planning campfires, fireworks, or outdoor burning of any kind should check current burn restrictions before proceeding — and in Red Flag conditions, the safest choice is no open burning at all.

Heat Safety for Kids: The Practical Guide

Children are more vulnerable to heat illness than adults because their bodies don't regulate temperature as efficiently, and they're often too engaged in play to notice or communicate distress until symptoms are already significant. Here's what to watch for and what to do.

Peak heat hours in Colorado Springs during this advisory are generally between 2 and 5 PM. If your family wants to be outside, morning activities before noon and evening activities after 7 PM are significantly safer than midday exposure. Shade matters, but in direct Colorado sun even shaded temperatures can be dangerous when the ambient temperature exceeds 100 degrees.

Hydration is the most important protective factor. Don't wait until your child says they're thirsty — by the time thirst registers, they're already mildly dehydrated. Give kids water consistently throughout the day, especially if they're active. Sports drinks can help replace electrolytes if kids are sweating heavily, but water is the baseline.

Know the signs of heat exhaustion in children: heavy sweating, cool and pale skin, a fast weak pulse, nausea, dizziness, and muscle cramps. If you see these symptoms, move the child to a cool environment, give them water to sip, and apply cool (not cold) wet cloths to the skin. If symptoms worsen or the child loses consciousness, call 911 — heat stroke is a medical emergency.

Never Leave Kids or Pets in a Parked Car

This is not an overstatement: a car parked in direct summer sun in Colorado can reach interior temperatures above 160 degrees Fahrenheit within an hour, and temperatures dangerous to a child can be reached within 10 to 20 minutes. Cracking windows does not meaningfully reduce interior temperature. There is no safe amount of time to leave a child unattended in a parked vehicle in these conditions.

Where to Stay Cool in Colorado Springs

If your home is not air-conditioned or you're looking for a free or low-cost cool environment, Colorado Springs has several options. Pikes Peak Library District branches provide air-conditioned public space with free access. Recreation Centers of Colorado Springs locations are another option, though entry fees apply. Colorado Springs City Hall and El Paso County offices may also serve as cooling locations — check the El Paso County and City of Colorado Springs websites for any designated cooling center announcements during this advisory period.

What's Next

The heat advisory runs through midnight Monday, July 14. The National Weather Service Pueblo office (which covers the Colorado Springs forecast area) updates advisories daily at weather.gov/pub — check there for any changes to the advisory timing or severity. If you have family members or neighbors who are elderly, very young, or without air conditioning, check on them during this advisory period. Extreme heat events are among the deadliest weather phenomena, and they disproportionately affect those who lack resources to stay cool.
