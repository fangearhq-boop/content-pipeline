Colorado's New School Phone Law Is Now in Effect — Here's What COS Families Need to Know

By Jamie Rivera

July 11, 2026 · Colorado Springs

Colorado's HB25-1135 — the state's new law governing student use of communication devices in schools — required every public school district to have a student device policy publicly posted by July 1, 2026. That deadline has now passed. The actual restrictions won't be enforced until students return to school in the fall, but the rules are set. For COS families, the question is: what does your district's policy actually say?

What HB25-1135 Does and Doesn't Do

This law doesn't create a single statewide ban on student phones. What it does is require every Colorado public school district to define its own policy on student communication devices — and make that policy publicly available. Districts had until July 1, 2026 to comply, and any district that already had a policy in place before August 31, 2025 is automatically exempt from writing a new one.

The result is a patchwork of district-level rules rather than one uniform standard. That means the rules at a D11 school may look different from the rules at a D49 school — which is why it's worth actually looking up your district's policy before school starts this fall.

Where COS Districts Stand

Academy District 49 is the clearest example in Colorado Springs. D49 approved their student phone policy on July 10, 2025 — nearly a full year before the HB25-1135 deadline — which means they were already exempt and compliant. Their policy bans phones for students in kindergarten through eighth grade during the school day. High school students in D49 may use their phones during lunch.

Colorado Springs School District 11 was cited as a model district by state officials even before HB25-1135 passed. D11 had already developed and implemented a student cell phone policy that addressed the same concerns the law now requires all districts to codify. Families in D11 can find the current policy on the district's website.

Academy District 20 is subject to the same requirement. D20 has faced significant leadership disruption this summer following the termination of former superintendent Jinger Haberer in May 2026, and specific policy details for D20 had not been confirmed publicly as of this writing. D20 families should check the district's website directly for their posted policy ahead of the fall school year.

What the Law Means in Practice This Fall

When school resumes in the fall, students in districts with phone-free policies will be expected to put away their devices for the duration of the school day — or for the portions of the day covered by the policy. D49's policy, for example, allows high schoolers to use phones at lunch, which means the restriction applies before and after that window.

Emergency communication is a common concern parents raise about phone policies. Most district policies include provisions for emergency contact — typically through the school office. If your child has a medical device or a specific communication need, check your district's accommodation process before school starts.

The research context behind laws like HB25-1135 is worth understanding. Multiple large studies have found associations between phone use during school and reduced academic performance, increased distraction, and social comparison dynamics that affect student well-being. That's the policy rationale driving both the state law and individual district decisions.

What's Next

Before school starts this fall, visit your district's website and find the posted student device policy. The policy should be publicly available now that the July 1 deadline has passed. If you can't find it or have questions about specific accommodations, contact your district office directly — the policies should be straightforward to locate.

For D11 families: check d11.org. For D49 families: check asd49.net. For D20 families: check asd20.org. Each district's policy is the authoritative source for what your child's school day will look like this fall.
