D49 Budget Crisis: What Falcon School District Families Need to Know

By Sarah Donnelly | COS Parenting | April 7, 2026

Falcon School District 49 has had a difficult start to 2026. Families in the Falcon, Peyton, and Powers Corridor areas of Colorado Springs are navigating a school district that declared a financial emergency in January, cut dozens of positions in February, and then faced a March investigation that revealed a significant administrative accounting error — with no administrative consequences.

How It Started: Fiscal Exigency in January

On January 8, 2026, the D49 board of education voted 4-1 to declare fiscal exigency — a formal acknowledgment that the district is in financial distress. The reasons cited by district leadership included flat revenue, declining student enrollment, rising healthcare and operating costs, and the loss of mill levy equalization funding.

Fiscal exigency is a significant designation in Colorado school law. It gives the board authority to take personnel actions — including nonrenewing tenured teacher contracts — that would otherwise require a different process.

The Cuts: 39 Positions, 12 Tenured Teachers

In February, the D49 board approved $1.8 million in budget cuts. The cuts eliminated 39 positions across the district and included the nonrenewal of contracts for 12 tenured teachers. The nonrenewal of tenured contracts is an unusual step — tenured teachers have protections that make nonrenewal more difficult outside of a fiscal exigency declaration.

District leadership described the cuts as necessary to keep the district financially viable. "We are stripping the district down to absolute essentials," one district leader told KOAA at the time.

The Accounting Error: $2M in Consequences, No Administrative Accountability

In March, KRDO published the results of an investigation that added a new layer to the story. A 2025 state audit had found that D49 administrators had incorrectly reported student FTE (full-time equivalent enrollment) counts to the state, due to errors in how bell schedules were entered into the reporting system. The state had overpaid D49 by approximately $2.66 million based on those incorrect counts.

D49 now faces roughly a $2 million fine to cover the recovery of those overpaid funds. No administrators have been dismissed or disciplined as a result of the error.

The juxtaposition has drawn attention from media outlets and parent advocates across the Pikes Peak region: teachers were let go over a budget crisis, and then it emerged that an administrative-level accounting error had materially contributed to that same crisis — without any administrator facing consequences.

What D49 Families Can Do

D49 school board meetings are open to the public and listed on the district's website at d49.org. The board meeting calendar and contact information for individual board members are available there. If you have questions about specific school programs, staffing levels, or the district's financial recovery plan, the district's public meetings are the most direct venue.

KRDO, KOAA, Rocky Mountain PBS, and the Pikes Peak Bulletin have all covered this story and will continue to report on it as D49 works through the fiscal year.

What's Next

D49 is still working through its financial recovery process. Families can monitor developments through the district's website at d49.org and through local news coverage. The next school board meeting will be listed on the D49 district calendar.
