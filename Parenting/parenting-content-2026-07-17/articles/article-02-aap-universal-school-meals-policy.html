Pediatricians Now Officially Back Free School Meals for Every Child

What the New AAP Policy Says — and What It Means for Colorado Springs Families

July 17, 2026 | By Sarah Morales | COS Parenting

The American Academy of Pediatrics has published a formal policy statement calling for universal free school meals for every student in the United States, regardless of family income. The statement, published in the July 2026 issue of Pediatrics, is the AAP's most direct policy position on school nutrition to date — and it directly addresses a misconception that has kept some families from encouraging their children to participate in school meal programs.

With D49 students starting Aug. 4, D11 on Aug. 12, and D20 on Aug. 13, this policy lands at the exact moment Colorado Springs families are thinking about the coming school year.

What the AAP Said

The AAP's Council on School Health and Committee on Nutrition jointly issued the policy statement, which calls on the U.S. to make universal free school meals a permanent national standard. Their formal recommendation: every student should have access to free school meals, not just those who qualify under current income thresholds.

This is significant because formal AAP policy statements carry weight in both medical practice and public policy. When the AAP issues a policy — not just a recommendation or a clinical guide, but a formal policy — it's a signal that the evidence has reached a threshold where pediatricians believe collective action is warranted. This statement is a direct call for policy change, not simply clinical guidance for individual practitioners.

The policy also calls on pediatricians to take active roles: serving on school wellness councils, forming community partnerships, and engaging in direct advocacy for school nutrition programs.

The Research Behind It — Including the Myth It Directly Addresses

The AAP's policy is backed by a body of research, and one finding in particular stands out for parents: participating in school meal programs is associated with improved diet quality. Children who eat school meals tend to eat better during the school day than those who don't.

The policy also directly takes on a common parental concern: that school lunches contribute to childhood obesity. The research reviewed by the AAP does not support that conclusion. School meal participation does not increase obesity risk. This matters because that belief has led some families to opt their children out of school meal programs — a decision that, according to the evidence, is not improving their children's health outcomes.

The AAP's policy also notes that school breakfast participation remains lower than optimal. Children who eat breakfast at school show improved academic performance and reduced food insecurity, and the AAP is calling on pediatricians to actively promote school breakfast access with families.

A companion study in the same issue of Pediatrics — "Statewide Universal School Meals Policies and Food Insecurity: Difference-in-Differences Analysis" — found that states that adopted universal school meal policies saw measurable reductions in food insecurity. This was the same finding that emerged from COVID-era universal meal waivers, when expanded access sharply increased participation and reduced food insecurity nationally. The AAP is calling for those waivers to become permanent policy.

The COS Angle

Colorado Springs families have a local context that makes this policy particularly relevant. D11 already operates one of the region's largest free summer meals programs — something our pipeline covered on July 9. El Paso County has significant rates of child food insecurity, and D11, D20, and D49 families will be navigating school meal enrollment processes as the new school year begins in just a few weeks.

The legislation that would make universal free school meals a federal standard — HR.8798, the Universal School Meals Program Act of 2026, championed by Sen. Bernie Sanders — is currently pending in Congress. Its outcome will shape whether Colorado districts can expand access beyond current income-based eligibility thresholds.

What's Next

As the school year approaches, COS families can take a few concrete steps. If you're in D11, D20, or D49, look up your school's meal program options before the first day. Even if your family doesn't qualify for free or reduced-price meals under current income guidelines, the AAP's evidence suggests that school meal participation is good for your child's diet quality.

If you want to engage on the policy level, the AAP's statement recommends that parents attend school wellness council meetings, contact their school boards, and let their congressional representatives know they support HR.8798. The full policy statement is available at publications.aap.org/pediatrics.

For D49 families, the first day of school is Aug. 4. D11 returns Aug. 12 and D20 returns Aug. 13. Now is the right time to check your school's meal enrollment process so your child can participate from day one.
