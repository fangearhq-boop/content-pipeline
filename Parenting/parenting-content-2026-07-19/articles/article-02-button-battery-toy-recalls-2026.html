Button Battery Toy Recalls: What Colorado Springs Parents Need to Know

By Jamie Rivera

July 19, 2026

The U.S. Consumer Product Safety Commission has issued multiple recalls in 2026 for children's toys that contain button cell batteries children can access and swallow. If you have bought inexpensive LED toys from Amazon in the past year, check them now.

Button cell batteries — the small, flat, silver coin-shaped batteries that power many LED toys — cause serious internal injuries when swallowed. The battery generates an electric current that produces hydroxide at the negative pole, which burns through soft tissue. Children can suffer permanent damage to the esophagus within two hours. The injuries can be fatal.

Which Products Are Recalled

Two products are at the center of the most recent recalls:

Oitnlaughter LED Finger Lights — Sets of 36 finger lights in animal and novelty designs, sold on Amazon by seller Delightmi from July 2025 through April 2026 for approximately $14. The CPSC recalled these because the battery compartments are accessible to children, meaning a child can remove and swallow the button batteries inside. If you have this product, stop using it immediately and contact Delightmi for a full refund. You will be asked to dispose of the product and send a photo of the disposal to the company.

Children's LED Light-Up Tutu Skirts — Light-up skirts sold on Amazon by seller Bmrwtg. The skirts contain button batteries in the LED components. Recall details and refund instructions are available through the CPSC recall database.

The CPSC has issued at least five similar recalls in 2026, all involving inexpensive LED toys sold through Amazon that contain accessible button batteries. The pattern spans a range of products — finger lights, clothing, play accessories — from multiple different sellers.

Why These Toys Pass Initial Review

Most of these products arrive with batteries pre-installed and functional, making the battery compartment easy to overlook during a first inspection. The battery covers are often held in place by a single screw or a snap closure that children can open without tools.

Federal law requires that battery compartments on children's toys be secured with a screw or fastener that requires a tool to open. When products fail this standard — or when the compartments degrade over time — they become dangerous. The CPSC issues recalls when it receives injury reports or when enforcement sampling finds violations.

What to Do Right Now

Check any small LED toys in your home, particularly ones bought from Amazon marketplace sellers in the past 12 to 18 months. Look at:

  • LED finger lights or glowing rings
  • Light-up clothing items, including tutus, skirts, or costume accessories
  • Small novelty toys with blinking or color-changing LED components
  • Play purses or accessory sets with LED elements

For any of these products, try to open the battery compartment using only your fingers or a coin. If you can open it without a screwdriver, keep it away from children until you can confirm it is not under recall or until you dispose of it.

If you have the Oitnlaughter LED Finger Lights from Amazon, contact Delightmi through your Amazon order history or through the email provided in your original order confirmation to request a refund.

If a Child Swallows a Button Battery

This is a medical emergency. Do not wait for symptoms. Go to an emergency room immediately and tell medical staff that a button or coin cell battery was swallowed. Do not give the child food or water. Bring the battery packaging if you have it so medical staff know the battery size.

The National Battery Ingestion Hotline is available at 800-498-8666 for guidance.

What's Next

The CPSC maintains an up-to-date recall database at cpsc.gov where you can search by product type or brand. Signing up for email alerts lets you receive new recalls as they are announced — a useful tool for families who regularly purchase toys online.

When buying toys with LED components in the future, test the battery compartment before giving the toy to a child. If it opens without a tool, treat it as inaccessible to children until it can be secured or returned.
