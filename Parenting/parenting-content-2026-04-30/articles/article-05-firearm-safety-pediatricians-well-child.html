Pediatricians Are Now Screening for Firearm Safety at Well-Child Visits — Here's What COS Parents Need to Know

By Keisha Williams | COS Parenting | April 30, 2026

If your child's pediatrician asks whether you have firearms at home at the next well-child checkup, it's not overstepping — it's part of an evidence-based approach to child safety that's increasingly being integrated into pediatric care. New research presented at the 2026 Pediatric Academic Societies (PAS) meeting in Boston shows that quality improvement programs are helping more doctors have this conversation with more families, more consistently.

For Colorado Springs families — one of the most military-connected communities in the country — this conversation is particularly relevant.

What the Research Found

The 2026 PAS meeting, held April 24-27 in Boston, brought together thousands of pediatric clinicians and researchers from around the world. Among the research presented was a quality improvement study titled "Increasing Firearm Screening in Resident Pediatric Well-Child Checks," presented April 27. The study found that structured training and process improvements can significantly increase the rate at which pediatricians ask about firearm storage during routine well-child visits.

The American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) has long recommended that all pediatricians ask parents about the presence of firearms in the home and discuss safe storage as part of routine anticipatory guidance. The research presented at PAS 2026 suggests that while the recommendation exists, consistent implementation has required targeted quality improvement work — and that work is showing results.

Why This Matters in Colorado Springs

Colorado Springs is home to Fort Carson, NORAD, Peterson Space Force Base, and Schriever Space Force Base. The city has one of the highest concentrations of active-duty military and veterans of any metropolitan area in the country. In households with active-duty military personnel and veterans, legal firearm ownership is common.

That's not a criticism — it's context. And it's exactly why the safe storage conversation matters here more than in many other cities. The primary risk that firearm safety screening is designed to reduce isn't crime: it's accidental injury and youth suicide. When children or teenagers access an unsecured firearm in their own home or a home they're visiting, the consequences can be catastrophic.

What Safe Storage Actually Means

Safe firearm storage doesn't mean giving up your guns or making them inaccessible for home defense. It means reducing the window of opportunity for a child to access an unsecured weapon. Practical, research-backed options include:

  • Locked gun safes, which can include biometric or quick-access safes for home defense firearms
  • Trigger locks, which are low-cost and widely available
  • Storing ammunition separately from the firearm
  • Storing firearms unloaded when not in active use

All of these measures reduce the risk of a child accessing a firearm without eliminating the ability of a responsible adult to do so quickly when needed.

What's Next

If you own firearms and have children in your home, it's worth reviewing your current storage setup before the conversation comes up at a checkup. If your pediatrician asks, take it as the health and safety conversation it's intended to be. And if you'd like resources on free or low-cost gun lock programs, contact your local community health organization or pediatrician's office directly — many practices now keep this information on hand.
