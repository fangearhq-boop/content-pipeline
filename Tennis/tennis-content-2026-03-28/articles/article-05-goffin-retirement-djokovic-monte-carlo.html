Goffin to Retire at Season's End; Djokovic Misses Monte Carlo

By Tennis Fanrecap | March 28, 2026

Two significant off-court developments emerged on Friday, March 27 — one a retirement, one an injury withdrawal — and both carry implications for the clay season that begins next week.

Goffin: A Career That Deserves Recognition

David Goffin announced via Instagram on Friday that 2026 will be his final year on the ATP Tour. The Belgian is 35 years old and cited a lingering knee injury and the cumulative toll of 17-plus years of professional tennis as the factors behind the decision.

Goffin's career peak was No. 7 in the world, reached in November 2017. He won six ATP singles titles and became the first Belgian player to break into the ATP Top 10 — a milestone that carries lasting significance in a country that has produced multiple champions. His run to the ATP Finals in London in 2017 remains one of his career highlights: he defeated both Rafael Nadal and Roger Federer at the event, beating two of the sport's greatest players within the space of a week.

He did not win the ATP Finals that year — Grigor Dimitrov took the title — but the performances cemented Goffin's status as a genuine threat at the highest level. In recent years, injury has made it difficult to sustain that level, and Friday's announcement ends a career that would have been even longer without them.

Goffin plans to release a personal documentary in autumn 2026 and has expressed interest in coaching and mentoring younger players after his playing career concludes.

Djokovic: Shoulder Forces Monte Carlo Absence

Novak Djokovic confirmed his withdrawal from the Monte Carlo Masters, scheduled for April 5-12, due to an ongoing right shoulder injury. The withdrawal means Djokovic will have missed Indian Wells, Miami, and now Monte Carlo — three consecutive significant events — as he manages the injury.

Monte Carlo will mark the first time Djokovic has been absent from the tournament since 2011, ending a run of 14 consecutive appearances. The Serb, 38, is the most decorated player in Monte Carlo history, and his absence removes a significant factor from the draw. Alcaraz, Sinner, and Zverev are expected to headline the event.

Djokovic's last match was a fourth-round loss to Jack Draper at Indian Wells. His next planned event is the Madrid Open (April 22), though reports suggest even that timeline remains uncertain pending his shoulder's response to rest and treatment. Djokovic's clay-court schedule, which has historically been a strength of his season, is now under genuine question.

What's Next

The clay-court season begins the week of March 30 with tournaments in Houston, Bucharest, and Marrakesh. Neither Goffin nor Djokovic is expected to compete that week. For Goffin, the remaining weeks of 2026 represent his final opportunities to compete at the events he has played throughout his career. For Djokovic, the target is a full return by Madrid or at the latest the Italian Open in May, with Roland Garros in June as the clay-season's ultimate goal.

The sport will have time to celebrate Goffin's career properly before year's end. For now, Friday's Instagram announcement is the start of a long goodbye from one of Belgium's most accomplished tennis players.
