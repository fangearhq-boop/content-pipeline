Gauff Survives Andreeva on Fifth Match Point to Reach Rome Semifinals

By Ryan Calloway | May 13, 2026 | Tennis Fanrecap

Coco Gauff had been here before. Serving it out. A match within reach. A crowd sensing the finish. And then, suddenly, not.

At 5-1 in the third set against Mirra Andreeva on Wednesday, Gauff was broken. The two-hour, 17-minute quarterfinal at the Internazionali BNL d'Italia stretched toward a nerve-wracking conclusion. The defending Roland Garros champion had to convert five match points before finally finishing the job — 4-6, 6-2, 6-4 — and booking her place in the Rome semifinals.

"That's what this tournament does to you," Gauff said with a smile afterward. "You can never just cruise."

A Match That Swung Three Ways

The opening set belonged to Andreeva. The young Russian — who had been carrying the tour's best clay record entering the match — moved well, redirected Gauff's heavy groundstrokes with precision, and broke the American to take the set 6-4.

Gauff recalibrated. The second set was a different player: more aggressive on her returns, pushing further inside the baseline, forcing Andreeva onto the back foot. She won it 6-2 and carried that momentum into the third.

But just when a clean victory appeared inevitable, Andreeva broke back from 5-1 down. Gauff had chances — five of them — before the final winning shot landed, the crowd exhaled, and the match ended.

The Champion's Thread

What separates tennis champions from good players is often not the ability to dominate — it is the ability to find a way when dominance disappears. Gauff has demonstrated that quality repeatedly since her Roland Garros title. She does not always perform cleanly. She does not always win in straight sets. But she wins.

This week in Rome has already been a test of that resilience. The 5-1 lead against Andreeva that briefly became a crisis is the kind of scenario that derails some players. For Gauff, it appeared to reset her focus rather than break it.

Cirstea Waits in the Semifinals

Gauff's semifinal opponent is Sorana Cirstea — the 36-year-old Romanian who has been the tournament's most surprising and most celebrated story. Cirstea is in her final professional season. This is her first career Rome semifinal. She reached it by defeating world No. 1 Aryna Sabalenka in the third round, No. 9 seed Linda Noskova in the round of 16, and Jelena Ostapenko in the quarterfinals.

The matchup presents Gauff with a different kind of challenge than Andreeva. Cirstea does not overpower opponents. She outthinks them — placing balls precisely, varying pace, constructing points with the tactical intelligence of a veteran who has seen every situation on tour.

On head-to-head record, Gauff enters the semifinal as the clear favourite. But Cirstea's week in Rome has made clear that records do not guarantee outcomes.

What's Next

The Rome semifinal is scheduled Friday. A Gauff victory would put her in the final with the likely prospect of facing Swiatek — her Roland Garros rival — for what would be a compelling preview of the French Open's expected title contenders.

For now, Gauff has earned her rest. Five match points. Two hours and 17 minutes. She came through. The Roland Garros champion moves on.
