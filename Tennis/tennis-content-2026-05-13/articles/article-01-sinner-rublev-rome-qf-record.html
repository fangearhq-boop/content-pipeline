Sinner Chases Sole Masters Record in Rome Quarterfinal vs. Rublev

By Elena Voss | May 13, 2026 | Tennis Fanrecap

Jannik Sinner steps onto the Campo Centrale clay on Wednesday carrying the weight of history — and, if Tuesday's dominant win over qualifier Andrea Pellegrino is any indication, he wears it without flinching.

The world No. 1 enters his Rome quarterfinal against No. 12 seed Andrey Rublev having strung together 31 consecutive ATP Masters 1000 victories — a number that ties Novak Djokovic's all-time record. Win No. 32 today, and Sinner stands alone.

"I feel good on this surface right now," Sinner said after dispatching Pellegrino 6-2, 6-3 on Tuesday. The match was surgical — Sinner broken only once across the entire tournament so far, his serve clicking, his forehand suffocating from the first point.

The Record in Context

Djokovic's original 31-match streak was built across multiple seasons and considered by many the pinnacle of Masters-level consistency. Sinner's current run is a single-season juggernaut. He has already claimed five consecutive Masters 1000 titles — an Open Era first — with wins at Paris, Indian Wells, Miami, Monte Carlo, and Madrid.

Rome is not just the site of a potential record. It is the one Masters 1000 event missing from Sinner's collection. Win this title, and the Italian joins Djokovic as the only player ever to complete a Career Golden Masters — claiming all nine Masters 1000 titles over the course of a career.

The symmetry is almost poetic: on the same court where Djokovic built his legacy, Sinner is writing his own chapter.

The Rublev Challenge

Rublev is not a player who arrives at a quarterfinal looking for moral victories. The Russian — a former top-five player and multiple Masters 1000 champion himself — fought past Georgian qualifier Nikoloz Basilashvili 3-6, 7-6(5), 6-2 in the previous round, showing the kind of resilience that can cause problems for anyone.

On clay, Rublev's heavy forehand and relentless baseline hitting can stretch even the best defenders. But Sinner's ability to absorb pace and redirect has been his defining strength all season. The world No. 1 creates errors from opponents rather than just making winners himself — a style that wears down even the most aggressive baseliners.

Sinner leads their head-to-head across all surfaces, and Rublev has rarely been able to sustain pressure against the Italian's defensive wall for extended periods.

Home Court, Heavy Expectations

Playing in his home country adds a layer of pressure that few players outside Italy understand. Sinner handles it differently than most. Rather than being energized or unsettled by the Campo Centrale roar, he seems to draw something quieter from it — a sense of obligation that sharpens rather than distracts.

He has spoken this week about how much winning Rome would mean — not just for the record, not just for the Career Golden Masters, but because of what the tournament represents to Italian tennis fans who have waited for a home champion since Adriano Panatta in 1976.

What's Next

A Sinner victory over Rublev sets up a potential semifinal against the winner of the Rafael Jodar vs. Luciano Darderi quarterfinal — two young talents who have both produced remarkable results to reach the last eight. A Darderi win, in particular, would deliver another Italian crowd favourite on the other side of the net.

The Rome final is on Sunday. Sinner's path through the draw remains open. The record. The Career Golden Masters. A home title. Three reasons, one tournament, one match at a time.
