Cirstea's Farewell Season Has a New Chapter: The Rome R16

By Elena Voss | Tennis Fanrecap

May 11, 2026

Sorana Cirstea, 36, playing her final professional season, shocked world No. 1 Aryna Sabalenka in Rome on Saturday — and now faces No. 9 seed Linda Noskova in the Round of 16.

Somewhere in the history of Sorana Cirstea's professional career, which began in the early 2000s when she was a teenager from Craiova, Romania, people stopped expecting her to win the big matches. It happened naturally, almost imperceptibly. She was good — always good — but not a champion. A veteran. A qualifier for draws. A name who endured.

On Saturday at the Foro Italico in Rome, Cirstea rewrote what her career means. She defeated world No. 1 Aryna Sabalenka 2-6, 6-3, 7-5 — and in doing so recorded the first win over a world No. 1 in over 20 years on the professional tour. She is 36 years old. She has already announced this will be her final professional season. And she is now in the Italian Open Round of 16.

How She Beat Sabalenka

The match was more complicated than the final scoreline suggests. Sabalenka won the first set 6-2 with typical authority, her groundstrokes generating the kind of pace that has made her the world's top-ranked player. Cirstea, a decade older and approaching the end of a career that has required a patient, crafty game more than a powerful one, regrouped.

She won the second set 6-3. In the third, Sabalenka began to move with restriction — a lower back and hip injury that grew more visible as the match progressed — and Cirstea used every weapon in her considerable repertoire: slices, angles, drop shots, and the kind of tactical patience that only comes from years of competition at the highest level. She closed out 7-5 in a third set that, at multiple points, could have gone either way.

Afterward, the emotion was evident. For Cirstea, it was more than a match result.

Now: Noskova in the R16

The Italian Open has delivered Cirstea a Round of 16 opponent who represents exactly the generational contrast her story has come to embody. Linda Noskova is 19 years old, Czech, seeded No. 9 in Rome, and a player many consider one of the next generation's great hopes on clay. The opposition could hardly be more symbolic: the veteran in her farewell year against the next great thing in hers.

Noskova is not here by accident. She is physically imposing, capable of hitting winners from anywhere on the court, and she has had a strong clay season. For Cirstea to reach the quarterfinals, she will need to replicate the tactical discipline that unseated Sabalenka — to absorb Noskova's power and redirect points, to stay in longer rallies, and to trust the craft she has spent two decades building.

The Larger Picture

Cirstea's run matters beyond the scoreboard. In a sport that often moves on quickly from its veterans, her week in Rome is a reminder that long careers carry meaning — that the accumulation of years and matches and hard-fought experience can, sometimes, produce a moment that none of the younger players in the draw could have generated in quite the same way.

She will not be a favourite against Noskova. But then, she was not a favourite against Sabalenka. The Italian Open, with its wide-open draw and 14-day runway to Roland Garros, has become an unexpected theatre for one of the season's most compelling stories.

What's Next

Cirstea vs Noskova is scheduled on Monday at the Foro Italico. A Cirstea win would put her in the Rome quarterfinals — a result she has never achieved at this tournament in her career. Whatever happens, the week in Rome has already delivered something worth remembering: a 36-year-old in her final season beating the world's best player for the first time, in the most Italian of settings.
