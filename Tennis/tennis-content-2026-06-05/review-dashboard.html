### Long-Form Post Mirra Andreeva is heading to her first Grand Slam final — and she got there in the most emphatic way possible. The 19-year-old Russian demolished Marta Kostyuk 6-1, 6-3 in just 76 minutes on Court Philippe-Chatrier on Thursday, with a performance that was technically close to flawless. Andreeva outplayed a player who had gone 17-0 on clay in 2026, who had beaten Iga Swiatek and Elina Svitolina at this tournament, and who held a 2-0 head-to-head advantage over Andreeva this season from the Brisbane and Madrid finals. None of that mattered on Thursday. Andreeva broke in the opening game and barely looked back, dropping just four games across the entire match. At 19 years and 37 days old, she becomes the fourth-youngest Roland Garros women's finalist in the last 30 years — only Martina Hingis, Kim Clijsters and Coco Gauff were younger when they reached the same stage of this tournament. The story wasn't over when the final ball landed, though. At net, there was no handshake between Andreeva and Kostyuk. Kostyuk has publicly stated she will not shake hands with Russian players while Russia's war in Ukraine continues — and she declined again on Thursday. The no-handshake policy applies to all Russian players except Daria Kasatkina, who has publicly spoken out against the war. What do you think — was the no-handshake the right call, or should tennis tradition prevail? ---

Chwalinska Makes History — First Qualifier in Roland Garros Final
NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post Before Maja Chwalinska arrived in Paris, she had won two matches on clay at the tour level. Her entire career. On Thursday she walked off Court Suzanne-Lenglen having beaten Diana Shnaider 7-6(4), 6-4 to become the first qualifier in the history of Roland Garros to reach the final. She did it ranked No. 114 in the world. She did it having come through qualifying rounds that most of the field never saw. The 24-year-old Polish left-hander is now in the company of Evonne Goolagong (1971) and Chris Evert (1973) as the only women to reach the Roland Garros final on their main draw debut. She is also just the second women's qualifier in the entire Open Era to reach a Grand Slam final. Chwalinska said after the match: "I don't know what's going on." That's probably the most honest thing anyone has said at this tournament. Saturday's women's final will be Andreeva vs Chwalinska — two players in their first Grand Slam final, separated by 106 ranking spots. Tennis doesn't get more unpredictable than this. What's your prediction — can Chwalinska pull off one more upset? ---

NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post Alexander Zverev has reached three Grand Slam finals. He has lost all three. 2020 US Open — he had a two-set lead over Dominic Thiem and still lost. 2024 Roland Garros — he was up two sets to one against Carlos Alcaraz and still lost. 2025 Australian Open — Jannik Sinner beat him in three sets. Friday at 2:30 PM Paris time, Zverev walks onto Court Philippe-Chatrier again, this time against 20-year-old Jakub Mensik of the Czech Republic. On paper, it's the most open path to a Grand Slam title Zverev has ever had. Alcaraz is out with a wrist injury. Sinner was beaten in the second round. Djokovic fell in the third. The draw is wide open. Mensik, for his part, has done nothing to make himself look like a pushover. The 20-year-old has beaten the No. 8 seed Alex de Minaur, recovered from a bagel in the first set to advance, and then took down the breakout star João Fonseca in the quarterfinals. He's the first Czech man to reach a Grand Slam semifinal in nearly a decade. It's experience and heartbreak vs youth and momentum. Will Zverev finally break through? ---

NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post Italy hasn't had a men's finalist at Roland Garros since Adriano Panatta won the title in 1976. That's 50 years. On Friday that changes. Guaranteed. Because Flavio Cobolli and Matteo Arnaldi — both Italian, both in the first Grand Slam semifinal of their careers — are playing each other tonight at 7 PM Paris time in the first all-Italian men's Grand Slam semifinal in history. Cobolli came in as the No. 10 seed and has been the tournament's model of consistency — he's dropped just one set in five matches and secured his debut in the ATP top 10 by reaching this stage. He beat Felix Auger-Aliassime in four sets in the quarterfinals. Arnaldi's story is arguably more remarkable. Ranked No. 104, he arrived as an unseeded wildcard. He's won all five of his matches, including advancing to the semifinal when Matteo Berrettini retired injured in the third round with Arnaldi already leading. No one expected him to still be here. Tonight one of them ends Italy's 50-year wait. The only question is which one. ---

### Long-Form Post Saturday's Roland Garros women's final is unlike anything the tournament has ever seen. On one side: Mirra Andreeva, 19, Russia, the No. 8 seed who just dismantled Marta Kostyuk 6-1, 6-3 to book her first Grand Slam final. On the other: Maja Chwalinska, 24, Poland, a qualifier ranked No. 114 in the world who had two career tour-level clay wins before arriving in Paris. Neither has ever played a Grand Slam final. History is guaranteed for one of them on Saturday. Andreeva enters as the clear favourite — she's been dominant all tournament, her movement and baseline game have been elite, and she has the rankings and experience to back it up. But Chwalinska has already proven that none of that matters in this draw. She beat Diana Shnaider to become the first qualifier in Roland Garros history to reach the final. The final is scheduled for Saturday June 6, not before 3:00 PM Paris time (9:00 AM ET). Who are you watching for? ---
