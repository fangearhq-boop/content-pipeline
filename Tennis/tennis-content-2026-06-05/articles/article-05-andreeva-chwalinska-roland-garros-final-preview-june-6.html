Andreeva vs Chwalinska: Everything You Need to Know About the Roland Garros Women's Final

By Marcus Cole | June 5, 2026

The Roland Garros women's final on Saturday June 6 will match two players who have never appeared in a Grand Slam final. It will match a 19-year-old seeded player against a 24-year-old who came through qualifying ranked No. 114 in the world. It will be, by any measure, a final that tennis has not precisely seen before.

Mirra Andreeva faces Maja Chwalinska. The match begins not before 3:00 PM Paris time (9:00 AM ET) on Court Philippe-Chatrier.

Mirra Andreeva

Andreeva, born April 29, 2007, is 19 years old. She is Russia's No. 8 seed. She reached the final on Thursday by defeating Marta Kostyuk 6-1, 6-3 in 76 minutes — a performance so controlled against a player who had gone 17-0 on clay in 2026 that it left observers reassessing how good she already is.

She is the fourth-youngest Roland Garros women's finalist in 30 years. This is her second consecutive Roland Garros semifinal, but her first final at any Grand Slam. Her game on clay — the movement, the clean ball-striking, the composure in high-leverage moments — has been among the strongest at this tournament.

Andreeva enters the final as the heavy favourite on every metric: ranking, seeding, form, experience relative to Chwalinska's clay results before Paris.

Maja Chwalinska

Chwalinska is 24, from Poland, and ranked No. 114 in the world. She came through qualifying to enter the main draw, having never won more than two matches on clay at the professional tour level in a full season. She reached the final on Thursday by defeating Diana Shnaider 7-6(4), 6-4.

She is the first qualifier in Roland Garros history to reach the final. She is also the third woman to reach the Roland Garros final on her main draw debut, joining Evonne Goolagong in 1971 and Chris Evert in 1973 — Goolagong won that year; Evert returned and won in 1974.

Chwalinska's run through the draw has defied every projection. She has been the underdog in every match she has played and has produced some of the fortnight's most compelling tennis in winning all of them. How she handles the final, with its spotlight and expectation and the weight of what she has already achieved, may be the dominant narrative of Saturday.

Key Questions

Can Chwalinska's run continue? She has beaten players ranked significantly above her at every stage, and her composure under pressure has been exceptional. But Andreeva's ceiling is higher, her game is more complete, and she arrives at the final having not dropped a set in her last three matches here.

How does Andreeva handle her first Grand Slam final? History offers examples of gifted teenagers performing below their level when the moment arrives for the first time. But Andreeva's performances at this tournament suggest a mental game that does not easily buckle.

Will history favour the qualifier? Goolagong won Roland Garros in 1971 on her first appearance in the final. The precedent is there, if distant and imperfect.

What's Next
