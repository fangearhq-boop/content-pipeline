Andreeva Dismantles Kostyuk 6-1, 6-3 to Reach First Grand Slam Final at Roland Garros

By Elena Voss | June 5, 2026

Mirra Andreeva walked off Court Philippe-Chatrier on Thursday having booked her place in a first career Grand Slam final with a performance that belied her 19 years. The Russian teenager defeated Marta Kostyuk 6-1, 6-3 in 76 minutes to reach the Roland Garros women's final — a result that felt almost as stunning for its manner as its destination.

Kostyuk arrived in Paris as one of the season's most compelling stories. The Ukrainian had gone 17-0 on clay in 2026, defeating Iga Swiatek and Elina Svitolina at this tournament, and carried a 2-0 advantage over Andreeva in their head-to-head this year from wins in the Brisbane and Madrid finals. None of it translated onto the court on Thursday.

Andreeva broke in the opening game and never let Kostyuk settle, building a 4-0 lead in the first set and closing out the opener in just 23 minutes. The second set offered a brief glimpse of a response when Kostyuk broke to make it 4-3, but Andreeva held her nerve, won the next two games, and sealed the match with her sixth break of the contest.

A Teenager Among Legends

Born on April 29, 2007, Andreeva was 19 years and 37 days old when she completed the win. She becomes the fourth-youngest Roland Garros women's finalist in 30 years. Only Martina Hingis, Kim Clijsters, and Coco Gauff were younger when they reached the same stage of this tournament — company that underlines how extraordinary a trajectory Andreeva is on.

This is her second Roland Garros semifinal in as many appearances at this stage. In 2025 she reached the last four and fell; now she goes further. At 19 she is the No. 8 seed, a top-10 fixture, and a player whose baseline game and footwork on red clay looked, for 76 minutes on Thursday, like the finished article.

The No-Handshake Moment

The match ended, and the players approached the net. There was no handshake.

Kostyuk has publicly stated she will not shake hands with Russian players while Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine continues — an ongoing policy she has maintained throughout her career and reaffirmed after Thursday's loss. The exception she has publicly named is Daria Kasatkina, who has spoken openly against the war.

The absence of a post-match handshake drew immediate reaction across social media. Some criticized the gesture as unsporting; others argued Kostyuk's stance, given the destruction the conflict has brought to her home country, is a principled one. Neither player addressed the moment at length at the net, and both departed the court without incident.

The backdrop does not diminish what Andreeva achieved. She outplayed a player who had been arguably the best on clay in the world over the previous two months, doing so with a composure that barely wavered from the first game to the last.

What's Next

Andreeva will face Maja Chwalinska in the Roland Garros women's final on Saturday, June 6, not before 3:00 PM Paris time (9:00 AM ET). Chwalinska, ranked No. 114 in the world, became the first qualifier in Roland Garros history to reach the final after defeating Diana Shnaider 7-6(4), 6-4 in Thursday's other semifinal. Neither player has ever appeared in a Grand Slam final. Andreeva enters Saturday as the clear favourite.
