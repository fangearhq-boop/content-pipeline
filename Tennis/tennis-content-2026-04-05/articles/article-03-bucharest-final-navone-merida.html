Bucharest Final: Navone Saved Match Points to Get Here. Now He Faces a Qualifier Who Had Nothing to Lose

By Ryan Calloway | Tennis Fanrecap | April 5, 2026

The Tiriac Open final in Bucharest on Sunday features two players who should not have needed to fight this hard just to get to the last match of the week. That both of them did is what makes the final worth watching.

Mariano Navone of Argentina, seeded seventh, survived a match that lasted 3 hours and 32 minutes against Botic van de Zandschulp. He trailed 5-7 in the first set, won the second in a tiebreak, and then held on through the third against a Dutch opponent who twice had him at match point. He saved both. Then he won 7-5.

Waiting for him in the final is Daniel Merida Aguilar of Spain, 21 years old, a qualifier who arrived in Bucharest this week without a single ATP Tour-level match win.

Navone's Marathon Path

Navone, who has played on the ATP Tour long enough to know what finals feel and look like — this is his third — came into his semifinal against van de Zandschulp as the higher-ranked player and the expected winner. Tennis rarely follows expectations on clay.

The match went the full distance. Van de Zandschulp, ranked eighth in the draw, pushed Navone to his limit and then past it twice — two match points, neither converted. Navone's ability to perform on the two most important points of the match demonstrated the kind of experience that separates players who have been here before from those who have not.

After 212 minutes of competitive tennis, Navone reached the final. He will need to recover quickly.

Merida Aguilar: The Qualifier With Nothing to Lose

Merida Aguilar's story is simpler and more dramatic for its simplicity. He entered Bucharest through qualifying. He had not won an ATP Tour-level match before this tournament. He is 21 years old and was not expected to be in the conversation for the title.

In his semifinal, he faced No. 3-seeded Fabian Marozsan of Hungary. After dropping the first set in a tiebreak 6-7(4), Merida Aguilar won the next two sets 6-3 and 6-1. The final two sets were not close. He had found something in the week that translated into a level of tennis significantly above what his ranking would suggest.

Qualifying runs like this are not simply about playing well. They require managing an escalating level of opposition across multiple rounds without the benefit of a protected ranking or seeding status. Merida did it without breaking.

Two Firsts, One Title

Both finalists share one biographical detail: neither has played an ATP Tour final before. For Navone, this is his third opportunity at a title. For Merida Aguilar, it is a first — arrived at in the most unlikely possible way, through the qualifying draw, without a prior tour win to his name.

The experience gap between a player in his third final and a player in his first is significant. Navone has been here before. He knows what the buildup feels like, what the match-day pressure feels like, and how to manage the occasion. Merida Aguilar has none of that reference point.

Whether that experience gap translates into a result, or whether the momentum and confidence of Merida's week overrides it, is the central question of the Bucharest final.

What's Next

The Bucharest champion earns 250 ATP ranking points. For Navone, a title would continue a strong clay season for Argentine players. For Merida Aguilar, any result this week — already a finalist with guaranteed ranking points — is a career breakthrough that will take him into the top 100 for the first time. The ATP clay season moves to the Monte Carlo Masters and then the ATP 500 at Barcelona. Sunday's winner carries the momentum from Bucharest into a month that culminates with Roland Garros.
