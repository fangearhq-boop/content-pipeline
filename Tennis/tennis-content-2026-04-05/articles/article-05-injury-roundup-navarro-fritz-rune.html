Clay Season Begins Without Them: The Health Struggles of Navarro, Fritz, and Rune

By Marcus Cole | Tennis Fanrecap | April 5, 2026

As the clay swing gets underway in Monte Carlo, three of tennis's most prominent names are watching from the sidelines — each managing a different health challenge, each with an uncertain timeline ahead.

Emma Navarro, Taylor Fritz, and Holger Rune were all expected to be significant players in 2026. Instead, all three are navigating difficult circumstances that have changed the shape of their seasons and, in some cases, their careers.

Emma Navarro: Fighting Through the Unknown

Navarro's 2026 has been defined by absence rather than results. The American, who climbed to a career-high ranking of No. 8 in the world, withdrew from both the Miami Open and the Credit One Charleston Open citing persistent health issues. Her ranking has dropped to No. 27, and her 2026 match record stands at 4-9.

In a statement, Navarro offered a candid explanation: "I have been dealing with some health struggles over the past year or so that I'm continuing to sort out." She provided no specific diagnosis or timeline for her return.

The vagueness of the announcement is characteristic of athletes who are still seeking answers themselves. What is clear is that the trajectory Navarro was on — top-10 player, Grand Slam contender, one of the WTA's rising American stars — has been interrupted in a way that requires attention before results.

Taylor Fritz: Six Months of Knee Pain

Fritz arrived at the Miami Open with his knee tendinitis already six months old and counting. He competed, but the condition clearly limited what the American No. 7 could sustain through a deep tournament run on hardcourts. He withdrew from Monte Carlo this week, and in a post-Miami statement, he did not shy away from what might come next: "If we're not seeing big improvements, it might be time to slow down and get it healed 100 percent."

Andy Roddick, the former No. 1 American who navigated his own late-career injury issues, publicly supported the idea of Fritz stepping back: the message being that protecting long-term health is worth short-term ranking points.

Fritz is 28 years old. His window as a top-10 player is long, but only if the structural issues in his knee are resolved rather than managed around.

Holger Rune: The Long Road Back From Achilles Surgery

Rune's situation is the most serious of the three. In September 2025, the Danish No. 1 ruptured his left Achilles tendon at the Stockholm Open — an injury that typically requires six to nine months of recovery before return to competition. His ranking has dropped from a peak inside the top 20 to No. 29.

Rune is targeting the Madrid Open in mid-May as his return tournament, which would represent approximately seven to eight months post-surgery. Reports suggest the recovery is progressing, and the targeting of Madrid rather than Roland Garros directly indicates the team is being careful about the timeline.

Achilles recoveries are notoriously variable. Athletes who rush back often face setbacks; those who allow full healing can return to prior form. Rune's talent is not in question — his ranking trajectory before the injury showed a player moving in the right direction. The question is simply how much of that momentum can be rebuilt once he is back on court.

What's Next

For Navarro, the priority is health before tennis. No competitive timeline has been given, and expecting one before she has answers herself is unrealistic. For Fritz, the next signal will come in whether he appears at the Madrid Open (May 1) or takes additional time. For Rune, Madrid remains the stated target — watch for any update in late April on whether his practice sessions are proceeding as planned. All three stories will develop over the next four to six weeks as the clay season moves from Monte Carlo to Madrid to Roland Garros.
