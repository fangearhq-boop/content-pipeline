Charleston Final: Starodubtseva's Cinderella Run Meets Pegula in WTA 500 Showdown

By Elena Voss | Tennis Fanrecap | April 5, 2026

Yuliia Starodubtseva was not supposed to be here. She entered the Credit One Charleston Open on a last-minute main-draw spot after a withdrawal, ranked No. 89 in the world. By the time the tournament reached final day, she had done something that made those entry circumstances feel almost beside the point.

Today, Starodubtseva faces top seed Jessica Pegula in the Charleston final — her first WTA Tour-level final. She is one match from a title that would be among the most unexpected results of the 2026 season.

How Starodubtseva Got Here

The Ukrainian's path through Charleston was defined by a willingness to take the ball early and punish opponents who let her dictate. She moved through the early rounds without dropping a set, and in the quarterfinals she handled Maya Kessler 6-4, 6-4 without drama.

Her semifinal against fifth-seeded Madison Keys was the defining match of her week. Keys is one of the WTA's most feared servers. She can make any opponent feel as though getting the ball in play is the biggest challenge. Starodubtseva neutralized her completely, winning 6-1, 6-4 in 73 minutes. She converted 6 of 8 break points. Keys had no answers.

Starodubtseva's ability to absorb Keys' serve, move it, and redirect with authority suggested a player playing well above her ranking on this surface.

Pegula's Path and What She Brings

Pegula, ranked No. 5 in the world and the top seed in Charleston, has had to work for her place in the final. In the quarterfinals she dropped the first set to No. 7-seeded Diana Shnaider before winning 3-6, 6-3, 6-2. In the semifinals against No. 4-seeded Iva Jovic, she fell behind 0-3 in the first set, recovered to take it 6-4, lost the second 5-7, then closed out the third 6-3.

The 30-year-old has won approximately 75 percent of her first-serve points through the tournament and is a reliable clay performer, if not typically considered a clay specialist. She brings consistency, experience, and an ability to stay in long baseline exchanges — exactly the kind of tools that matter in a final against an in-form opponent.

The Starodubtseva Equation

No. 89 versus No. 5. Late withdrawal entry versus defending-level top seed. First WTA final versus proven tour veteran.

The numbers suggest Pegula. The form through the week suggests the gap might be smaller than the rankings imply. Starodubtseva's semifinal demolition of Keys was not a lucky result — it was a clinical performance by a player who had found her game at the right time.

Finals present a different challenge from semifinals. The occasion, the opponent's caliber, and the extended preparation time all shift the dynamic. How Starodubtseva handles her first career final may determine as much as any tactical consideration.

What's Next

The Charleston champion earns 280 WTA ranking points and the Credit One title. For Starodubtseva, any result this week — finalist guaranteed — is a career-high achievement and will push her into the top 70 for the first time. For Pegula, a Charleston title would be a strong start to her clay season ahead of the European swing. The WTA clay season continues with Stuttgart and Madrid on the horizon, before Roland Garros in late May.
