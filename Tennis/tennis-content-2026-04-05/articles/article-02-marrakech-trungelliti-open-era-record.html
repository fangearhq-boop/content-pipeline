At 36, Trungelliti Becomes the Oldest First-Time Finalist in Open Era History at Marrakech

By Marcus Cole | Tennis Fanrecap | April 5, 2026

When the Grand Prix Hassan II in Marrakech reached its final on Sunday, the two players standing across the net from each other represented something tennis rarely produces: a genuine Open Era first.

Marco Trungelliti of Argentina, 36 years old, walked onto the clay court in Marrakech for his first-ever ATP Tour-level final. In doing so, he became the oldest player in Open Era history to debut in an ATP final, surpassing Victor Estrella Burgos of the Dominican Republic, who was 34 when he reached his first final in 2015.

A Career of Persistence

Trungelliti is not a player who suddenly appeared on tour. He has been grinding through the professional ranks for years — Challengers, Futures, qualifiers, the extended career of a professional player who has made a living in the sport without the spotlight that accompanies a major title.

He entered Marrakech ranked outside the top 100. His run through the draw was defined by the kind of experience that comes from playing thousands of professional matches. In the semifinals, he faced Luciano Darderi of Italy — the defending champion and top seed in the tournament — and defeated him 6-4, 7-6(2). The tiebreak was clinical.

His projected ranking jump from the week: approximately 41 places, enough to push him into the top 100 for the first time in his career regardless of the final's result.

The 19-Year-Old on the Other Side

Waiting in the final was Rafael Jodar of Spain, 19 years old. Jodar also had never played an ATP Tour final before this week. He reached the final by defeating Camilo Ugo Carabelli of Argentina 6-2, 6-1 in a one-sided semifinal performance.

The age gap between the two finalists — 17 years — is the most visible number attached to this final, and it tells a story by itself. One player has been competing in professional tennis for longer than the other has been playing competitively. One arrived at his first final at the end of a long career arc; the other arrived at the beginning.

What the Record Means

The Open Era began in 1968 with the first professional Grand Slams. In nearly six decades of professional men's tennis, no player had reached his debut ATP Tour final for the first time at 36 years of age. Estrella Burgos, at 34, had held that distinction since 2015.

Records like this one often feel arbitrary in isolation. In context, they reflect something real: the difficulty of reaching a first ATP final at any age, and the extraordinary persistence required to still be competing at a level where that is possible at 36. Most professional players who last this long have adjusted their ambitions. Trungelliti apparently had not.

Argentine Clay Season in Full Voice

Trungelliti's final appearance is part of a broader Argentine presence on clay this week. While he competed in Marrakech, Mariano Navone was in the Bucharest final after saving two match points in a marathon semifinal. The two tournaments running simultaneously on clay featured Argentine players in both finals — a point of national pride ahead of Roland Garros season.

What's Next

For Trungelliti, a debut ATP final at 36 — Open Era record already in hand — means the clay season continues with a ranking that will take him into events he previously could not access directly. For Jodar, the experience of a first ATP final at 19 is the kind of early-career marker that shapes a player's understanding of what is possible. The European clay swing continues with the ATP 1000 at Monte Carlo, then Madrid and Rome before Roland Garros. For a 36-year-old Argentine who just made tennis history in Marrakech, those weeks ahead carry a different weight.
