Monte Carlo 2026: The No. 1 Race Between Alcaraz and Sinner Officially Begins on Clay

By Elena Voss | Tennis Fanrecap | April 5, 2026

The Rolex Monte-Carlo Masters draw is set, the clay has been rolled, and the most compelling subplot of the 2026 clay season is officially underway. For the next two months, every match between Carlos Alcaraz and Jannik Sinner will carry the weight of a ranking race so tight it could break either way on any given week.

Entering Monte Carlo, Alcaraz holds No. 1 with 13,590 ranking points. Sinner stands at No. 2 with approximately 13,400 — a gap of roughly 190 points. In most circumstances, 190 points is a comfortable buffer. In this case, it is almost nothing.

The Points Math That Makes Monte Carlo Critical

The reason this week matters so much comes down to points asymmetry. Alcaraz won the Monte Carlo title in 2025 and is defending every one of those 1,000 ranking points. Sinner missed the tournament last year and has zero points to defend.

That creates the conditions for a ranking swing of historic proportions. If Sinner reaches the semifinals while Alcaraz exits before the quarterfinals, Sinner takes the No. 1 ranking for the first time this season. If the two of them meet in the final — widely considered the likeliest outcome if both advance — the winner is mathematically guaranteed to be world No. 1 by Sunday night.

Sinner Arrives in Career-Best Form

For Sinner, this moment arrives at the peak of his momentum. In March, the Italian did something no man had done in the Open Era: he won both the Indian Wells Masters and the Miami Open without dropping a single set. The Sunshine Double has been completed before, but never in such dominant fashion.

Across his run through both hardcourt Masters events, Sinner barely looked troubled. His first serve percentage held, his movement was sharp, and his ability to reset points under pressure looked unshakeable. He enters Monte Carlo as perhaps the hottest player on tour, regardless of where his ranking sits.

Alcaraz: Defending Champion, Familiar Ground

Alcaraz has his own advantages. He knows what it takes to win here — he did it in 2025 — and clay is arguably his best surface, or at least his most natural. The Spaniard is comfortable with the slower ball, the heavy spin, and the longer points that Monte Carlo tends to produce.

His draw opens against the winner of Sebastian Baez versus Stan Wawrinka. Wawrinka, who won Monte Carlo in 2014, is playing what is widely expected to be one of his farewell tournaments in the twilight of his career. Whether he can challenge Baez and then potentially face the world No. 1 in the next round is one of the week's sentimental storylines.

Nine Withdrawals Open the Draw

The Monte Carlo field took significant hits before a ball was struck. Nine players withdrew, including Novak Djokovic, who is targeting the Madrid Open as his clay season entry point. Taylor Fritz, ranked No. 7, is dealing with persistent knee tendinitis and may face a longer absence. Jack Draper opted to stay in London to build his fitness ahead of the summer. Ben Shelton chose to play Houston instead. Holger Rune remains sidelined after rupturing his left Achilles at Stockholm last September.

The departures widen the bracket for both Alcaraz and Sinner, reducing the number of dangerous opponents they could face before the quarterfinals. Alexander Zverev, Lorenzo Musetti, Alex de Minaur, Felix Auger-Aliassime, and Daniil Medvedev round out the top seeds and represent the most likely path through the draw.

What's Next

Main draw first-round action begins Monday. Alcaraz and Sinner both have first-round byes as the top two seeds, meaning their earliest possible entry is Tuesday's second round. Watch for first-round upsets in the bottom half of the draw, where the open spots from withdrawals could allow lower-ranked players to advance further than expected. The quarterfinals on Friday will clarify whether the Alcaraz-Sinner final is truly on track — and whether the No. 1 ranking is about to change hands for the first time in months.
