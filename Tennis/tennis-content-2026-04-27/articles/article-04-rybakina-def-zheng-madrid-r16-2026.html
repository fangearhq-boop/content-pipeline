Rybakina Grinds Past Zheng Again in Three Sets, Advances to Madrid Round of 16

By Ryan Calloway | Tennis Fanrecap | April 27, 2026

Elena Rybakina has now beaten Qinwen Zheng twice in 2026 by coming back from a set down. The formula is becoming familiar: absorb the Chinese player's winner-heavy first set, wait for the unforced errors to accumulate, and find a way through in the third.

On Sunday at the Mutua Madrid Open, it worked again. Rybakina, the No. 2 seed, defeated Zheng 4-6, 6-4, 6-3 in 2 hours and 21 minutes to advance to the round of 16. It was not always clean, and it was not always comfortable — but it was effective, which is all that matters in a tournament at this stage.

The Numbers Tell the Story

The statistics from this match read like a study in contrasts. Zheng generated more winners — 24 to Rybakina's 19 — and struck more aces, nine to seven. For a single set, she was the better player. The problem was her unforced errors: 45 over the course of the match, against Rybakina's 30. That 15-error differential was decisive.

Rybakina won the match 93-86 in total points — a margin that reflects a tight contest overall, but one where consistency and patience ultimately prevailed over aggression and volatility. In clay-court tennis, where slower surfaces extend rallies and amplify errors, Rybakina's approach proved better suited to the conditions.

An Unusual Interruption

The match also featured a notable moment that had nothing to do with tennis: Rybakina stopped play mid-game to dispute a serve that the electronic line-calling system had ruled in. The Kazakh player indicated she disagreed with the call and conveyed her concern to the umpire before play resumed.

It was a brief but telling moment in a match that had its share of tension. The electronic line-calling technology used at Madrid has been a topic of discussion throughout the tournament, and this exchange added another chapter to that conversation.

Madrid's R16 Picture Taking Shape

Rybakina's win adds another top player to what is becoming a compelling WTA round of 16 in Madrid. With Iga Swiatek having retired from the tournament due to illness, the path to the title has opened. Sabalenka remains the clear favourite, but Rybakina — a two-time WTA 500 winner this spring after Stuttgart — is among those with genuine title credentials.

She also holds a head-to-head advantage over most players in the draw. Rybakina is the player opponents least want to face when they need consistent hitting and tactical discipline across three sets. Her ability to absorb a first-set loss and refocus is a rare and valuable skill.

What's Next

Rybakina faces Anastasia Potapova in the Madrid round of 16. Potapova, who advanced on Sunday as well, is a capable player — but the matchup favours Rybakina based on current form and ranking. A quarterfinal win would set up a potential semifinal against Sabalenka, which would be one of the marquee matches of the clay season.

For now, Rybakina has done what she needed to do: grind through a difficult three-setter against a high-quality opponent and reach the last 16. One match at a time.
