Gauff Fights Through Stomach Virus to Reach Madrid R16; Kostyuk Ends Pegula's Tournament

By Elena Voss | Tennis Fanrecap | April 27, 2026

A stomach virus has been moving through the field at the Mutua Madrid Open, and the evidence has been visible on court. Iga Swiatek retired from her third-round match on Sunday afternoon, citing illness. Hours later, Coco Gauff was vomiting into a bin during her own third-round match against Sorana Cirstea — and then went on to win it.

Gauff defeated Cirstea 4-6, 7-5, 6-1 in 2 hours and 21 minutes, fighting through what was clearly significant physical distress. She requested a medical timeout during the latter stages of the match. She returned and won the final set 6-1. It was a display of resilience from the No. 3 seed — one that will only reinforce the sense that Gauff is capable of competing through adversity when she needs to.

The Match: Cirstea Made Her Work

The scoreline does not fully capture how difficult Gauff's afternoon was. Cirstea, a seasoned clay-court competitor, took the first set 6-4 and pushed Gauff deep into the second before the American found her range. The 7-5 set win was hard-earned — Gauff visibly uncomfortable throughout, her movement occasionally compromised by the illness.

What followed in the third set was remarkable. Whatever Gauff was feeling physically, it did not show in her tennis. She won the final set 6-1 in a dominant 30-minute stretch, serving well and constructing points with the kind of clarity that defines her best performances. A medical timeout is not usually followed by a 6-1 set, but then Gauff has never been a player who follows expected patterns.

A Tournament-Wide Illness Narrative

Gauff's win carries a broader storyline. Swiatek's retirement earlier on Sunday prompted the Polish player to confirm that a virus was "somewhere on site" and spreading among players. Swiatek felt so unwell she said she "had zero energy and zero stability." Gauff faced similar symptoms and found a different outcome — not because the illness was less severe, but because she found a way to compete through it.

The virus narrative is now one of the defining subplots of the Madrid second week. How many more players are affected, and how severely, remains to be seen. But Gauff's win shows it can be done: you do not have to retire. You can find your best tennis even when your body is not cooperating.

Kostyuk Stuns No. 5 Seed Pegula

While Gauff was managing her own crisis, Marta Kostyuk was producing one of the cleaner upsets of the tournament. The Ukrainian defeated No. 5 seed Jessica Pegula 6-1, 6-4 in a commanding performance that left little doubt about the margin.

Kostyuk hit 20 winners over the course of the match. Pegula responded with nine. The difference in offensive output was stark — Kostyuk came in with a clear game plan and executed it with a control that kept the fifth seed from ever finding her rhythm. Pegula had been one of the more dangerous players in the upper half of the WTA Madrid draw; her exit opens the path for others.

What's Next

Gauff faces Linda Noskova in the round of 16 today — a Monday clash after Sunday's draining effort. Whether the virus lingers is the key question. If Gauff can recover overnight, she enters as the favourite given her higher ranking and current clay-court form.

Kostyuk also advances to the R16. Her path through the draw now potentially runs toward the Sabalenka-Osaka winner in the quarterfinals, assuming she can clear her R16 opponent first.

The WTA side of Madrid is increasingly wide open. Swiatek is gone. Pegula is gone. The field has narrowed to those with the form, fitness, or will to keep playing. Gauff has demonstrated all three.
