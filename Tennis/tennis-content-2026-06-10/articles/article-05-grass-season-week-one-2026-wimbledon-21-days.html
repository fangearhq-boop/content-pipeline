Grass Season Week One — Three Tournaments, 21 Days to Wimbledon

By Elena Voss | June 10, 2026

Wimbledon begins July 1. That is 21 days from today. The grass season that precedes it is, by design, the most compressed fortnight in tennis — a narrow window where players recalibrate their game, adjust their footwork, and rediscover whether grass suits them in this particular year of their career.

This week, three simultaneous grass-court events are running: the BOSS Open in Stuttgart, the Libema Open in 's-Hertogenbosch, and the HSBC Championships at Queen's Club in London. Each is a different draw, a different city, a different set of results — and together, they are providing the most complete two-day Wimbledon preview of the season.

Who Has Stood Out So Far

Nick Kyrgios won his opening match in Stuttgart without conceding a single break point to Corentin Moutet. Emma Raducanu dropped six points in her opening set against Anna Blinkova at Queen's Club. Katie Boulter came from a set down to defeat No. 8 seed Leylah Fernandez. Defending Stuttgart champion Gabriel Diallo was knocked out in the first round in 's-Hertogenbosch.

Two days of grass tennis have already produced upsets, dominant performances, and returned players making statements. The surface is doing what grass always does: redistributing power in ways that clay and hard-court form cannot predict.

The Wimbledon Picture

There is no dominant pre-Wimbledon favourite right now. Carlos Alcaraz has not appeared on the grass swing. Jannik Sinner — a player who has never won Wimbledon — has no grass appearances this week. The reigning Roland Garros champions, Alexander Zverev and Mirra Andreeva, enter the grass season without points to defend but with significant clay momentum.

That openness is significant. Wimbledon 2026 does not have a player who has dominated the grass season in a way that makes them the prohibitive choice. Last year's grass-court season produced a different picture; this year's is, so far, genuinely contested.

Surface Dynamics

Grass court tennis rewards specific skills. The serve matters more than on clay — an elite serve limits break opportunities and controls sets. Net play, volleys, and sliced backhands all become more effective on a surface where the ball stays low. Movement is different: sliding is not available, and the footing is less stable, which rewards players with compact, efficient footwork over those who rely on heavy topspin and looping groundstrokes.

Kyrgios has all of the grass-court skills. Raducanu's flat ball-striking is natural on the surface. Boulter, who grew up playing on grass in British junior tennis, has the serve and movement that the surface rewards. The players who are thriving early are not a surprise.
