### STORY 4: WTA Athens Inaugural

#### Long-Form Post — 8:00 AM ET

A new tournament opened on the WTA calendar this week, and it comes with a significant piece of history attached: the Vanda Pharmaceuticals Athens Open is the first WTA event held in Athens in 35 years.

The tournament runs July 13-19 at the Athens Olympic Tennis Centre — the venue built for the 2004 Summer Olympics. Top seeds include Clara Tauson, Barbora Krejcikova, and Maria Sakkari, who is playing in front of a home crowd for what appears to be her first WTA home tournament in years. Sakkari opened with one of the most dominant results of the week: a 6-0, 7-6(1) win over Liudmila Kudermetova on Day 1.

The rest of the draw delivered too. Krejcikova beat Viktoriya Tomova 6-3, 6-3 on Tuesday. Tauson handled Hibino 7-5, 6-4. Zheng Qinwen, the 2024 Olympic gold medalist, upset sixth-seeded Bouzas Maneiro 7-5, 6-1 on opening day — a statement from a player who never needs much time to impose herself.

A new tournament, a historic venue, and Greece's best player on home soil. It's a good start for Athens.

Are you following the Athens Open this week — and do you think Sakkari can win it?

### STORY 1: Wawrinka's Farewell at Gstaad

#### Long-Form Post — 10:30 AM ET

On Tuesday at the EFG Swiss Open in Gstaad, Stan Wawrinka stepped onto the court for what may have been his final match at the tournament where it all started.

Wawrinka made his ATP debut at Gstaad more than two decades ago. He returned this week at 41 and fell to Portugal's Jaime Faria 6-7(8), 6-4, 6-4 in two hours and 38 minutes. The match was honest and competitive — Faria earned the win — and that seemed right for the occasion. Wawrinka has never been the kind of champion who wanted sentiment to carry him through.

What followed was the part that mattered to the crowd. Tournament organizers called the ceremony after the match — former Swiss Davis Cup teammates gathered on court, a standing ovation rang through the Gstaad stands, and the tournament presented Wawrinka with a custom pair of skis bearing his nickname "The Man" and the geometric print from the shorts he wore during his iconic 2015 Roland Garros title run. That detail — the Roland Garros print — is the kind of touch that lands. Someone at the tournament paid attention.

Wawrinka's farewell tour of the ATP continues through the end of 2026. Three Grand Slams. One of the most admired careers in the sport. And a match at Gstaad to close a chapter that started there.

### STORY 2: Canadian Open Entry List

#### Long-Form Post — 12:30 PM ET

The National Bank Open in Montreal published its 2026 entry list this week, and one number defines the field: 71 of the top 72 ATP players are entered. Tournament organizers described it as the strongest draw in the event's history.

The list reads like the post-Wimbledon standings: Jannik Sinner, who won the 2023 Canadian Open and just defended his Wimbledon title, is in the field. Novak Djokovic — who reached the Wimbledon semifinals before losing to Sinner — is confirmed. Alexander Zverev, who won Roland-Garros and reached the Wimbledon final, will also be in Montreal. Felix Auger-Aliassime plays the event on home soil for the ninth time. Wild cards go to Gaël Monfils and Gabriel Diallo. Denis Shapovalov earns direct entry.

The one name absent from the list: Carlos Alcaraz. His wrist injury — tenosynovitis sustained at the Barcelona Open in April — has now cost him Roland-Garros, Wimbledon, and the Canadian Open. A recent video showed him hitting at his academy, which is the first public indication of progress, but no official timeline has been provided. Tennis365 reported this week that there are growing concerns he may not return in 2026 at all.

On the women's side in Toronto, 72 of the top 75 WTA players are entered. The draw is expected around July 29-30, with main draw play beginning August 3.

Who do you think wins the Canadian Open this year — and do you believe Alcaraz makes it back for the US Open?

### STORY 3: Clay Courts Day 3

#### Long-Form Post — 3:00 PM ET

Three ATP 250 events are running simultaneously this week — Bastad, Gstaad, and Umag — and Day 3 produced the kind of results that remind you why the clay swing matters.

The most talked-about match came from Umag, where French qualifier Maxime Droguet eliminated fifth-seeded Zizou Blockx 3-6, 6-2, 6-0 on Tuesday. The deciding stretch was not close. Droguet used drop shots effectively and won 77% of his first-serve points in the final two sets. Top seed Flavio Cobolli, who reached the Wimbledon quarterfinals last week, now enters the draw today for the first time — he plays qualifier Roman Burruchaga in his R2 opener.

In Bastad, Grigor Dimitrov won his first-round match over Dalibor Svrcina 7-6(5), 1-6, 6-4 and advances to face Nuno Borges on center court today. Rublev, the top seed, beat Andrea Pellegrino 6-3, 6-2 and stays in contention. Wednesday's schedule also features Darderi against Altmaier and Van de Zandschulp against Tirante.

In Gstaad, Tsitsipas beat Buse 6-4, 6-4 and Cerundolo advanced in three sets. The day's most anticipated match is defending champion Alexander Bublik against Quentin Halys — Bublik will need to justify his top billing against a player coming in with momentum from a competitive R1 win.

All three events run through July 19. The clay swing is just getting started.

### STORY 5: Alcaraz Wrist Update

#### Long-Form Post — 6:00 PM ET

Carlos Alcaraz's name does not appear on the Canadian Open entry list for the National Bank Open in Montreal. His absence is the latest point in a timeline that has now stretched across three months and three missed majors.

The wrist injury — diagnosed as tenosynovitis — was sustained on April 14 at the Barcelona Open. Since then, Alcaraz has not played a competitive match. He missed Roland-Garros. He missed Wimbledon, where he was the defending champion. And now the Canadian Open entry list carries his absence as a footnote in an otherwise historically deep field.

Tennis365 reported this week that there are growing concerns within tennis circles that Alcaraz may not return to the tour in 2026 at all. The report stopped short of calling the season over — and a recently circulated video showed him hitting balls at his training academy, which is the first sign of physical progress — but no official timeline has come from his team.

The US Open is scheduled to begin August 24. If Alcaraz misses Canada and Cincinnati, that becomes his only remaining realistic target in 2026. The complication: wrist tendon injuries do not always cooperate with deadlines. The ranking math is significant too — his 2025 Wimbledon and 2025 Canadian Open points will expire without defense, which will drop him from No. 3 further down the list.

Alcaraz is 23 years old. The career is long. But 2026 is starting to look like a year that the injury owned.

Do you think Alcaraz makes it back for the US Open — and if he does, how competitive can he be?
