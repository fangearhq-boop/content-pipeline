Gstaad Gave Wawrinka His Start. On Tuesday, It Said Goodbye.

By Marcus Cole July 15, 2026

The Match That Closed a Chapter

Stan Wawrinka walked onto the clay at the EFG Swiss Open in Gstaad on Tuesday, and it meant something specific that he did. This is the tournament where he made his ATP debut more than two decades ago. He arrived then as an unknown Swiss teenager. He arrived this week at 41, with three Grand Slam titles and a career that defined what late-blooming excellence looks like at the highest level of the sport.

He lost to Portugal's Jaime Faria 6-7(8), 6-4, 6-4 in two hours and 38 minutes. The match was competitive, honest, and earned by the younger man — Faria pushed Wawrinka to a tiebreak in the first set, dropped the second when Wawrinka fought through, and then won the third cleanly. For Wawrinka, who has never been the kind of champion who wanted sentiment to substitute for competition, it was probably the right kind of goodbye: not a walk, not a retirement, but a real match against a real opponent who won it fair.

The Ceremony That Followed

What came after the match was the part that mattered to the crowd. Gstaad brought out former Swiss Davis Cup teammates to stand with Wawrinka on the court. The tournament presented him with a custom pair of skis engraved with his nickname "The Man" and decorated with the geometric print that appeared on the shorts he wore during his iconic 2015 Roland Garros title run. That detail — the Roland Garros print — is the kind of touch that tells you someone at the tournament paid close attention. It is not a generic sponsor gift. It is a reference to the specific match, the specific moment, the specific outfit that an entire generation of tennis fans will picture when they think of Wawrinka at his best (per ATP Tour).

The standing ovation from the Gstaad crowd was not a surprise. Wawrinka has always had a particular resonance with European tennis audiences, who watched him grow from a promising youngster into one of the most formidable one-day players the sport has seen. His capacity to produce something breathtaking on his best days — the diagonal running forehand, the backhand that did not exist in any textbook — made every match worth watching even when his ranking or form suggested otherwise.

What Three Grand Slams Looked Like

Wawrinka won his first Grand Slam at the 2014 Australian Open, defeating Rafael Nadal in the final. He was 28 years old. In tennis terms, that is late — most champions who win at that age have already accumulated several major titles by their mid-twenties. Wawrinka had not. He had been ranked just outside the top five for years, playing in Djokovic and Federer's shadow, collecting results that were good but not the result.

Then he won the Australian Open. Then the 2015 Roland Garros, defeating Djokovic in the final — wearing those geometric shorts, hitting that backhand. Then the 2016 US Open, again defeating Djokovic. Three Grand Slams. Three times he beat the best player in the world to win one. That is a specific kind of record. It does not belong to someone who won in the absence of competition. It belongs to someone who, on three occasions, showed up at the biggest stage and was simply better than the person who was supposed to be unbeatable (per ATP Tour).

The Farewell Tour Continues

Gstaad is not the last match. Wawrinka confirmed that his farewell tour runs through the end of the 2026 season, which means there are still months of tennis ahead. More tournaments, more matches, more ceremonies in cities that will want to say goodbye on their own terms. The Swiss legend has given everyone plenty to honor.

The Gstaad ceremony had a particular weight, though, because of the history. Making your debut at a tournament and then closing it there is not something most players get to do. Most careers end abruptly — injury, form, the simple arithmetic of the ranking system. Wawrinka has been able to choose his ending, and he chose to make sure Gstaad was part of it.

What's Next

Wawrinka will continue on the ATP calendar through the end of 2026. The clay swing wraps up this week and next before the hard-court season begins in North America. Which events he enters as the tour moves into August will become clear as he settles into the final months of a remarkable career. For the Gstaad crowd, though, Tuesday may have already felt like the goodbye that mattered most.
