Alcaraz Is Out of the Canadian Open. Is His 2026 Season Over?

By Elena Voss July 15, 2026

The Entry List Tells the Story

The National Bank Open in Montreal published its 2026 ATP player entry list this week. The field contains 71 of the top 72 players in the world. The 72nd is Carlos Alcaraz, who is not in the field. His name also did not appear at Roland-Garros. It was absent from Wimbledon. The Canadian Open is the third major event in a row that will take place without him (per Tennis365).

The injury that caused all of it happened on April 14 at the Barcelona Open. Alcaraz retired from his quarterfinal match with tenosynovitis in his right wrist — an inflammation of the tendon sheath that, when it reaches the level of severity that produces a mid-match retirement, does not resolve in days. It has now been three months. He is still not playing.

The Timeline of What He Has Missed

To understand what the wrist has cost Alcaraz, it helps to list the events in sequence. After retiring from Barcelona on April 14, he withdrew from Madrid (where he was the defending champion) and Rome. He was then ruled out of Roland-Garros, ending any chance of defending his 2025 title. He withdrew from Wimbledon, where he had won in 2023 and 2024. And now Canada, where he won the 2025 National Bank Open in Montreal, is proceeding without him.

Each of those absences comes with a ranking consequence. Grand Slam titles and Masters 1000 victories carry the sport's most valuable ranking points. When a player cannot defend those points the following year, the numbers drop. From No. 3 in the current ATP rankings, Alcaraz will lose points from his 2025 Wimbledon and 2025 Canadian Open victories as those weeks pass without defense. The fall in ranking will accelerate the longer the wrist keeps him out.

The Current Signals

A video circulated recently on social media showing Alcaraz hitting at his training academy in Spain. It was described as the first visible evidence of physical activity since his withdrawal, and for his supporters, it was a reason for some optimism. His team has not issued an official update on his timeline, and no target tournament has been announced (per Tennis365).

Tennis365 reported this week that concerns are growing within tennis circles that his 2026 season may effectively be over. That framing is cautious — it reflects sentiment and concern rather than a medical determination — but it is also based on the fact that no return timeline has been offered and no events beyond Canada are confirmed. Tendon injuries in the wrist are notoriously unpredictable because wrist mechanics are involved in almost every shot. Rushing back creates the risk of re-injury. His team appears to be treating this carefully rather than targeting a specific date.

What the US Open Represents

The US Open begins August 24 — approximately 40 days from today. If Alcaraz does not return for the Canadian Open or the Cincinnati Masters (which immediately precedes the US Open), New York becomes the only remaining target in 2026 for a player who entered the year as one of the three most anticipated competitors in the sport.

Whether he can compete at the US Open on short preparation, following months away from the tour, is a genuine question. The hard-court conditions in New York place specific demands on wrist mechanics — heavy topspin, fast bounces, and high-contact rallies. A wrist that is healing but not fully tested would face a difficult proving ground immediately.

Alcaraz is 23 years old, which means the career perspective is long. Injuries at this stage, even prolonged ones, have not historically ended the careers of elite players his age. But 2026 is increasingly looking like a season that belongs to the wrist injury more than to the player.

What's Next

The National Bank Open entry list is set. The Canadian Open draw will be released around July 29-30. Alcaraz's name will not be in it. Whether his team announces a US Open entry — and what condition he arrives in if they do — is the next significant update to watch. Until then, 71 of 72 top players will compete in Montreal without him.
