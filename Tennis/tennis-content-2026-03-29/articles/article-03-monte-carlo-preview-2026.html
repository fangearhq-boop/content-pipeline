Monte Carlo 2026: Clay Season Opens With Alcaraz vs. Sinner as the Central Storyline

By Tennis Fanrecap | March 29, 2026

The hard-court season is over. The Sunshine Doubles have been completed. The Miami trophies have been lifted. Now comes clay — and it begins next week at one of tennis's most historic venues.

The Monte Carlo Masters runs April 5-12 at the Monte Carlo Country Club in Roquebrune-Cap-Martin, France, perched above the Mediterranean Sea. With a 56-player draw — the smallest on the Masters circuit — and a non-mandatory entry format that adds intrigue to the field, Monte Carlo is the annual statement match for clay-court ambition.

Alcaraz: The Man to Beat on Red Clay

Carlos Alcaraz won Monte Carlo in 2025, then added Rome and Roland Garros to make the 2025 clay season his personal domain. He arrives as defending champion, which means he carries a significant points burden — but also the psychological edge of knowing exactly how to win on this surface.

Alcaraz's early exit at Miami (lost to Sebastian Korda in the third round) will have stung. He enters Monte Carlo with something to prove, and red clay is where his game — aggressive, physical, built for long rallies — comes fully alive. He has won the last two Monte Carlo titles he contested.

Sinner: Arriving on Form No One Can Match

The complicating factor for everyone in the Monte Carlo draw is Jannik Sinner. The world No. 2 just completed the Sunshine Double, winning Indian Wells and Miami without dropping a set at the latter tournament. He arrives on the best run of form of his career, facing a surface — clay — that has historically been his relative weakness.

Sinner's career record at Monte Carlo stands at 10-4. He has never won the title. But his game has continued to develop, his physicality and baseline depth are ideally suited to clay conditions, and his mental composure has never been better demonstrated than in the Miami final against a server as formidable as Lehečka. Monte Carlo feels like a genuine opportunity.

Notable Absences and Wildcard Storylines

Novak Djokovic will not be at Monte Carlo, citing his ongoing right shoulder injury. It is his first absence from the tournament since 2011 — ending a run of 14 consecutive appearances. Djokovic last played at Indian Wells, where he lost to Jack Draper in the fourth round, and has not competed since. He is expected to target the Madrid Masters in late April as his clay-season debut.

Holger Rune is also absent with an Achilles injury, removing one of the most dangerous potential dark horses on clay.

Wildcards add a sentimental dimension to the draw. Stan Wawrinka and Gaël Monfils — two of the most beloved clay-court entertainers of their generation — have received wildcards. Both are in the final stages of their careers, and either could make a last memorable run on the courts they have graced for years. Matteo Berrettini and 17-year-old Moïse Kouamé (who became the youngest Masters 1000 winner since Rafael Nadal in 2003 when he won a match at Miami last week) also hold wildcards.

What's Next

Qualifying begins April 4. The main draw commences April 5, with the final scheduled for April 12. Draw details will be released in the coming days, and the seeding structure will reflect the post-Miami rankings — placing Alcaraz as No. 1 seed and Sinner as No. 2.

Alexander Zverev, Lorenzo Musetti (2025 finalist), and a full complement of clay specialists round out a competitive field that, while missing Djokovic and Rune, retains genuine depth. The Alcaraz-Sinner collision — if the draw delivers it in the later rounds — would be among the most watched clay-court matches in years.
