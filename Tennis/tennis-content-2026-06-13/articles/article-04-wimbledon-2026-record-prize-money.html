Wimbledon Sets Record £64.2 Million Prize Fund for 2026

By Ryan Calloway June 13, 2026

The Number and What It Means

The All England Club announced Friday that the 2026 Championships will carry total prize money of £64.2 million, a 20% increase from the £53.5 million distributed in 2025. The £10.7 million uplift is the largest single-year increase in the tournament's 149-year history. Each singles champion collects £3.6 million, up from £3 million last year. Runners-up receive £1.8 million, an increase of roughly 18.4%.

Players who lose in the first round of singles take home £80,000, up over 21% from 2025. That number matters more than it might seem — for players ranked outside the top 100 who qualify for Wimbledon, a first-round check is often a meaningful portion of their annual tournament income. The qualifying competition prize fund rises 25% to £6.2 million, the biggest proportional jump across all categories.

The Context: Player Pressure Worked

This announcement does not exist in a vacuum. At Roland Garros just weeks ago, players including Aryna Sabalenka and Coco Gauff publicly cut their press conference appearances short in a coordinated protest over Grand Slam prize money distribution. The players' stated case: Roland Garros generated roughly 395 million euros in revenue in 2025 but paid players only about 14.3% of that, compared to the 22% share at standard ATP and WTA events. Players demanded a larger cut, and they demanded it loudly.

Wimbledon's 20% increase is a direct response to that pressure. The announcement came before the Wimbledon fortnight begins, signaling that the All England Club was aware the conversation was coming and moved first. Players had collectively lobbied for total prize money of £71 million. The gap between the ask and the offer is roughly £7 million. Some players will view this as a significant win. Others will treat it as a down payment on a longer campaign.

What's Next

Wimbledon 2026 runs June 29 through July 12. The draw takes place Friday, June 26. Expect the prize money conversation to resurface at the US Open later this summer, as players and tournaments continue negotiating how Grand Slam revenue gets divided. The Wimbledon announcement sets a new benchmark for what the other three Grand Slams may face as demands in the months ahead.
