Djokovic Returns at Rome After Six-Week Absence: The Comeback Starts Now

By Ryan Calloway | May 5, 2026

The 38-year-old confirmed his Rome entry after a right shoulder injury cost him three consecutive Masters events. His target is Roland Garros — and his 25th Grand Slam title.

Novak Djokovic has returned to professional tennis. After more than six weeks away from Tour competition — his longest absence in recent memory — the 38-year-old confirmed his entry into the 2026 Internazionali BNL d'Italia in Rome, beginning what may be the most consequential comeback of his career.

The injury that sidelined him was to his right shoulder, an issue that first presented at the BNP Paribas Open at Indian Wells in late March. What was initially hoped to be a manageable setback extended further than anticipated: Djokovic withdrew from the Miami Open, skipped the Monte-Carlo Masters, and pulled out of the Madrid Open, where he had been entered and hoped to compete. Three consecutive Masters events missed. Three weeks of clay-court competition lost.

The Rome draw placed him against Hungarian Marton Fucsovics or a qualifier in his opening match. He is in the bottom half of the draw, in a quarter that also features 8th seed Lorenzo Musetti — the Italian crowd's beloved home favourite — as well as 11th seed Jiri Lehecka and 23rd seed Casper Ruud. The path to the final runs through accomplished clay-court players, but none of them are Sinner, who occupies the opposite half of the bracket.

Training Signals Progress

Djokovic was spotted training in Marbella in the days before Rome, and the reports from those sessions were encouraging. His movement looked fluid, and his shoulder appeared to be bearing the load of full practice sessions without setbacks. The cautious optimism around his Rome participation had been building since late April.

He addressed his injury situation directly in the days before Rome. "I'm continuing my recovery in order to be back soon," Djokovic said when withdrawing from Madrid. Since then, his communications have grown more confident, culminating in the Rome confirmation. He told Radiotelevisión Española: "At least for Roland Garros I'm ready," framing Rome explicitly as preparation for the clay-court Grand Slam that begins qualifying on May 18.

What's at Stake in Paris

The Roland Garros motivation is significant. Djokovic has won the French Open three times — in 2016, 2021, and 2023 — and holds 24 Grand Slam titles overall. A 25th title at Roland Garros would represent a deeply personal milestone for a player who has spent years rewriting the record books. He lost the 2026 Australian Open final earlier this season, making Roland Garros the next opportunity.

The landscape in Paris looks unusual. Carlos Alcaraz, the two-time defending champion, has withdrawn due to a wrist injury. Jannik Sinner enters as the overwhelming favourite. That changes the calculus around Djokovic's realistic chances — but "unrealistic" is a word that has rarely applied to Djokovic at Grand Slams. He has won Roland Garros from positions of doubt before.

What's Next

Djokovic's first match in Rome will come Wednesday or Thursday in the ATP main draw's early rounds. His performance against Fucsovics or his qualifier opponent will provide the first real indication of where his game is after six weeks of rehabilitation. If he moves through comfortably and the shoulder holds, he could build match sharpness across three or four rounds before Roland Garros qualifying begins May 18. The main draw in Paris starts May 25. Djokovic's return to form — and to fitness — will be among the most closely watched storylines of the clay-court swing's final fortnight.
