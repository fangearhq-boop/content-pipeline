Sinner Arrives in Rome Targeting Sixth Consecutive Masters 1000 Title

By Marcus Cole | May 5, 2026

After becoming the first man ever to win five consecutive Masters titles, Sinner opens his Rome campaign Wednesday — and the home crowd at the Foro Italico will be in full voice behind him.

When Jannik Sinner dismantled Alexander Zverev 6-1, 6-2 in the Madrid final on Sunday — in just 57 minutes — he crossed a threshold no player in the 36-year history of the ATP Masters 1000 series had ever reached. Five consecutive titles. Roger Federer never managed it. Neither did Rafael Nadal. Neither did Novak Djokovic. Sinner did it on a Sunday afternoon in Madrid, and now he has arrived in Rome to add a sixth.

The numbers behind his current streak are staggering. Sinner is 28-0 in Masters 1000 matches since losing a set in the 2025 Paris final. Those 28 consecutive victories have been spread across five cities, two different surfaces, and five different final opponents. He is 30-2 on the 2026 season overall. His ATP ranking sits at 14,350 points — a lead of 1,390 over the injured and absent Carlos Alcaraz.

The scale of the achievement only becomes clear when you consider what it took. The Paris Masters, Indian Wells, Miami, Monte-Carlo, and Madrid are five of the nine ATP Masters 1000 events. Sinner has now won all five — consecutively — at a time when the tour's second, third, and fourth-best players have all been competing at full strength.

Home Soil, New History

Rome adds an emotional dimension that the other venues could not fully provide. The Foro Italico is Italian tennis, and Sinner is Italian tennis personified. The crowd that greets him when he walks out for his first-round match — against American Alex Michelsen or Austrian Sebastian Ofner — will be among the loudest he has played in front of this year. The Italian fans have followed his rise from promising junior to world No. 1 with genuine pride, and a sixth consecutive title, in Rome, would be something the sport has simply never seen.

Sinner is positioned in the top half of the draw, separated from Novak Djokovic, who occupies the opposite half. His section includes Felix Auger-Aliassime, Ben Shelton, and Daniil Medvedev — formidable opponents for any player not currently on a 28-match Masters winning streak. For Sinner, these are simply the next names on the bracket.

The Record in Context

Djokovic holds the record for most consecutive Masters 1000 titles played, winning four in a row on three occasions, and Nadal matched that at his clay-court peak. Djokovic also won at five consecutive Masters events he entered across late 2014 and 2015, but the calendar interruption — Madrid fell between Monte-Carlo and Rome, and he skipped it — means the true consecutive standard remained at four until Sinner cleared it last Sunday.

Sinner himself said after Madrid: "I think there is a lot of work behind it. A lot of dedication and sacrifice I put in every day. Obviously it means a lot to me, seeing these results." The understatement was characteristic — the results speak for themselves.

What's Next

Sinner's Rome campaign begins Wednesday May 6. The Foro Italico clay suits his aggressive baseline game, and his dominant clay-court record this season — combining Monte-Carlo and Madrid titles with a 30-match ATP winning streak on clay going into Rome — suggests he will be very difficult to stop. After Rome, Roland Garros qualifying starts May 18. Sinner has never won the French Open and enters Paris as the overwhelming favourite to claim it. The question of whether anyone can disrupt the most dominant sustained run in recent Masters history is becoming harder to answer with each passing Sunday.
