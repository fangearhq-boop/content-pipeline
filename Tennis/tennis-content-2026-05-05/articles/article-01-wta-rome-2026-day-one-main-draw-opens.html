WTA Rome 2026: Main Draw Opens at Foro Italico as Paolini Begins Title Defense

By Elena Voss | May 5, 2026

The 83rd Italian Open women's draw is underway — and defending champion Jasmine Paolini faces the deepest field of the clay season.

The 2026 Internazionali BNL d'Italia opened its women's main draw on Tuesday at the Foro Italico in Rome, launching what promises to be the most compelling WTA 1000 event of the clay season. The 83rd edition of the Italian Open — offering €7,228,080 in prize money — runs through May 17, the day before Roland Garros qualifying begins.

The field entering this week includes eight of the world's finest players as seeds, all of whom advanced past the Madrid Open with their rankings largely intact from the season's most recent major reshuffle. At No. 1, Aryna Sabalenka arrives as the betting favourite, having posted a 26-1 record on the 2026 season. Elena Rybakina is the second seed, Coco Gauff the third, and Iga Swiatek — the four-time Roland Garros champion — is the fourth seed, entering on what she hopes will be a return to clay-court form.

The remaining seeds — Jessica Pegula (5), Amanda Anisimova (6), Elina Svitolina (7), and Mirra Andreeva (8) — complete the top tier. Andreeva, who reached the Madrid final before losing to Marta Kostyuk, moved up to a career-best No. 7 in the world rankings after Madrid and enters Rome as one of the most dangerous unseeded threats to the top four.

Paolini's Defense Begins

The most emotionally charged storyline of the tournament belongs to Jasmine Paolini, who enters as the 9th seed and the defending champion. The Italian won the 2025 Rome title on these same red clay courts at the Foro Italico, defeating Gauff in the final to claim what was the biggest clay-court victory of her career. She is also defending her doubles title alongside Olympic partner Sara Errani.

Being seeded 9th in a field where you are the defending champion is an unusual position, but it reflects the depth of the current WTA tour rather than any perceived weakness in Paolini's game. The crowd at the Foro Italico will be vocally behind her throughout. The question is whether the support, combined with her title-defence experience on these courts, can carry her deep into the second week.

The draw has placed Gauff (No. 3) and Paolini (No. 9) in the same quarter, meaning the pair could potentially meet in the quarterfinals — a rematch of the 2025 final. Andreeva (No. 8) is also projected into that section, meaning the quarterfinal bracket in the bottom half of the draw may be the most competitive stage of the entire tournament.

First-Round Matchups

Sabalenka, who has been the dominant force in women's tennis for over two years, opens her campaign against either Barbora Krejcikova or Elsa Jacquemot. Gauff faces either Yulia Putintseva or Tereza Valentova in her opener. Swiatek's first match is against either Caty McNally or Daria Kasatkina — two players capable of troubling the Polish world No. 4 on her best day.

Rybakina, who has been one of the most powerful ball-strikers on clay in recent seasons, opens against either Maria Sakkari or a qualifier. Rybakina's flat, heavy groundstrokes translate well to red clay despite her natural preference for faster surfaces, and she is widely regarded as the player most capable of challenging Sabalenka in the final if both navigate their halves cleanly.

What's Next

The WTA Italian Open runs through Saturday May 17, with the women's final scheduled for that afternoon. The ATP main draw begins on Wednesday May 6, meaning both tournaments overlap for most of the week at the Foro Italico. Rome is the final WTA 1000 event before Roland Garros qualifying begins May 18 and the main draw starts May 25. For players like Paolini, Swiatek, and Andreeva, this week represents their last major chance to tune up and build momentum before Paris. The Roman clay has a way of separating the contenders from the pretenders — and by Friday, the field will have narrowed considerably.
