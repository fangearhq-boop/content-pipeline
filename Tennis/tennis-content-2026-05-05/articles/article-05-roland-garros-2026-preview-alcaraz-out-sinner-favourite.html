Roland Garros 2026: Alcaraz Out, Sinner Clear Favourite as Qualifying Opens in 13 Days

By Marcus Cole | May 5, 2026

The French Open landscape shifted dramatically when Alcaraz confirmed his withdrawal. Sinner enters Paris chasing the one Grand Slam that has so far eluded him.

Roland Garros 2026 qualifying begins in 13 days. The main draw starts May 25. And the French Open — the clay-court Grand Slam at the heart of the tennis calendar — will unfold without its two-time defending men's champion, Carlos Alcaraz, who has officially withdrawn due to a right wrist injury.

Alcaraz's absence is the defining development of the pre-Roland Garros period. The Spaniard had already missed Barcelona and Madrid due to the wrist, and the decision to pull out of Paris confirms that what initially appeared to be a minor issue has become a significant injury. Alcaraz, 23, won Roland Garros in both 2024 and 2025, and his withdrawal removes the player who had been regarded as the only realistic challenger capable of beating Jannik Sinner on clay on his best day.

Sinner as the Overwhelming Favourite

Sinner enters Roland Garros as the clear No. 1 seed and the overwhelming betting favourite. He has won five consecutive ATP Masters 1000 titles — a record he set with his Madrid victory last Sunday — and his clay-court form entering Paris is as dominant as any player's in recent memory.

The French Open is the only clay-court Grand Slam, and it represents the one major title that Sinner has not yet won. His Grand Slam record includes two Australian Open titles (2024 and 2025) and the 2024 US Open — but Roland Garros has remained elusive. His opponents on clay this season have been unable to sustain enough quality over the full three sets required to beat him, and that pattern is expected to continue into Paris.

The draw will not be released until late May, but the field around Sinner is already clearer than it has been in years. Without Alcaraz, the second most dangerous challenger is likely to come from Alexander Zverev, the world No. 3 who reached the Roland Garros final in 2024.

Djokovic's Uncertain Path

Novak Djokovic represents the wild card in the Roland Garros field. He has confirmed his intention to compete and told Spanish television "at least for Roland Garros I'm ready," but his actual fitness level after a six-week absence following a right shoulder injury is still being determined through his Rome return. Djokovic would be pursuing his 25th Grand Slam title — and his fourth French Open crown — if he competes and advances deep into the Paris draw.

History argues against dismissing Djokovic at Roland Garros regardless of injury. He has won the tournament through adversity before, and his tactical intelligence on clay gives him an edge that pure power-hitters cannot replicate. Whether his shoulder holds up through a best-of-five-sets Grand Slam is the question that Rome will begin to answer.

WTA Landscape

On the women's side, Coco Gauff defends the title she won in 2025. The American comes into the clay swing as the world No. 3, having dropped one place in the WTA rankings following the Madrid Open, where she exited as the defending finalist. Aryna Sabalenka — the world No. 1 — enters Paris as the seeding favourite and arguably the deepest player in the women's field. Iga Swiatek, the four-time Roland Garros champion, is seeded No. 4 and will be the crowd's sentimental choice in Paris. Elena Rybakina is the second seed.

What's Next

Roland Garros qualifying begins May 18, and the main draw opens May 25. Both the men's and women's semifinals are scheduled for June 5-6, with the finals on June 7. Rome — which runs through May 17 — serves as the final significant preparation event for the world's top players before Paris. By the time the draws are released in the final week of May, the Rome results will have reshaped the form guide considerably. For now, the 2026 French Open looks like Sinner's tournament to lose.
