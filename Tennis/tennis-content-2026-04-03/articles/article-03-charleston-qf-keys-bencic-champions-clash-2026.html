Keys vs. Bencic: A Charleston Quarterfinal Between Former Champions With Very Different 2026 Stories

By Ryan Calloway | Tennis Fanrecap | April 3, 2026

Two former champions on one green-clay court. One semifinal spot. The Credit One Charleston Open's quarterfinal of the week delivered a matchup that justified every day of the tournament leading up to it.

Keys: Current Form, Championship Pedigree

Madison Keys arrived at the Charleston quarterfinals in the cleanest form of any player in the field. The 2025 Australian Open champion dispatched Anna Bondar 6-2, 7-5 in the round of 16 — a win defined by authority rather than drama. Keys plays aggressive, flat-strike tennis from the baseline, taking the ball early and redirecting pace. That style, which can sometimes struggle on slower surfaces, is actually well-suited to Charleston's green clay, where the low bounce keeps the ball at a height that favors her groundstroke mechanics.

Keys is a veteran of the WTA's major weeks who has long been considered one of the tour's most dangerous players when she hits peak form. Her 2025 Australian Open title validated that ceiling. At Charleston, she has been consistent, clear-minded, and technically precise. She entered the quarterfinal as one of the more credible title threats in the draw.

Bencic: A Story Running Deeper Than Her Seeding

Belinda Bencic's seeding at No. 3 is not the most important number in her Charleston story. The more resonant number is 2016 — the year she won this tournament for the first time, establishing herself as one of the players most naturally gifted for green clay. A decade later, she came back.

Bencic is the Paris 2024 Olympic gold medalist. She took time away from the tour to start a family, then returned to professional tennis — and kept winning. Her game has always suited green clay. The flat groundstrokes that she built her career around, the ability to absorb pace and redirect it, the comfort at the net: all of it translates well to the lower bounce and faster conditions of Charleston's unique surface.

This quarterfinal was not nostalgia dressed up as tennis. It was a legitimate contender making a real run at a tournament she has won before, on a surface that has always been kind to her.

Style Contrast on Green Clay

The technical contrast between Keys and Bencic was one of the things that made this quarterfinal interesting to watch. Keys plays power tennis — aggressive, linear, generating pace from her own racquet. Bencic plays change-of-pace tennis — absorbing power, redirecting angles, varying the rhythm of a rally.

On green clay specifically, the surface's lower bounce rewards the first style by keeping the ball in Keys' comfort zone. But it also rewards Bencic's style by shortening points and making unusual angles more accessible. Neither player had a clear surface advantage. That balance, with two championship-level games meeting in a quarterfinal, produced the kind of match that Charleston at its best reliably delivers.

What's Next

The winner of this quarterfinal advances to the Charleston semifinals, where defending champion Jessica Pegula is already waiting. Whoever comes through — Keys building on a strong 2026, or Bencic writing one of the sport's more compelling comeback chapters — will face the defending champion in a semifinal that carries real title implications.

Charleston's green clay does not make anything easy. The semifinals will be no different. But the quarterfinal between Keys and Bencic was a reminder of what makes this tournament one of the most genuinely compelling events on the WTA calendar.
