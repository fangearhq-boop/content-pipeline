Charleston QF Day: Pegula Into the Semifinal as Keys-Bencic Showdown Takes Center Stage

By Elena Voss | Tennis Fanrecap | April 3, 2026

Friday was quarterfinal day at the Credit One Charleston Open — and the green clay, as always, made everyone work for it.

Pegula Grinds Through Another Thriller

Jessica Pegula is in the semifinals. Again. That should be an uncomplicated sentence for the No. 1 seed and defending champion at Charleston, but nothing about her week has been uncomplicated. The Post and Courier described Friday's quarterfinal win over No. 7 seed Diana Shnaider as "yet another thriller" — a phrase that tells you everything you need to know about how Pegula has navigated this tournament.

Three matches in, three consecutive hard fights. That's not the path anyone would choose, but it is the path Charleston's green clay tends to produce. The surface — lower bounce, faster conditions, longer rallies — is unique on the WTA calendar. There is no other event like it. Ranking points and seedings matter less here than anywhere else on tour.

Shnaider reached the quarterfinal by eliminating No. 9 seed Leylah Fernandez 6-3, 6-0 in the round of 16 — a dominant result that signaled she was playing excellent tennis this week. Pegula had to deal with all of that and more. She kept going. She is into the semifinals and still on track to defend her 2025 Charleston title.

Keys vs. Bencic: The QF Matchup of the Week

The marquee quarterfinal on Friday brought Madison Keys against Belinda Bencic — two former champions, two very different stories, and one semifinal spot to split between them.

Keys entered the quarterfinal in commanding form. The 2025 Australian Open champion beat Anna Bondar 6-2, 7-5 in the round of 16 without the drama that has defined Pegula's week. Keys has never won at Charleston, but her game — flat, aggressive, take-the-ball-early tennis — is well-suited to the green clay. She was not here to take up space in the bracket. She was here to win.

Bencic brought something entirely different to the quarterfinal. The 2016 Charleston champion, Paris 2024 Olympic gold medalist, and post-maternity comeback story knows this tournament from the inside. She won here. She knows what the green clay rewards. Her flat groundstrokes and net instincts, calibrated over years of playing this specific surface, made her one of the more dangerous players in the draw regardless of her seeding position.

A QF between a current Grand Slam champion and a former Charleston champion — both in real form, both with a legitimate path to the final — is the kind of match that defines a tournament's character. Charleston 2026 is better for having had it.

Jovic-Kalinskaya: Youth Meets Experience

The other major QF storyline featured teenager Iva Jovic against No. 8 seed Anna Kalinskaya. Jovic, the No. 4 seed who has been the tournament's breakout story all week, reached the quarterfinals by defeating Sofia Kenin 7-5, 7-5 — a result that spoke to her ability to compete at the highest level under real pressure.

Kalinskaya is not a straightforward opponent for anyone. She advanced past Paula Badosa to reach the quarterfinal and is aggressive from the baseline in a way that creates problems. Jovic's run through Charleston has been built on her own aggressive baseline game — a style that fits the green clay's bounce well — but facing a player of Kalinskaya's level is a different kind of test.

The Charleston crowd has been invested in Jovic all week. The Post and Courier captured it last week: "Teen tennis star charms Charleston Open crowd." The quarterfinal against Kalinskaya was the week's most direct test of whether Jovic's breakout has real depth or is limited to softer early-round draws.

What's Next

The Credit One Charleston Open semifinals take place this weekend, with Pegula awaiting her opponent from the lower half of the draw. The winner of Keys-Bencic joins her in what promises to be a final worth watching regardless of who gets there.

Charleston's green clay has delivered its usual mix of survival stories, champion performances, and emerging-player moments in 2026. The semifinals — and eventual final — will determine who handles this singular surface best when it matters most. Pegula has been fighting for every point all week. If she lifts the trophy again, it will have been earned through the hardest possible route.
