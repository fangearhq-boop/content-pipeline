Majchrzak vs de Minaur: Libema Open Final Pits Cinderella Against Champion

By Elena Voss | June 14, 2026 | Tennis Fanrecap

Kamil Majchrzak arrived at the Libema Open in 's-Hertogenbosch ranked roughly No. 77 in the world and without a single ATP title to his name. By Sunday, he had eliminated both the world's No. 4 and the tournament's third seed. Now he stands one match away from history — and between him and that first ATP title stands Alex de Minaur, the defending 2024 champion.

The final is scheduled for 2:30 PM on Centre Court. It is the kind of storyline that draws casual fans in: one man chasing his first trophy against another who already knows what winning here feels like.

Majchrzak's Week of Upsets

The numbers tell a story that most tennis fans would struggle to believe if they hadn't watched it unfold. In the quarterfinal, Majchrzak defeated Felix Auger-Aliassime — the world No. 4 and top seed — 6-4, 6-3 in just 69 minutes. He won 85.1% of service points throughout that match. FAA, one of the most dangerous players on any surface, was never able to build momentum or find a break.

In the semifinal, Majchrzak produced an even more striking result. Against Daniil Medvedev, the No. 3 seed and former world No. 1, he won the opening set in a tiebreak 7-4 and then dominated the second set 6-1. That the Medvedev match finished in two sets was something few predicted — Medvedev is renowned for his grinding, three-set resilience. Majchrzak didn't give him the chance.

This is already the best week of Majchrzak's professional career by any measure. Entering the final, he has beaten two top-5 players in back-to-back days. Whatever happens Sunday, that is a permanent part of his biography.

de Minaur: The Defending Champion's Road

Alex de Minaur's path to Sunday's final was also not straightforward. The Australian sixth seed lost the first set to Adrian Mannarino in the semifinal before producing what one reporter described as 10 consecutive games won to close the match out. De Minaur's speed and relentless court coverage are his signature weapons, and on grass — where the surface can be unforgiving for players who like to grind from the baseline — he has shown a particular ability to turn defense into offense.

De Minaur won this tournament in 2024, and 's-Hertogenbosch has clearly become one of his favorite stops on the calendar. Arriving as a defending champion carries its own weight, and de Minaur is one of the most experienced players in the field this week.

What's at Stake

For Majchrzak, the stakes are straightforward: win and you claim the first ATP title of your career. At a stage of his career where many expected him to remain a quality top-100 player without ever reaching a final, that outcome would represent a definitive breakthrough.

For de Minaur, a second Libema Open title would reinforce his status as one of the best grass-court players in the world — and provide strong Wimbledon momentum. Wimbledon begins June 29, and de Minaur is annually among the names mentioned as a threat at SW19.

The world ranking implications also matter. A Majchrzak victory would catapult him up the rankings significantly. A de Minaur victory extends a grass-court record that already makes him one of the most dangerous players heading into the Championships.

What's Next

The Libema Open final takes place Sunday at 2:30 PM on Centre Court in 's-Hertogenbosch, Netherlands. Following the tournament, both players are expected to head to London ahead of Wimbledon. The Wimbledon draw takes place on June 26, with qualifying running June 22-25. The Championships open Monday, June 29.
