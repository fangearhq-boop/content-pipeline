Kitzbuhel Opens Week Two of Clay — Defending Champion Bublik Leads a Competitive Alpine Draw

By Elena Voss | Tennis Fanrecap

July 20, 2026 — Kitzbuhel, Austria

Week two of the post-Wimbledon clay swing began Monday with the Generali Open Kitzbuhel, the ATP 250 event held in the Austrian Alps that runs through Friday July 25. Defending champion Alexander Bublik returns as the No. 1 seed in a field that includes Valentin Vacherot, Arthur Rinderknech, Tomas Martin Etcheverry, Sebastian Baez, and Matteo Berrettini. The ATP 250 Estoril Open in Portugal also opens this week, giving the second week of clay two parallel events for players chasing ranking points before the North American hard-court season.

The Draw

Bublik is the defending champion and the natural focus of any Kitzbuhel preview, but his game — built around a fast serve, unconventional shot-making, and an ability to produce winners from anywhere — does not always translate to consistent clay results. He won here in 2025. Whether the same approach works when the draw is fully set against him for an entire week is a fair question.

The field around him is legitimate. Rinderknech (No. 3 seed) has been active on clay this season and reached the Gstaad semifinals last week before losing to Tsitsipas. Etcheverry (No. 4 seed) is an Argentine clay specialist who won titles on the surface in 2025. Vacherot (No. 2 seed) is a clay-comfortable French player. The top four all receive first-round byes, meaning the seeded bracket stays intact into the quarterfinals unless there's early trouble.

Berrettini's presence in the draw is worth noting. The Italian has been dealing with chronic hip issues that kept him out of Gstaad and Kitzbuhel last week. If he takes the court this week, it marks a return after an extended absence — and Berrettini on any surface when healthy is a threat to reach a final.

The Kitzbuhel Factor

Kitzbuhel has a history of producing results that the rankings do not predict. The altitude — the Kitzbuhel Tennis Club sits at roughly 760 meters — affects ball speed and bounce in ways that can benefit players with flatter ball-striking over those who rely on heavy topspin. Combined with a compressed draw that gives every player meaningful matches from round one, the event tends to favor players who can impose their game early rather than build into a tournament over two weeks.

The Umag and Gstaad finals last week were won by a first-time ATP champion (Merida) and a first-time finalist turned champion (Collignon). Kitzbuhel has a tradition of rewarding players outside the top 50 who arrive with a focused game plan for the conditions.

What's Next

The final at Kitzbuhel is scheduled for Friday July 25. The parallel Estoril Open (Portugal) also runs through July 25. Together, these two events conclude the second and final week of the post-Wimbledon European clay swing before the ATP Tour pivots to North American hardcourts.

For the players entered, every match win this week adds ranking points before the Montreal-Cincinnati-US Open stretch. For Kitzbuhel specifically, the defending champion is there, the draw is open, and the Austrian Alps tend to produce at least one finish that surprises the rankings.
