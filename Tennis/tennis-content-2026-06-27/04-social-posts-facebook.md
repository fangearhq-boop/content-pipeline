### STORY 1: Ethan Quinn Wins Mallorca — First ATP Title

#### Long-Form Post — 8:00 AM ET

Ethan Quinn just had the best week of his career — and he's only 22. 🎾

In the Mallorca Championships final on Saturday, Quinn dominated No. 2 seed Alejandro Davidovich Fokina 6-1, 6-2 to claim his first ATP Tour title. It wasn't just the result — it was the scoreline. Quinn had already dispatched Nuno Borges 6-1, 6-2 in the semifinal, a 56-minute demolition that set the tone for a week where nothing — and no one — slowed him down.

Three years ago, Quinn was winning the NCAA Singles Championship for the University of Georgia. He turned professional in June 2023 and spent the intervening years building his ranking from 239th. He arrived at Mallorca ranked No. 63, had never previously reached an ATP quarter-final, and left a champion. His new career high will be around No. 47.

He's also the third American to reach a Mallorca final (after Sam Querrey in 2021 and Christopher Eubanks in 2023) — and the first to win it.

Wimbledon starts Monday. Quinn heads in with something most players don't have: momentum.

What an introduction to the ATP Tour. 🎾

### STORY 2: Muchova Wins Bad Homburg — Osaka Retires in First Career Grass Final

#### Long-Form Post — 10:30 AM ET

Karolina Muchova is the Bad Homburg champion — but the story of Saturday's final belongs to Naomi Osaka. 🎾

Osaka reached the first WTA grass-court final of her career at Bad Homburg this week. That was, by itself, a milestone for a player who has often expressed complicated feelings about the grass-court season. She beat Ekaterina Alexandrova 6-2, 6-2 in the quarter-final and Wang Xinyu in the semi-final — building momentum that suggested she was finally finding her grass-court game.

Then, in the final, a right foot injury changed everything.

Osaka received a medical timeout for the foot issue in the second set. She'd already lost the first 6-1, and was trailing 1-0 in the second. After 49 minutes, she retired. Muchova, who was playing her first tournament at Bad Homburg, claimed the title — her third career WTA crown and second of 2026 (she also won in Doha earlier this year).

The result that matters most now isn't the scoreline — it's Osaka's fitness. Wimbledon begins Monday June 29. A foot injury that forced retirement in Saturday's final raises serious questions about how she prepares and how she starts the Championships.

For Muchova, it's a title and a confidence boost. For Osaka, the grass season is now about recovery.

### STORY 3: Wimbledon 13 Withdrawals — Who's Missing?

#### Long-Form Post — 12:30 PM ET

Wimbledon 2026 begins Monday — and the injury list is longer than anyone expected. 🎾

Thirteen players will not compete at the Championships, a combined men's and women's total that represents one of the most significant pre-tournament absence lists in years. On the men's side: Carlos Alcaraz (right wrist — hasn't played since the Barcelona Open in April), Lorenzo Musetti (thigh), Holger Rune (Achilles and knee — a cautious decision by his team), Sebastian Korda, Tomas Machac, Valentin Vacherot, and Reilly Opelka.

The women's side has its own headline: WTA No. 9 Victoria Mboko is out. The 19-year-old Canadian slipped on the grass at Queen's Club during her second-round match against Karolina Pliskova, injuring the MCL on her left knee and retiring from the match. She won't play Wimbledon — and the injury also ended her doubles partnership with Serena Williams. (The pair had won their Queen's Club doubles opener together.) Darja Vidmanova, the 2025 NCAA singles and doubles champion, takes Mboko's spot in the main draw. Also absent: Hailey Baptiste, Sonay Kartal, Markéta Vondroušová, and Veronika Kudermetova.

One note to look forward to: Wimbledon 2026 introduces video reviews for the first time in the tournament's history. The grass stays the same. The lines will now get called differently.

Championships start June 29. Finals July 11-12.

### STORY 4: Eastbourne Finals — Humbert vs Bergs, Keys vs Maria

#### Long-Form Post — 3:00 PM ET

The last Wimbledon warm-up concludes Saturday — and both the men's and women's finals at Eastbourne are compelling. 🎾

In the men's draw: Ugo Humbert takes on Zizou Bergs. Humbert reached the final by defeating British crowd favourite Jack Draper 7-5, 6-3 in the semi-final. Bergs earned his spot in more dramatic fashion — coming from a set down to defeat wildcard Toby Samuel 4-6, 7-6(5), 6-2, saving one of the best stories of Eastbourne week for the climax.

In the women's draw: Madison Keys faces Tatjana Maria. Keys is a former Eastbourne champion who has always loved this surface. Both she and Maria advanced to the final after their semi-final opponents retired — making their head-to-head clash the first match of the day that went the full distance.

The practical stakes: the winner adds a grass-court title going into Wimbledon, which begins Monday June 29. Both finalists in each draw walk away with ranking points and form regardless of result.

Eastbourne has been an underrated week of tennis — and it ends with four players who are all capable of doing damage in the second week of Wimbledon.

Who wins today's men's final — Humbert or Bergs?

### STORY 5: Wimbledon Draw — Fritz vs Draper, Andreeva's Historic Chance

#### Long-Form Post — 6:00 PM ET

Wimbledon 2026 starts Monday — and the draw, which was released Friday, has given us plenty to talk about before the first ball is struck. 🎾

In the men's half, the first round is unusually loaded. No. 6 seed Taylor Fritz faces British wildcard Jack Draper — a clash that felt almost poetic given that Draper had just lost his Eastbourne semi-final to Humbert hours before the draw was revealed. Fritz had skipped Eastbourne entirely to rest; Draper had just played a hard match. Now they open Wimbledon against each other.

Also in the first round: No. 11 Casper Ruud vs. Hubert Hurkacz — a match that would headline the quarterfinals in most years. Defending champion Jannik Sinner (No. 1) plays Miomir Kecmanovic. Novak Djokovic (No. 7) opens against Wu Yibing. The big structural story: Sinner and Djokovic are in the same top half, meaning the potential Wimbledon semi-final is Sinner vs. Djokovic.

In the women's half, the headline is Mirra Andreeva. The No. 5 seed and Roland Garros champion opens against Magda Linette — and if she can win Wimbledon, she would complete the Roland Garros/Wimbledon double. The last woman to do it was Justine Henin in 2006. That's the kind of historic company we're talking about. Andreeva is 18 years old.

Also worth watching: Serena Williams (wildcard) is in the same quarter of the draw as No. 3 seed Iga Swiatek (defending Wimbledon champion). A potential third-round meeting between the two would be one of the most watched tennis matches in years.

Wimbledon. Monday. It's almost here.
