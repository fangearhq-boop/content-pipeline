Sinner's Dream Dies at Roland Garros: World No. 1 Crumbles in Heat, Exits in Five Sets

By Ryan Calloway | Tennis Fanrecap | May 30, 2026

He was two sets up, 5-1 in the third, one game away from the round of 16. It was supposed to be the year Jannik Sinner finally completed his Career Grand Slam. Instead, the world No. 1 exited Roland Garros on Thursday in one of the most shocking collapses the tournament has ever seen.

No. 56 Juan Manuel Cerundolo defeated Sinner 3-6, 2-6, 7-5, 6-1, 6-1 in the second round, claiming 18 of the final 20 games after cramps reduced the Italian to a shadow of himself in the brutal Paris heat. The temperature peaked at 33 degrees Celsius (91.4 degrees Fahrenheit) on Thursday, and by the time Sinner served for the match at 5-1 in the third set, his body was already failing him.

The Moment Everything Changed

Courtside microphones captured Sinner saying he felt "dizzy and nauseous" as he began to falter. He required a medical timeout at 5-4, 0-40 in the third set — a decision that triggered immediate controversy among commentators and former players watching from the stands and television booth.

Jim Courier, calling the match for TNT, was direct in his criticism. "This is absolute baloney," he said on air. "Rules are being bent." The cramping rule in tennis has long been a grey area; players are not supposed to receive treatment for cramping during a match, as the condition is considered a natural physical response rather than an injury. But officials allowed the timeout to proceed.

Eighteen-time Grand Slam champion Chris Evert shared similar concerns, saying Sinner "shouldn't have been allowed to leave court." The debate over the timeout will likely continue long after Roland Garros concludes, given the stakes of the match and the profile of the players involved.

Cerundolo Seizes His Moment

Whatever the controversy surrounding the timeout, it did not help Sinner. He was unable to recover his physical condition, and Cerundolo — sensing the shift — played with increasing confidence and aggression. The Argentine won seven games in a row from 5-4 in the third to claim the set, then dominated the fourth and fifth sets entirely.

Cerundolo becomes the lowest-ranked player to defeat a world No. 1 at Roland Garros since 1998. The 26-year-old from Buenos Aires had never previously beaten a player ranked inside the top 20 at a Grand Slam, making the manner of this victory all the more extraordinary.

"I knew I had to stay patient," Cerundolo said afterward. "In five sets, anything can happen. I just kept fighting."

Career Grand Slam Quest Continues to Wait

For Sinner, the defeat carries a particular sting. He entered Roland Garros having won five consecutive Masters 1000 titles in 2026 — an unprecedented achievement in the Open Era. He had swept Indian Wells, Miami, Monte Carlo, Madrid, and Rome, and arrived in Paris as the overwhelming tournament favorite, with defending champion Carlos Alcaraz sidelined by a wrist injury.

Roland Garros is the only major Sinner has not won. A title here would have completed the Career Grand Slam — a set achieved by only six men in the Open Era. That quest now moves to 2027.

Sinner did not speak at length to media after the defeat, citing his physical condition. He indicated he would rest before deciding on his Wimbledon preparation.

What's Next

Cerundolo advances to the third round, where he will face an opponent from the bottom half of the top portion of the draw. For Sinner, the clay season is over. He will regroup, address whatever physical issues contributed to Thursday's collapse, and target the grass-court season beginning in June.

Thursday's result leaves the men's draw wide open. With both Sinner and Novak Djokovic now eliminated, the tournament favorite has shifted dramatically toward No. 2 seed Alexander Zverev — but with Joao Fonseca's stunning run continuing, anything appears possible in Paris this fortnight.
