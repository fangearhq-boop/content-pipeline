Open Era First: Roland Garros Men's R16 Has No Former Grand Slam Champions

By Marcus Cole | Tennis Fanrecap | May 30, 2026

In more than 50 years of Open Era tennis at Roland Garros, the men's round of 16 has always included at least one former Grand Slam champion. This year, for the first time, it will not.

The exits of No. 1 seed Jannik Sinner in the second round and three-time champion Novak Djokovic in the third have produced a draw that is unprecedented in modern tennis history. Only two of the original top eight seeds remain. The entire top half of the bracket — the portion that contained Sinner, Djokovic, and the clear path to the final — is now genuinely open.

How It Happened

Sinner, the world No. 1 and the tournament's overwhelming pre-tournament favorite, fell to No. 56 Juan Manuel Cerundolo in the second round on Thursday. The Italian led 2-0 sets and 5-1 in the third before cramps struck in the 33-degree heat, and Cerundolo claimed 18 of the final 20 games.

One day later, Djokovic's bid to win a record 25th Grand Slam title was ended by 19-year-old Joao Fonseca, who came back from 0-2 sets down in a 4-hour, 53-minute epic on Chatrier. Defending champion Carlos Alcaraz had already withdrawn before the tournament began with a wrist injury.

The result: three of the four men who have shared every major singles title since 2019 are now absent from the draw at the halfway point. The fourth, Alexander Zverev, is the last man standing among the favorites.

Zverev Steps Into the Vacuum

No. 2 seed Alexander Zverev made a statement on Saturday, defeating Quentin Halys 6-4, 6-3, 5-7, 6-2 to advance to the round of 16. The German reached the Roland Garros final in 2024 and is playing some of the most consistent clay-court tennis of his career.

With Sinner and Djokovic gone, Zverev has become the clear tournament favorite. Oddsmakers have installed him at plus-120 to win the title — but in a draw this unusual, those odds feel like they could shift dramatically with any result in the second week.

"I focus on my own game," Zverev said after his Saturday victory. "I know the draw has changed. But I have to win my matches, and that's what I intend to do."

Fonseca: The Story of the Tournament

If Zverev is the rational pick, Joao Fonseca is the emotional one. The 19-year-old Brazilian, who came back from two sets down in both rounds two and three, has captured the tournament's imagination in a way few players at this age have managed before.

Fonseca is not seeded and has never before made it this far at a Grand Slam. But after defeating Djokovic in a five-set thriller, it would be foolish to rule him out of contention. The clay suits his game, and his mental strength under pressure has been extraordinary.

What's Next

The round of 16 matches on the men's side are scheduled to begin on Sunday, May 31. Zverev is expected to be the bookmakers' favorite in his half of the draw. Fonseca, Cerundolo, and a group of unseeded players will compete in what is shaping up to be the most unpredictable Roland Garros men's draw in the Open Era.

Whoever wins the 2026 Roland Garros men's title will do so without having beaten either of the two biggest names in the sport — and that, perhaps more than anything else, will define their place in the history of this tournament.
