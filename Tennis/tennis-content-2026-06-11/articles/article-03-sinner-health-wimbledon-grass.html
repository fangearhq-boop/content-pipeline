Sinner Gets Hospital All-Clear and Will Skip All Grass Warm-Ups Before Wimbledon Title Defense

By Marcus Cole | Tennis Fanrecap | June 11, 2026

Jannik Sinner has been given a clean bill of health. The world No. 1 and defending Wimbledon champion spent approximately four hours at San Raffaele Hospital in Milan undergoing comprehensive cardiac testing — including a cardiac MRI, Holter monitor, and stress electrocardiogram — following his shocking second-round exit at Roland Garros.

The results showed no underlying pathology. Sinner was cleared. The bandage observed on his wrist in photos from outside the hospital was a standard post-arterial-blood-test wrap, not an injury.

The plan going forward: no Halle, no Queen's Club, no grass warm-up matches before Wimbledon. His team confirmed that returning to competition in time to be ready for the All England Club on June 29 is the sole objective.

What Happened at Roland Garros

On May 28, Sinner was leading Juan Manuel Cerundolo 6-3, 6-2, 5-1. He was two games away from the round of 16, the heavy favourite to win a Roland Garros title that would complete his career Grand Slam. Then his body stopped cooperating.

Severe cramps. Dizziness. Difficulty moving. The temperature at Roland Garros that day reached 33 degrees Celsius. Cerundolo won 18 of the final 20 games. The final score was 3-6, 2-6, 7-5, 6-1, 6-1 — one of the most dramatic collapses by a world No. 1 in Open Era history.

The medical question was immediate: was this heat-related and situational, or was something structural being missed? The hospital visits were the answer to that question. The cardiac tests provided the most comprehensive read possible on Sinner's cardiovascular health. The verdict was clean.

The Decision to Skip Grass Warm-Ups

This is the first time in Sinner's career at this ranking level that he will arrive at a Grand Slam without any warm-up tournament on the same surface. He has played Halle or Queen's Club in every prior Wimbledon preparation season. The decision to skip both events this year is deliberate and medical in origin.

The risk is real but manageable. Sinner's best surface is arguably grass — he won Wimbledon last year — but match sharpness on a new surface typically requires a week of competitive play before the timing fully clicks. Without warm-up matches, he will arrive at the All England Club relying entirely on practice sessions and institutional knowledge of his game on grass.

The counterargument: a rested, healthy Sinner at 80% is still a more dangerous Wimbledon player than most opponents at their peak. And a rested body is far better than a body pushed into competition before it is ready.

The Wimbledon Picture

Sinner returns to the All England Club as the defending champion. He won the 2025 title with a 4-6, 6-4, 6-4, 6-4 victory over Carlos Alcaraz — a player who subsequently withdrew from Roland Garros 2026 with a wrist injury and whose Wimbledon status remains uncertain.

In Sinner's absence from competitive tennis over the next 18 days, the grass swing will produce form players: winners and semi-finalists from Stuttgart, Queen's Club, Berlin, and 's-Hertogenbosch. Sinner's team will be watching. When he arrives in London, he will arrive with information but no recent match data of his own.

That is the trade-off his team has accepted. Health first. Wimbledon second.

What's Next

Sinner is reported to be returning to Monte Carlo for practice. His team has kept the specifics of his recovery protocol private, which is standard practice for a No. 1 player protecting competitive information. The key milestones between now and June 29: confirming his Wimbledon entry, travelling to London, and completing the practice sessions at the All England Club that precede the first round of the draw.

No news is likely to be good news between now and Wimbledon Monday. The only update that matters will come on the first day he walks onto a grass court in SW19.
