Sinner Eyes Career Grand Slam: What Stands Between the World No. 1 and Paris Glory

By Marcus Cole | Tennis Fanrecap | May 22, 2026

Jannik Sinner has won everything in tennis. The Australian Open. The US Open. Wimbledon. Five Masters 1000 titles in a row. The Career Golden Masters. The world No. 1 ranking for most of the past two years. There is one thing left: Roland Garros. That tournament starts Sunday, and Sinner is the story of it.

A title at the French Open would make him the seventh man in Open Era history to complete the Career Grand Slam — winning all four major titles at least once. The six before him include Rod Laver, Andre Agassi, Roger Federer, Rafael Nadal, Novak Djokovic, and Carlos Alcaraz. Alcaraz, the two-time defending champion, withdrew in April with a wrist injury. The field is open. Sinner is the favourite by a margin that makes some previews feel almost redundant.

The Path Opens on Sunday

Sinner's first-round opponent is French wild card Clément Tabur. The match is scheduled for Court Philippe-Chatrier, the main stage at Roland Garros, on Sunday May 24. Tabur is a home crowd favourite but a first-time Grand Slam participant at this level. For Sinner, it is the first step on what could be a two-week journey to history.

His draw, by most analysis, is the most favourable he has had at Roland Garros. The biggest clay-court threats are either absent or positioned in his draw in ways that require them to be at their best across multiple rounds just to reach Sinner. He avoids the most dangerous title threats until the later stages.

John McEnroe, previewing coverage for TNT Sports, assessed the field bluntly: "To me, ultimately, it's Sinner against the field, and I'd take Sinner right now."

The Streak Behind Him

Sinner arrives in Paris on a 29-match winning streak — one of the longest in the Open Era. That run began at Indian Wells in March and includes titles at Indian Wells, Miami, Monte Carlo, Madrid, and Rome. At Rome, he completed the Career Golden Masters, becoming only the second man in history to win every Masters 1000 event, after Djokovic.

His clay-court record this season: 6 matches played, 6 matches won. He won Monte Carlo without dropping a set and took Rome with a 6-4, 6-4 final over Casper Ruud. The version of Sinner who opens at Roland Garros on Sunday is not carrying uncertainty about his form on clay. He has answered those questions already.

His best result at Roland Garros is a quarterfinal in 2020. Paris, historically, has been his least successful major. That context makes this year's opportunity — with Alcaraz absent and the draw more open than it has been in years — all the more meaningful.

What Could Stop Him

Clay at Roland Garros over two weeks is its own argument. Five-set matches have reversed form lines before. Alexander Zverev, seeded No. 2, is one of the few players with the physical tools to challenge Sinner across a full match on this surface. Djokovic, seeded No. 3, has won here three times. Even in suboptimal form, he has the tactical archive to cause problems.

Sinner himself will know that the streak, the form, and the favourable draw do not guarantee anything inside the draw. What they guarantee is that he arrives as the right person, in the right moment, for the biggest opportunity of his career.

What's Next

Sinner opens against Clément Tabur on Court Philippe-Chatrier on Sunday, May 24. The men's singles final is scheduled for Sunday, June 7. Six wins stand between Jannik Sinner and the only Grand Slam title he does not yet own. The conversation about what he has built in 2026 will be defined by what happens on that clay over the next two weeks.
