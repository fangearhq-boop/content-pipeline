Roland Garros Women's Draw: Four Favorites, Zero Safe Picks

By Ryan Calloway | Tennis Fanrecap | May 22, 2026

The 2026 Roland Garros women's draw has been set since Thursday, and the more you study it, the more it resists a clear favourite. The four top seeds — Aryna Sabalenka, Elena Rybakina, Iga Swiatek, and Coco Gauff — are all capable of winning. All of them have significant reasons to be concerned. This is the deepest women's draw Roland Garros has produced in years, and the brackets have been structured in a way that guarantees at least two of those four names will be eliminated before the final.

Top Half: Sabalenka vs Gauff

Aryna Sabalenka, the world No. 1, and defending champion Coco Gauff share the top half of the draw. The projected semifinal matchup between them would be a rematch of the 2025 Roland Garros final, where Sabalenka fell short despite being the favourite heading in.

Sabalenka arrives in Paris having never won here. She is a two-time Australian Open champion and has been ranked No. 1 for most of the past year. But her recent results on clay have raised genuine questions. In Madrid, she lost to Hailey Baptiste, a player ranked outside the top 50. In Rome, she fell in the third round to Sorana Cirstea. When her serve is clicking and her groundstrokes are finding corners, she is the best player in the world. The challenge is that Roland Garros over two weeks gives opponents time to find her patterns.

Gauff, the No. 4 seed, opens against fellow American Taylor Townsend in an intriguing first-round match. As defending champion she knows every corner of the draw — and knows how to win here when her game is clicking. Her projected quarterfinal opponents, Anastasia Potapova or Ekaterina Alexandrova, are beatable. But a semifinal against Sabalenka, if both reach it, would be the match of the tournament in the women's draw.

Bottom Half: Swiatek's Mine Field, Rybakina's Breakthrough Bid

The bottom half is where Roland Garros's most intriguing narratives are running. Iga Swiatek, the three-time Roland Garros champion coming into 2026, enters as No. 3 seed. She won this title in 2020, 2022, 2023, and 2024. She has more clay Grand Slam titles than anyone currently in the draw. And she could face Jelena Ostapenko in the third round — a player who leads their head-to-head 6-0.

Swiatek is also working with a new coach. Francisco Roig, who guided Rafael Nadal for 17 years, joined her setup in April. The relationship is still developing. Swiatek won her matches in Madrid before losing to Elina Svitolina in the Rome semifinal. Her clay credentials are not in doubt. What is less clear is whether she is the dominant Roland Garros force she was in previous seasons.

Elena Rybakina, No. 2, arrives as the 2026 Australian Open champion. She is a player whose flat, powerful ball-striking can neutralise the clay surface's advantages for heavy-topspin opponents. She has never gone past the quarterfinals at Roland Garros. With Sabalenka and Gauff in the other half, this is arguably the clearest path Rybakina has had to a Roland Garros final.

The Dark Horse: Svitolina

Elina Svitolina enters as the No. 7 seed following her Rome WTA 1000 title, where she became the first player in tournament history to beat three top-4 opponents in one event — defeating Rybakina, Swiatek, and Gauff on consecutive days. She has 20 career titles and has never won a Grand Slam. Her clay form in 2026 is the best argument for an outsider winning Paris this year.

What's Next

Roland Garros 2026 women's first-round matches begin Sunday, May 24. The women's singles final is scheduled for Saturday, June 6. The title remains genuinely open among the top four seeds and a handful of dangerous contenders — which is exactly what makes this Roland Garros worth watching from the first ball.
