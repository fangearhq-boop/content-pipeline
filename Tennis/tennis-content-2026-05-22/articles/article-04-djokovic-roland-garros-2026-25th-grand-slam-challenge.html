Djokovic Chases 25th Slam With Just One Clay Match All Season

By Elena Voss | Tennis Fanrecap | May 22, 2026

The goal has not changed. Novak Djokovic wants Grand Slam No. 25. He is three Roland Garros titles deep on this clay, and the tournament that begins Sunday gives him another chance to extend beyond the 24 majors that already stand as the most in the history of the sport. What has changed is the path getting here. Djokovic enters the 2026 French Open having played exactly one clay-court match this year — a first-round loss to Giovanni Mpetshi Perricard at Rome in May.

That is the central tension of his Roland Garros story in 2026: the ambition is historic, and the preparation has been minimal.

A Season Interrupted

Djokovic's 2026 has been defined by his right shoulder injury. He reached the Australian Open final in January, which gave the year a promising start. But the shoulder problem forced him out of the BNP Paribas Open at Indian Wells in March, and he has not played a meaningful clay-court match since. Miami, Monte Carlo, Madrid, and Geneva — the traditional clay warm-up events before Roland Garros — came and went without Djokovic in the draw.

The Rome tournament in May was his return. Against Mpetshi Perricard, a powerful young French server who was once ranked inside the top 25, Djokovic lost in the first round. It was a concerning sign for a player whose edge has always come from tactical and physical precision accumulated over weeks of match play.

One Clay Match — The Same Opponent Again

The Roland Garros draw has produced an almost darkly symbolic first-round fixture: Djokovic opens against Mpetshi Perricard, the same player who beat him in his only clay match of the season. For Djokovic, the match is a chance for immediate redemption and a statement about his readiness. For Mpetshi Perricard, it is an opportunity to confirm that his Rome result was not an aberration.

Djokovic said this week that he would be ready. He has been practicing in Paris and has reportedly reshaped his coaching setup ahead of the tournament. He is a three-time Roland Garros champion — 2016, 2021, and 2023 — and knows this court better than almost anyone alive. Experience at this scale does not disappear because of a difficult clay warm-up.

The Draw and the Math

Djokovic is the No. 3 seed and is placed in the opposite half of the draw from Jannik Sinner, the world No. 1. This means a Djokovic-Sinner final is possible if both players get through seven rounds of matches. The path between Djokovic and the final, however, includes "tricky tests" even in the absence of the top seeds. His quarter of the draw has players capable of extending him across five sets on clay.

The scenario where Djokovic wins Roland Garros 2026 is not impossible. It requires him to find match sharpness inside the tournament rather than arriving with it — a form of on-the-job preparation that even the best players in the sport rarely manage at Grand Slam level. It requires six wins from a field that includes multiple players peaking on clay.

It requires, in short, another chapter of the Djokovic career that has already defied more conventional wisdom than any player in history.

What's Next

Djokovic opens against Giovanni Mpetshi Perricard at Roland Garros on Sunday, May 24. The men's final is June 7. A title would be his 25th Grand Slam — two ahead of Sinner, three ahead of Alcaraz, and further than any man in history. Paris has given him three titles. He is in Paris to take another.
