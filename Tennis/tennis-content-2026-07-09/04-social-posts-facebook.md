### STORY 1: Fery Into Wimbledon Semifinals — Long-Form

Arthur Fery walked onto Centre Court on Wednesday ranked No. 114 in the world with six career tour-level wins to his name. He walked off having beaten the No. 9 seed in straight sets — 6-4, 7-6(4), 6-0 — and booked a place in the Wimbledon semifinals.

He is 23 years old. He had never made it past the second round in his three previous Wimbledon appearances. He is a British wildcard. And he is now the second men's wildcard in the Open Era to reach the Wimbledon semifinals.

The first was Goran Ivanisevic, in 2001. Ivanisevic won the title.

Fery's run has captivated British tennis in a way that doesn't happen often. He is the fifth British man to reach the Wimbledon last four in the Open Era, joining Roger Taylor, Tim Henman, Andy Murray, and Cameron Norrie. He is also the lowest-ranked semifinalist at Wimbledon since Ivanisevic himself — who was ranked No. 125 that year.

He faces Alexander Zverev on Friday. The Roland Garros champion, the No. 2 seed, the man who just ended seven straight losses to Taylor Fritz.

Does the Ivanisevic parallel hold? That question is why Friday feels like something special.

What do you think — does Fery have a shot against Zverev?

### STORY 2: Zverev Defeats Fritz — Long-Form

Going into Wednesday, Alexander Zverev had lost seven consecutive matches to Taylor Fritz. He had been 5-10 against the American all-time. The streak started at Rome in 2024 and had grown into something that felt almost impossible to end.

In one hour and 59 minutes, he ended it.

Zverev defeated Fritz 6-4, 6-4, 6-2 on Day 10 to reach his first-ever Wimbledon semifinal. He is 29. He has been to the semifinals of the Australian Open, the French Open, and the US Open — now Wimbledon completes the set. He is only the fifth active player to have reached the final four at all four Grand Slams.

There is also this: Zverev won Roland Garros earlier this year. If he wins Wimbledon, he becomes the first man since Rafael Nadal in 2008 to claim both titles in the same calendar year.

Fritz received a medical timeout for a right knee issue in the second set and said the knee started bothering him three games into the match. It does not diminish what Zverev did — the dominance in his game on Wednesday was real, regardless of the circumstances.

His opponent Friday is Arthur Fery. World No. 114. A British wildcard. The man who just hit a 6-0 third set against No. 9 Cobolli.

The Paris-London double is on the table. Who is going to stop him?

### STORY 3: Women's Day 10 — Long-Form

Wednesday's women's quarterfinals at Wimbledon were efficient and commanding. No drama needed — just clean tennis from two players who have built their games for this surface.

Marta Kostyuk defeated Jasmine Paolini 6-3, 6-2 in 69 minutes. Paolini was the 2024 Wimbledon finalist. Kostyuk broke her early in both sets and controlled the match from start to finish. Straight sets in just over an hour at Wimbledon, against a previous finalist, to reach her first semifinal here.

Linda Noskova beat Elise Mertens 6-3, 7-5. She won 79% of her first-serve points. She hit 30 winners. She looked like someone who had figured out exactly what she was doing on grass this fortnight.

Here is the result: Wimbledon 2026 is now guaranteed to crown a first-time women's champion. Gauff, Muchova, Kostyuk, Noskova — none of them has won Wimbledon before. All four are in the last four. The Venus Rosewater Dish is going to someone new.

For the 10th straight year.

### STORY 4: Women's SF Preview — Long-Form

Thursday at Wimbledon is for the women. Both semifinals are on Centre Court, and both are worth watching from the first ball.

First up: Coco Gauff against Karolina Muchova. Gauff, 22, is the only player in this semifinal group who has won a Grand Slam before — she claimed the 2023 US Open and the 2025 French Open. Muchova, 28, has the experience of a previous Wimbledon quarterfinal and won Bad Homburg just 10 days ago. They know each other's game. This will be a contest.

Second: Marta Kostyuk against Linda Noskova. These two have never played each other at a Grand Slam. Kostyuk is 22, Noskova is 21 — combined age of 43. Both reached their first Wimbledon quarterfinals this year. Both won on Wednesday without breaking a sweat. Now they play each other.

No matter what happens on Thursday, Wimbledon 2026 will have a first-time women's champion. This is the 10th consecutive year that has been true — and it is worth pausing to recognize how extraordinary that run has become.

### STORY 5: Men's SF Preview — Long-Form

Friday brings both men's semifinal matches at Wimbledon, and the contrast between the four players on court could not be more striking.

On Centre Court: Arthur Fery (British wildcard, No. 114 in the world, six career tour-level wins coming in) against Alexander Zverev (No. 2 seed, Roland Garros champion, just ended a 0-7 losing streak to reach his first Wimbledon last four).

On Court 1: Jannik Sinner (world No. 1, defending champion, has not dropped a set all tournament) against Novak Djokovic (No. 7, 39 years old, played a 5-hour-15-minute quarterfinal just 48 hours earlier while managing an ankle and calf injury).

Think about those matchups for a moment. A wildcard nobody picked to get past the first week against a man who just won Roland Garros. The youngest, most efficient defending champion at a Grand Slam right now against a 39-year-old legend who almost certainly knows this could be his last meaningful run.

The only precedent for a wildcard winning Wimbledon was Ivanisevic in 2001, also ranked in the 120s.

These are the matches Friday has waiting. Which one are you watching first?
