Arthur Fery Becomes Second Wildcard in Open Era History to Reach Wimbledon Semifinals

By Marcus Cole | Tennis Fanrecap | July 9, 2026

Wimbledon, London

Six career tour-level wins. World No. 114. A wildcard who had never advanced past the second round in three previous Wimbledon appearances. And on Wednesday, Arthur Fery walked off Centre Court having beaten No. 9 seed Flavio Cobolli 6-4, 7-6(4), 6-0 to reach the Wimbledon men's singles semifinals.

He is the second men's wildcard in the history of the Open Era to reach a Wimbledon semifinal. The first was Goran Ivanisevic, in 2001, ranked No. 125 at the time of the draw. Ivanisevic won the title that year. The parallel is there, unmissable, whether Fery wants it or not.

"Can't believe it," Fery said after the match, laughing. "Honestly, can't believe it."

The Match That Sealed It

The first two sets were tight — a break in the first, a tiebreak in the second that Fery took 7-4. Then came the third, and with it one of the most emphatic displays of the fortnight. Fery won it 6-0, dominant on serve, aggressive at the net, ending the match in a way that left little doubt about who the better player was on this day.

Cobolli is a legitimate top-10 player who reached the quarterfinal by defeating No. 5 seed Alex de Minaur in straight sets two days earlier. He arrived on Centre Court as the seeded player for a reason. He did not look like one when the third set began.

Fery is 23 years old and has been handed the kind of Wimbledon draw that only comes around once. He played it correctly every time.

Fifth British Man in the Open Era

Before Fery, four British men had reached the Wimbledon singles semifinals in the Open Era: Roger Taylor, Tim Henman — who reached the last four four times — Andy Murray, who went on to win the title twice, and Cameron Norrie, who made the 2022 semifinal. Fery joins that list at 23, ranked No. 114, without the infrastructure of a established ATP career behind him.

He is also the lowest-ranked men's Wimbledon semifinalist since Ivanisevic, who was No. 125 in 2001. The two situations are different in detail — Ivanisevic had been ranked No. 2 in the world at his peak, while Fery has not yet built that résumé — but the bracket position is similar, and the challenge ahead is the same.

Fery's father, Loic Fery, is a hedge fund manager and president of French football club FC Lorient. The family wealth has been part of the story told about Arthur since he entered professional tennis — the contrast between his privileged background and the grassroots British tennis system that produced Murray. But what Fery is doing on court this fortnight is not about background. It is about tennis.

What's Next

Fery faces Alexander Zverev on Friday in the Wimbledon semifinal on Centre Court. Zverev is the No. 2 seed, the Roland Garros champion, and a player who just ended a seven-match losing streak against Taylor Fritz in comprehensive fashion. He is one of the best players in the world, and a very good grass-court player.

The precedent says it is possible. In 2001, the wildcard beat everyone put in front of him. Fery has beaten everyone put in front of him this fortnight. Friday, the draw gets as hard as it gets. But getting here was already historic.
