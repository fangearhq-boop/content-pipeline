### Long-Form Post The Fery-tale had to end somewhere. On Friday at Wimbledon, it ended against the formidable Alexander Zverev — 7-6(0), 6-2, 6-4. But let's be honest: the story of Arthur Fery isn't about the loss. It's about everything that happened before it. Arthur Fery arrived at Wimbledon as a 23-year-old British wild card ranked No. 114 in the world. Two weeks later, he departs as world No. 36 — a career high — and the new British No. 1, displacing Cam Norrie. The prize money from this run? Approximately $1.2 million. His entire career earnings before Wimbledon? $884,000. In two weeks, he more than doubled everything he'd earned in his professional career. He also did something only one other man in the Open Era has done: reach the Wimbledon men's semifinals as a wild card. The first was Goran Ivanisevic in 2001. Ivanisevic won the title that year. As for Zverev — he's into his first Wimbledon final. He arrived as the reigning Roland Garros champion. A win on Sunday would complete the Channel Slam (winning Roland Garros and Wimbledon in the same year) — a feat achieved only by Rod Laver, Björn Borg, Roger Federer, Rafael Nadal, and Novak Djokovic. Zverev has a chance to write himself into that company. The Fery story is extraordinary. But Zverev's moment is arriving too. ### Long-Form Post Something genuinely historic is happening at Wimbledon today, and it involves a small city in the Czech Republic called Přerov. Population: 43,000. Current Grand Slam finalists produced: two. Karolina Muchova, 29, and Linda Noskova, 21, are both from Přerov — and today they face each other in the Wimbledon women's final on Centre Court. This is the first all-Czech women's Grand Slam final in history. It's also the first women's Grand Slam final between two players of the same nationality since Venus and Serena Williams met at Wimbledon in 2009. There is no precedent for what they're playing for. The title is guaranteed to go to a first-time Wimbledon champion. Muchova is 29 and in her second Grand Slam final (she reached the 2023 French Open final). She's the oldest first-time Wimbledon finalist since Nathalie Tauziat in 1998. She got here by saving a match point in the tiebreak against Coco Gauff in a semifinal that had the entire tennis world on edge. Noskova is 21 and playing in her first Grand Slam final. She dismantled Marta Kostyuk 6-4, 6-4 in just 79 minutes — barely breaking a sweat in the semifinal. Czech women have won Wimbledon in 2023 (Marketa Vondrousova) and 2024 (Barbora Krejcikova). If either Muchova or Noskova wins today, that's three of the last four Wimbledon women's titles going to the Czech Republic. Match begins not before 4:00 PM BST / 11:00 AM ET on Centre Court, after the men's doubles final. Who are you rooting for today? Muchova or Noskova? 🇨🇿🎾 ### Image Post Caption Both from the same Czech city of 43,000. Both in the Wimbledon final. History happening today. 🇨🇿🎾 #Wimbledon
