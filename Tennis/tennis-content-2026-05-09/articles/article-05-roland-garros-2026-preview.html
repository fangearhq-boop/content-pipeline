Roland Garros 2026: With Alcaraz Out and Djokovic Doubtful, Sinner's Path to Paris Has Never Been Clearer

By Ryan Calloway | Tennis Fanrecap

May 9, 2026

Roland Garros qualifying begins May 18, the main draw May 24. In 15 days, Paris will ask the questions that Rome has already begun to answer — and the answers increasingly point in one direction.

The Italian Open is the last Masters 1000 event before Roland Garros. By the time Foro Italico's red clay has played its final point, every player still standing in Paris will have a story — and those who have exited will carry their own.

After Friday's stunning result in Rome, the Roland Garros picture has sharpened considerably. Novak Djokovic — a three-time Roland Garros champion and one of the few players capable of challenging Jannik Sinner on clay — lost to a 20-year-old qualifier while visibly hampered by physical problems. His fitness ahead of Roland Garros is now a genuine question rather than a formality. Carlos Alcaraz, the reigning two-time Roland Garros finalist and defending Wimbledon champion, confirmed weeks ago that a wrist injury rules him out of Paris entirely.

Into this landscape walks Sinner, with his 30-2 record, five consecutive Masters 1000 titles, and a career yet to include a Roland Garros trophy — the one Grand Slam that would complete his Career Grand Slam.

The ATP Men's Draw: Sinner vs. Everyone Else

Without Alcaraz and with Djokovic's participation in serious doubt, the ATP draw at Roland Garros shapes up as the clearest path Sinner has ever had to a Paris title. Alexander Zverev, who reached the Roland Garros final in 2024, is the strongest remaining challenger. Daniil Medvedev, Casper Ruud, and Holger Rune represent additional threats, but none has the recent form or consistency to match Sinner across five sets on clay.

Sinner has never won Roland Garros. His best result at the event has been a semifinal appearance. But the players who have historically denied him in Paris — Alcaraz, Djokovic, Nadal — are either absent or aging out of their peak clay-court ability. If there was ever a year for the world No. 1 to complete the Career Grand Slam on clay, the evidence strongly suggests it is this one.

The WTA Women's Draw: Swiatek's Return, Sabalenka's Pursuit

The women's draw carries its own narrative weight. Iga Swiatek, the four-time Roland Garros champion who was stopped in the semifinals last year, arrives at Paris hungry for redemption. She demonstrated at Rome — where she defeated Madison McNally after returning from a viral illness in Madrid — that her clay-court game remains elite when she is fully healthy.

Aryna Sabalenka, the current world No. 1, has not won Roland Garros despite reaching the final. It remains the conspicuous gap in her resume, and her dominant form in 2026 — including a pair of straight-sets victories at the Italian Open — suggests she is approaching peak clay-season readiness.

Coco Gauff, who won Roland Garros in 2023, returns as a former champion with a point to prove after a clay season disrupted by illness. Mirra Andreeva, at just 19, is increasingly being discussed not as a breakout candidate but as a genuine contender. And Elena Rybakina, targeting what would be a rare Australian Open and Roland Garros double, brings one of the most dangerous serves in women's tennis to a surface where even clay cannot fully neutralize pace.

The Timeline

Qualifying for Roland Garros begins on May 18. The main draw commences on Sunday, May 24, with the tournament concluding June 7. Players who fall early in Rome — including Djokovic, who must now decide whether to compete in Paris at all — will have more than a week to rest, recover, and prepare.

For those still competing in Rome, the Italian Open final week doubles as the most important Roland Garros preparation available. Every match played in these conditions, on this surface, with this kind of pressure, is direct preparation for what Paris demands.
