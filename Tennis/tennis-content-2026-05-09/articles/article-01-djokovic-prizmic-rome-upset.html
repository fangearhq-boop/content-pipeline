Djokovic Falls to Prizmic in Rome — First Opening-Round Loss in 18 Appearances

By Marcus Cole | Tennis Fanrecap

May 9, 2026

A physically hampered Novak Djokovic suffered the most unexpected result of the Italian Open so far, losing 2-6, 6-2, 6-4 to 20-year-old qualifier Dino Prizmic — the first opening-round defeat of his Rome career.

For 17 previous Rome appearances, Novak Djokovic had never lost in the opening round. The six-time Italian Open champion had arrived at Foro Italico with a record that bordered on mythological. But on Friday evening, that record ended in the most visible of fashions.

Dino Prizmic, a 20-year-old Croatian who had to earn his place in the main draw through qualifying, defeated Djokovic 2-6, 6-2, 6-4 in 2 hours and 17 minutes — delivering the biggest win of his career and the most jarring result of the 2026 clay season so far.

The match began in Djokovic's favor. He swept through the first set with the kind of ruthless efficiency that has defined his career on clay, conceding little and giving Prizmic nothing to work with. But the balance shifted dramatically in the second set, when the physical reality of Djokovic's situation became apparent.

The 38-year-old Serbian had not played a tour-level match in 57 days, returning from a right shoulder injury that had kept him out since Indian Wells in March. The rust, and something more concerning, showed. He was bending down and placing his hands on his knees between points. He trudged slowly to his chair at changeovers. His movement — usually one of the most complete in the history of the sport — was labored and imprecise.

Before the match, Djokovic had acknowledged his concern: "I'm not completely happy with my physical condition." He battled on regardless, and when pressed afterward, offered a quote that carried the weight of the night: "I'm pleased at least that I fought 'til the end."

Prizmic Seizes His Moment

For Prizmic, the significance of the result cannot be overstated. The Croatian, ranked well outside the top 50, had clawed his way into the draw through qualifying. Now he had beaten a 24-time Grand Slam champion — and done it in three sets on one of the sport's most storied clay courts.

Prizmic played with relentless aggression, closing the net on short balls and unleashing powerful groundstrokes that had Djokovic scrambling in the second and third sets. He broke Djokovic's serve three times across the match and served with a confidence that belied his age and ranking.

The 20-year-old advances to the round of 32, where he will face another significant test. But Rome 2026 will always mark a turning point in his career — the day he took down a living legend.

The Roland Garros Question

Djokovic's performance in Rome was not merely a match result — it was a referendum on his fitness ahead of Roland Garros, which begins its main draw on May 24, just 15 days away.

The Serb had structured his entire clay-swing plan around arriving at Paris in peak condition. Rome was meant to be the confidence-builder, the chance to shake off the rust from his shoulder injury and rediscover his feel for clay. Instead, he leaves the Italian capital with more questions than answers.

With Carlos Alcaraz already confirmed out of Roland Garros due to a wrist injury, Djokovic represented the last credible threat to a Jannik Sinner coronation in Paris. The picture now is stark: the Italian No. 1, who has compiled a 30-2 record in 2026 and won five consecutive Masters 1000 titles, enters Roland Garros as the undisputed favorite.

For Djokovic, who is chasing a 25th Grand Slam title at age 38, the coming days of rest and treatment will be critical. His team will need to make a difficult calculation: risk further physical distress by playing in Paris, or protect his long-term health. The answer is unlikely to become clear until much closer to the Paris start date.

What's Next

Novak Djokovic will return to his team and assess his fitness ahead of Roland Garros 2026. A decision on his Paris participation is expected within the coming days. Meanwhile, Dino Prizmic continues his Italian Open campaign into the round of 32, where another test awaits the young Croatian who just made history.

For the Italian Open itself, Djokovic's exit means the men's half of the draw opens up significantly. Jannik Sinner makes his Rome debut tonight against Sebastian Ofner — and the home crowd will be watching with renewed expectation.
