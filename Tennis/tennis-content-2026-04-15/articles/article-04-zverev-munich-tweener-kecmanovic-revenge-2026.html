Zverev Opens Munich Defense with Tweener Winner, Defeats Kecmanovic in Three Sets

By Elena Voss | Tennis Fanrecap

April 15, 2026

Alexander Zverev opened his BMW Open title defense in Munich the only way the moment demanded: with a between-the-legs tweener winner in the third-set tiebreak that sent the crowd into a frenzy.

The German No. 1 seed defeated Serbian Miomir Kecmanovic 6-3, 3-6, 7-6(2) in a match that was never as comfortable as the score ultimately suggests. After dominating the opener, Zverev unravelled in the second set, allowing Kecmanovic to level. But in the tiebreak — the moment of maximum pressure — Zverev rose to it, winning 7-2 and sealing the match with the kind of shot that reminds you why he remains one of the sport's most complete players.

The Tweener Moment

Between-the-legs shots are usually the preserve of desperate defense, players scrambling to retrieve shots hit behind them at full stretch. Zverev's tweener in the Munich tiebreak was something different — executed with confidence and precision at a critical juncture, eliciting a roar from the home crowd that appreciated both the audacity and the skill.

The ATP Tour's official write-up specifically called out the tweener as a highlight moment of the match, and the shot will feature prominently in any Munich highlights package.

A Revenge Story

The "revenge" element is real. Zverev has a score to settle with Kecmanovic from recent months, and the headline from ATP Tour noted as much — calling the win a "revenge" result. Playing in front of home fans in Munich, Zverev produced the kind of performance that proves he is motivated for the clay season after a mixed start.

Zverev is the defending Munich champion after winning the 2025 BMW Open title in a final over Ben Shelton. The German is deeply familiar with the Munich clay, the MTTC Iphitos courts, and what it takes to win here.

Diallo Awaits in R16

Gabriel Diallo is next for Zverev in the R16. The Canadian, who stands over two meters tall and has a serve that rivals any player on tour, is capable of putting pressure on the world's best on any surface. Zverev will need to maintain sharper consistency across three sets if needed.

But based on Tuesday's performance, Zverev has the tools. The tweener was just the exclamation point on a win that says: I am here to defend this title.

What's Next

Zverev's path at Munich runs through Diallo in the R16 and then, potentially, Joao Fonseca or Arthur Rinderknech in the quarterfinals. On the other side of the draw, defending finalist Ben Shelton remains standing. A Zverev-Shelton final rematch of 2025 is a very real possibility.

The BMW Open final is set for Sunday, April 19. In Munich, on clay he knows well, Zverev will have very few better chances to add to his Masters title collection in 2026.
