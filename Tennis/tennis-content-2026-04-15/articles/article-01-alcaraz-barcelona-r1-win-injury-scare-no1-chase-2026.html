Alcaraz Opens Barcelona with Win and Wrist Scare, Eyes Path Back to No. 1

By Elena Voss | Tennis Fanrecap

April 15, 2026

Carlos Alcaraz has no time to feel sorry for himself. Less than 48 hours after losing the Monte Carlo Masters final to Jannik Sinner — the match that temporarily cost him the world No. 1 ranking — the Spaniard returned to his favorite hunting ground in Barcelona and reminded everyone why he is still one of the two best players on the planet.

Alcaraz defeated Finnish qualifier Otto Virtanen 6-4, 6-2 in 85 minutes on Wednesday, booking his place in the last 16 of the Barcelona Open Banc Sabadell. The victory was not without concern: Alcaraz received a medical timeout for a wrist issue between sets, setting off alarm bells in the Spanish crowd. But he emerged for the second set and was dominant, describing the sensation afterward as a "sharp pain" that subsided quickly.

"It was a sharp pain, but it went away," Alcaraz said. "I felt good after that."

A Patient First Set, A Clinical Second

The match unfolded carefully in the opener. With the score tied 4-4, Virtanen held two points to potentially take the lead, but Alcaraz answered by stepping further inside the baseline, extending the rallies and forcing the qualifier into errors. The decisive break arrived, and Alcaraz closed out the set 6-4.

After the medical break, Alcaraz reset and played the second set with authority. He never faced a break point after the restart, winning 6-2 to complete a composed opening win.

Virtanen, ranked just inside the top 100 and playing on a wildcard entry, gave a solid account of himself but was ultimately outclassed once Alcaraz found his range. The 21-year-old Spaniard extended his career record at Barcelona to 15-3.

The No. 1 Calculation

Everything in Barcelona this week runs through one question: Can Alcaraz win the title and return to No. 1?

The short answer is yes — but only if he wins the whole thing. Alcaraz currently sits at approximately 13,240 ranking points, trailing Sinner by around 110 to 160 points. Crucially, he is defending only 330 points from his 2025 Barcelona final appearance. If he claims the title this week, he would earn 500 points and shed the 330 defensive load, climbing to approximately 13,410 — enough to leapfrog Sinner, who is not competing this week.

The math is clean. The execution is the hard part.

Machac Waits in the R16

Standing between Alcaraz and the quarterfinals is Czech No. 8 seed Tomas Machac, who put together one of the best performances of the early rounds by coming back from a first-set bagel to defeat Sebastian Báez 2-6, 6-4, 6-1.

Machac is not an easy opponent on clay. His flat, heavy groundstrokes can neutralize the spin-heavy game that dominates the red dirt, and he has the footwork and endurance to stay in extended exchanges. Alcaraz leads their head-to-head, but Machac knows how to produce results in this kind of environment.

Their R16 clash is expected to take place Wednesday or Thursday.

What's Next

Alcaraz's projected path at Barcelona runs through Machac in the R16, then potentially Andrey Rublev in the quarterfinals and Alex de Minaur or Arthur Fils in the semifinal. Second seed Lorenzo Musetti is on the opposite side of the draw, and the pair could meet in the final.

Sinner, meanwhile, takes the week off after his Monte Carlo triumph. The Italian knows that even if Alcaraz wins Barcelona, his own form this season — three Masters 1000 titles in three attempts — gives him every reason for confidence heading into the clay swing.

But for now, the Barcelona clay belongs to Alcaraz. It is where he has always felt most at home. And this week, with the No. 1 ranking on the line, he has every reason to be at his very best.
