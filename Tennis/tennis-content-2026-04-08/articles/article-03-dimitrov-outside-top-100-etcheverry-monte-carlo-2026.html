Grigor Dimitrov Falls Outside ATP Top 100 for First Time in 14 Years After Monte Carlo Loss

By Ryan Calloway | April 8, 2026

Tomás Etcheverry's 6-4, 2-6, 6-3 win at the Rolex Monte-Carlo Masters ended 14 years of unbroken ATP Top 100 residency for the former world No. 3.

Some milestones are celebrated. Some arrive quietly, carrying the weight of what came before them.

Grigor Dimitrov's loss to Tomás Etcheverry at the Rolex Monte-Carlo Masters on Tuesday was the second kind. The Bulgarian, 35, fell 6-4, 2-6, 6-3 in the first round — and the result dropped him outside the ATP Top 100 for the first time in 14 years.

Fourteen years of unbroken residency inside tennis' elite tier. Gone in an afternoon on the Monaco clay.

The Career That Promised a Grand Slam

To understand what this number means, you have to go back to who Grigor Dimitrov was at his best.

He turned professional in 2008 and climbed steadily through the rankings with a game built around an elegant single-handed backhand and aggressive net approaches that drew immediate comparisons to Roger Federer. The nickname "Baby Federer" was attached early and proved difficult to shake — a compliment that, over time, also became a kind of weight.

At his peak in 2017, Dimitrov reached world No. 3. That same year, he won the ATP World Tour Finals, defeating David Goffin in a memorable final in London. Three Grand Slam semifinals and countless victories over top-10 opponents confirmed that he belonged at the highest level. A Grand Slam title seemed not only possible but likely.

The years since have been harder. Shoulder injuries interrupted his late 20s. The relentless emergence of new generations — first Medvedev and Zverev, then Alcaraz and Sinner — compressed the space at the top of the rankings. Dimitrov remained a top-50 player for most of that stretch, flashing his best form in occasional weeks, but unable to string together the sustained results of his peak.

Etcheverry's Performance

Tomás Etcheverry deserves credit for what he produced. The Argentine, a committed clay-court performer, won a three-set match against a former top-3 player with composure and conviction. The 6-4 first set, a 2-6 second in which Dimitrov found better solutions, and a decisive 6-3 third — Etcheverry earned every game of it.

The win advances Etcheverry deeper into the Monte Carlo draw and gives him valuable ranking points in a clay swing that suits his game. But the story the tennis world will remember from this match is the number on Dimitrov's side of the rankings table.

What's Next

Dimitrov's focus will turn to rebuilding points and form on the clay circuit. At 35, there remains the possibility of a late-career resurgence — professional tennis is full of them — but the immediate challenge is straightforward: accumulate wins and get back inside the top 100. Etcheverry, meanwhile, continues his Monte Carlo campaign and looks to build on a result that confirms his standing on the clay.
