### STORY 1: Berrettini def. Medvedev 6-0, 6-0

**Long-Form Post (8:00 AM ET)**

When was the last time you watched a top-10 player lose 6-0, 6-0? At a Masters 1000?

That is exactly what happened at Monte Carlo on Wednesday when Matteo Berrettini — entered as a wildcard — dismantled world No. 10 Daniil Medvedev in 49 minutes.

Medvedev won 17 points. Total. Across two sets. He smashed his racket. He had no answers.

Berrettini, for his part, was almost disbelieving of his own performance. "I think it was one of the best performances of my life," he said afterward. "I think I missed three shots in the entire match and it is not easy against a tricky player like Daniil."

Monte Carlo's official site called it the first time a top-10 player had been double-bagled at tour level in a decade.

This is more than a scoreline. Berrettini was once a legitimate top-5 player and a Wimbledon finalist who lost years of his career to hand and heart surgery. To produce THIS against THIS opponent — in just 49 minutes — is one of the more extraordinary individual performances we will see all clay season.

Who is going to stop him this week?

### STORY 2: Alcaraz def. Báez 6-1, 6-3

**Long-Form Post (10:30 AM ET)**

Carlos Alcaraz walked onto the Monte Carlo clay Tuesday looking every bit the defending champion he is.

The world No. 1 dispatched Sebastián Báez 6-1, 6-3 in 70 minutes — clean, dominant, and exactly the start he needed in a week where the stakes could not be higher.

Here is the No. 1 ranking situation in real-time numbers: Alcaraz arrives at Monte Carlo defending 1,000 points from his 2025 title. Jannik Sinner, who begins this week ranked No. 2, has zero points to defend. When they entered, the gap between them was roughly 190 ranking points — close enough that a strong Sinner run and an early Alcaraz exit could flip the rankings.

Alcaraz is doing his part. He is through to the third round.

Sinner, who demolished Humbert 6-3, 6-0 in R2, also awaits ahead. The two most dominant players in the world are on course to meet in the final.

Defending a title while chasing down a rival who has everything to gain is the best kind of pressure. So far, the champion is handling it.

### STORY 3: Dimitrov falls outside top 100

**Long-Form Post (12:30 PM ET)**

Some milestones arrive quietly. This one stings.

Grigor Dimitrov lost to Tomás Etcheverry at Monte Carlo on Tuesday — 6-4, 2-6, 6-3 — and the result sent him outside the ATP Top 100 for the first time in 14 years. Fourteen years of unbroken residency inside tennis' elite tier, gone.

For a player once labeled the "Baby Federer," whose game seemed built for Grand Slam runs, this is a hard landing. Dimitrov reached world No. 3. He won the 2014 World Tour Finals. He made three Grand Slam semifinals and developed one of the most beautiful backhands in the sport.

The years since have not been kind. A serious shoulder injury, fitness struggles, and the relentless climb of a new generation of players have slowly pushed him down the rankings. And now, for the first time since 2012, he sits outside the top 100.

Etcheverry, to his credit, earned this win — the Argentine competed hard and deserved three sets over a former top-3 player. But the story at Monte Carlo on Tuesday was the number next to Dimitrov's name when the rankings updated.

Tennis moves fast. Careers are fragile. Today reminded us of both.

### STORY 4: Bergs def. Rublev

**Long-Form Post (3:00 PM ET)**

Zizou Bergs had never played in the Monte Carlo main draw before this week.

He has a win there now.

The Belgian, 26, knocked out No. 15 seed Andrey Rublev 6-4, 6-1 on Wednesday to advance to the third round in his very first appearance at this level. In professional sports, that is about as good a debut as you can have.

Bergs has been building steadily on the tour over the past couple of seasons — solid serve, reliable from the back, no obvious weakness. Monte Carlo's clay suits his game, and he showed that fully against a Rublev who has been inconsistent in recent weeks.

The clay season is still young. This win puts Bergs in the last 16 at a Masters 1000 — and makes him a name to watch as the Riviera clay swing continues.

### STORY 5: ATP Rankings

**Long-Form Post (12:30 PM ET)**

The clay openers have reshuffled the ATP rankings, and the movement tells a clear story about who enters the rest of the season with momentum.

Ben Shelton is America's top-ranked player again. The big-serving 22-year-old climbed back to world No. 8 this week, reclaiming the American No. 1 title above Taylor Fritz. It has been a quietly excellent start to Shelton's clay campaign and his ranking reflects it.

Tommy Paul now sits at No. 18 after winning his first career clay title at Houston last weekend. Paul saved three championship points when he trailed 3-5 in the final set — and then ran out the match 6-1, 3-6, 7-5 over qualifier Román Burruchaga. His first title on clay makes him a champion on all three surfaces.

Rafael Jodar, 19, jumped 32 places to a career-high No. 57 after winning the Marrakech ATP 250 — becoming the youngest champion in that tournament's history in the process. His rise over the past 12 months is one of the better stories in men's tennis.

And then there is Marco Trungelliti. The Argentine is 36 years old and just broke into the ATP Top 100 at a career-high No. 76 — becoming the oldest man to do so in the Open Era. He reached the Marrakech final from qualifying and played every match at his absolute best. Some people win their best tennis at 36.

The rankings are moving. The clay season is just getting started.
