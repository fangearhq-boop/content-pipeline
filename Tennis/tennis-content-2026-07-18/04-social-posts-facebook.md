### STORY 1: Merida Wins Umag

**Long-Form Post (8:00 AM ET)**

A 22-year-old Spaniard walked onto a Croatian clay court on Saturday morning with a chance to win his first ATP Tour title. By the time he walked off, Daniel Merida had done exactly that.

Merida defeated Damir Dzumhur 6-4, 6-1 in the Croatia Open Umag final — and the numbers around the result are remarkable. It was just his 7th career ATP tour-level event. He is only the third-youngest finalist in Umag this decade, trailing only Carlos Alcaraz and Jannik Sinner on that list.

That is extraordinary company.

Dzumhur, 34 and ranked No. 108, had made one of the most emotionally charged runs of the week. The Bosnian veteran had survived a 3-hour-42-minute quarterfinal on Thursday, threatened to walk off court over a disputed line call, then stayed to win. He reached his first Umag semifinal in four attempts. He reached his first ATP Tour final. He had earned the biggest match of his season.

Saturday, facing Merida, the week's momentum ran out.

For Merida, it is just beginning. His name is in the Umag record books alongside Alcaraz and Sinner. What he does next will be worth watching.

### STORY 2: Bastad Final Preview

**Long-Form Post (10:30 AM ET)**

The Bastad final is set. Sunday. Andrey Rublev vs. Adolfo Daniel Vallejo.

Rublev, the No. 1 seed and the 2023 Bastad champion, defeated Alejandro Tabilo in Saturday's semifinal and enters the final with an 11-2 record in ATP clay-court semifinals. He has done this before. He knows how finals feel on clay.

Vallejo has never done this before. That is precisely what makes Sunday remarkable.

The Paraguayan, ranked outside the top 100, defeated Luciano Darderi in the other semifinal to become the first player from his country to reach an ATP Tour final since Ramon Delgado in approximately 2002. He is 35-11 on clay so far in 2026. He is not in the final by accident.

On one side of the net: the No. 1 seed, the defending champion, the experience and the ranking. On the other: a player from a tennis-small nation rewriting his country's history with every match.

Sunday's final in Bastad starts at 2 PM local time. Something will be decided.

### STORY 3: Gstaad Final

**Long-Form Post (12:30 PM ET)**

The chaos at Gstaad found its natural conclusion on Saturday.

After a week that eliminated defending champion Alexander Bublik, two-time champion Casper Ruud, and seed after seed in a cascade of upsets, the Swiss Open semifinals produced two more stories worth telling.

Juan Manuel Cerundolo of Argentina defeated Raphael Collignon 3-6, 6-3, 7-5, surviving a tricky first set to advance in three. Cerundolo had become the top seed remaining in the draw after Friday's carnage, and he held up his end.

The bigger result was Alexander Shevchenko.

The Russian defeated No. 3 seed Stefanos Tsitsipas 7-6(5), 6-2 — a dominant, composed performance that sends him into his first final at a major clay event. Shevchenko won the tiebreak, then closed out in straight sets. Tsitsipas's week at Gstaad ends in the semifinals.

Sunday: Cerundolo vs. Shevchenko. Two players nobody predicted would be in the Gstaad final when the week began. The Swiss clay this summer has consistently refused to follow the script.

### STORY 4: WTA Athens Final Preview

**Long-Form Post (3:00 PM ET)**

Barbora Krejcikova is in the final of the Athens Open.

The Czech No. 1 seed defeated Clara Tauson in Saturday's semifinal, continuing a strong week that included her first-ever head-to-head win over Zheng Qinwen in the quarterfinals. Krejcikova is a former Grand Slam champion. She arrived in Athens with form. She is leaving with a spot in Sunday's final.

Her opponent depends on the other semifinal.

Maria Sakkari — the Greek No. 4 seed playing in front of her own home crowd — faces Alina Korneeva Saturday evening. Sakkari has been dominant all week: she has dropped very few games. The Athens crowd has been electric behind her. If she advances, Sunday's final is a dream matchup for the city: a beloved Greek champion against one of the world's best, on the court where WTA tennis returned after a 35-year absence.

The first WTA event in Athens since 1991 is delivering a weekend worth watching.

### STORY 5: Dzumhur's Emotional Umag Week

**Long-Form Post (6:00 PM ET)**

This time last week, Damir Dzumhur was not expected to be anywhere near a tennis final.

The 34-year-old from Bosnia and Herzegovina, ranked No. 108, has been grinding the ATP tour for years — consistent enough to keep his ranking, never quite reaching the biggest matches. Umag this week was different.

Thursday's quarterfinal set the tone. Dzumhur beat fourth seed Matteo Arnaldi in 3 hours and 42 minutes — a match that included a threatened mid-match walkout over an electronic line-call dispute and a third-set tiebreak that went right to the edge. He stayed. He won. "This is why we love the sport," he said afterward.

Then came his first Umag semifinal in four trips to the tournament. Then came his first ATP Tour final.

Saturday, facing Daniel Merida, the result was 6-4, 6-1. The final scoreline came quickly, and it was not kind to Dzumhur.

But the scoreline is not the week. The week was a 34-year-old who has refused to stop competing reaching the biggest match of his 2026 season. The week was the walkout he didn't take. The week was why, after all these years, he is still on tour.
