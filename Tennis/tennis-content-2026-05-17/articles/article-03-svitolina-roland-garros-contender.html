From Rome to Paris: Why Svitolina Is the Player to Watch at Roland Garros

By Ryan Calloway | Tennis Fanrecap

May 17, 2026

The last WTA 1000 event before Roland Garros has wrapped up, and the player walking away from Rome with the trophy is Elina Svitolina. More than the title, it is the manner of the victory — and the quality of opposition she dispatched along the way — that makes her the most compelling name heading into the French Open.

What She Did at Rome

Svitolina defeated the world's top players in four consecutive match days. No. 2 Elena Rybakina fell in the quarterfinals. No. 4 Iga Swiatek was eliminated in a three-set semifinal that Svitolina won despite being outpointed early. No. 3 Coco Gauff — the defending Roland Garros champion — was beaten in Saturday's final 6-4, 6-7(3), 6-2.

That is a combined ranking of Nos. 2, 3, and 4 beaten in less than a week. On clay. On the same court where Roland Garros clay specialists come to sharpen their games for Paris. The performance raises an uncomfortable question for every other player in the draw: if she can do that here, what can she do at the French Open?

Her clay final record is now 8-0. She has never lost a clay court final. Not once. That number — 8-0 — is not a streak or an accident. It is a pattern that says something fundamental about how Svitolina performs when the pressure is highest on the red dirt.

The Seeding That Follows Her to Paris

Svitolina's Rome title lifts her to the No. 7 seed at Roland Garros, a jump of three positions from her pre-tournament ranking of No. 10 in the world. She is the only top-10 woman to have won an outdoor clay title in the 2026 season — a distinction that means something when you're assessing who actually knows how to perform in these conditions.

As the No. 7 seed, she will avoid the top four in her quarter of the draw until the quarterfinals at the earliest. That gives her a relatively clear path to the second week — where, based on this week's form, she would be a serious threat to any opponent.

The Title She Doesn't Have

For all her achievements on clay, Svitolina has never won Roland Garros. Her best result at the French Open is a semifinal. That context matters because it means the Grand Slam title remains the missing piece — and momentum from a performance like this week's in Rome can carry players further than pre-tournament seedings and rankings might suggest.

When asked whether she could win Roland Garros this year, Svitolina did not deflect the question. She has earned the right to take it seriously.

"Definitely was an extremely tough battle," she said after Saturday's final. "I'm very happy with the way I could handle my nerves, not only today but also throughout this tournament. I think the two weeks, it's been tough, but I'm very happy with the way I've been playing, the way my body been reacting on these tough matches."

What's Next

Roland Garros qualifying begins Monday, May 18. The main draw ceremony is Thursday, May 21. The main draw starts May 25. Svitolina arrives in Paris as the tournament's No. 7 seed, the last WTA 1000 champion, and the player in the best form on the women's clay circuit. The conversation about who can win the French Open this year now runs through her.
