Ruud's Rome: Five Finals, the Longest Wait in the City

By Marcus Cole | Tennis Fanrecap

May 17, 2026

Casper Ruud has walked onto the court for a Rome Masters final five times. He has not won one. That number — five finals, zero titles at the same event — places him in a category all his own among modern tennis players who have reached the pinnacle of a tournament and been unable to take the last step.

On Sunday, he faces the task of changing that narrative against the most forbidding possible opponent.

The Accumulation of Near-Misses

Ruud's four previous Rome finals came in 2020, 2021, 2022, and 2024. Each time, he arrived as a legitimate contender. Each time, the title went to someone else. The pattern is not a product of bad form or undeserved appearances — Ruud has proven consistently that he belongs in the biggest matches in tennis. The final step has simply not been there.

The story extends beyond Rome. Ruud has reached three Grand Slam finals without winning a major: Roland Garros 2022, the US Open 2022, and Wimbledon 2023. Seven career major and Masters appearances in finals. Zero titles at the biggest level.

These numbers have the potential to define a career in the way that missed penalties define footballers — the moments that stay with a player long after the victories are forgotten. Or they can serve as motivation. Ruud has chosen, year after year, to return to these courts and try again.

This Time: Sinner

Sunday's opponent is Jannik Sinner — world No. 1, on a 33-match Masters 1000 winning streak, with a 5-0 head-to-head record against Ruud in which Sinner has lost a combined total of very few games. Sinner is chasing the Career Golden Masters, the Italian crowd is behind him, and the narrative of the match has been written before a single ball is struck.

"Try not to think about the big wave," Ruud said when asked about facing Sinner. It is a phrase that reveals a player who understands the mountain in front of him and has chosen a practical, focused approach rather than pretending the challenge is manageable.

Ruud earned his final berth by defeating Italy's Luciano Darderi 6-1, 6-1 in the semifinals — a performance that was arguably his best of the tournament. He dropped only two games against a player who had beaten Alexander Zverev and created one of the week's best stories. The form is there. The intent is clear.

What a Win Would Mean

If Ruud defeats Sinner today, it would rank among the most significant upsets in recent Masters 1000 history. It would end one of tennis's most persistent final-round droughts at a single event. And it would deliver a first Masters 1000 title that his record of appearances suggests he has always deserved.

The fact that the odds, the rankings, the head-to-head, and the streak all point against him is, in some way, exactly the situation Ruud has spent his career navigating. He has never been the prohibitive favourite in these finals. He has always shown up anyway.

What's Next

After Rome, both finalists head to Roland Garros, where qualifying begins Monday and the main draw starts May 25. For Ruud, Roland Garros holds its own set of complicated memories — he was the runner-up there in 2022, beaten in the final on the same red clay that has brought him so close at Rome. He will arrive in Paris with whatever outcome Sunday brings and prepare for another two weeks on the surface where his game has always been most competitive.
