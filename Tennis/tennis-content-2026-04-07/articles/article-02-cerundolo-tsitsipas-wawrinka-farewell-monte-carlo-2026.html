Cerundolo Ends Tsitsipas's Perfect Monte Carlo Record; Wawrinka Says Farewell to the Tournament He Won

By Elena Voss | Tennis Fanrecap | April 7, 2026

Two storylines ran through the early rounds at Monte Carlo in 2026, one about a career declining faster than expected, the other about a career that has been one of the more decorated of its era arriving at its final chapter. Both resolved on the same day.

Cerundolo Breaks the Streak

Stefanos Tsitsipas had won every opening match he had played at Monte Carlo across eight career appearances. That streak ended on Monday when Francisco Cerundolo, the 16th seed, defeated him 7-5, 6-4 in a result that went the distance but was settled with authority in the closing stages of each set.

Cerundolo, who has become one of the more reliable clay-court performers on tour, picked up his 15th win of the 2026 season in the process. For him, the win extends a run of form that has been building through the opening weeks of the year. For Tsitsipas, the loss lands at a particularly difficult moment. He is now projected to fall outside the ATP Top 60 — a position that would have seemed almost unimaginable for a player who was at No. 3 in the world just two years ago and who reached the Roland Garros final in 2021 and 2023.

The decline has been sharp and, at times, difficult to watch for a player whose on-court personality brought such energy to the game. Cerundolo's win was not a fluke. It was the consequence of a player who met the surface and the moment with more clarity than his opponent.

Cerundolo now faces Jannik Sinner in the round of 16, having just beaten the 16th seed in a straight-set first-round win. The Argentine 16th seed will step onto Court Rainier III next with both momentum and a result to validate. He will need all of it against the world No. 2.

Wawrinka's Last Match in Monaco

Stan Wawrinka lost to Sebastian Baez 7-5, 7-5 on Monday in a first-round match that was the third-seeded Argentine's route into a meeting with Carlos Alcaraz but was also something else entirely for the Swiss veteran.

Wawrinka, who turned 41 in March, is in his final year on the ATP Tour. He announced in advance of the 2026 season that he would retire at year's end — a plan he has said he intends to follow without building any formal farewell tour around it. He will play. He will compete as long and as well as he can. And when the season ends, it ends.

Monte Carlo, though, has particular resonance for Wawrinka. He won his only ATP Masters 1000 title here in 2014, defeating his friend and compatriot Roger Federer in the final. It was the result that signaled Wawrinka's arrival among the game's elite, and it preceded the run of Grand Slam titles — the Australian Open in 2014, Roland Garros in 2015, the US Open in 2016 — that define his legacy.

Losing to Baez on Monday was not the departure he might have hoped for. Post-match, he was straightforward about the difficulty of what he is doing and why he is still doing it.

"Of course these days and weeks are really, really difficult, but in the end it's worth it," Wawrinka said. "I know it's my last year trying to do the best I can. Hopefully I can win a few matches this year to enjoy that feeling of winning."

He is not asking for farewell ceremonies. He is asking for a few more wins. That is the kind of player Wawrinka has always been.

What's Next

Francisco Cerundolo faces Jannik Sinner in the Monte Carlo round of 16. Tsitsipas will fall in the ATP rankings following his first-round exit. Wawrinka's 2026 season continues at other tournaments as he finishes his final year on tour.
