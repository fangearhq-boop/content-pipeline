Tommy Paul Saves Three Championship Points to Win Houston — His First ATP Clay Title

By Ryan Calloway | Tennis Fanrecap | April 7, 2026

It very nearly went the other way.

Tommy Paul trailed Roman Andres Burruchaga 3-5 in the third set of the Fayez Sarofim & Co. U.S. Men's Clay Court Championship final in Houston on Sunday, looking at three championship points that would have handed the title to a qualifier who had run an extraordinary week to reach his first ATP final. Paul saved all three. He then won four straight games to close out a 6-1, 3-6, 7-5 victory in 2 hours and 42 minutes — and with it, the first clay title of his career.

The Comeback That Defined the Week

The first set had belonged entirely to Paul, a clinical 6-1 that suggested the outcome was settled. Burruchaga's response in the second set told a different story. The Argentine qualifier won it 6-3, leveled the match, and by the time he held a 5-3 lead in the deciding set with championship points in hand, the tournament had turned fully on its head.

What Paul did next is the reason this title means something beyond the result. He is one of the more mentally composed players on the American circuit, but the situation tested that composure directly. Three times Burruchaga was a point from winning. Three times Paul survived. He then found clean tennis when he needed it most, winning through to 7-5 with the kind of directness that suggested the crisis had actually steadied him rather than shaken him.

Across the week, Paul saved 9 of 12 break points he faced in the tournament — a number that reflects how often he was tested and how often he found the answer. The Houston title was not a comfortable week. It was a hard-fought one, which makes the result more appropriate.

A Gap in the Trophy Cabinet Filled

This is Paul's fifth ATP Tour title. He had previously won on hard courts and had a grass-court victory to his name. Clay was the surface where his results had been good enough to suggest a title was coming without it ever quite arriving. Houston delivers it.

The timing could not be better. Paul now enters the European clay swing — Madrid, Rome, Roland Garros — with recent title form on the surface, which is the kind of confidence injection that changes how a player approaches a tournament draw. Knowing you can win on clay is different from knowing you might be able to.

Paul knows he can win on clay. He has the evidence now.

Burruchaga's Remarkable Run

The result should not overshadow what Burruchaga achieved this week. He arrived in Houston as a qualifier — a player who had to win his way into the main draw — and reached his first ATP singles final. He pushed a top player to the edge of a title in the deciding set. That is a week that will register meaningfully on his ranking and more meaningfully on his belief going forward.

In the doubles, Ben Shelton and Andres Andrade won the Houston title.

What's Next

Tommy Paul carries his first clay title into the European swing, with the Madrid Open, Rome Masters, and Roland Garros ahead on the schedule. He enters that stretch with form, confidence, and a result that removes any doubt about his capability on the surface.
