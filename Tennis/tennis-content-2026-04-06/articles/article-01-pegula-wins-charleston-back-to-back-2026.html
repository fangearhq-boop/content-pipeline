Pegula Wins Charleston: First Back-to-Back Champion Since Serena Williams in 2013

By Elena Voss | Tennis Fanrecap | April 6, 2026

Jessica Pegula did not allow a dramatic final. She did not need one.

The top seed defeated qualifier Yuliia Starodubtseva 6-2, 6-2 on Sunday to win the Credit One Charleston Open, claiming her 11th career WTA singles title and becoming the first back-to-back champion at this tournament since Serena Williams defended the title in 2013. The match lasted 82 minutes. Starodubtseva had almost no answers.

A Dominant Performance

Starodubtseva had been one of the stories of the week — a qualifier ranked No. 89 who entered on a late withdrawal spot and worked her way to a first career WTA final, including a clinical dismantling of fifth-seeded Madison Keys 6-1, 6-4 in the semifinals. The expectation was that her momentum, combined with her aggressive ball-striking off both wings, might at least create complications for Pegula.

It did not happen. Pegula controlled the match from the opening service game, broke Starodubtseva early in both sets, and produced the kind of baseline consistency that makes her so difficult to beat when she is fully switched on. She dropped just eight games across the entire final.

There was no dramatic momentum swing, no moment where the qualifier's week-long confidence asserted itself. Pegula was simply better on the day, and by a significant margin.

The Serena Comparison

The last player to win back-to-back Charleston titles was Serena Williams, who won in 2012 and 2013. That context underlines how difficult defending on this surface tends to be. Charleston's green clay plays differently from the red clay of Europe, and the draw changes significantly year to year.

Pegula won the 2025 edition and returned this week as the top seed. She absorbed pressure in earlier rounds — she needed a third set to get past Iva Jovic in the semifinals — but found her best tennis when the occasion demanded it.

This is her second title of 2026. She won Dubai in February. Two titles before mid-April is a strong return for a player who has sometimes been described more as a consistent presence than a finisher.

Starodubtseva's Week

A Charleston final, reached from a late-entry qualifying spot at ranking No. 89, is not a failure by any measure. Starodubtseva will leave South Carolina with a career-high ranking and 280 WTA points from a runner-up finish — results that will move her well inside the top 70.

The Keys win, in particular, demonstrated real quality. Dominating a top-10 server in straight sets, converting break points cleanly, not blinking under the occasion — those are signs of a player whose ranking does not reflect her ceiling. She will be taken more seriously from here.

But in the final, Pegula's experience and precision were the difference. Finals are a different match-type, and Starodubtseva's first one brought a learning experience alongside the points.

What's Next

Pegula takes Charleston momentum into the European clay swing. Stuttgart and Madrid are the next major stops on the WTA calendar before Roland Garros in late May. As the No. 5 in the world, she enters the clay season with form behind her and the back-to-back Charleston title as a confidence marker. For Starodubtseva, the job now is converting this week's breakthrough into consistent results that push her further into the top tier of WTA competition.
