Jodar Wins Marrakech at 19: Youngest Champion in Tournament History Claims First ATP Title

By Marcus Cole | Tennis Fanrecap | April 6, 2026

One year ago, Rafael Jodar was outside the top 900 in the world. On Sunday in Morocco, he won his first ATP Tour title.

The 19-year-old Spaniard defeated Marco Trungelliti of Argentina 6-3, 6-2 in 69 minutes in the Grand Prix Hassan II final in Marrakech, claiming the youngest-ever championship in the tournament's history. It was the resolution of a final that had attracted attention for reasons beyond tennis alone — Jodar is 19, Trungelliti is 36, a 17-year age gap that ranked as the fifth-largest between finalists in ATP Tour history since 1990.

How the Final Unfolded

The scoreline was decisive but the trajectory of the week told a more complex story. Jodar entered as one of the younger players in the draw with limited top-level experience. Trungelliti had already made Open Era history simply by reaching the final at age 36 — surpassing Victor Estrella Burgos, who reached his debut ATP final at age 34 in 2015.

In the match itself, there was no extended narrative of momentum shifting. Jodar played with a controlled aggression that belied his age, moving efficiently and holding his nerve through key moments in the first set. Once he established the 6-3 margin, the second set followed a similar pattern. Trungelliti, despite the occasion, could not find the consistency he needed to extend the match into a third set.

The Rise of Jodar

The trajectory is worth sitting with for a moment. Outside the top 900 twelve months ago. An ATP Tour champion at 19. The rise required consistent work through the Challenger circuit and a willingness to compete in the kinds of draws where young players either develop quickly or stall.

Jodar's title in Marrakech is not a fluke result built on a soft draw. He earned it in a final against a player who had already shocked expectations by getting there at 36. That opponent — Trungelliti — had himself been the human-interest story of the week until the final day.

The ranking climb from this title will push Jodar roughly 32 spots higher into new career-high territory. More importantly, it gives him something to build from. First ATP titles often arrive in clusters of confidence; the players who win early tend to win again.

Trungelliti's Record Stands

Trungelliti leaves Morocco without the title, but not without recognition. His place in the Open Era record book — oldest player to reach a debut ATP final — is permanent. That record does not belong only to players who win. It belongs to the ones who arrived, which he did, at age 36 in a draw full of younger, more highly ranked opponents.

For the Argentine, the question now is whether this final serves as an opening chapter or a career highlight. At 36, the window is narrow. But reaching the final of an ATP event at an age when most players have either retired or settled into elder-statesman roles is itself a kind of answer about what he is capable of.

What's Next

Jodar returns to the clay circuit as a first-time champion. His schedule through the spring clay season will draw more attention than it would have seven days ago. He enters the conversations around the next generation of Spanish clay-court players, a lineage with obvious historical weight. For Trungelliti, the focus shifts to whether the form that brought him to a final can carry him further into the clay season. Both players write their next chapters on the red clay of Europe.
