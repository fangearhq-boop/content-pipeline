Roland Garros 14 Days Away — Injury Chaos Reshapes the Favorites on Both Sides

By Marcus Cole | Tennis Fanrecap

May 10, 2026

The French Open main draw begins May 24 with the pre-tournament picture more uncertain than at any point in recent memory — Alcaraz is out, Djokovic is doubtful, and now Sabalenka is nursing a back injury after her Rome exit.

Two weeks from today, the French Open main draw begins at Roland Garros in Paris. On the men's side, the tournament has never looked more predictable. On the women's side, it has rarely looked more wide open. Both statements, as of May 10, are true at the same time — and together they tell the story of a clay season that has delivered upheaval at every turn.

The cascade of withdrawals and doubts began weeks ago. Carlos Alcaraz, the defending men's champion, confirmed his absence due to a wrist injury that has kept him off the clay circuit for the entire spring. Without the Spaniard, Jannik Sinner's path to a first Roland Garros title — and the completion of the Career Grand Slam — became dramatically clearer.

Then came Novak Djokovic's first-round exit in Rome, a loss to qualifier Dino Prizmic that exposed the physical limitations of his return from a shoulder injury. Djokovic remains on the Roland Garros entry list, but his fitness is genuinely unclear. He is chasing a 25th Grand Slam title, a number that would stand alone in the history of the sport. The pressure is immense. The body may not cooperate.

Sabalenka's Injury Changes the Women's Picture

On the women's side, Saturday brought news that tilted the balance further. Aryna Sabalenka, the world No. 1, left the Italian Open in the third round with a lower back and hip injury, having lost 2-6, 6-3, 7-5 to Sorana Cirstea. Sabalenka called for a medical timeout in the final set and afterward spoke openly about the pain limiting her ability to rotate fully through her groundstrokes.

She said she hopes to recover in time for Paris. But Roland Garros begins in 14 days, and the nature of a back injury — and its timeline — is inherently uncertain. If Sabalenka cannot find full fitness, the women's field is robbed of its most dominant player at the event she has never won.

That leaves an extraordinary number of players with a legitimate claim to the women's title. Iga Swiatek is a four-time Roland Garros champion, the most successful women's player on this surface in the modern era, and arrives in form. Coco Gauff is the defending champion. Elena Rybakina won the Stuttgart title earlier this spring and is dangerous on any surface. Mirra Andreeva has been arguably the most consistent player on clay since February.

Sinner Stands Alone

On the men's side, the picture is stark in its clarity. Jannik Sinner enters Roland Garros as the overwhelming favorite — the only player in the field who has demonstrated, match after match and tournament after tournament, the level required to win on clay in Paris.

He has compiled a 31-2 record in 2026. He has won five consecutive Masters 1000 titles, an unprecedented run in the 36-year history of the series. His M1000 winning streak stands at 29 matches, tying Roger Federer for third all-time. And he is the only player to have reached the semifinals at all nine Masters events on tour.

He has never won Roland Garros. That is the one major title that has eluded him. The completion of the Career Grand Slam — winning all four Slams in a career — would follow from a Paris title, making the significance of this fortnight in May impossible to overstate.

With Alcaraz absent, Djokovic doubtful, and the rest of the field producing no consistent challenger to Sinner's level, the question in the men's draw is not whether Sinner lifts the trophy, but how he does it.

What's Next

Roland Garros qualifying begins May 18. The main draw opens May 24 and runs through June 7. Decisions on Djokovic's participation and Sabalenka's fitness are expected in the coming days as the two biggest question marks in Paris begin to resolve. Sinner continues his Italian Open campaign in the round of 16 against Alexei Popyrin — a tournament he is using as both preparation and, potentially, his final stop before lifting the trophy that has eluded him longest.
