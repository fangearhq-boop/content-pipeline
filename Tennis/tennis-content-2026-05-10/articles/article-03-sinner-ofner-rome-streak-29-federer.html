Sinner's Rome Campaign Opens With Dominant Win — M1000 Streak Reaches 29, Tying Federer

By Ryan Calloway | Tennis Fanrecap

May 10, 2026

Jannik Sinner opened his Italian Open with a dominant 6-3, 6-4 win over Sebastian Ofner in 1 hour 31 minutes, extending his ATP Masters 1000 winning streak to 29 consecutive matches — tying Roger Federer for the third-longest run in series history.

Jannik Sinner does not ease into tournaments. He does not take time to find his rhythm or ease off against lower-ranked opponents in the first week. He arrives, and he performs, at the level of the world's best player.

His Italian Open second-round match against Sebastian Ofner on Saturday was another demonstration of why that reputation is fully deserved. Sinner defeated the Austrian 6-3, 6-4 in one hour and 31 minutes — breaking in the fourth game of the first set and the opening game of the second, and never conceding a single break point at any stage of the match.

The result extended his ATP Masters 1000 winning streak to 29 consecutive matches, tying Roger Federer for the third-longest run in the history of the series, which dates back to 1990. Only two players in 36 years have strung together a longer unbroken sequence at this level.

What 29 Straight Means

The ATP Masters 1000 events are where the sport's best players are routinely tested against each other. There are no easy weeks, no soft draws at the top half. To win 29 consecutive matches at this level — across different surfaces, different opponents, different pressure situations — is a demonstration of consistency that very few players ever approach.

Sinner's 29-match streak ties Federer's mark for third all-time. Sinner is 23 years old. He compiled the streak while also winning five consecutive Masters 1000 titles — a feat that has never been achieved in the 36-year history of the series. He is the only player to reach the semifinals at all nine Masters events. He began 2026 at 31-2 after the Ofner win.

The numbers are extraordinary. And none of them fully capture how it feels to watch him play.

Rome: The Last Piece

Of the nine Masters 1000 events held around the world, Jannik Sinner has won eight. Miami. Monte Carlo. Madrid. Toronto. Cincinnati. Shanghai. Paris. And most recently, five straight titles through the indoor and outdoor swing of early 2026.

The one missing: Rome. The Italian Open. The tournament held in his home country, on the red clay of Foro Italico, in front of crowds that have waited half a century for one of their own to lift the trophy. The last Italian to win the Italian Open in the men's singles was Adriano Panatta — in 1976.

Fifty years. And now the best Italian player in the history of the game is in the draw, at the peak of his powers, with a 29-match Masters streak behind him. The Career Golden Masters — winning all nine Masters events — has never been completed by anyone. If Sinner wins in Rome, he becomes the first.

What's Next

Sinner faces Alexei Popyrin in the Italian Open third round. The Australian defeated Jakub Mensik 6-3, 2-6, 6-4 in over two hours to earn his place in the last 16. The match will be a sterner test than Ofner provided. But if the Sinner of the first two rounds at Foro Italico continues to play, the Career Golden Masters quest is very much alive.
