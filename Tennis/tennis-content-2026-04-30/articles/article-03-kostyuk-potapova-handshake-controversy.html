Kostyuk Refuses Handshake With Potapova Before Madrid Semifinal

By Ryan Calloway | April 30, 2026 | Tennis Fanrecap

Before Marta Kostyuk and Anastasia Potapova step onto the Madrid Open court for their semifinal Thursday night, there will be no pre-match handshake. Kostyuk confirmed as much Thursday morning, and her reasoning is not new — but it is no less significant for the repetition.

Kostyuk is Ukrainian. Potapova is Russian. Russia's war against Ukraine continues. And Kostyuk, one of the WTA's most prominent voices on the issue, has made clear she does not consider the gesture appropriate while that conflict persists.

A Principled Stance

Kostyuk is not the first Ukrainian player to decline a handshake with Russian or Belarusian opponents. Elina Svitolina began the practice after Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 and has maintained it consistently throughout her career since. Kostyuk has followed the same path — treating the pre-match and post-match handshake not as a personal slight against her opponent, but as a political statement she considers necessary.

Potapova holds both Russian and Austrian citizenship and has not publicly condemned the war. She has spoken of her love for her home country. The two players' differing positions place them in an uncomfortable position every time they share a court.

The Tennis Case for Kostyuk

Whatever one makes of the political dimension, Kostyuk arrives as the clear match favorite. She is on a 10-match winning streak and has not dropped a set in her Madrid campaign. She beat Noskova convincingly 7-6(1), 6-0 in the quarterfinals and has been one of the most consistent players on the clay swing.

Potapova, for her part, has been one of the tournament's great stories: she entered Madrid as a lucky loser and has beaten Rybakina and former world No. 1 Pliskova to reach the semifinals. She is the first lucky loser to reach a WTA 1000 semifinal in the modern era. But Kostyuk's form, head-to-head record (she won their last two meetings, including a 6-3, 6-2 win in Madrid last year), and the overall level of tennis she has shown this week make her the overwhelming favorite.

The Broader Context

The WTA and ATP have grappled with how to handle the political tensions brought on by the war since 2022. Both tours initially banned Russian and Belarusian players, then reversed course and allowed them to compete under neutral flags. The personal dynamics between players from Ukraine and Russia have never fully normalized.

Kostyuk's refusal is not a disruption of a neutral atmosphere — it is a reminder that for her, and for many of the players from Ukraine competing on tour, the atmosphere has never been fully neutral.

What's Next

Kostyuk and Potapova play Thursday at 9:30 PM Madrid time (approximately 3:30 PM ET). The winner advances to Saturday's Madrid final, where they will face either Mirra Andreeva or Hailey Baptiste. For Potapova, it would cap the most extraordinary lucky loser run in recent WTA history. For Kostyuk, it would mark a long-overdue first WTA 1000 final appearance.

Whatever unfolds on court, the build-up to this semifinal has already made it one of the most talked-about matches of the 2026 clay season.
