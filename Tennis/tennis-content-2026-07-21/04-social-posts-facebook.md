### STORY 1: Tsitsipas Wins Gstaad

**Long-Form Post** — [8:00 AM ET]

We need to issue a correction from yesterday's roundup — and the correction is actually a better story.

We reported that Raphael Collignon won the Swiss Open Gstaad. The actual winner was Stefanos Tsitsipas, who defeated Collignon 6-4, 6-7(3), 6-3 in the final on Saturday to claim his 13th career ATP title.

Tsitsipas is now the first Greek player to win the Gstaad title. The win ends a 16-month drought that started after he lifted the trophy in Dubai back in March 2025. Not a short wait for a player who was once ranked No. 3 in the world.

The match required three sets. He dropped the second in a tiebreak before closing out the third 6-3. That kind of performance — dropping a set and still finding a way to win — is not the same as the dominant kind of week. But it is the kind of week that produces titles.

He climbs 34 spots in the PIF ATP Rankings this week, moving up to No. 51. ATP Tour named him Mover of the Week. The US Open summer has just become more interesting.

What does Tsitsipas need to do to push back into the top 30 before the year is out?

### STORY 2: Kitzbuhel Day 2

**Long-Form Post** — [10:30 AM ET]

Week two of the post-Wimbledon clay swing is underway at the Generali Open Kitzbühel — and day one delivered the kind of chaos that makes the ATP 250 level worth watching.

The biggest story is what happened to the No. 8 seed, Juan Cerundolo. He went out 6-2, 6-1 to Marco Trungelliti in what was not close by any measure. Seeds can fall. That one fell hard.

Meanwhile, top seed Alexander Bublik — who is also the defending champion here — had to work in three sets to get past Facundo Diaz Acosta, winning 4-6, 6-3, 6-4. He survived. He has not looked unbeatable. That might matter by the weekend.

Some context on the draw: Flavio Cobolli was originally seeded No. 1 here. He withdrew before the tournament began, not due to injury but to take a recovery break after a first-round loss in Umag. Jan-Lennard Struff and Lorenzo Sonego came in as replacements. Bublik moved up to the top slot.

Day 2 is in progress now with Molcan and Ofner on court, Struff scheduled, and Vacherot and Rinderknech also in action.

Which match at Kitzbühel this week are you most looking forward to?

### STORY 3: Estoril Opens

**Long-Form Post** — [12:30 PM ET]

The Millennium Estoril Open started this week in Cascais, Portugal, and it has a cast of characters that makes it worth following.

Andrey Rublev is the top seed. He won the Bastad final just 48 hours before this draw began, defeating Luciano Darderi in two sets. Darderi — the runner-up — is the No. 2 seed at Estoril, which means these two could potentially meet again this week under different conditions.

Home favourite Nuno Borges is in the field. He knows this surface, this country, and what it means to play in front of his own crowd. He will be loud support all week.

And then there is Stan Wawrinka, who is scheduled to play Tuesday in what is the first time he has ever entered the Estoril Open. He won the Portugal Open in Oeiras back in 2013 — the predecessor tournament to this one — but this specific event, at this venue, is new for him. In his final professional year, he is still finding tournaments to play for the first time.

The day 2 order of play is set. Wawrinka plays on the stadium court. This is worth watching.

What are you expecting from Rublev on clay this week after the Bastad performance?

### STORY 4: Prague + Hamburg Day Two

**Long-Form Post** — [3:00 PM ET]

The post-Wimbledon women's tour shifted to hard courts in Prague on Monday, and day one already had a result worth noting.

Lucie Havlickova, ranked around No. 226, defeated Alycia Parks — ranked around No. 66 — 6-2, 6-4. A significant upset in terms of ranking gap. Parks had been expected to progress. Havlickova had other ideas.

Defending champion Marie Bouzkova opened 7-5, 7-5 to protect her title and her No. 1 seeding. Oceane Dodin won 6-3, 6-3 and No. 8 seed Daria Snigur came through in three sets.

Worth noting: Prague is played on hard courts. After five weeks of clay and two weeks of Wimbledon grass, this is one of the first hard-court events of the season. The surface transition hits different players at different speeds.

Over in Hamburg, the MSC Hamburg Ladies Open (WTA 250, clay) opened Monday as well. Oleksandra Oliynykova is the top seed. Day 2 is underway today with a full schedule including Oliynykova's opening match.

Two simultaneous WTA events in European clay and hard courts — a good problem to have as a tennis fan.

Who do you have getting through Prague this week?

### STORY 5: Wawrinka Farewell

**Long-Form Post** — [6:00 PM ET]

Stan Wawrinka is 41 years old and playing professional tennis for the last time.

He has won three Grand Slam titles — the Australian Open (2014), Roland Garros (2015), US Open (2016). He was once No. 3 in the world. He played some of the most purely enjoyable tennis of the modern era on his best days, built on a backhand that most other players can only describe.

This year, he is saying goodbye to the events that mattered to him. He got a special ceremony at Gstaad. He walked off the courts at Wimbledon and Roland Garros knowing it was the last time. Earlier this month he said: "I don't want to retire, but I know it's time to stop."

Today in Cascais, he is playing the Estoril Open for the first time in his career. He won its predecessor — the Portugal Open in Oeiras in 2013 — but never entered this specific event during his career. He is, at 41, still finding places he has never been.

His plan is to end it all at the Swiss Indoors in Basel in October. Home tournament. Home crowd. A proper send-off for one of the best players of his generation.

He will not be coaching afterward. He wants a quieter life. He has earned it.

Which Wawrinka performance do you think of first when you think about his career?
