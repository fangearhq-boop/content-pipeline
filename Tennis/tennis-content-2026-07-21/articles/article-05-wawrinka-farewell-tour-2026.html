Wawrinka's Final Year: Three Slams, One Backhand, and a Goodbye That Keeps Going

By Elena Voss | Tennis Fanrecap

July 21, 2026

Stan Wawrinka said this year: "I don't want to retire, but I know it's time to stop."

That sentence contains more than one truth. He is 41 years old. He won the Australian Open, Roland Garros, and the US Open across three consecutive major runs. He was once No. 3 in the world. He built a career on one of the most technically specific backhands the sport has ever seen — a one-hander with pace and angle that the best players in the world could not solve on the biggest stages.

He is also still playing. In Portugal. This week. For the first time.

The Farewell Tour, Stop by Stop

The 2026 season has been, in large part, a tour of goodbyes. Wawrinka received a formal farewell ceremony at Gstaad earlier this month, where he has been a regular and beloved figure over the years. He made what he described as his final appearances at Wimbledon and at Roland Garros, tournaments where he has won, lost, struggled, and triumphed over the course of a career now measured in decades.

Each tournament on the 2026 calendar has had a different weight for him than for any other player in the draw. When the last Swiss player exits a tournament, he does not simply move on to the next one in the usual professional sense. He marks something.

The Estoril Open this week is, by his own account, his last clay-court event. He played the predecessor to this tournament in 2013 in Oeiras and won it. He had never entered this specific event. At 41, in the final summer of a professional career that began when most current top-10 players were in school, he found one more first.

Three Titles That Defined the Career

Wawrinka won the Australian Open in 2014, defeating Rafael Nadal in the final. He won Roland Garros in 2015, defeating Djokovic in the final. He won the US Open in 2016, defeating Djokovic again in the final. All three victories came against top-ranked opponents. All three came in the context of being considered the clear underdog. All three are remembered not just for the results but for what happened during the matches themselves — the backhand down the line, the relentlessness, the refusal to yield even when the math suggested he should.

No other active player has three Grand Slam titles and that specific professional biography. He is not Roger Federer or Novak Djokovic in terms of volume. He is something more singular: a player who arrived at three of the sport's biggest stages and was better than whoever was across the net on that particular fortnight, three separate times.

What Happens After Basel

Wawrinka plans to play his final professional match at the Swiss Indoors in Basel in October. It is his home tournament — one of the most storied indoor events on the ATP calendar — and ending there has a logic that anyone familiar with his career would recognize. He has said he does not plan to move into coaching after retiring. He wants, by his own description, a quieter life.

Whatever a quieter life looks like for a three-time Grand Slam champion is genuinely unknown. What is known: Basel in October, and then something else.

What's Next

Wawrinka plays Tuesday at Estoril on the stadium court. If he wins, he faces Nuno Borges — a Portuguese home favourite who will have a full crowd behind him. If Wawrinka wins that, he advances to the quarterfinals of a clay event he has never played before, in the final summer of his career, at 41.

From Estoril, the schedule leads to the Canadian Open, Cincinnati, and the US Open — hard courts in North America, where the tour transitions out of the European summer. Then Basel. Then the end.

He plays on. Until October. And then the backhand rests.
