Bad Homburg WTA Semifinals: Osaka Eyes Career-First Grass Final as Ruse Meets Muchova for Second Spot

By Elena Voss | Tennis Fanrecap | June 26, 2026

The Bad Homburg Open WTA 500 reached its semifinal stage on Friday, June 26, with two compelling matches scheduled on Centre Court. Naomi Osaka, facing Wang Xinyu in the first semifinal at 1 PM, stood one win from her first career WTA grass-court final — a milestone she has never reached despite more than a decade on tour. In the second semifinal at 3 PM, world No. 105 Elena-Gabriela Ruse faced No. 11 seed Karolina Muchova in what had the makings of the upset duel of the week.

The Bad Homburg final is Saturday, June 27, two days before Wimbledon begins. For anyone reaching that final, it is the ideal pre-Championships preparation.

Osaka's Grass Revival

Osaka's run to the Bad Homburg semifinal has been one of the more eye-catching results of the grass swing. Her quarterfinal victory over Ekaterina Alexandrova — 6-2, 6-2 in under an hour, without conceding a service game — was the kind of clinical performance that signals a player at their peak physically and mentally. That win was also, according to the WTA, Osaka's first career top-20 victory on grass, and gave her a first grass-court semifinal in seven years.

Wang Xinyu arrived at the semifinal rested. The Chinese player advanced by walkover after Elina Svitolina withdrew with a right hip injury before their scheduled quarterfinal — and while walkovers are an awkward form of advancement, the additional rest could work in Wang's favour against an Osaka who has already played three matches this week.

For Osaka, the opportunity is too significant to overthink. A grass-court final, with Wimbledon on the horizon, would be a career-first achievement on a surface where she has historically struggled. She arrives in form, the conditions suit her game, and the path to the final ran through Alexandrova rather than the tournament's heavyweights.

Ruse the Giant-Killer Meets Muchova

Elena-Gabriela Ruse has been the tournament's most dramatic storyline. The Romanian, ranked 105th in the world, came into Bad Homburg without any expectation of reaching the final four. She left the quarterfinals having beaten Emma Navarro 6-4, 6-2 — Navarro being the player who had just knocked out top seed Iga Swiatek in the quarterfinals. The consecutive upset victories from Swiatek onward were not Ruse's doing, but beating Navarro directly was.

Muchova, the No. 11 seed and world No. 11, is the class act in the second semifinal on paper. The Czech came through Clara Tauson in a rollercoaster quarterfinal — losing the first set 1-6 before winning 6-2, 6-4 to advance. She has the grass-court pedigree and the variety of shots to deal with whatever Ruse brings. But Ruse has shown that confidence on grass can override rankings, and she has been here all week proving exactly that.

Andreeva Already Out — Going to Wimbledon Without Grass Preparation

Roland Garros champion Mirra Andreeva, the No. 5 seed at Bad Homburg and No. 5 seed at Wimbledon, did not make the semifinals. She lost to Alexandrova in the second round — her first match since winning the French Open. It means Andreeva arrives at Wimbledon without having played a competitive grass-court match other than her opening loss at Bad Homburg. The draw that was made Friday gives her Magda Linette in round one — a manageable opener that may allow her to find her grass-court footing without an immediate high-stakes test.

What's Next

The Bad Homburg Open final is Saturday, June 27, on Centre Court. The two finalists will be decided by the end of Friday afternoon. Wimbledon begins Monday, June 29, where Osaka is seeded below the top eight and faces a competitive bracket. The Bad Homburg title is a WTA 500 event — the points and momentum carry real weight heading into the grass-court Grand Slam.
