Alexander Zverev Wins Roland Garros 2026: First Grand Slam Title After Three Final Defeats

By Elena Voss | June 8, 2026

Germany waited thirty years for another men's Grand Slam champion. The wait ended on Sunday afternoon on Court Philippe-Chatrier, when Alexander Zverev defeated Flavio Cobolli 6-1, 4-6, 6-4, 6-7(5), 6-1 to win the 2026 Roland Garros title — his first major and the 25th title of his career.

It was Zverev's fourth Grand Slam final. He had lost the previous three: to Dominic Thiem in five sets at the 2020 US Open, to Carlos Alcaraz at the 2024 Roland Garros, and to Jannik Sinner at the 2025 Australian Open. On Sunday, the fourth final was finally the one that counted.

How the Match Unfolded

Zverev was dominant from the opening game. He took the first set 6-1 with the efficiency of a player who had waited long enough and was not willing to let the moment pass. Cobolli found his footing in the second, breaking through Zverev's serve and levelling the match at one set apiece with a 6-4 scoreline.

The third set was Zverev's again, 6-4, and the German appeared to be closing in on the title when the fourth set evolved into a contest in its own right. At 5-5 in the fourth set, a controversial line-call — an ace from Zverev ruled good by the human line judge despite the mark appearing outside — kept the set alive. The tiebreak that followed went to Cobolli 7-5, sending the match into a deciding fifth set.

In the fifth, Zverev produced the most commanding tennis of the afternoon. He dropped just one game, winning 6-1 and claiming the Coupe des Mousquetaires on his 125th Grand Slam match victory — more Grand Slam match wins than any player had accumulated before their breakthrough title.

German Tennis History

Zverev joins Boris Becker and Michael Stich as the only three German men in the Open Era to win a Grand Slam singles title. Becker won six majors between 1985 and 1996; Stich won Wimbledon in 1991. The last German men's Grand Slam title before Sunday was Becker's 1996 Australian Open victory, thirty years and several generations of German tennis ago.

At 29, Zverev has arrived at the summit of the sport. The record — three final defeats before a breakthrough — echoes players like Pete Sampras and Andre Agassi, who also required multiple attempts to claim the first of their major titles. The difference is that most players who fail in their first three Grand Slam finals do not reach a fourth.

Zverev did. And he won it.

Cobolli's Performance

Flavio Cobolli reached the final as the No. 10 seed, advancing after Matteo Arnaldi withdrew due to a viral illness in the semifinal. That walkover was only the fourth in Open Era Grand Slam semifinal history. Cobolli had earned his place in the last four legitimately, defeating Lorenzo Musetti and Felix Auger-Aliassime along the way.

On Sunday, the Italian gave a creditable account of himself before the fifth set turned one-sided. He held his nerve through the fourth set, pushed the final to five, and competed until Zverev's quality became overwhelming. As a runner-up, Cobolli exits the tournament having become the first Italian man to reach a Grand Slam final since Adriano Panatta in 1976.

What's Next

Alexander Zverev's next competitive appearances will be elsewhere, as he withdrew from the Stuttgart BOSS Open following the Roland Garros final weekend. The grass-court season — Stuttgart, s-Hertogenbosch, Queen's Club, and ultimately Wimbledon — begins this week for the rest of the tour. For Zverev, the immediate task is to process what Sunday meant and begin planning a Wimbledon campaign as a newly minted Grand Slam champion. Full tournament results are available at rolandgarros.com.
