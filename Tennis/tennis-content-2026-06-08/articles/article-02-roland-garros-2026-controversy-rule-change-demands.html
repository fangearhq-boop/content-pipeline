Roland Garros 2026 Controversy: Physio Visit and Line-Call Error Renew Demands for Rule Changes

By Marcus Cole | June 8, 2026

Alexander Zverev won the 2026 Roland Garros men's singles final. He also walked off Court Philippe-Chatrier at the centre of two controversies that have reignited debate about the rules of professional tennis and the French Open's resistance to modernisation.

Two separate incidents during Sunday's five-set final have generated significant reaction in the 24 hours since the match ended — one involving on-court medical assistance, the other a line-call that television replays appeared to contradict. Both have raised questions that the sport's governing bodies will need to address.

The Physiotherapist Incident

In the fourth set, Zverev's physiotherapist left the player box during an active phase of the match — not during a changeover, when such visits are typically permitted — to hand the German something at courtside. The German had been visibly struggling with cramping and received a massage during a subsequent changeover, but the initial interaction drew immediate scrutiny.

Former world No. 1 Jim Courier, watching from the broadcast position, questioned how the visit was allowed under the rules. Former coach Brad Gilbert noted that under standard ATP protocol, drinks and other items are delivered to players by ball kids, not by personnel from the player box. The implication from both observers was that the interaction broke established precedent, whatever the strict technical rules may allow.

The ATP Tour rules do allow physiotherapists to attend to players, but the circumstances of when and how that access is granted during a match are defined. Whether Sunday's incident fell inside or outside those parameters has not been formally adjudicated.

The Line-Calling Error

The second controversy emerged at 5-5, 40-30 in the fourth set, when a Zverev serve down the centre line was called in by the human line judge. Television replays showed the mark on the clay clearly landing outside the service box. On any of the other three Grand Slam stages — the US Open, Australian Open, or Wimbledon — electronic line calling would have immediately overruled the call.

Roland Garros remains the only Grand Slam still relying solely on human line judges for calls. The French Tennis Federation has maintained this position despite repeated calls to modernise, and the clay surface — where ball marks are visible and reviewable — has been used as one argument against the urgency of change. Sunday's incident reversed that logic: the visible mark showed the call was wrong, and it still stood.

The error came at a pivotal moment in the fourth set. Cobolli won the fourth set 7-5 in the tiebreak and then earned a runner-up finish rather than the title. Whether the call materially changed the outcome cannot be known, but the circumstances were among the highest-stakes in which such an error could occur.

The Wider Debate

The call for Roland Garros to adopt electronic line calling is not new. The Australian Open made the switch years ago; the US Open followed; Wimbledon implemented it. The French Open's continued use of human judges has been a point of contention for some time, and the 2026 final has added fresh urgency to the argument.

Both controversies from Sunday involve systemic issues — not errors by individual officials, but structural gaps in how the sport governs itself on the court's biggest stage. They may not change the result of the 2026 Roland Garros final. They will shape the conversation heading into 2027.

What's Next

The ATP Tour and Roland Garros organising committee have not issued formal statements on either incident as of Monday. Whether the French Tennis Federation will address the line-calling question ahead of the 2027 tournament remains to be seen. For now, both controversies sit alongside the result in the public record of the 2026 French Open. Current ATP Tour rules and tournament regulations are available at atptour.com.
