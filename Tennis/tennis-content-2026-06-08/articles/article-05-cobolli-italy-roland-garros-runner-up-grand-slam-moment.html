Cobolli's Roland Garros Run: Italy's First Grand Slam Finalist Since Panatta in 1976

By Marcus Cole | June 8, 2026

The last Italian man to reach a Grand Slam final was Adriano Panatta. The year was 1976. Panatta won Roland Garros that year, beating Harold Solomon in a five-set final — the most recent Grand Slam title for an Italian man in history. Fifty years later, Flavio Cobolli stood on the same court and became the first Italian man since Panatta to play in a major final.

Cobolli lost, 6-1, 4-6, 6-4, 6-7(5), 6-1, to Alexander Zverev. The result does not diminish what his 2026 Roland Garros run represented for Italian tennis — and for Cobolli personally.

How He Got There

Cobolli entered the 2026 Roland Garros draw as the No. 10 seed. His route to the final was not a collection of dominant wins so much as a series of demonstrations that he belonged at this level. He defeated Lorenzo Musetti and Felix Auger-Aliassime — both established top-10 players — in reaching the final four. His game on clay has matured significantly over the past two seasons, built around a serve that generates free points and a backhand that holds up under sustained pressure.

The semifinal arrived differently. Matteo Arnaldi, who had also impressed during the fortnight, withdrew from their all-Italian matchup due to a viral illness. Cobolli advanced by walkover — one of only four in Open Era Grand Slam semifinal history — without hitting a ball in the penultimate round. There were those who questioned whether that walkover diminished his achievement. The answer is no. You cannot control who withdraws from your half of the draw. You can only win the matches in front of you, and Cobolli had done that convincingly.

The Final

Cobolli won the second and fourth sets of Sunday's final, giving him two of the five. He forced a deciding set by taking the fourth-set tiebreak 7-5 after Zverev was given the benefit of a disputed line call at 5-5. In the fifth set, Zverev's quality was conclusive — 6-1, the match, the title.

The Italian fought with what he had. He exits Roland Garros 2026 as a runner-up, a career-high world No. 10, and the carrier of a fifty-year piece of Italian tennis history.

Italy's New Generation

Cobolli is not alone. Matteo Arnaldi, whose illness denied him a chance at his own slice of history in the Roland Garros semifinal, surges 70 places up the ATP rankings this week to No. 34 — his best career ranking. The two players represent a generation of Italian men's tennis that has been building toward this moment for several years.

Italian tennis has a long history of clay-court excellence, but the generation preceding Cobolli and Arnaldi did not produce a Grand Slam finalist on any surface. That drought is now over. Whether the title follows — this year at Wimbledon, next year in Paris, or eventually on any surface — is the next chapter.

Cobolli is 24 years old. He has time.

What's Next

Cobolli withdrew from the Stuttgart BOSS Open following the Roland Garros final weekend. His next scheduled appearances will be on the grass-court swing leading to Wimbledon. He arrives at the All England Club this summer as a top-10 player, a Grand Slam finalist, and a figure who has already made Italian tennis history.
