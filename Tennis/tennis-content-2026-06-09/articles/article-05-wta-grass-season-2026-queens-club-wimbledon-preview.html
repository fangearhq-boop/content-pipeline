WTA Grass Season 2026: Zero Days Off After Roland Garros, Wimbledon 22 Days Away

By Marcus Cole | June 9, 2026

Roland Garros concluded on Sunday, June 8. Queen's Club began on Monday, June 9. Between the completion of the most prestigious clay-court Grand Slam on the calendar and the start of the grass-court season's first major WTA event, professional tennis offered its players zero days of recovery. The HSBC Championships at Queen's Club — a WTA 500 event — opened before the final weekend's flights home had been booked.

That transition defines June tennis. It is compressed, demanding, and producing in 2026 a set of story lines that the clay season did not fully anticipate.

Andreeva Arrives with Everything to Gain

Mirra Andreeva won Roland Garros 2026, defeating Maja Chwalinska 6-3, 6-3 in the women's final. She is now ranked No. 6 in the world and heads into the grass season having not won a WTA-level title on the surface before. That means no points to defend at any grass event. She can only gain. Coming off a Grand Slam title at 19, with the confidence that brings, the question is not whether Andreeva can compete on grass — it is how quickly she can adjust to the surface and whether her Roland Garros momentum carries.

Aryna Sabalenka retains the WTA No. 1 ranking after Roland Garros, though she lost earlier in the draw than expected — falling in the quarterfinal to Daria Shnaider after leading the second set and serving for the match. Sabalenka has never won Wimbledon. The grass season represents the one gap in her recent Grand Slam record, and it will define part of her 2026 narrative from here.

Queen's Club: Surface Adjustment in Real Time

Day 1 at Queen's Club demonstrated the grass season's signature unpredictability immediately. No. 5 seed Qinwen Zheng lost to Jaqueline Cristian in straight sets. Harriet Dart beat world No. 35 Liudmila Samsonova from a set down. Grass rewards different instincts — forward pressure, serve weight, low-ball handling — and players ranked highly on clay or hard courts do not automatically carry that form to the surface.

The remaining weeks of the WTA grass swing include events in Birmingham and Eastbourne before Wimbledon begins July 1. Each tournament provides points, practice, and a chance to calibrate. The players with the best grass-court records in recent years — Sabalenka, Rybakina (No. 2), Gauff (No. 7) — all arrive with the knowledge that the surface suits them but that the draw can intervene at any moment, as it did for Zheng on Monday.

Serena Williams and the Story Beneath the Story

Serena Williams' doubles return at Queen's Club this week adds a layer to the grass season that goes beyond rankings and results. The four-year absence, the partnership with 19-year-old Victoria Mboko, the explicit statement that she does not need to win: all of it places the grass season of 2026 in a wider context. It is a season of transitions — a new Roland Garros champion in Andreeva, a returning icon in Williams, a generation of younger players testing themselves against the sport's most demanding surface.
