### STORY 1: Sinner def. Zverev — FB Long-Form

**Posting time:** 8:00 AM ET

Jannik Sinner is a back-to-back Wimbledon champion — and if you blinked during the first-set tiebreak, you might have wondered whether this was going to be his day.

Sinner lost the first-set tiebreak 9-7 to Alexander Zverev, who was sharp, aggressive, and completely in the match from the first ball. But what happened over the next three-and-a-half hours is why Sinner is now the No. 1 player in the world by every metric that matters: he stayed with it. He raised his level. In the second-set tiebreak — the match's true turning point — Sinner won 7-2 and barely looked threatened. From that moment, the match was his.

He won 80% of his first-serve points to Zverev's 71%. He won 65% of second-serve points. He hit 15 aces. He won in 3 hours, 46 minutes. He claimed his fifth Grand Slam title and became the 10th male player to win consecutive Wimbledon men's singles titles.

After the trophy ceremony, Sinner said to Zverev on-court: "You made it happen in Paris. Today you were so, so close. If you keep playing like this I'm sure you're going to have this one at home as well."

For Zverev, it is the second Grand Slam final lost. He won Roland Garros in May and needed one more to complete the Channel Slam — only six players have ever done it in the Open Era. He came close. Sinner was better on Sunday.

In his press conference, Sinner put the moment in perspective: "There is no failure if you don't win a Grand Slam. These are very, very rare days. Now I've had five in my whole life."

Five. At 24 years old.

### STORY 2: Djokovic Won't Retire Yet — FB Long-Form

**Posting time:** 10:30 AM ET

Novak Djokovic is not retiring — but he was honest enough on Sunday to admit exactly where things stand.

After losing his Wimbledon semifinal to Sinner 6-4, 6-4, 6-4, the 39-year-old sat down for a post-tournament press conference that the tennis world had been waiting for. He confirmed he plans to play the 2026 US Open. He said he wants to return to Wimbledon at least one more time. He did not set a retirement timeline.

What he did do was give a remarkably clear-eyed assessment of where he is relative to the field: "He was just a level or more better." Those words, about Sinner, were striking in their simplicity. No excuses about his limited match play this year, no complaints about the draw. Just: Sinner was better.

He also acknowledged that his grass-court sharpness wasn't where it needed to be — understandable for a player who played just 13 matches in 2026 before Wimbledon. And yet: he still reached the Wimbledon semifinal, the 8th consecutive time he has done so at this event. He beat Safiullin and Fabio Fognini before a grueling 5-hour-15-minute quarterfinal against Carlos Alcaraz — the longest QF in Wimbledon history — that left him with an ankle and calf injury heading into the Sinner match.

"Reaching two Slam semis and finals in three Slams in 2026 would be a very good result for 99% of players," he said. He's right. He's also Novak Djokovic, which means the bar has always been different.

The US Open starts August 24. One more chapter, at least.

### STORY 3: Finals Day Round-Up — FB Long-Form

**Posting time:** 3:00 PM ET

Wimbledon 2026's final day of competition delivered stories beyond the men's singles championship.

In the men's doubles final, Henry Patten of Great Britain and Harri Heliövaara of Finland defeated Marcelo Arévalo and Mate Pavić 7-6(4), 7-6(3) to claim the title. Patten became the first British player to win the Wimbledon men's doubles title twice in the Open Era — a record that adds to an extraordinary week for British tennis at Wimbledon 2026, topped by Arthur Fery's run to the men's singles semifinal.

In the women's doubles, Guo Hanyu of China and Kristina Mladenovic of France defeated Gabriela Dabrowski and Luisa Stefani 6-3, 7-5 for their first major doubles title together.

And then there was the boys' singles. Cruz Hewitt — yes, the son of two-time Wimbledon champion Lleyton Hewitt — reached the boys' final without dropping a set in earlier rounds, beating four seeded players including No. 2 seed Jamie McKenzie along the way. He lost to American Jordan Lee 6-4, 4-6, 7-5. It was 23 years ago that Lleyton Hewitt won this same grass court. The Hewitt legacy is clearly not finished.

Tennis Australia said Cruz Hewitt "takes positives from runner-up finish." Those positives are real — reaching a Grand Slam junior final without dropping a set in the earlier rounds is a performance that turns heads at every academy and tour circuit on the planet.

Quite a fortnight at SW19.

### STORY 4: Wimbledon 2026 by the Numbers — FB Long-Form

**Posting time:** 6:00 PM ET

Two weeks of tennis produced a remarkable list of numbers and records at Wimbledon 2026. Here's how the fortnight looked by the numbers.

Jannik Sinner now has 100 career Grand Slam match wins. He's 24 years old. Novak Djokovic surpassed Roger Federer's all-time Wimbledon match win record — reaching 108 — during the tournament. Linda Noskova became the 10th consecutive different women's Wimbledon champion, extending a streak that has made this the most unpredictable major of the modern era. Arthur Fery, entered as a wildcard ranked No. 114, became the first unseeded British player — male or female — to reach a Wimbledon men's singles semifinal in the Open Era.

### STORY 5: Post-Wimbledon Clay Season — FB Long-Form

**Posting time:** 9:00 PM ET

Wimbledon concluded on Sunday. On Monday morning, the tennis calendar turned the page — all the way back to clay.

Three ATP 250 events begin the week of July 13: the Nordea Open in Bastad, Sweden; the Gonet Geneva Open in Gstaad, Switzerland; and the Croatia Open in Umag, Croatia. All three run through July 19. On the WTA side, the Iasi tournament in Romania also opens Monday.

It's a sharp contrast. Two weeks ago the grass at the All England Club was centre stage. This week it's red clay at seaside and Alpine venues across Europe. The players who thrive on grass — big servers, net rushers — will yield the spotlight to baseliners and counter-punchers.

The summer clay swing builds toward Kitzbühel (ATP, July 20) and Hamburg and Prague (WTA, July 20+) before the tour makes its hard-court pivot toward the North American summer and the US Open starting August 24.

For now: three weeks of European clay, some rest, and then the sprint to Flushing Meadows.
