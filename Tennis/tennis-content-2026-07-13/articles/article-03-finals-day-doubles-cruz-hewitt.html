Wimbledon 2026 Finals Day: Patten Wins Men's Doubles Again; Cruz Hewitt Reaches Boys' Final

By Ryan Calloway | Tennis Fanrecap

July 13, 2026

Wimbledon 2026 produced stories beyond the singles finals. Henry Patten became the first British player to win the Wimbledon men's doubles title twice in the Open Era. Cruz Hewitt — son of two-time Wimbledon champion Lleyton Hewitt — reached the boys' singles final before falling to American Jordan Lee. And Guo Hanyu and Kristina Mladenovic claimed the women's doubles title for their first major championship together.

Men's Doubles: Patten Makes History

Henry Patten of Great Britain and Harri Heliövaara of Finland defeated Marcelo Arévalo and Mate Pavić 7-6(4), 7-6(3) in the men's doubles final, claiming the Wimbledon title in two tight tiebreak sets. For Patten, the win carries a specific historical weight: he becomes the first British player to win the Wimbledon men's doubles title twice in the Open Era. The achievement adds to what has been an extraordinary fortnight for British tennis at SW19, headlined by Arthur Fery's run to the men's singles semifinal.

Heliövaara, the Finnish half of the partnership, lifted his first Wimbledon title alongside Patten. The pair proved a formidable team on the grass, dealing efficiently with Arévalo and Pavić — a strong pairing that included the player who had won the mixed doubles earlier in the tournament.

Women's Doubles: Guo and Mladenovic

Guo Hanyu of China and Kristina Mladenovic of France defeated Gabriela Dabrowski of Canada and Luisa Stefani of Brazil 6-3, 7-5 to claim the women's doubles title. It was the first Grand Slam doubles title together for Guo and Mladenovic, and a significant breakthrough for a pairing that had been building toward this moment.

For Dabrowski, the runner-up finish adds to a career defined by major doubles success — she has won multiple Grand Slam doubles titles across her career. For Stefani, reaching the Wimbledon final represents one of the highest-profile results of her career in doubles.

Cruz Hewitt: A Name Returns to Centre Court

The most emotionally resonant story of Wimbledon 2026's final days may have belonged to the boys' draw. Cruz Hewitt — the son of Lleyton Hewitt, who won Wimbledon in 2003 and again in 2001 at Queen's Club — reached the boys' singles final, exactly 23 years after his father's Wimbledon triumph.

Cruz did it the hard way. He dropped no sets in reaching the final, beating four seeded players including No. 2 seed Jamie McKenzie along the way. In the final, he faced American Jordan Lee and lost 6-4, 4-6, 7-5 — a result that required a third-set comeback from Lee to decide. It was a tight, quality boys' final that came down to the wire.

Tennis Australia described Hewitt as taking "positives from runner-up finish." That framing is correct. A player who drops no sets on the way to a Grand Slam junior final, beats the second seed, and loses a tight three-setter in the final has done something exceptional. This result will appear in his career biography regardless of what follows.

Lleyton Hewitt, a two-time Wimbledon champion and Australian Davis Cup captain, was presumably watching. The Hewitt name has history at the All England Club. It does not appear to be finished.

Mixed Doubles: Arévalo and Ostapenko (Previously Covered)

The mixed doubles was settled on the penultimate day of the Championships. Marcelo Arévalo of El Salvador and Jelena Ostapenko of Latvia defeated Marc Polmans and Storm Hunter 4-6, 7-5, 6-2. Ostapenko became the seventh female player in 60 years to win majors in all three disciplines. Arévalo became the first player from El Salvador to win a Wimbledon title. The result was reported in Saturday's pipeline.

What's Next

With Wimbledon 2026 fully concluded, the grass-court season is over. The ATP and WTA tours begin their European clay swing immediately, with events in Bastad, Gstaad, Umag, and Iasi all starting the week of July 13. The North American hard-court summer — Cincinnati, the US Open — will follow in August. For players like Henry Patten, the grass has been very kind. The clay courts of Europe are a different challenge entirely.
