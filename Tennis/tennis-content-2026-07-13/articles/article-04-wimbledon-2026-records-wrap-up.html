Records Made and Broken

Jannik Sinner reaches 100 Grand Slam match wins. He is 24 years old. He did it while winning his fifth Grand Slam title and becoming the 10th male player to defend the Wimbledon men's singles title. The century of Grand Slam wins is a milestone that took his predecessors significantly longer to reach.

Novak Djokovic surpasses Roger Federer's all-time Wimbledon match win record. Djokovic reached 108 Wimbledon match wins during the tournament, passing Federer's mark of 105 (equaled July 4, passed subsequently). He is now the all-time leader in men's Wimbledon match wins — a record that may well stand for decades.

Linda Noskova is the 10th consecutive different women's Wimbledon champion. A streak that began in 2017 continues unbroken. The All England Club women's singles draw has produced a different champion every single year for a decade. It is the most striking sign of the depth and parity in the WTA's modern generation.

Henry Patten becomes the first British man to win Wimbledon men's doubles twice in the Open Era. Patten and Heliövaara's victory over Arévalo and Pavić in two tiebreak sets makes Patten's name a permanent fixture in the All England Club's doubles record books.

Story of the Tournament: Arthur Fery

The most remarkable individual story of the fortnight did not end with a title. Arthur Fery, a 23-year-old British wildcard ranked No. 114, became the first unseeded British player — male or female — to reach a Wimbledon singles semifinal in the Open Era. He is only the second wildcard in men's history to reach the Wimbledon semifinal, after Goran Ivanisevic in 2001 — who went on to win the title.

Fery had six career tour wins before the tournament began. By the time he lost to Zverev in the semifinal, his ranking had jumped from No. 114 to No. 36 — a career high — and he had earned approximately £1.2 million in prize money, compared to £884,000 in total career earnings before Wimbledon. British No. 1, career high, all from a single wild card drawn at the All England Club.

Women's Singles: The Czech Duopoly

The women's final was the first all-Czech Grand Slam singles final in history. Noskova, 21 and seeded No. 9, defeated compatriot Karolina Muchova (No. 10, 29) 6-2, 5-7, 6-3 in just under two and a half hours. Noskova became the youngest Wimbledon women's champion since Petra Kvitova in 2011 — and Kvitova watched from the Royal Box. It was the third Czech Wimbledon women's title in four years (Vondrousova 2023, Krejcikova 2024, Noskova 2026). No country outside the Czech Republic won the Wimbledon women's title in 2023, 2024, or 2026.

Noskova dedicated the title to her late mother, who died in 2024. Muchova, ever gracious, told her on-court: "I'll start with Linda, my ex-friend." Both players are from Přerov, Czech Republic, population 43,000. It was the first same-nationality women's Grand Slam final since the Williams sisters at Wimbledon in 2009.

What Didn't Happen: The Channel Slam

Zverev won Roland Garros in May. He reached the Wimbledon final. He lost. The Channel Slam — winning the French Open and Wimbledon in the same year — eluded him. Only six men have completed it in the Open Era: Laver, Borg (three times), Nadal, Federer, Djokovic, and Carlos Alcaraz in 2024. Zverev came closer than almost anyone else who has tried and failed. He will be back on this court.

What's Next

Wimbledon 2027 begins in approximately 52 weeks. Between now and then: three ATP clay 250s this week (Bastad, Gstaad, Umag), followed by the summer hard-court swing through North America, the US Open in late August, and then the indoor hard-court season through autumn. The Wimbledon storylines — Sinner's dynasty, Djokovic's final chapter, the Czech women, Fery's rise — will continue to develop. The grass courts of the All England Club will sit quiet. For now.
