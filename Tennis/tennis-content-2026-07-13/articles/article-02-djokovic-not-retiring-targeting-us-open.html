Djokovic Won't Retire Yet — "I Want to Come Back to Wimbledon One More Time"

By Elena Voss | Tennis Fanrecap

July 13, 2026

Novak Djokovic sat down for a post-tournament press conference at Wimbledon on Sunday and addressed the question the tennis world has been asking: he is not retiring. The 39-year-old says he wants to return to Wimbledon at least one more time and is targeting the 2026 US Open as his next major.

The Press Conference

After losing his Wimbledon semifinal to Jannik Sinner 6-4, 6-4, 6-4 on Friday, Djokovic did not speak at length publicly until Sunday's post-tournament press conference. What followed was one of the most candid public reflections of his career.

He confirmed he will play the US Open in August. He confirmed he plans to return to Wimbledon. He did not announce retirement. And he offered the most honest assessment of Sinner's current level that any rival has given so far: "He was just a level or more better."

That line was striking not for what it said about Sinner — everyone can see Sinner is playing the best tennis of his generation — but for what it revealed about Djokovic. He is not someone who volunteers that kind of clarity about opponents. He has rarely conceded technical superiority to anyone. The fact that he said it says something about where he believes Sinner sits right now, and perhaps something about where Djokovic himself knows he is.

A Measured Defense of His 2026

Djokovic was careful to note that his 2026 season, viewed from the outside, might actually look quite good: reaching the Wimbledon semifinal and winning earlier rounds at other majors. "Reaching two Slam semis and finals in three Slams in 2026 would be a very good result for 99% of players," he said.

He is right. He arrived at Wimbledon having played only 13 matches in 2026 before the tournament began. He beat Safiullin in the fourth round, pushed through the injury he picked up in a punishing five-hour-fifteen-minute quarterfinal against Carlos Alcaraz, and still reached the last four at a Grand Slam. The sharpness on grass was not there — he acknowledged as much — but the results were still remarkable for a player in his situation.

The ankle and calf injury he played through in the second week was a visible constraint. He had 48 hours to recover from the Alcaraz match before facing Sinner. He lost in straight sets. Those are conditions that make straight-set defeats comprehensible, even for a player at his level.

Wimbledon, One More Time

What stood out most from his comments was the clarity about Wimbledon specifically. He did not speak in vague terms about "seeing how the body feels" or "taking it one tournament at a time." He said he wants to come back to this event. That statement, from a player who has spent the last 18 months under genuine retirement speculation, reads as a commitment — or as close to one as Djokovic gives.

He has won Wimbledon seven times. He is eight consecutive Wimbledon semifinals into one of the most sustained stretches of excellence at a single major in the Open Era. The idea that he returns for one more run — at 40, having spent the US Open hard-court swing rebuilding his sharpness — is entirely plausible. It is what he does.

The Sinner Factor

The uncomfortable reality for Djokovic, and he knows it, is that the player he would need to beat to win another major is Jannik Sinner. He has now lost to Sinner in consecutive Wimbledon semifinals, in consecutive years, in straight sets both times. Sinner has won their last several meetings. Djokovic's window for beating him is not closed — the 39-year-old's game still holds up — but the gap is real, and the statistical record of recent meetings does not favor him.

None of that changes what he said Sunday. He is not done. He wants one more Wimbledon. He is playing the US Open. The chapter continues.

What's Next

Djokovic is expected to use the clay-court European summer to build match sharpness before heading to the North American hard-court swing in August. The 2026 US Open begins August 24 at Flushing Meadows in New York. If he plays and advances deep into the draw, retirement speculation will quiet. If he reaches another final — against Sinner or anyone else — it will generate the kind of conversation that only he can produce at 39.
