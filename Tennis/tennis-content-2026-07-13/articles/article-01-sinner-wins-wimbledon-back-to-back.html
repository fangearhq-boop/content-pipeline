Sinner Defeats Zverev to Win Back-to-Back Wimbledon Titles — His Fifth Grand Slam

By Marcus Cole | Tennis Fanrecap

July 13, 2026

Jannik Sinner is a two-time Wimbledon champion. The world No. 1 defeated Alexander Zverev 6-7(7), 7-6(2), 6-3, 6-4 in 3 hours and 46 minutes on Centre Court Sunday, coming from a set down to claim his fifth Grand Slam title and become the 10th male player to win consecutive Wimbledon men's singles titles.

A Set Down, Then Dominant

Zverev came out firing. The German No. 2 seed — arriving at his first Wimbledon final having already won Roland Garros in May — served at 80% in the first set and edged the tiebreak 9-7. He had 17 aces on the day, hit more first-serve percentage points than Sinner, and, at that early stage, looked like a man who might complete the Channel Slam.

Sinner's response was quiet and methodical. He absorbed the first-set loss without panic, found his rhythm, and made the second-set tiebreak the match's decisive moment. He won it 7-2. From that point, the match belonged to the Italian.

Sinner won 80% of his first-serve points in the match, to Zverev's 71%. He won 65% of second-serve points to Zverev's 61%. Those margins are not enormous, but on grass, over four sets, they accumulate. Once he found the range on return, Sinner did not let go. He took the third and fourth sets 6-3 and 6-4 to seal the title in 3 hours and 46 minutes.

Five Titles, 24 Years Old

The trophy Sinner lifted on Sunday was his fifth Grand Slam title. He has won back-to-back Wimbledon titles (2025, 2026), and his collection also includes the Australian Open. He becomes the 10th male player to defend a Wimbledon title — a list that includes Roger Federer, Pete Sampras, Bjorn Borg, and Novak Djokovic. He is 24 years old.

The magnitude of that is not easy to convey in real-time. Sinner reached 100 career Grand Slam match wins at this tournament, a milestone that took Federer and Djokovic longer to reach. At the rate he is competing, and with no visible ceiling on his improvement, the conversation about where he ends up in the all-time rankings is no longer premature — it has already started.

On-court, Sinner found words for Zverev that were generous and honest: "You made it happen in Paris. Today you were so, so close. If you keep playing like this I'm sure you're going to have this one at home as well." He meant it. Zverev is 29, at his peak, and has now lost his second Grand Slam final. He will be back.

Zverev and the Channel Slam

For Zverev, Sunday's defeat carries a particular weight. He won Roland Garros in May — his first Grand Slam title — and arrived at Wimbledon with a chance to complete the Channel Slam, winning the French Open and Wimbledon in the same year. Only six men have done it in the Open Era: Rod Laver, Bjorn Borg (three times), Rafael Nadal, Roger Federer, Novak Djokovic, and Carlos Alcaraz in 2024.

Zverev was the best player at Wimbledon this fortnight not named Jannik Sinner. He dismantled Arthur Fery in the semifinal and reached his first Wimbledon final in commanding fashion. But in the final itself, his superior first-serve percentage did not translate into the margin he needed. Sinner was simply more consistent from the baseline, more accurate on return, and better in the moments that mattered.

Sinner in His Era

In his press conference, Sinner offered a perspective that captured something true about this stage of his career: "There is no failure if you don't win a Grand Slam. These are very, very rare days. Now I've had five in my whole life."

Five. He said it almost as if reminding himself. The 2026 Wimbledon title goes down as one of the most complete performances of his career — he dropped the first set and then responded with three straight without losing his grip. That kind of composure is what separates elite champions from great players.

What's Next

Sinner will rest before the North American hard-court swing begins in August, with the US Open scheduled for late August. Zverev will begin his clay-court summer campaign at one of the European ATP 250 events, with Hamburg also on the horizon. The race for year-end No. 1 is, for now, a formality — but the season has months still to run, and the competition behind Sinner remains fierce. The tennis world now turns its attention to clay in Bastad, Gstaad, and Umag before pivoting to hard courts and the summer's final Grand Slam.
