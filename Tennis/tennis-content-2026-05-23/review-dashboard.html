Sabalenka — "Feeling 100%": The Physical Recovery and Mental Reset
NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post Window: 12:30 PM ET Aryna Sabalenka came to Paris knowing this tournament is the one she hasn't won. This year, she says she's ready to fix that — and she's been unusually specific about why. She struggled physically during the clay-court swing. A hip and lower back issue troubled her through Rome, where she lost in the third round to Sorana Cirstea. But in media day interviews at Roland Garros this week, she made a point of separating that from where she stands now. "I struggled at the beginning of the clay-court season physically, to be honest, but right now I feel 100 per cent," she said. More striking was what she said about the 2025 Roland Garros final — a match she led and lost to Coco Gauff 6-7(5), 6-2, 6-4. Sabalenka has thought a lot about what went wrong inside that match. The conclusion she reached: "My emotions were destroying my game, and my level was dropping dramatically when I would just start overreacting on everything." Her strategy now involves keeping what's happening internally hidden from opponents. "Making sure that my opponent doesn't see what's going on in my head — and at the same time, to perform better and to stay in the zone, it was a huge improvement over the years in my career and really helped me to level up." This is the version of Sabalenka that arrives as No. 1 seed and opens against Jessica Bouzas Maneiro. If she runs the gauntlet of her projected draw, she could face Gauff again in the semifinals. Will Sabalenka win her first Roland Garros in 2026? She certainly seems to believe she's ready. ---

Players Stage Media Protest Over Roland Garros Prize Money
NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post Window: 3:00 PM ET Roland Garros hasn't started, and it already has its biggest story — and it's happening off the court. Top players have spent the past week protesting what they describe as an unfair share of the tournament's revenues. Roland Garros generated 395 million euros in 2025 — a 14 percent increase from the year before. Player prize money represents 14.3 percent of that total. At a standard ATP or WTA event, players receive 22 percent of event revenue. Players, led by world No. 1 Aryna Sabalenka and defending champion Coco Gauff, are demanding a 22 percent increase in prize money. The response from the tournament: no. Tournament director Amélie Mauresmo confirmed this week that prize money for 2026 will not change, despite a 9.5 percent increase already baked in from last year's levels. The players have responded with a media protest: press conferences this week have been limited to 15 minutes per session, rather than the standard format. Sabalenka put the reasoning plainly: "I am No. 1, I must set an example and fight for all the players." This is not a boycott of the tournament itself. All top players are competing. But the tension between the players and the organization running one of the sport's most commercially successful events is the conversation happening in the corridors at Roland Garros this week — alongside everything else. Do you think the players' prize money demands at Roland Garros are fair? ---

Swiatek's Ostapenko Problem — The 6-0 Nemesis Lurking in Round Three
NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post Window: 9:00 PM ET Of all the dangerous names in the Roland Garros women's draw, the one that should worry Iga Swiatek the most might be the one sitting in her projected third round: Jelena Ostapenko. Swiatek is the No. 3 seed. She is a four-time Roland Garros champion. She is one of the most decorated players of her generation on clay. She opens against Emerson Jones on Monday. And then, if everything goes according to seeding and form, she meets Jelena Ostapenko. The head-to-head record between them is 6-0 in Ostapenko's favour, all-time. Six matches. Six wins for Ostapenko. Only one of those victories came on clay — but the record is the record. Swiatek has not found an answer to Ostapenko's game across any surface in any of their meetings. Ostapenko is the 2017 Roland Garros champion. She won here with the kind of high-risk, all-court aggression that disrupts the tactical rhythms of more consistent players — precisely what makes her so dangerous against Swiatek in particular. This isn't a guaranteed outcome. Ostapenko has to win two matches first. The draw could open differently. But if Ostapenko is in the third round and Swiatek is the No. 3 seed, this is the match the Roland Garros women's bracket should be watching. Does Swiatek finally crack the Ostapenko code at Roland Garros? ---
