14.3 Percent: The Number Behind the Roland Garros Prize Money Fight

By Ryan Calloway | Tennis Fanrecap | May 23, 2026

Before the first ball of Roland Garros 2026 has been struck in anger, the tournament's biggest controversy is already underway — and it has nothing to do with the draw.

Top players, led by world No. 1 Aryna Sabalenka and defending champion Coco Gauff, have staged a coordinated media protest this week over what they describe as an inequitable distribution of Roland Garros revenues. Press conferences since Friday have been limited to 15 minutes per session, a deliberate signal to the tournament organization and to the public watching from outside the grounds.

The Numbers at the Centre of the Dispute

Roland Garros generated 395 million euros in revenue in 2025 — a 14 percent year-on-year increase, per Al Jazeera and Tennis Majors reporting. Of that total, player prize money represents 14.3 percent. At a standard ATP or WTA event, the player share of event revenue is 22 percent.

The 2026 Roland Garros prize pool reflects a 9.5 percent increase from 2025 levels — a raise that players and their representatives argue is inadequate relative to the growth in tournament revenues. Their demand: a 22 percent increase, bringing the player share of revenue in line with what other major events distribute.

Tournament director Amélie Mauresmo addressed the situation directly this week. Her position: the prize money will not change for 2026. "Roland Garros is not going to move," per Tennis Majors. She also noted that player feedback is "taken seriously" by the organisation.

What Players Are Doing

The protest is a media availability restriction — not a boycott of the tournament itself. All top players are competing. But limiting press conferences is a meaningful gesture in a sport where media relations are contractually significant and where Grand Slam access policies are tightly controlled.

Sabalenka framed the protest in terms of responsibility: "I am No. 1, I must set an example and fight for all the players," she said, per Puntodebreak.com. Gauff voiced support for the action.

The 15-minute cap applies to the structured press conference format. It reduces the volume of content journalists can gather from top players during a fortnight when media demand for access is at its highest.

Broader Context

The prize money debate at Grand Slams is not new. The four major tournaments — Roland Garros, Wimbledon, the US Open, and the Australian Open — generate revenues that dwarf those of regular tour events, and the player share of those revenues has long been a point of contention. Wimbledon and the US Open have both faced similar pressure in recent seasons.

The argument from tournament organisations typically centres on operational costs and reinvestment into the sport's infrastructure. The argument from players centres on the share of revenues that end up in player accounts versus other stakeholders.

The 14.3 percent figure is the number driving the 2026 Roland Garros conversation. Whether it changes for 2027 will depend in part on what happens inside the negotiations that run parallel to this fortnight's competition.

What's Next

The media protest is active for the duration of Roland Garros 2026. Press conferences for top players remain limited to 15 minutes per session. The tournament organisation has indicated no changes to prize money will occur for this year's event. The singles champion will receive €2.55 million. First-round matches begin Sunday May 24; the women's final is June 7 and the men's final June 8.
