Sabalenka: "Feeling 100%" and Ready to Fight for the Roland Garros Title She's Never Won

By Marcus Cole | Tennis Fanrecap | May 23, 2026

Aryna Sabalenka is the world No. 1. She has won the Australian Open three times and the US Open. She reached the Roland Garros final last year. She has never won here.

In media day sessions at Roland Garros this week, Sabalenka was more direct than usual about what has stood between her and a Paris title — and what she believes has changed. The conversation covered two threads: her body, and her mind.

The Physical Recovery

Sabalenka's clay-court season before Roland Garros was interrupted by a hip and lower back issue that emerged during the swing in April and May. She lost in the third round at Rome to Sorana Cirstea, and the injury drew questions about her fitness arriving in Paris. She addressed those questions directly at media day.

"I struggled at the beginning of the clay-court season physically, to be honest, but right now I feel 100 per cent," she said, per the Roland-Garros.com official press conference transcript.

The timing matters. The clay-court season is over, and Roland Garros is a two-week competition starting Sunday. Coming in physically healthy — rather than managing an injury across five sets — is the baseline requirement for a title run at this tournament.

The Mental Shift

The more significant part of Sabalenka's media day statements concerned what happened inside the 2025 Roland Garros final, which she lost to Coco Gauff 6-7(5), 6-2, 6-4.

Sabalenka won the first set in a tiebreak. She then lost the next two sets and the match. In analysing that outcome since, she identified a specific pattern: "My emotions were destroying my game, and my level was dropping dramatically when I would just start overreacting on everything."

Her stated solution is two-pronged: keep the internal state hidden from opponents, and stay in the competitive zone across the length of big points. "Making sure that my opponent doesn't see what's going on in my head — and at the same time, to perform better and to stay in the zone, it was a huge improvement over the years in my career and really helped me to level up," she said.

This is a significant evolution in how Sabalenka talks about pressure moments. She has historically been one of the more outwardly emotional players on tour — which can generate enormous momentum when the energy is positive, and significant vulnerability when it tips the other way. The shift she describes is a decision to manage that exposure more deliberately.

The Draw She Faces

Sabalenka is the No. 1 seed and opens Sunday against Jessica Bouzas Maneiro. Her projected path places her in the top half of the women's draw, where the semifinal matchup on paper is against Gauff — the same player who beat her in the 2025 Roland Garros final.

That projected rematch is five rounds away. First comes Bouzas Maneiro, then a field that includes potential quarterfinal challengers in Svitolina, the Rome champion, among others. Roland Garros is not a straightforward bracket for anyone, regardless of seeding.

But Sabalenka has said everything she can say in a pre-tournament press conference. The clay answers the rest of it.

What's Next

Sabalenka opens her 2026 Roland Garros campaign against Jessica Bouzas Maneiro on Day 1, Sunday May 24. She is the No. 1 seed and the tournament's highest-ranked player. A title in Paris would add Roland Garros to a collection that already includes three Australian Open titles and one US Open. The women's final is scheduled for Saturday, June 7.
