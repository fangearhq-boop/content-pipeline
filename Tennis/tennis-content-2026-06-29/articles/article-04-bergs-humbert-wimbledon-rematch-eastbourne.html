Bergs vs Humbert at Wimbledon: A Rematch Three Days in the Making After Eastbourne

By Elena Voss | June 29, 2026

The two players who contested Sunday's Eastbourne final — with all its rain delays and drama — are drawn in the same first-round section at Wimbledon. They play Tuesday.

Tennis schedules rarely produce what Wimbledon 2026 has managed to produce in its very first week: Zizou Bergs and Ugo Humbert, who played each other in an Eastbourne final on Sunday — a final that started Saturday, got rained out after three games, and resumed Sunday morning — will play each other again on Tuesday in the Wimbledon first round. Same players, same surface, same week.

Bergs won Eastbourne 3-6, 6-1, 6-4. He leaves as the champion, the first Belgian to win an ATP title on grass, and a player who arrived on a six-match tour losing streak and leaves it. Humbert, the seven-time ATP champion and one of the most effective servers on the tour, lost a final he had led for three games before the rain interrupted everything.

Why the Rematch Matters

The draw for Wimbledon was made on Friday June 26, two days before the Eastbourne final was played. At that point, Bergs and Humbert were opponents on a schedule — names on a bracket. After Sunday, they became something more interesting: a player who just won a title on grass against the man he beat to get it, with a Grand Slam match as the immediate next fixture.

The question the Wimbledon rematch raises is simple but unresolvable until Tuesday: does what happened at Eastbourne matter? In one reading, Bergs goes in with confidence, form, and the psychological edge of a man who showed he could win when it mattered. In another, Humbert — who was leading 2-1 when the rain came and had dominated the first set — goes in knowing the match was much closer than the final scoreline suggests and that the circumstances on a Grand Slam court will be different.

Humbert is not a player whose form disappears after a loss. He has seven ATP titles because he can produce quality at short notice. His serve — among the best in the game when it is working — is as effective at the All England Club as anywhere on tour.

The Precedent

The situation — two players meeting in an ATP final and then again in a Grand Slam first round days later — is extraordinarily rare. It requires the draw to land in a specific way and the results of a warm-up event to align with it. The Wimbledon qualifying draw was finalized before Eastbourne concluded, which means this is the product of timing rather than design. The result is a first-round Wimbledon match with more narrative weight than most third-round matches carry.

What's Next

Bergs and Humbert meet on Tuesday at a court yet to be assigned. The winner advances to the Wimbledon second round. The loser returns to the draw with one more loss at this venue and the knowledge that their grass-court fortnight ended earlier than either would have wanted.

Whatever happens Tuesday, the Eastbourne week has already changed the narrative around Bergs. First ATP title. First Belgian grass champion. The match Tuesday will determine whether it also becomes the start of a Wimbledon run.
