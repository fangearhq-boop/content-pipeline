Zizou Bergs Wins Eastbourne in Rain-Delayed Final, Claims First ATP Title and Belgian Grass History

By Elena Voss | June 29, 2026

Bergs defeated Humbert 3-6, 6-1, 6-4 in a final split across two days by rain — coming from a set down to win his first tour title and become the first Belgian man to win on grass.

Zizou Bergs arrived in Eastbourne having lost six consecutive tour-level matches. He left it as a champion. The 27-year-old Belgian defeated Ugo Humbert 3-6, 6-1, 6-4 on Sunday in the Lexus Eastbourne Open final — a match that had to be halted by rain on Saturday after just three games, with Humbert leading 2-1, and resumed Sunday morning on the eve of Wimbledon.

What made the victory more than a statistical entry is what it represents for Belgian tennis. Bergs became the first Belgian man to win an ATP title on grass. He had reached two ATP Tour finals before this — at Auckland in early 2025 and at 's-Hertogenbosch on grass that same summer — and lost both. The Eastbourne final was his third attempt. He won it despite losing the first set convincingly.

How the Final Unfolded

Saturday's plan was a normal grass-court final. The reality was three games — all holds, Humbert serving and leading 2-1 — before the rain arrived and covered the courts. Both players returned Sunday to finish what they had started. What followed was a sharp shift in momentum.

Humbert, the seven-time ATP champion and one of the most effective servers on the tour, had clearly found his rhythm in that brief opening set. He won it 6-3 and carried the energy of a player who knew what he was doing on this surface. Then Bergs changed everything. The second set lasted less than thirty minutes. Bergs won it 6-1, breaking twice and not allowing Humbert to settle into any kind of pattern. The third set was more contested but followed the same direction: Bergs in control, Humbert unable to reassert the authority of the opening set. At 6-4, it was over.

Bergs had come in with the longest losing streak of recent months. The standard explanation would be that a player in that situation is low on confidence. What Bergs showed at Eastbourne is that losing streaks are not predictors — they are conditions, and conditions can change inside a week.

The Correction

A note on reporting: the Eastbourne men's final attracted wide coverage during its rain stoppage on Saturday. At that point, Humbert was leading 2-1 and the match was described as a Sunday resumption. Some early reports speculated on the outcome without confirming it. The result — Bergs winning 3-6, 6-1, 6-4 — is confirmed from multiple sources including the official ATP Tour and the Lawn Tennis Association.

What's Next

Zizou Bergs faces Wimbledon on Tuesday — and, specifically, he faces Ugo Humbert in the first round. The two players were drawn against each other in the main draw before the Eastbourne final was played, creating one of the rarer situations in tennis: a grass-court title match between two players who will meet again at a Grand Slam three days later. Bergs goes into that match as the Eastbourne champion. Humbert goes in as the man who held the advantage for three games and then watched it disappear.

Humbert, for his part, remains one of the more dangerous players in the first week of Wimbledon. His serve can win points from any position, and he has beaten higher-ranked opponents on this surface before. The rematch will not follow the script of the final automatically — the conditions are different, the stakes are higher, and both players will have spent Sunday evening processing what happened in ways that will only become clear on Tuesday.
