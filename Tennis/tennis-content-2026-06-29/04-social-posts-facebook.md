### STORY 1: Bergs Wins Eastbourne

#### Long-Form Post — 8:00 AM ET

Zizou Bergs is a Grand Slam week champion. 🎾

The Belgian world No. 27 defeated Ugo Humbert 3-6, 6-1, 6-4 in the Eastbourne final on Sunday — a final that started Saturday, got rained out after three games, and had to resume Sunday morning, the day before Wimbledon.

It was a remarkable comeback. Humbert dominated the first set. Then Bergs flipped everything — winning the second set 6-1 and controlling the third to close out his first ATP title. He arrived in Eastbourne having lost six straight tour-level matches. He leaves as a champion.

The Belgian milestone here is worth noting: Bergs is the first Belgian man to win an ATP title on grass. He had reached two ATP finals before — Auckland 2025 and 's-Hertogenbosch 2025 — without winning either. The third final was different.

Now he has to come back and do it again: Bergs and Humbert are drawn in the same first-round section at Wimbledon, and they play on Tuesday. Same opponents, same surface, three days later. Tennis rarely produces a storyline like this.

### STORY 2: Raducanu Withdraws

#### Long-Form Post — 10:30 AM ET

Emma Raducanu has withdrawn from Wimbledon 2026.

The British No. 1 announced Sunday night that a scan had revealed a stress fracture in her lower right leg. She had been managing what she called a "lower-leg niggle" since the end of the clay season but pushed through to reach the Queen's Club final two weeks ago. On Sunday, the scan made the decision for her.

The timing is brutal. Raducanu was scheduled to open on Court 1 today against Antonia Ruzic — playing at Wimbledon, her home Grand Slam, in front of a crowd that was ready for her. Instead she'll be watching. Her replacement is Darja Semenistaja of Latvia, ranked 112th in the world and called up as lucky loser after losing in Wimbledon qualifying.

This is the second time Raducanu has missed Wimbledon in four years, and it comes just weeks after she showed some of her best tennis of the year at Queen's. The stress fracture means a recovery timeline measured in weeks, not days.

She joins Carlos Alcaraz (wrist) and 16 others on the Wimbledon 2026 withdrawal list. Eighteen players out. The draw doesn't get thinner — it just gets less predictable.

### STORY 3: Wimbledon Day 1

#### Long-Form Post — 12:30 PM ET

Wimbledon 2026 has begun. 🌿

The outside courts got underway at 11 AM local time, and the early results are in: Belinda Bencic (No. 11 seed) opened with a clean 6-2, 6-1 win over British wild card Mika Stojsavljevic. Marton Fucsovics beat Luca Van Assche 6-3 before the Frenchman retired at 4-0 in the second set.

Centre Court is where the afternoon belongs. The order of play: defending champion Jannik Sinner opens against Miomir Kecmanovic. Aryna Sabalenka, the No. 1 seed who has never won Wimbledon, follows against qualifier Teodora Kostovic. Novak Djokovic — seven-time champion, 39 years old, 13 matches played in 2026 — closes Centre Court against Wu Yibing.

It's an extraordinary Day 1 lineup. Sinner leads the H2H vs Kecmanovic 4-0 and has never dropped a set to him. Sabalenka's serve makes her dangerous on any surface. Djokovic's fitness is the story no one can resolve until it plays out on the grass.

This is what Wimbledon looks like when it starts.

### STORY 4: Bergs-Humbert Rematch

#### Long-Form Post — 3:00 PM ET

Here's a Wimbledon storyline you don't often see.

Zizou Bergs and Ugo Humbert just played an Eastbourne final that had to be split across two days by rain. Bergs won it Sunday morning — 3-6, 6-1, 6-4. First ATP title for the Belgian.

Then both players went home, packed their bags, and drove to Wimbledon.

Where they found out they're playing each other again on Tuesday.

Three days. Two matches. Same surface. Same two players. The second match is a Wimbledon first-round match at a Grand Slam. Bergs goes in as the champion of the week. Humbert goes in as the seven-time ATP champion who just lost a final and is looking to prove it was an off day.

These are the kinds of stories that make the grass season feel different from every other time of year.

### STORY 5: Serena Williams

#### Long-Form Post — 6:00 PM ET

Serena Williams plays her first Grand Slam singles match tomorrow in nearly four years.

The 44-year-old accepted a Wimbledon wildcard — the eighth and final women's spot — for singles, and she also holds a doubles wildcard alongside Venus. Tomorrow on Day 2, she faces 20-year-old Maya Joint in the first round.

There isn't really a fair way to frame this match-up statistically. Serena won seven Wimbledon singles titles. She's been to 12 Wimbledon finals. She knows every blade of grass at the All England Club. What no one knows is how her 44-year-old body will respond to three sets of Grand Slam tennis after three-plus years away from the singles game.

That uncertainty is exactly what makes Tuesday worth watching. It doesn't have to be perfect to be important. Serena Williams, at 44, walking out to Centre Court in front of a full Wimbledon crowd, playing her first Grand Slam singles match since the 2022 US Open — that means something regardless of the result.
