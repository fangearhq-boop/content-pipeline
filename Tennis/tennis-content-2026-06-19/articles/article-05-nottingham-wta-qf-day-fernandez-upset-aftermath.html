Nottingham WTA QF Day: Fernandez Gone, Four Players Left to Settle It

By Marcus Cole | June 19, 2026 | Tennis Fanrecap

The Lexus Nottingham Open entered its quarterfinals Friday without the player who had been seeded to win it. Leylah Fernandez, ranked No. 12 in the world and the tournament's top seed, was eliminated in an earlier round by Turkey's Zeynep Sonmez, 6-4, 7-6(1). What's left is a quarterfinal slate of four matches between players who, if you told them before the tournament they'd be in the last eight, most would have accepted without hesitation.

The Fernandez Upset

Zeynep Sonmez, ranked outside the world's top 100, beat Fernandez in straight sets — 6-4, 7-6(1) — with the second set decided decisively in the tiebreak. Fernandez's exit was not the product of an injury or an implosion; Sonmez played to a level on grass that Fernandez couldn't match on this particular day. Grass-court tennis at the WTA 250 level rewards players who can shorten points and execute under pressure, and Sonmez delivered.

For Fernandez, the early exit is a setback in terms of grass-season confidence before Wimbledon. She'll head to SW19 without a deep run to point to from this week's warm-up. For Sonmez, the win over the top seed may be the highest-ranked player she's defeated on grass in her career.

Friday's Quarterfinals

The four QF matches are: Marie Bouzkova vs Tatjana Maria, Karolina Pliskova vs Sloane Gibson, Sara Bouzas Maneiro vs Emma Navarro, and Yafan Wang vs Viktoriya Golubic. The draw doesn't feature a marquee singles star with a deep grass pedigree, but the matches will be competitive.

Pliskova, the former world No. 1 who won the Wimbledon girls' title early in her career and has reached a Wimbledon singles final, is perhaps the most experienced grass-court player remaining and arrives in this QF having won her matches through the early rounds. If her serve is firing on the Nottingham surface, she's a legitimate title candidate.

Navarro, the American rising player who has been building her clay and grass-court results progressively, is another player worth watching. Her ability to hit through the ball consistently makes her competitive across surfaces.

The Tournament Picture

The Nottingham Open runs June 13-21, with a $283,347 prize fund. It is a WTA 250 event — the smallest in terms of ranking points of the three concurrent grass-court events, alongside the WTA 500 in Berlin and the ATP 500 in Halle. The Nottingham final on Sunday June 21 wraps up simultaneously with those other two finals, giving the tennis calendar its final grass-season results picture before Wimbledon qualifying begins the next morning.

For many players in Nottingham's QF, the tournament offers a last chance to accumulate match play on grass before the main draw. That value is more practical than the ranking points suggest — for a player who hasn't won a match on the surface in several months, a run to the Nottingham semifinal or final can recalibrate serve-and-volley instincts and baseline depth in ways that practice sets simply don't replicate.

What's Next

Nottingham semifinals are scheduled for Saturday June 20, with the final Sunday June 21. The winner earns WTA 250 points and, perhaps more importantly, arrives at Wimbledon having completed a full grass-court tournament week — something not every player in the main draw will be able to say.
