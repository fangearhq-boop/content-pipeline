Sabalenka Survives Osaka in Madrid Thriller for 150th WTA 1000 Win

By Elena Voss | Tennis Fanrecap | April 28, 2026

Aryna Sabalenka and Naomi Osaka had played 12 times before Monday night in Madrid. All 12 matches were on hard courts. Sabalenka won every one of them. When they met in the round of 16 at the Mutua Madrid Open — the first clay-court encounter of their rivalry — the evidence suggested another routine win for the world No. 1. For a full set and the better part of another, it did not look routine at all.

Osaka won the first set in a tiebreak, racing to a 5-0 lead before closing it. She broke in the second set to lead 2-1. Sabalenka trailed a set and a break in front of the Caja Mágica crowd, and it is fair to say that very few watching expected what came next. Sabalenka won the next 12 games. She closed the match 6-7(1), 6-3, 6-2 in 2 hours and 20 minutes, sealing it with an ace.

The Comeback and the Milestone

It was Sabalenka's 150th WTA 1000 match win — a number that places her among the elite players in the history of the format. It was also her 17th consecutive WTA quarterfinal, a streak that underlines the relentless, churning consistency that has defined her career over the past two seasons.

The context of the first set made the turnaround even more striking. The tiebreak was a rout in Osaka's favor: the Japanese four-time Grand Slam champion played the bigger points better, and for a brief window it appeared that clay might be the surface that finally interrupted Sabalenka's dominance. The second and third sets told a different story. Sabalenka stepped up her level significantly, converted breaks in the eighth game of the second set and in the fifth and seventh games of the third.

Osaka could not generate consistent pressure on the Sabalenka serve after the first set. The final scoreline — 6-3 and then 6-2 — may understate how competitive the second set was, but the third was a near-clinical exercise from the defending champion.

Defending Champion, Still On Track

Sabalenka is the defending Madrid champion, having won the title here last year. She is now in the quarterfinals, where a win would put her in the semifinals and keep alive the prospect of what would be a historic three-peat at this tournament.

She has been one of the most complete players on the tour all year. She entered this match with a 25-1 record in 2026. The one loss came months ago, early in the season. Since then she has been almost unbeatable, and Monday night's result — even with the first-set aberration — reinforces that.

Her quarterfinal opponent is No. 32 seed Hailey Baptiste, who advanced past Belinda Bencic in a match decided by a 16-14 tiebreak in the second set — the longest tiebreak of the 2026 season. Bencic held six match points in that tiebreak; Baptiste saved them all. The two met at the Miami Open earlier this year, where Sabalenka won 6-4, 6-4 on hard. Clay, as Monday night demonstrated, introduces variables that hard courts do not.

What's Next

Sabalenka meets Hailey Baptiste in the Madrid Open quarterfinal. If she wins, a potential semifinal against either Mirra Andreeva or Leylah Fernandez awaits. Sabalenka is the defending champion and the clear favorite, but the draw has already produced enough upsets this week to make any confident prediction feel premature.

For Osaka, the clay-court season continues. This was her first Madrid appearance of 2026, and she showed on Monday night that she can compete with the very best on the surface. Winning the first set of a match against Sabalenka is not something many players manage. She will carry something meaningful from this loss into the weeks ahead.
