Stan Wawrinka Says Goodbye to Roland Garros: "It's Because of This Tournament I Wanted to Play Tennis"

By Marcus Cole | Tennis Fanrecap | May 26, 2026

The 2015 champion lost his final French Open match to Dutch lucky loser Jesper de Jong on Day 2 — then received a tribute that matched the scale of his contribution to the sport.

Stan Wawrinka first came to Roland Garros as a 19-year-old in 2004. He returned 21 times. He won the title in 2015 — defeating Novak Djokovic from two sets down in one of the greatest Grand Slam finals ever played. He was a finalist again in 2017. And on Monday afternoon, at the age of 41, he walked off Court Simonne-Mathieu for the last time after a 6-3, 3-6, 6-3, 6-4 first-round loss to Dutch lucky loser Jesper de Jong.

The loss barely mattered. The standing ovation that followed told the real story.

The Tribute

When the match ended, Roland Garros gave Stan Wawrinka everything it had. Roger Federer, Rafael Nadal, and Novak Djokovic — between them the holders of 68 Grand Slam titles — appeared on the big screens to pay their respects to a man who won three of his own in a career marked by resilience, late-career reinvention, and unforgettable major moments.

Tournament director Amélie Mauresmo and French Tennis Federation President Gilles Moretton stepped onto the court to present Wawrinka with a commemorative plaque: a glass case containing a section of the Roland Garros clay, a piece of the ground where he became a champion in 2015.

The crowd chanted his name. Some cried.

"It's Because of Roland Garros I Wanted to Play Tennis"

When the moment came to speak, Wawrinka said what he felt: "It's hard to say goodbye to you here. It's never easy to say goodbye to something you love so much, and you dedicate all your life to it."

He reflected on what this tournament has always meant: "It's because of Roland Garros that I wanted to become a tennis player."

And on the reception he received: "More than what I could have expected — finishing with so much support, so much love from the people. It was exactly the reason why I kept playing for so long."

When spectators called out "we love you, Stan," he responded simply: "Me too, I love you."

A Legacy in Three Grand Slams

Stan Wawrinka retires at the end of the 2026 season as one of the most accomplished players of his era. His three Grand Slam titles — Australian Open 2014, Roland Garros 2015, US Open 2016 — came at an age when most players are beginning to decline. He was 28 when he won his first, 30 when he won his second, and 31 when he won his third.

His 2015 Roland Garros title remains one of tennis history's great upsets. He had never previously won a match against Djokovic on clay. He defeated him in the final, losing the first two sets and turning the match completely, 4-6, 4-6, 6-3, 6-4, 6-4. It is still discussed as one of the greatest single-match performances in the history of the French Open.

His opponent on Monday, Jesper de Jong, was in Paris as a lucky loser after Arthur Fils withdrew with a hip injury. He played well and deserved to win. But the result was a footnote in a day that belonged entirely to Wawrinka.

What's Next

For Stan Wawrinka, Roland Garros 2026 is the end of a chapter but perhaps not the end of the story. He plans to continue competing through the remainder of the 2026 season before retiring. Whether he plays Wimbledon, the US Open, or further tour events, he will carry the knowledge that Paris loved him — and that on one afternoon in 2015, he was the greatest tennis player in the world. The clay courts will look different next May without him. But the section in that commemorative glass case will always remember that he was here.
