Monfils Bids an Emotional Goodbye to Roland Garros: "I Love You, I Owe You Everything"

By Elena Voss | Tennis Fanrecap | May 26, 2026

Gaël Monfils lost his final Roland Garros match to Hugo Gaston in five sets — then delivered a farewell speech that brought the Chatrier crowd to silence.

Gaël Monfils played his last match at Roland Garros the way he has always played tennis: going down fighting, finding ways to make it dramatic, refusing to go quietly. He lost to compatriot Hugo Gaston 6-2, 6-3, 3-6, 2-6, 6-0 in the night session on Court Philippe-Chatrier on Day 2. But first, he won the third and fourth sets to force a fifth. Even at the end, there was one more comeback.

When the final point was struck, the atmosphere on Chatrier shifted. This was no longer a tennis match. It was a farewell ceremony decades in the making.

Brothers on Court

Three men walked onto Court Philippe-Chatrier to be with Monfils in his last moment on Paris clay: Richard Gasquet, Gilles Simon, and Jo-Wilfried Tsonga. Together, they represented a generation of French tennis that delighted Roland Garros crowds for more than 20 years — four players who came through the youth ranks together, competed on the Davis Cup team together, and grew up in public view, their careers intertwined through every high and every low.

"These guys — I owe them so much," Monfils said, his voice thick with emotion. "They're truly my brothers in arms, my brothers, plain and simple."

On the big screens around Chatrier, tribute videos played from Roger Federer, Rafael Nadal, Novak Djokovic, Carlos Alcaraz, and Jannik Sinner — five men who collectively hold more Grand Slam titles than any group in the sport's history, all taking a moment to honour the Frenchman.

His Last Words to Paris

When Monfils stepped up to address the crowd, the court fell quiet. He had spent 20 years feeding off the energy of Paris — the shouts, the songs, the standing ovations — and now it was his turn to give something back.

"Every year, when I come to play at Roland-Garros, I get chills," he said. "Every time I'm here, it's magical, it's something incredible. I created something strong, unique, exceptional with you, and I truly love you. I'm going to miss you all so much."

Then, in the words that will define this farewell: "Roland Garros, I love you, I owe you everything, goodbye until we meet again."

A Career Defined by Paris

Monfils ends his Roland Garros career with a record of 40 wins and 18 losses — one of the most successful records by a French player in the Open Era at their home Slam. His greatest run came in 2008, when he reached the semi-finals before losing to Roger Federer in four sets. He came close again on multiple occasions, but the title at the tournament he loved most eluded him.

In a career that also took him to a US Open final in 2016 and saw him reach No. 6 in the world rankings, Monfils became something more than a tennis player to the French public. He was an entertainer, an acrobat, a crowd magnet who could make a half-volley lob look like performance art. The diving shots. The impossible retrieves. The moments when it seemed as though the laws of physics did not apply.

Roland Garros was where all of it felt most alive.

What's Next

Monfils has said his aspiration is to keep competing and reach the age of 40 still playing professional tennis. His final Roland Garros is behind him, but his career may not yet be over. As for Roland Garros 2026, the tournament moves on with the title contenders — Sinner, Sabalenka, Gauff, Swiatek — taking centre stage from Day 3 onward. But the crowd on Monday night was not thinking about the draw or the seedings. They were thinking about a man who gave them 20 years of joy. And they let him know it.
