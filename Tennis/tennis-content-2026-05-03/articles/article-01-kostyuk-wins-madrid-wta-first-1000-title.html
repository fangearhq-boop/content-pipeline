Kostyuk Wins Madrid WTA Final 6-3, 7-5 — First WTA 1000 Title, 12-0 on Clay in 2026

By Elena Voss | Tennis Fanrecap

May 3, 2026 — Madrid, Spain

Marta Kostyuk crossed the finish line at the Mutua Madrid Open on Saturday with a backflip, a trophy, and a clay-court record that demands to be taken seriously. The 23-year-old Ukrainian defeated Mirra Andreeva 6-3, 7-5 in 1 hour and 21 minutes to claim her first WTA 1000 title and cement herself as one of the most dangerous players on the surface in 2026.

It was the third title of Kostyuk's career and by far the largest. She had never won a tournament above the WTA 250 level before stepping onto the Manolo Santana Court on Saturday. She left it as a champion, the first Ukrainian player to win the Madrid Open since the tournament's founding in 2009.

Twelve and Zero

The numbers behind Kostyuk's clay season are striking. She finished the Madrid week 12-0 on the surface in 2026 — 11 wins in WTA main draw matches and one more in the Billie Jean King Cup. She is 17-4 on the year overall, a record that would look even cleaner if not for three losses to Aryna Sabalenka and one to Elena Rybakina, the two players who have spent most of 2026 trading the world No. 1 ranking.

Against everyone else on clay, Kostyuk has been flawless. She dropped only one set across the entire Madrid draw. In the final, she was efficient and composed — going to a tiebreak in neither set, keeping unforced errors in check, and converting her break opportunities consistently.

Andreeva, just 18 years old and seeded ninth at the tournament, was no soft opponent. She had beaten Sabalenka earlier in the week and arrived in the final having won 12 of her last 13 matches on clay this season. The final was Andreeva's third at a WTA 1000 event, a feat no teenager had achieved since the format began in 2009. In defeat, she too made history — the kind that comes up short only because the opponent on the other side of the net happened to be even better on this particular afternoon.

The Backflip Heard Around Tennis

When Andreeva's final shot landed wide on match point, Kostyuk did not pump her fist or drop to her knees. She sprinted toward the corner of the court and launched into a backflip that immediately became the image of the Madrid Open. Martina Navratilova called it "insane." Social media agreed.

It was a moment that captured something about Kostyuk's personality — expressive, unapologetic, fully herself. Throughout the tournament, she continued her practice of declining handshakes with Russian opponents, a political statement she has maintained since Russia's invasion of Ukraine began. The Madrid crowd was, on the whole, respectful. The tennis was the story.

Historical Significance

Kostyuk is the seventh player born in the 2000s to win a WTA 1000 title — a generation that is arriving on the biggest stages in global tennis. That she is the first Ukrainian to win Madrid adds a layer beyond the sporting moment. Ukrainian players have faced enormous logistical and emotional burdens competing on tour since 2022. A Madrid title, complete with a trophy, ranking points, and a backflip, is not a small thing.

What's Next

Kostyuk heads to Rome and then Roland Garros carrying a clay record of 12-0 and the kind of momentum that is genuinely difficult to stop. She is no longer a dark horse — she is a contender. Andreeva, in defeat, established herself as a perennial WTA 1000 final presence. The next few weeks on clay will tell us whether either player can translate their Madrid form to Paris, where the conditions are heavier, the draws are deeper, and the margins are smaller. For now, the trophy goes to Ukraine.
