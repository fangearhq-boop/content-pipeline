Roland Garros 2026: 15 Days Away, Wide-Open Draw, Sinner the Favourite

By Ryan Calloway | Tennis Fanrecap

May 3, 2026 — Madrid, Spain

Roland Garros begins May 18. Fifteen days from now, the second Grand Slam of 2026 will open its gates at Stade Roland-Garros in Paris, and the men's draw in particular shapes up to be one of the most genuinely open in recent memory.

The reason is straightforward: the two players who would normally share top billing are both compromised. Carlos Alcaraz, the two-time defending champion, officially withdrew April 24 with a right wrist injury. He has also skipped Rome, meaning he will arrive in Paris without any clay warm-up matches. Novak Djokovic has a shoulder problem that has kept him off the court since Monte-Carlo. His Roland Garros participation remains uncertain.

Into that gap steps Jannik Sinner, the world No. 1 and the most dominant player in men's tennis across the last eight months.

Sinner's Opportunity

Sinner has not dropped a set across four consecutive Masters 1000 events. He arrives in Paris, however the Madrid final concludes today, having demonstrated that his baseline game on clay is operating at a level that his opponents have yet to find an answer for. Without Alcaraz — who won Roland Garros in both 2024 and 2025 — and with Djokovic in serious doubt, the path to a first Sinner Roland Garros title is as clear as it has ever been.

There are still legitimate challengers. Zverev has historically performed well at Roland Garros. Casper Ruud reached three Grand Slam finals on clay. Taylor Fritz, Daniil Medvedev, and Carlos Alcaraz's absence does not erase depth throughout the draw. But none of them currently carry anything close to Sinner's momentum or recent results.

The Women's Draw

The WTA field coming into Roland Garros is loaded. Coco Gauff arrives as the defending champion, having won the title in 2025, and she will be one of the genuine favourites. Aryna Sabalenka, who was the finalist in 2025 and is currently ranked No. 1, has spoken openly about her desire to complete her Grand Slam collection — Roland Garros remains the one major she has not won. Elena Rybakina brings one of the most effective all-court games on any surface.

Iga Swiatek, a four-time Roland Garros champion, has been working her way back to her best form this clay season. And Marta Kostyuk, who finished Madrid on Saturday with a 12-0 clay record in 2026, arrives in Paris with the kind of momentum that makes even the most established favourites pay attention.

Entry Lists and Structure

The official entry lists have been released by Roland Garros. The tournament runs from May 18 to June 7 — three weeks on the clay of the Bois de Boulogne. The draw ceremony will come later in the week before the event. Qualifying also takes place in the days preceding the main draw.

The schedule means that the Madrid finalists — whoever they are on both tours — will have limited recovery time before Rome, and then limited recovery time before Paris. The clay season compresses quickly at this stage of the calendar.

What's Next

Before Roland Garros, Rome. The Italian Open is the final Masters 1000 and WTA 1000 event before Paris, and it serves as the last meaningful form guide ahead of the Slam. Sinner, Sabalenka, and most of the top players will use Rome to sharpen match sharpness and fine-tune clay patterns. Then Paris. The entry lists say the talent is there. The draws will determine the routes. Fifteen days.
