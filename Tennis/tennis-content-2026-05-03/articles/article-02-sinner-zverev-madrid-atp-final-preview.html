Sinner vs. Zverev: Madrid ATP Final Preview — Five Straight Masters Titles on the Line

By Marcus Cole | Tennis Fanrecap

May 3, 2026 — Madrid, Spain

It is the fifth time this season that Jannik Sinner and Alexander Zverev have met at Masters 1000 level. Every previous time, Sinner won the semifinal and went on to claim the title. Today, for the first time, Zverev gets a chance to stop him in the final itself.

The ATP Madrid Open final is scheduled not before 5 PM CEST (11 AM ET) on the Manolo Santana Court at the Caja Mágica. The stakes are considerable on both sides of the net.

What Sinner Is Chasing

Sinner arrives having won 27 consecutive Masters 1000 matches. His four most recent titles at this level came in unbroken sequence: Paris in the autumn of 2025, then Indian Wells, Miami, and Monte-Carlo in 2026. A victory today would give him five consecutive Masters 1000 titles — a feat that has never been achieved in the 36-year history of the series. Not by Rafael Nadal. Not by Novak Djokovic. Not by Roger Federer.

He leads his head-to-head with Zverev 9-4 overall and has won the last eight meetings in a row. The most recent Zverev victory came at Cincinnati in 2024. In the fifteen months since, Sinner has not given him a single win.

The Italian has also never won the Madrid title. Every other Masters event has yielded at least one trophy. Madrid has been the one gap. He could close it today while simultaneously making history.

What Zverev Brings

Zverev is not a stranger to this stage or this city. He won Madrid in 2018 and 2021 and has accumulated 30 career match wins at the Caja Mágica. A third title today would place him alongside Roger Federer and Novak Djokovic as the only men other than Nadal to win Madrid three or more times.

He earned his place in this final by dispatching Belgian wildcard Alexander Blockx 6-2, 7-5 in the semifinal — 35 winners, 85 percent of first-serve points won, and not a single break point faced. The performance was authoritative and the tennis was clean. Whatever doubts follow Zverev into big moments, his game this week has given him reason to believe.

Zverev said of Sinner before the final: "Tennis is very easy for him right now." The acknowledgment carries weight — it is both accurate and, perhaps, a frame he is trying to overcome.

The Head-to-Head and Its Limits

Statistics are not destiny. The 8-0 run that Sinner has built against Zverev in their recent meetings describes the past, not the future, but it does describe something real: Sinner has found something in Zverev's game that he can consistently exploit, and Zverev has not yet found the counter. A Grand Slam final, a Masters final, an indoor hard court, an outdoor clay court — the surface and stakes have not changed the result.

For Zverev to break that pattern today, he will likely need to dictate the baseline exchanges from the outset rather than allowing Sinner to build pace, keep his first-serve percentage near the elite level he showed in the semifinal, and find a way to create discomfort in the return games — something he has done against almost everyone on tour except the man across the net today.

What's Next

Whoever lifts the trophy today in Madrid faces Rome and Roland Garros on the horizon. For Sinner, a fifth consecutive Masters title would make his arrival at Roland Garros — where he is already the heavy favourite given Alcaraz's absence and Djokovic's doubt — feel almost inevitable. For Zverev, a win today would reset the head-to-head narrative, restore confidence in tight moments, and give him genuine momentum heading into his best Grand Slam surface. The match begins in a matter of hours. It is the biggest match in tennis today.
