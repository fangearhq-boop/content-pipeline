Italian Open Entry Lists: Sinner, Djokovic Lead ATP; Sabalenka, Gauff, Swiatek Head WTA Field

By Marcus Cole | Tennis Fanrecap

May 3, 2026 — Madrid, Spain

The Italian Open in Rome is the last major stop before Roland Garros, and the entry lists suggest both the ATP and WTA tournaments will carry genuine weight heading into Paris. With the draw date approaching, the fields for the combined Masters 1000 and WTA 1000 event are taking shape — and the contrast with what the draw looks like at Roland Garros itself is already clear.

ATP Field: Sinner Leads, Djokovic Returns, Alcaraz Out

Jannik Sinner leads the ATP Italian Open entry list as the world No. 1. He arrives in Rome, assuming he competes after the Madrid final today, having won four consecutive Masters 1000 titles. A fifth title in Rome would extend that streak to an unprecedented six — a record that was already thought impossible before Sinner started winning them consecutively.

Novak Djokovic is in the draw, marking his return to competitive clay after sitting out Monte-Carlo and Madrid with a shoulder problem. His participation in Rome is significant partly because it means Roland Garros participation remains on the table — his presence in Rome suggests he is trying to build match sharpness before Paris. Whether his shoulder holds across a full draw at a Masters event remains to be seen.

Carlos Alcaraz is among the withdrawals. The two-time defending Roland Garros champion has not played since Barcelona and will not take the court until Paris. For Rome, his absence opens up the draw and gives the midfield an opportunity to accumulate ranking points.

WTA Field: All the Major Names

The WTA Italian Open entry list is arguably stronger than the ATP's. Aryna Sabalenka, the world No. 1, is in the draw, as are Elena Rybakina, Coco Gauff, Iga Swiatek, and Alex Eala. The field represents, collectively, a significant portion of the clay-season talent that will converge on Roland Garros.

Swiatek is a four-time Roland Garros champion who has been building form this clay season. Gauff arrives as the defending Roland Garros title holder. Rybakina won Stuttgart earlier in the clay swing. And Sabalenka, despite being the world No. 1, has never won Roland Garros — it remains the one Grand Slam she has not claimed.

Marta Kostyuk, fresh from her Madrid title on Saturday, enters Rome as a genuine dark horse. Her 12-0 clay record in 2026 is not something the field can dismiss. Emma Raducanu's participation is listed as uncertain.

The Context: Rome as the Final Rehearsal

The Italian Open serves a specific purpose in the clay-season calendar: it is the last chance to accumulate match time on clay before Roland Garros begins May 18. The heavy red clay at Foro Italico plays somewhat differently from the faster surface of Madrid's Caja Mágica, and the conditions in Rome — often warmer, slower, and more physically demanding — are closer to what players will face at Roland Garros.

For players returning from injury, like Djokovic and potentially Raducanu, Rome is a critical test. For players in form, like Sinner and Kostyuk, it is an opportunity to arrive in Paris having not stopped winning.

What's Next

The Italian Open draw date has not yet been confirmed as of this writing. Once the draw is released, the route to Rome clay supremacy will become clearer. Matches begin shortly after the Madrid players complete their finals this weekend. The gap between Madrid's last match and Rome's first is short — deliberately so in the ATP and WTA calendar design. The clay swing does not pause for recovery. Paris waits on the other side.
