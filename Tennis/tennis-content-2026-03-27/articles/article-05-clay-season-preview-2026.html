Clay Season 2026: What to Expect as the Withdrawal Wave Mounts

March 27, 2026 · Tour Overview

The Miami Open hasn't even finished — the semifinals are today — and the clay-court withdrawal lists are already growing. The transition from the hard-court Sunshine Double to red clay is always turbulent, but the 2026 version comes with an unusually long list of players navigating injuries, illness, and scheduling decisions.

The Withdrawal Picture Heading Into Clay

On the WTA side, the Miami Open drew a total of 13 withdrawals — a substantial number for a WTA 1000 event. Former Grand Slam champions Barbora Krejcikova (two major titles, former No. 2) and Markéta Vondroušová (2023 Wimbledon champion) are both sidelined with long-term issues. Former world No. 1 Karolína Plíšková is also absent. The WTA has faced growing concern over the volume of back injuries on tour this season — a category that tends to linger and complicate planning for the red clay, where movement demands are heavier.

Emma Raducanu withdrew from Miami after battling a chest infection through the Middle East swing. She retired from a match in Doha and lost in Dubai before a brief return at Indian Wells. Raducanu has already entered her first clay-court event in Linz and has approximately three weeks to recover before that tournament begins (per The Tennis Gazette).

Naomi Osaka has not competed since the Australian Open, where she withdrew mid-match with a recurring abdominal injury. She also missed the Qatar Open and Dubai Championships. Her return timeline for clay remains unclear, with no official announcement on which events she plans to enter.

On the Men's Side

Holger Rune has missed multiple events this spring due to a combination of a severe Achilles injury (sustained in Stockholm in 2025) and an ongoing wrist issue. He withdrew from events in Santiago, Rio, and the BNP Paribas Open at Indian Wells, and will also miss Miami. The next few weeks of clay events will mark his first potential return windows.

Novak Djokovic withdrew from Miami with a right shoulder injury, but the ATP Tour reported the issue is not considered serious, and the former world No. 1 is expected to return for the clay swing. That would be a significant addition to events in Monte-Carlo or Madrid, where Djokovic has historically been a factor.

The Surface Shift and What It Changes

The move to clay reshuffles competitive hierarchies in ways that matter. Carlos Alcaraz — who won Monte-Carlo, Rome, and Roland Garros in 2025 — begins the clay swing as the heavy defending points leader on the surface, with 4,300 points to protect across those combined results. A subpar clay season would tighten the No. 1 rankings race significantly against Jannik Sinner, who has more modest points to defend on red clay.

Meanwhile, the first clay events — in Houston (ATP 250), Bucharest (WTA 250), and Marrakesh (ATP 250) — serve as tune-up opportunities for players who skipped or exited early from Miami, and as potential banana-peel moments for those carrying physical concerns. The clay season proper ramps up in late April with Monte-Carlo, Barcelona, and Madrid before the Roland Garros preparation intensifies in May.

What's Next

The 2026 clay-court season begins next week with simultaneous events in Houston (WTA 500), Bucharest (WTA 250), and Marrakesh (ATP 250). The marquee clay-court events — Monte-Carlo (April 12), Barcelona (April 20), and Madrid (April 27) — follow in quick succession. Roland Garros begins May 25 at Stade Roland Garros in Paris. The full withdrawal picture for early clay events will become clear over the next several days as entry lists are finalized.
