Charleston SF Day Set: Keys, Jovic, Starodubtseva Join Pegula in Final Four

By Elena Voss | Tennis Fanrecap | April 4, 2026

Credit One Charleston Open — Charleston, South Carolina | WTA 500 | Green Clay

Saturday at the Credit One Charleston Open delivered one of the most compelling quarterfinal days in recent tournament memory, setting a semifinal lineup built around three American players and a qualifying-round Cinderella. The final four are in place, and the possibility of an all-American final is no longer a long shot.

Madison Keys, the No. 5 seed, had to rally from a set down to dispatch Belinda Bencic 4-6, 6-3, 6-2 in a quarterfinal that swung decisively in the second set. Bencic — the 2016 Charleston champion and Paris 2024 Olympic gold medalist — took the opening set with clean, flat groundstrokes that seemed calibrated perfectly for the green clay. But Keys found her range with her heavy forehand, disrupted Bencic's timing through the middle exchanges, and closed out the match with authority in the third. The win confirmed Keys as one of the genuine title threats going into the final weekend.

The day's other completed quarterfinal belonged to teenager Iva Jovic. The 17-year-old No. 4 seed, who has charmed the Charleston crowds all week with a style that is impossible to ignore, defeated Anna Kalinskaya 6-3, 6-4 in what was a notably composed performance. Kalinskaya had beaten Paula Badosa in the round of 16 and entered the quarterfinal as a formidable opponent. Jovic handled the moment without any visible hesitation.

The Starodubtseva Story

The most striking element of the Charleston semifinal draw is the presence of Yuliia Starodubtseva. The Ukrainian player entered the tournament through qualifying — meaning she had to win additional matches before the main draw even started — and she has not stopped winning since. Her quarterfinal win over McCartney Kessler 6-4, 6-4 was her latest clean performance in a run that nobody predicted when the draw was announced.

Starodubtseva now faces Madison Keys in the first semifinal. For a qualifier to reach the last four at a WTA 500 event is an achievement in itself. To do it on the green clay of Charleston, against a deep draw, makes it more remarkable still. She is the tournament's genuine surprise package.

The Semifinal Pairings

The brackets for Saturday's semifinals are:

  • SF1: No. 1 seed Jessica Pegula vs. No. 4 seed Iva Jovic
  • SF2: No. 5 seed Madison Keys vs. qualifier Yuliia Starodubtseva

Pegula, the defending champion, has been forced to fight for every match this week. She has not cruised through a single result but has found ways to advance in what has been the most competitive Charleston defense in recent years. A Pegula-Keys final would pit two of the most powerful American baseliners against each other. A Pegula-Jovic final would put a 17-year-old against the defending champion in one of the sport's great youth-versus-experience matchups.

Charleston's Place in the 2026 Clay Season

The Credit One Charleston Open holds a unique position on the WTA calendar as the only green-clay event on tour. The surface sits between hard courts and red clay in terms of bounce and pace — faster than the red clay of Paris but more forgiving than hardcourt. It rewards all-court players and punishes those who rely purely on raw power without constructing points.

The depth of American presence in this semifinal — three of the four players carrying US passports — reflects both the strength of current American women's tennis and the historically strong relationship between American players and the Charleston surface. Keys, Pegula, and Jovic are three very different players, but all three have found their games on these green-clay courts this week.

What's Next

The Charleston semifinals are scheduled for Saturday. The final is Sunday, April 5. Whoever wins the title earns WTA 500 ranking points and the distinction of Charleston champion on green clay. Watch for Keys's forehand vs. Starodubtseva's consistency as the key tactical matchup in SF2, and for whether Jovic's composure holds against Pegula's experience and defensive depth in SF1.
