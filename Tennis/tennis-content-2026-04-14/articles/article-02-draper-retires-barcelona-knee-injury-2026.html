Another Setback: Jack Draper Retires at Barcelona with Knee Injury

By Marcus Cole | April 14, 2026

In his fourth tournament back from a five-month absence, the British No. 1 was forced to withdraw mid-match against Etcheverry. He was winning when it happened.

Jack Draper won the first set. He was playing well, moving cleanly on the clay, and took the opening frame 6-3 against Tomas Martin Etcheverry in the Barcelona Open first round on Monday. Then something shifted.

A physio appeared between the second and third sets. Tape was applied below Draper's right knee. He returned to the court, pushed through to 4-1 down in the third set, and then shook hands. Retirement. Another one.

"I will work my way out of this," Draper said afterward, according to the official ATP Tour report. It was the kind of statement that reveals both determination and the quiet exhaustion of a player who has spent the better part of a year fighting against his own body.

The Injury Timeline Keeps Growing

In August 2025, at the US Open, Draper began what would become a five-month absence from the ATP Tour. The cause was a bone stress injury in his left arm — a debilitating problem that required extensive rest and rehabilitation. He returned to the tour in February 2026.

Barcelona was his fourth tournament back. Eight matches into his return to professional tennis, he was pulling out mid-match with a different injury to a different part of his body.

The frustration is compounded by the quality of his play when healthy. He had won the opening set against Etcheverry, a player who defeated him without much difficulty in their early-career meetings. For at least one set on Monday, Draper looked like the player his ranking once suggested he was.

A Career Interrupted

The statistics are difficult to reconcile with the talent Draper has shown in flashes across his career. At 24, he has beaten Novak Djokovic. He has been ranked inside the ATP top 15. He has the serve, the backhand, and the footwork to compete at the very highest level of the game.

But his career record for the last twelve months reads more like an injury report than a results sheet. The left arm. The knee. The days lost to treatment and rehabilitation rather than match play.

Draper's team has repeatedly emphasized long-term thinking — prioritizing recovery and durability over forcing returns to the tour. That approach may be the correct one for his career arc, but it offers little comfort in a week when his clay season is over before it started.

What the Clay Season Loss Means

For Draper, the European clay swing — Madrid, Rome, Roland Garros — was supposed to be a chance to demonstrate his range beyond the grass courts where he is most comfortable. His Barcelona withdrawal likely removes him from consideration for the entire clay stretch, depending on the severity of the knee problem.

Wimbledon, which begins in late June, now becomes the focal point. Draper has been considered a legitimate Wimbledon contender for the last two years, and his performance at the 2025 event showed what is possible when he is fully fit. But a knee injury in April creates uncertainty about whether he can sustain the training load required to peak for grass court conditions.

The British tennis community will be following his recovery closely. At 24, there is still time — but the injury history is accumulating faster than the match wins.

What's Next

No timeline has been given for Draper's recovery from the right knee issue. His team will assess the nature of the problem before determining whether he can return for any clay events or whether the priority shifts entirely to grass court preparation.

In his absence, Tomas Martin Etcheverry advances to the Barcelona second round and could face a significant opportunity in the draw, with Draper's projected route now open to other players. Etcheverry has been one of the more consistent clay performers on the ATP Tour in 2026, and the Barcelona draw presents a genuine chance to reach the later rounds.

For Draper, the immediate focus will be healing. "I will work my way out of this" is a promise that his fans and teammates will hold onto as they wait for the news that he is back on court and, this time, staying there.
