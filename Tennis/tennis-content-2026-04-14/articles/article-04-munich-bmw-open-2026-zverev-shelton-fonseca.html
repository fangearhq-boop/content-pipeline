Munich Day 2: Zverev's Home Chance, Shelton's Aces, and Fonseca's Next Test

By Elena Voss | April 14, 2026

The BMW Open by Bitpanda is one of the ATP's most under-discussed clay events. With Zverev headlining at home and Shelton already through, this week deserves more attention.

While Barcelona commands most of the ATP headlines this week, the BMW Open by Bitpanda in Munich deserves a closer look. The ATP 500 clay event — running April 13 to 19 — has a field that would make most hardcourt 500s jealous, and a home favorite who arrived on a mission.

Alexander Zverev opens his campaign on Tuesday against Miomir Kecmanovic. Zverev is No. 2 seed at Munich and a former champion on clay. Last week, he reached the Monte Carlo semifinal before falling to Jannik Sinner in a competitive match. A reset was needed. He is getting it at home, on German soil, in front of fans who have followed his career since the junior circuit.

Zverev's Motivation and the Home Factor

Zverev has not won a major title despite multiple Grand Slam final appearances, and his clay-court record at events below the Slam level is strong. Munich is a place where he has felt comfortable before, and the draw has set up a potentially favorable path.

His potential final opponent, based on the draw structure, is Ben Shelton — an American who has been serving brilliantly and improving his clay-court movement throughout the first months of 2026. But Zverev leads that H2H 5-0. On clay, with the home crowd, his advantage would be significant.

The Kecmanovic first-round match should be relatively straightforward for the German. Kecmanovic is a solid clay-court player, but Zverev at his best on German soil is a different proposition to the player who can occasionally look tentative at neutral venues.

Shelton's Aces and the American Threat

Ben Shelton arrived at Munich on the back of a Monday first-round win over fellow American Emilio Nava, 7-6(4), 3-6, 6-3. The scoreline flatters Nava somewhat — Shelton fired 14 aces and rallied from a break down in the deciding set to advance.

That performance extended Shelton's record to 21-1 against players outside the top 100 since the 2024 Wimbledon fortnight. The statistic speaks to a player who simply does not lose matches he is supposed to win — a foundation that is essential for deep runs at tournaments where momentum builds through the early rounds.

Shelton's path in Munich includes potential matches against Flavio Cobolli and possibly Joao Fonseca before a projected semifinal against the draw's top seed. His serve on clay is a genuine weapon, and his improving footwork has made him less exploitable on the slower surface than he was twelve months ago.

Fonseca's Clay Continuation

Joao Fonseca's Monte Carlo quarterfinal last week established the Brazilian teenager as a genuine clay-court threat. He defeated Matteo Berrettini to reach the last eight in Monte Carlo, becoming one of the youngest players to reach a Masters 1000 quarterfinal in the Open Era on clay.

In Munich, he faces Alejandro Tabilo on Tuesday — another clay-court specialist who will test his ability to back up last week's result with consistent aggression. Fonseca's game is built on explosive shot-making rather than grinding defense, which means his clay results can be inconsistent depending on how well he maintains his level across three-set matches.

The other Tuesday storyline involves Stefanos Tsitsipas, who faces Fabian Marozsan. Tsitsipas — now outside the top 60 after his Monte Carlo first-round exit — needs a run in Munich to arrest his rankings slide before the bigger clay-court events in May.

What's Next

Munich's second round will determine whether the draw follows its projected path or produces the kind of upset that makes clay-court tennis unpredictable. The Zverev vs. Kecmanovic match is the marquee Tuesday contest, with results from the Fonseca and Tsitsipas matches potentially setting up fascinating quarterfinal matchups later in the week.

The tournament title would be a significant clay statement for whoever claims it — Munich awards full ATP 500 points and serves as a genuine preview of the Madrid and Rome fields in May. Zverev, Shelton, and Fonseca are the names to watch as the week unfolds.
