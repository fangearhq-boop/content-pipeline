Felix Auger-Aliassime Calls Wimbledon's Medical Timeout Rule "A Disgrace" — and Proposes a Fix

By Ryan Calloway | Tennis Fanrecap | July 7, 2026

Wimbledon, London

Felix Auger-Aliassime won his round of 16 match at Wimbledon. He was not particularly happy about how it played out.

Talking to reporters after his fifth-set victory over Alejandro Davidovich Fokina, FAA called the medical timeout rule in professional tennis "a disgrace of a rule" — and described exactly what prompted the reaction. When FAA was serving for the match at 5-4 in the fourth set, Davidovich Fokina called for a physio. He received treatment. He came back and won the set. FAA then won the fifth 6-1 to close out the match, but the sequence in the fourth sat with him.

The Complaint and the Proposal

FAA's frustration is not with the concept of medical timeouts. Players sustain genuine injuries during matches, and the sport has an obligation to accommodate them. His frustration is with the timing — specifically with the option to call for a physio at a moment that is structurally advantageous to the player doing so.

His proposed fix is specific: any player who calls for a physio during an opponent's service game should forfeit every point in that game. The logic removes the incentive to use a medical timeout tactically without eliminating the right to receive treatment. If the issue is genuinely urgent, the player takes the forfeit and gets treatment. If the issue is not urgent enough to accept that penalty, the rule has done its job.

Andy Roddick, commenting on the situation, said he personally would not have shown FAA's restraint. That is a notable endorsement from a former world No. 1 who played in an era when the debate was just as contentious.

An Ongoing Debate

The argument about medical timeouts in professional tennis is not new. Players have raised it across multiple generations of the sport. The difficulty has always been in separating genuine medical need from tactical use — two things that can look identical from the outside and are difficult to regulate from within.

What makes FAA's intervention different is the specificity. He is not simply complaining. He is describing a rule change with clear parameters and a clear rationale. Whether the ATP and the Grand Slams will act on it is a separate question. That a player of FAA's standing — entering the Wimbledon quarterfinals and preparing to face Novak Djokovic — is devoting press conference time to it suggests the feeling within the locker room is strong.

What's Next

FAA faces No. 7 Novak Djokovic in the Wimbledon quarterfinals on Day 9 — a match with generational stakes and head-to-head history tied at 1-1. Whatever comes of his post-match comments, FAA enters the quarterfinal as a genuine threat. The medical timeout debate will continue beyond this tournament. For now, the conversation has a quote attached to it: "A disgrace of a rule."
