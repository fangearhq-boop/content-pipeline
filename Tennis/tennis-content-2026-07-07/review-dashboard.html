### Long-Form Post HISTORY MADE AT WIMBLEDON. Arthur Fery — 23 years old, ranked outside the top 100, playing on a wildcard — has done something no British wild card has ever done in the Open Era: reached a Grand Slam quarterfinal. Fery defeated No. 28 Grigor Dimitrov 7-5, 3-6, 4-6, 6-4, 7-6(10-7) on Day 8 at Wimbledon, coming back from two sets to one down on Centre Court. At one point, Dimitrov needed just two service holds to close the match. He never got them. Fery has been one of the most exciting young British players on the circuit, but nothing in his career to this point had suggested a result of this magnitude. He's also the first wildcard to reach the men's quarterfinals at Wimbledon since Nick Kyrgios in 2014. His next opponent: No. 9 Flavio Cobolli — who just upset No. 5 Alex de Minaur on the same day. The fairytale continues on Wimbledon grass. What a fortnight. What a story. Who do you think wins the Fery-Cobolli QF? ### Image Post Caption Arthur Fery. 23 years old. Wild card. On Centre Court, Coco Gauff (No. 7) faces Jessica Pegula (No. 4) in the first all-American women's quarterfinal between two top-10 seeds at Wimbledon since the 2009 Serena–Venus final. Pegula leads their head-to-head 5-3 overall, and 1-1 on grass. Also on Centre Court: No. 3 Felix Auger-Aliassime against No. 7 Novak Djokovic. FAA carries momentum after a five-set battle in the round of 16. Djokovic, 39, is chasing his 25th Grand Slam title and eighth Wimbledon championship. Their head-to-head is tied 1-1 — but this is the first time they have ever played on grass. On No. 1 Court (a scheduling decision that has drawn criticism): No. 1 Jannik Sinner, the defending champion, faces Jan-Lennard Struff. Sinner has not dropped a set all tournament. Then Naomi Osaka (No. 14) faces Karolina Muchova (No. 10) — a rematch of the Bad Homburg final just nine days ago, which Muchova won. Four matches. All of them matter. ### Long-Form Post Felix Auger-Aliassime did not mince words after his Wimbledon round of 16 victory. When his opponent, Alejandro Davidovich Fokina, called for a medical timeout while FAA was serving for the match at 5-4 in the fourth set, Fokina received treatment — and when he came back, he won the set. FAA won the fifth set 6-1 to close out the match, but what happened in the fourth stayed with him. "A disgrace of a rule," FAA said afterward. His proposed fix: any player who calls for a physio during their opponent's service game should forfeit every point in that game. The logic removes any tactical incentive without eliminating the right to receive treatment. Andy Roddick said he personally would not have shown FAA's restraint. The debate over medical timeouts in professional tennis has been ongoing for years. FAA's comments — specific, direct, and backed by a concrete proposal — have put it back at the center of the sport's biggest tournament. Do you think the rule needs to change? ### Image Post Caption "A disgrace of a rule." Felix Auger-Aliassime is calling out Wimbledon's medical timeout rules — and has a specific proposal to fix it.
