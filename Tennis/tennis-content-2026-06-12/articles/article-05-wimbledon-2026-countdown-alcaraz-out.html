Wimbledon 2026: 17 Days Out, Alcaraz Withdraws, Sinner Stands Alone

By Marcus Cole | Tennis Fanrecap | June 12, 2026

Carlos Alcaraz will not defend his Wimbledon 2023 and 2024 titles. The Spaniard has withdrawn from the 2026 Championships due to a right wrist injury, removing one of the tournament's central figures from a draw that now opens in 17 days. For Jannik Sinner, who beat Alcaraz in last year's final and arrives in 2026 as the world No. 1 defending champion, the path to a second consecutive Wimbledon title just became more direct.

Wimbledon 2026 runs June 29 to July 12. The draw ceremony is June 26. The seedings are based on rankings from June 22. In 17 days, the most famous tennis tournament in the world begins. Here's where things stand.

Men's: Sinner as the Clear Favourite

Jannik Sinner won Wimbledon 2025 with a 4-6, 6-4, 6-4, 6-4 victory over Alcaraz that showed his ability to close out a match against the world's other elite player. With Alcaraz now out of the 2026 draw entirely, Sinner enters as the undisputed favourite.

His preparation has been unconventional — a cardiac scare at Roland Garros led to hospital tests, and he's withdrawn from every official grass-court event. But he is confirmed for the Giorgio Armani Tennis Classic at Hurlingham Club from June 23 to 27, which gives him competitive grass preparation before the fortnight begins. No pathology was found in his medical tests.

Who challenges him? The grass swing is generating candidates. Alexander Bublik, whose explosive serve and grass-court instincts make him dangerous on fast surfaces, is at the Stuttgart quarterfinals this week. Taylor Fritz, Stuttgart's defending champion, is testing his fitness after a knee issue. Ben Shelton, the American top seed in Stuttgart, is showing the kind of dominant grass form that could carry through to Wimbledon. Félix Auger-Aliassime, at a career-high No. 4, has been clinical in 's-Hertogenbosch.

None of these players has yet proven they can sustain form over a Wimbledon fortnight. Sinner has. That gap remains the defining feature of the men's draw.

Women's: Swiatek Defends, Rybakina and Andreeva Threaten

Iga Swiatek's Wimbledon 2025 win — a 6-0, 6-0 final over Amanda Anisimova that remains the most one-sided women's Grand Slam final in recent memory — completed her career Grand Slam. For a player who had struggled on grass, it was a profound statement.

Defending on grass is harder than winning it the first time. The mental adjustment from chasing to defending, combined with the surface's inherent variance, makes repeat wins difficult. Rybakina, the 2022 Wimbledon champion, is the player who most resembles a natural grass-court champion among the current WTA field. Her serve, her flat groundstrokes, and her composed baseline game are all built for the surface.

The wild card is Mirra Andreeva. The 19-year-old Russian won Roland Garros 2026 — becoming the youngest champion at the clay major since Monica Seles in 1992 — and now navigates a surface transition to grass in 17 days. Andreeva's ceiling is enormous, and her physical and mental composure is already remarkable for her age. Whether she can transfer clay momentum to grass on this timeline is one of Wimbledon's most compelling questions.

British Hopes

Queen's Club this week is the British tennis showcase, with Emma Raducanu and Katie Boulter both in action on the same grass courts they'll eventually need to translate into Wimbledon performance. Boulter has been in excellent form this week. Raducanu won her first-round match convincingly before facing Sorana Cirstea in the Round of 16.

Cameron Norrie, meanwhile, will be at Hurlingham alongside Sinner later in the month — competitive grass reps before entering the Wimbledon draw as the British player most likely to go deep in the men's bracket.

What's Next

The draw ceremony on June 26 will set the brackets. Until then, the grass swing continues: Stuttgart and 's-Hertogenbosch finals are Sunday June 14, Queen's Club final also Sunday. Players who win here carry confidence, grass-court rhythm, and in some cases ranking points into the All England Club. For Sinner, the question isn't who beats him — it's whether his health holds. For everyone else, the question is whether they can find the consistency to last two weeks on the most storied surface in sport.
