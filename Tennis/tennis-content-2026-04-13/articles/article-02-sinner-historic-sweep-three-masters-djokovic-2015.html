Sinner Sweeps Indian Wells, Miami and Monte Carlo — Only the Second Player Since Djokovic in 2015 to Win the Season's First Three Masters 1000 Titles

By Marcus Cole | April 13, 2026

With four consecutive Masters 1000 titles and 22 consecutive Masters 1000 wins, Sinner has entered a conversation reserved for Nadal and Djokovic.

When Novak Djokovic won the Indian Wells Masters, the Miami Open, and the Monte-Carlo Masters in 2015, he completed one of the most impressive runs of sustained excellence in the history of the Masters 1000 series. For the next 11 years, no player would repeat that achievement.

On Sunday, Jannik Sinner joined Djokovic as the only players to accomplish it.

Sinner's 7-6(5), 6-3 win over Carlos Alcaraz in the 2026 Monte Carlo final completed a sweep of the first three Masters 1000 tournaments on the calendar — Indian Wells (March 8-16), Miami (March 19-30), and now Monte Carlo (April 6-13). No player had done this since Djokovic's 2015 season. Before Djokovic, no player had done it in the Open Era.

Four Consecutive Masters 1000 Titles

The Monte Carlo title also extended Sinner's run of consecutive Masters 1000 titles to four. His streak began at the Paris Masters in November 2025, where he beat the field on indoor hard courts to close out his year. He then won Indian Wells and Miami on outdoor hard courts in March before adding Monte Carlo on clay.

Only Rafael Nadal and Novak Djokovic had previously won four or more consecutive Masters 1000 titles. The list now has three names: Nadal, Djokovic, Sinner.

Sinner's active Masters 1000 win streak stands at 22 consecutive matches. He has not lost a Masters 1000 match since his first-round defeat at the Madrid Open in May 2025.

How This Season Has Unfolded

The arc of Sinner's 2026 season is one of gradual but relentless accumulation. He entered the year as the world No. 2, with the No. 1 ranking having shifted to Alcaraz during his absence from events late in 2025. He won Indian Wells in March, closing a 1,190-point gap. He then defended and extended that form at Miami, adding another title and another points buffer.

Entering Monte Carlo, Sinner had no points to defend — he missed the 2025 edition during his suspension. Alcaraz was defending all 1,000 points from his 2025 title. The ranking calculation was simple: win Monte Carlo, and Sinner returns to No. 1. He did exactly that.

The breadth of his 2026 achievements is worth pausing on. He has won on hard courts in the desert, in South Florida humidity, and now on clay in Monaco. Each environment carries different demands. Three titles, three environments, three months.

Context: What Djokovic Did in 2015

Djokovic's 2015 season is widely regarded as one of the three or four greatest single-season performances in men's tennis history. He won three Grand Slam titles and six Masters 1000 events that year. His sweep of the first three Masters 1000 tournaments was part of a year that ended with him holding the No. 1 ranking by the widest margin in the Open Era.

Sinner is not in that conversation yet — it is April, and there are months and multiple Grand Slams still ahead. But the early-season accomplishment is genuinely comparable, and the company it puts him in is unmistakable.

What's Next

Sinner begins Monday as the ATP world No. 1, with the clay season's two biggest remaining events — the Madrid Open and Roland Garros — still ahead. His 22-match Masters 1000 winning streak will face new tests from Alcaraz, who enters the Barcelona and Madrid draws motivated by Sunday's loss. The question is no longer whether Sinner can perform on clay. The question now is how long the streak lasts, and which tournament — if any — stops it before Paris.
