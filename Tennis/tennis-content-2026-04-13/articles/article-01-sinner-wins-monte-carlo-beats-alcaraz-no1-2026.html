Sinner Beats Alcaraz in Straight Sets to Win Monte Carlo, Reclaim World No. 1

By Elena Voss | April 13, 2026

A 7-6(5), 6-3 victory in windy Monaco gives Sinner his first clay Masters 1000 title and ends a 1,190-point ranking gap in two hours and 15 minutes.

Jannik Sinner is the 2026 Rolex Monte-Carlo Masters champion, and the world's new No. 1 tennis player — again.

In a final that carried the weight of months of anticipation, Sinner defeated Carlos Alcaraz 7-6(5), 6-3 on Sunday afternoon at the Monte Carlo Country Club to claim the first clay Masters 1000 title of his career. The match lasted 2 hours and 15 minutes in blustery conditions on the French Riviera. Alcaraz, the defending champion and a player who had won his previous 17 clay matches, committed 45 unforced errors and could never quite find the foothold his game required.

Sinner begins his 67th week as ATP world No. 1 on Monday. Alcaraz, who had held the ranking for 66 weeks, sits second at 13,240 points. Sinner moves to 13,350.

How the Match Unfolded

The opening set was everything the occasion promised. Both players held serve with relative comfort through the first 10 games, each forcing the other to earn every point. The first set tiebreak was intense — Sinner trailed briefly before seizing the moment with clutch serving and a 7-5 finish that gave him the psychological edge without decisively breaking Alcaraz's game.

The second set opened with a twist. Alcaraz broke early and led 3-1, appearing to settle into the kind of rhythm that has undone opponents all week. But Sinner broke back without drama, then won four consecutive games to close out the match. When Alcaraz pushed a forehand return long on match point, the title was Sinner's on his first opportunity.

Post-match, Sinner was characteristically measured: "I still need a little bit of time to realise what happened," he said in his on-court interview. "Winning on clay at this level — this means a lot to me."

The Significance of Clay

This was only the second career meeting between Sinner and Alcaraz on clay — and the first at a major event. Clay has historically been framed as Alcaraz's natural domain. He is a two-time Roland Garros champion. His heavy topspin and explosive lateral movement translate particularly well to slower surfaces. His 45 unforced errors on Sunday were not a reflection of his clay-court ability in general; they were the product of windy conditions, a tight first set, and an opponent who kept every ball in play until the moment was right.

For Sinner, winning on clay in this setting carries a different kind of meaning. His 2026 dominance has been built primarily on hard courts — Indian Wells and Miami in March. Monte Carlo adds a different layer. He now holds Masters 1000 titles on two surfaces in the same calendar season.

A Week in Monte Carlo

Sinner's run through the Monte Carlo draw was the opposite of comfortable. He opened with a 6-3, 6-0 destruction of Humbert, then survived a three-set test against Machac — his first dropped set in 37 at Masters 1000 level — before dispatching Felix Auger-Aliassime 6-3, 6-4. His semifinal against Alexander Zverev was perhaps the most commanding performance of the fortnight: 6-1, 6-4 in 83 minutes, winning 80 percent of first-serve points in the opening set.

By the time the final arrived, his M1000 win streak stood at 21. It now stands at 22.

What's Next

Sinner returns to the top of the rankings heading into the busiest weeks of the clay season. The Barcelona Open (ATP 500, April 13-19) and the Madrid Open are next on the calendar before Roland Garros in late May. Alcaraz, who exits Monte Carlo with his title-defense points gone but his season record still formidable at 18-3, will be expected to reassert himself quickly. Their clay-court rivalry has now produced one match. There will be more before Paris.
