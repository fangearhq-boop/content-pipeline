WTA Rome Semifinal Day: Four Champions, Two Matches, Ten Days to Roland Garros

By Ryan Calloway | Tennis Fanrecap | May 14, 2026

Rome, Italy

It would be difficult to construct a more compelling pair of women's semifinals than the ones scheduled for Thursday at the Foro Italico. Iga Swiatek against Elina Svitolina. Coco Gauff against Sorana Cirstea. A four-time Grand Slam champion faces a former Rome champion. A defending Grand Slam champion faces a 36-year-old in her first career Rome semifinal. The stakes are not just a place in the final — they are a form guide for Roland Garros, which opens its main draw in 10 days.

Swiatek vs Svitolina: Form Meets Experience

Iga Swiatek has been building through this tournament to something that looks increasingly like the player who won Roland Garros four times. She dismissed Jessica Pegula 6-1, 6-2 in the quarterfinals on Wednesday, conceding just three games — a performance that recalled the relentless, baseline-dominating tennis that made her the undisputed queen of clay for much of the past four years.

Swiatek made a coaching change that has drawn attention. Francisco Roig — who spent nearly two decades as Rafael Nadal's coach during the peak of his clay mastery — is now the voice in her corner. The early results in Rome suggest the partnership is working. Swiatek moves through the ball with authority again, her high-bouncing topspin forehand finding the corners, her defensive retrieval sharper than it has looked in months.

Svitolina, meanwhile, arrives in the semifinal after saving 16 break points to beat No. 2 seed Elena Rybakina 2-6, 6-4, 6-4 in one of the most mentally demanding quarterfinal wins of the Rome tournament. The Ukrainian has not been in a Rome semifinal since winning the title in 2018. She is here again because of her refusal to be broken under pressure.

The matchup is a study in contrasts: Swiatek's power and topspin against Svitolina's tactical precision and tenacity. On clay, where Swiatek's game is most effective, the four-time Roland Garros champion holds the advantage. But Svitolina played against Rybakina's power all match and won. She will not be intimidated by the bigger stage.

Gauff vs Cirstea: Champion vs Fairytale

Coco Gauff is the defending Roland Garros champion, the No. 3 seed at Rome, and one of the most complete players on the WTA tour. She is also in her fourth Rome semifinal and knows what is required to reach and win a final here. Thursday is not new territory for Gauff. It is, in every sense, for Cirstea.

Sorana Cirstea is 36 years old and playing what she has announced will be her final professional season. She reached the quarterfinals by beating world No. 1 Aryna Sabalenka in the third round — a result that stunned the tournament — and then defeated Jelena Ostapenko 6-1, 7-6(7) to reach her first career Rome semifinal in more than two decades on tour.

Gauff, for her part, was far from comfortable in the quarterfinals. She required five match points to close out a 4-6, 6-2, 6-4 win over Mirra Andreeva, leading 5-1 in the third set before being broken and having to find a way to win under renewed pressure. She did find it — that capacity to close out difficult moments is part of what makes her the defending champion at Roland Garros.

The question on Thursday is whether Gauff, who has looked sharp but not dominant, can find her best level against a player who has nothing to lose. Cirstea's clay game — her variety, her ability to change pace, her willingness to come to the net — is particularly difficult to read on a slow surface. The 36-year-old has beaten the world No. 1 this week. She will not be playing conservatively against the No. 3.

The Roland Garros Lens

Everything that happens Thursday in Rome is filtered through the knowledge that Roland Garros is 10 days away. The Italian Open is the final WTA 1000 of the clay swing before Paris. Whoever wins today is almost certain to arrive at Roland Garros carrying confidence. Whoever loses will need to recalibrate quickly.

Swiatek, if she wins in Rome, arrives at the French Open having reclaimed her clay dominance with a title. Gauff, if she wins, arrives as defending champion with Rome form. And if Svitolina or Cirstea advances, they become the story of the women's draw at Roland Garros — the veteran having one last extraordinary week.

What's Next

The women's final at the Italian Open is scheduled for Saturday, May 16. The winner faces Roland Garros qualifying beginning Sunday, May 18, and a main draw that opens May 24. The draw ceremony in Paris takes place Thursday, May 21 at 2 PM local time.

Both semifinals are scheduled for Thursday afternoon on Campo Centrale. The order of play and exact starting times are set by the tournament, with the first semifinal beginning in the early afternoon Rome time.
