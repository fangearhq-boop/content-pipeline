Sinner Chases Sole Masters Record in Rome Quarterfinal Against Rublev

By Elena Voss | Tennis Fanrecap | May 14, 2026

Rome, Italy

Jannik Sinner walks onto Campo Centrale on Thursday afternoon carrying the weight of history and the noise of a home crowd hungry for a record. His quarterfinal against No. 12 seed Andrey Rublev at the Internazionali BNL d'Italia is about more than a spot in the semifinals. It is, quite possibly, the most significant ATP match of the entire clay season so far.

Sinner enters the match with 31 consecutive ATP Masters 1000 wins — tied with Novak Djokovic for the all-time record in the 36-year history of the Masters series. A victory over Rublev on Thursday gives Sinner sole possession of the greatest sustained dominance streak the format has ever seen.

The Record Within Reach

The streak began to look historically significant as Sinner won his third, fourth, and fifth consecutive Masters titles in 2026. It became truly remarkable when he surpassed Roger Federer's previous mark and matched Djokovic's 29-win run from 2011. On Tuesday at the Foro Italico, Sinner defeated Andrea Pellegrino 6-2, 6-3 in the round of 16 — the win that tied Djokovic at 31 and set up Thursday's record-breaking opportunity.

The comparison to Djokovic is fitting. Djokovic's 31-match Masters streak, achieved in 2011, stood as one of the most durable records in professional tennis for 15 years. It required consistent excellence across multiple tournaments, multiple surfaces within the hard and clay Masters calendar, and the ability to navigate pressure matches without a slip. Sinner has now matched it on clay in Rome — the only Masters 1000 tournament he has never won.

That convergence is not lost on anyone watching. Sinner could break Djokovic's record at the precise tournament where he has never held the title. A win over Rublev does not just extend the streak — it advances Sinner's Career Golden Masters quest, making him one match closer to becoming only the second player in history (after Djokovic) to hold all nine Masters titles simultaneously.

Rublev Aware of the Stakes

Rublev, who defeated Nikoloz Basilashvili to reach the quarterfinals, arrives knowing he is the obstacle between Sinner and a place in tennis history. The Russian is a former top-five player who has beaten Sinner before and is comfortable on clay. But Sinner leads their head-to-head record convincingly and has been near-flawless in his Rome campaign, conceding very few games in his earlier rounds.

Rublev, for his part, was philosophical in his pre-match interview. He acknowledged Sinner's dominance while noting that in a single match, anything is possible. It is the answer every player gives before facing Sinner in 2026 — because statistically, they are rarely wrong about the outcome.

The Foro Italico Factor

The atmosphere at the Foro Italico is a unique force in tennis. The Italian crowd is passionate, knowledgeable, and loud in ways that other Masters venues are not. When Sinner won his fifth consecutive title in Madrid two weeks ago, it was in front of a crowd respectful but detached. In Rome, on Italian soil, the stakes feel different.

The last Italian man to win the Italian Open was Adriano Panatta in 1976 — a 50-year wait that Sinner could end this week if he reaches and wins the final. The crowd does not need a scorecard to understand what is at stake. Every point Sinner wins is cheered not just for the point, but for what it represents.

What's Next

Should Sinner defeat Rublev, he will face either Luciano Darderi or Casper Ruud in the semifinals on Friday. Darderi — Italian-Argentinian and playing in front of his adopted home crowd — beat Rublev's compatriot to reach the semis, while Ruud also advanced Wednesday. The Italian Open final is scheduled for Sunday, May 17.

Beyond Rome, Roland Garros qualifying begins Sunday, May 18. The main draw starts May 24. Sinner, who has never won Roland Garros, arrives as the overwhelming men's favourite — and every win in Rome adds weight to that status. If Thursday becomes a record-breaking afternoon at the Foro Italico, it may also become the defining moment of Sinner's clay season before the French Open even begins.
