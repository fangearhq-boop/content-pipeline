Darderi Survives Smoke Suspension, Bagels Jodar in Third; Ruud Also Advances in Rome QF

By Elena Voss | Tennis Fanrecap | May 14, 2026

Rome, Italy

Wednesday night at the Foro Italico had a moment nobody expected: the tennis stopped because of football. Smoke from the Coppa Italia final, being played across the city at the Olimpico, drifted over Campo Centrale mid-match and forced a suspension in the quarterfinal between Luciano Darderi and Rafael Jodar. The players left the court and waited. When they returned, Darderi shut Jodar out of the third set entirely, winning 6-0 to claim his first career Masters 1000 semifinal.

Final score: Darderi def. Jodar 6-7, 7-5, 6-0. On the other court, Casper Ruud defeated Karen Khachanov 6-1, 1-6, 6-2 to set up a semifinal between the Norwegian and the Italian-Argentinian rising star.

Darderi's Comeback and the Smoke Break

The first set was tight. Both players traded chances. Jodar — 19 years old, already the youngest Rome quarterfinal player since Djokovic in 2007 — served and moved with the confidence that has made him one of the most exciting young Spaniards on the ATP tour this season. He claimed the first-set tiebreak and appeared to have the momentum.

Darderi steadied in the second set, creating the opportunities that define his game — mixing pace, spin, and angles in a way that can disrupt opponents who expect straightforward baseline exchanges. He won it 7-5, levelling the match at one set each.

Then came the interruption. Smoke had been visible in the distance during the second set as Rome's two major sporting events of the evening — the Foro Italico tennis and the Olimpico football — competed for the city's attention. When the smoke drifted directly over the court, play was suspended. It was a surreal scene: one of tennis's most prestigious outdoor venues halted by the aftermath of a football final a few kilometres away.

After play resumed, Jodar did not find his rhythm again. Darderi — who had been building through the second set — delivered a 6-0 third set, one of the most dominant closing performances of the tournament. Every game was clinical. Jodar, who had beaten No. 2 seed Alexander Zverev earlier in the week, had nothing left to give.

Darderi's Historic Night

Luciano Darderi, 23, was born in Buenos Aires and raised in Italy. He represents the azzurri — the Italian national identity — in a week when Italian tennis has had plenty to celebrate. His progression through the draw — including his earlier upset of Zverev — has made him one of the stories of the 2026 Italian Open alongside the Sinner record pursuit and Cirstea's extraordinary run.

The semifinal appearance is the deepest Darderi has gone at any Masters 1000 event. He faces Casper Ruud on Friday.

Ruud Survives Khachanov's Second-Set Surge

Casper Ruud's path through the quarterfinals was less dramatic but no less competitive. He took the first set against Karen Khachanov 6-1 with swift efficiency, then was pulled into a second set the Russian dominated, winning 6-1 in return. The third set went Ruud's way 6-2, a composed closing performance from the Norwegian who knows how to win on clay after reaching the Roland Garros final twice.

Ruud, who has been a consistent clay-court performer throughout his career, now faces Darderi — a younger player with home support and significant recent momentum. Both players know the other side of the draw contains either Sinner (if he beats Rublev in Thursday's quarterfinal) or the Rublev-quarterfinal winner. A title run from either Ruud or Darderi would be one of the more unlikely Rome stories in recent years.

Jodar Exits After Breakthrough Run

Rafael Jodar's Italian Open is over, but his week has been remarkable. The 19-year-old from Alcorcón reached the quarterfinals in his first Italian Open at the Foro Italico, becoming the youngest player to advance this far at the event since Novak Djokovic in 2007. He beat Alexander Zverev — the No. 2 seed — en route to the quarterfinals, a win that would represent a career highlight for most players at any age.

Jodar is one of the youngest players on the ATP tour and already has a clay title in 2026. His trajectory suggests Rome was not a one-off. The main draw of Roland Garros is 10 days away — a tournament where his age and clay ability may place him as one of the more interesting first-round picks in the draw.

What's Next

The ATP semifinals at the Italian Open take place Friday, May 15. Darderi faces Ruud in one half; the other semifinal features the winner of Thursday's Sinner-Rublev quarterfinal. The men's final is scheduled for Sunday, May 17. Roland Garros qualifying begins Sunday, May 18.
