Svitolina Saves 16 Break Points, Upsets Rybakina to Reach First Rome Semifinal Since 2018

By Marcus Cole | Tennis Fanrecap | May 14, 2026

Rome, Italy

The match should not have gone to three sets. It nearly did not go to a second. But Elina Svitolina has built a career on the art of surviving moments that should end her. In Rome on Wednesday, she survived 16 of them.

Svitolina defeated No. 2 seed Elena Rybakina 2-6, 6-4, 6-4 in the quarterfinals of the Internazionali BNL d'Italia, saving 16 break points across the match to advance to her first Rome semifinal since she won the title here in 2018. She faces Iga Swiatek in Thursday's semifinal — a matchup between two of the most accomplished clay-court players of their generation.

A First Set That Could Have Ended Everything

Rybakina opened the match with the kind of controlled aggression that has made her one of the most dangerous clay-court players on tour this year. She moved through the first set with authority, taking it 6-2 and appearing to be headed for a comfortable victory over a player who had not been seeded at Rome.

The second set changed everything. Svitolina — who has always played her best tennis in environments where others tighten — began finding her returns. Rybakina continued to create break opportunities, but they stopped converting. The 16 break points saved across the match tells the story: Rybakina was on Svitolina's serve repeatedly, but Svitolina's serve and net game held.

The second set went to Svitolina 6-4. The third followed the same pattern. Rybakina pressed, created chances, and Svitolina absorbed, redirected, and ultimately closed out the match 6-4 in two hours and 23 minutes.

The Art of Saving Break Points

Sixteen saved break points in a single match is an exceptional number. At the elite level of the WTA, a break point is rarely wasted — top players convert at high rates. Rybakina is one of the best servers on the women's tour, which means when she was returning, she was creating genuine pressure. Svitolina's ability to hold under that volume of sustained pressure speaks to something that statistics alone cannot fully measure.

Svitolina has been through significant periods of adversity in her career. She was ranked outside the top 100 for a stretch due to injury and the circumstances of her return to professional tennis. In Rome this week, she has played with a confidence and clarity that suggests a player who has found her footing again — not just in her tennis, but in her sense of herself as a competitor.

The 2018 Champion Returns to the Last Four

Svitolina won the Internazionali BNL d'Italia in 2018, defeating Maria Sharapova in the final. It remains one of the signature titles of her career and her most significant result on Roman clay. The Foro Italico suits her game — the surface plays slower than indoor hard courts, which rewards the patient, precise style she has always excelled at.

Her return to a Rome semifinal after eight years carries both sentiment and practical significance. She faces Swiatek — who has been resurgent under new coach Francisco Roig and demolished world No. 5 Jessica Pegula 6-1, 6-2 in Wednesday's quarterfinals — in what promises to be a compelling contrast of styles.

What's Next

Svitolina faces Swiatek in Thursday's WTA semifinal at the Foro Italico. Swiatek holds the overall head-to-head edge and arrives in considerable form, but Svitolina's performance against Rybakina suggests she is not here simply to make up the numbers. The winner of their semifinal meets either Coco Gauff or Sorana Cirstea in Saturday's final.

Beyond Rome, Roland Garros begins its main draw on May 24. Rybakina, who was considered one of the pre-tournament favourites to challenge at the French Open, now exits Rome without a semifinal appearance. Her clay preparation is not over — but Thursday's defeat will require reassessment. For Svitolina, the opposite is true: if she can win in Rome, she arrives in Paris with a title and a momentum that would make her one of the most interesting dark horses in the women's draw.
