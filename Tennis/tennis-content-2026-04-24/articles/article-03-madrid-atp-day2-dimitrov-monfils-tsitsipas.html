Madrid Day 2 ATP: Three Veterans, Three Very Different Outcomes

By Ryan Calloway | Tennis Fanrecap

April 24, 2026

Thursday's second round of the ATP draw at the Mutua Madrid Open delivered three contrasting stories involving some of the game's most recognisable veterans. Stefanos Tsitsipas clawed through in a three-set drama. Grigor Dimitrov extended a worrying losing streak. And Gael Monfils walked off a Madrid court for what might have been the last time.

Tsitsipas: Two Points From the Exit Door

Stefanos Tsitsipas arrived in Madrid after three consecutive defeats, his ranking having slipped considerably from its peak. A first-round assignment against Patrick Kypson should have been manageable. It nearly wasn't.

Tsitsipas dropped the first set 3-6, then found himself in a second-set tiebreak where he was two points away from losing the match and exiting before the third round. He held his nerve at the critical moment, eventually taking the tiebreak 8-6. The third set followed a similar tight pattern, with Tsitsipas winning 7-6(4) to take the match 3-6, 7-6(6), 7-6(4).

It was the kind of win that leaves a player equal parts relieved and honest with himself. The level was not what Tsitsipas once consistently produced, but a win is a win — and on clay at a Masters 1000, staying in the draw is always the first task. His losing streak, which had reached three matches, is now snapped.

Dimitrov: A Difficult Year Gets Harder

For Grigor Dimitrov, Madrid offered no respite. The Bulgarian fell 6-4, 6-4 to world No. 96 Adolfo Daniel Vallejo in straight sets. The loss was Dimitrov's sixth consecutive defeat of 2026, leaving his record at approximately 2-10 on the year.

It is a striking decline for a player who, in his prime, reached a career-high ranking of No. 3 in the world and was considered one of the most technically gifted players on tour. The groundstrokes that once seemed effortless now appear to be generating less pace and precision. His movement on clay, never his strongest surface, has not compensated.

Dimitrov is still just 30 years old, which in today's game is far from over. But if this stretch of form continues into the French Open preparation period, questions about whether he can return to the top tier of men's tennis will only grow louder.

Monfils: A Quiet Goodbye?

Gael Monfils received a wildcard into Madrid — a gesture of respect for one of the tour's most beloved entertainers. The 40-year-old, who turned professional in 2001 and has spent more than two decades delighting crowds around the world, stepped onto the court against Camilo Ugo Carabelli and lost 6-3, 6-4.

What stood out was that Monfils did not convert a single break point opportunity across the entire match. His athleticism, the quality that once made him one of the most exciting players in the world to watch, was less prominent than in his peak years. Ugo Carabelli, ranked No. 57, played a composed, consistent match and was not troubled.

Whether Monfils returns to Madrid remains to be seen. He is still competing, still training, still finding reasons to be on court. But for a player who grew up watching this tournament and has given so much to the sport, Thursday's exit carried an elegiac quality that did not go unnoticed.

What's Next

Tsitsipas advances to the third round and will face a tougher test — the draw will produce opponents with more firepower than Kypson. Dimitrov's early exit means he turns his attention to the clay swing events ahead, with the Italian Open in Rome potentially offering a reset. For Monfils, what comes next in his career may be more open than it has been in years. The torch in Madrid, and in men's tennis more broadly, continues to pass to a younger generation — represented this week by players like 19-year-old Rafael Jódar, already in the second round at his home tournament.
