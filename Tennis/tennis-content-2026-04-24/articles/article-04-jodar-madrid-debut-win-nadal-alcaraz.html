Jódar Joins Nadal and Alcaraz in Madrid History — A Home Win, 12 Kilometres From Home

By Elena Voss | Tennis Fanrecap

April 24, 2026

Twelve months ago, Rafael Jódar was ranked outside the world's top 650. On Wednesday afternoon, he walked onto a court at the Mutua Madrid Open — the tournament he grew up watching from roughly 12 kilometres away — and won a main draw match at a Masters 1000 event before his 20th birthday. He is now in the same sentence as Rafael Nadal and Carlos Alcaraz. The third Spaniard under 20 in history to do it at this event.

Jódar defeated Jesper de Jong 2-6, 7-5, 6-4 in a match that required everything he had. He lost the first set. He came back to win the next two. The crowd at the Caja Mágica — his crowd, in his city — was with him throughout.

A Rise That Defies Easy Explanation

The fastest way to understand Jódar's 2026 is to look at the numbers: 14 wins, 7 losses, one ATP title, one Masters 1000 second round (so far). His maiden tour-level title came in Marrakech earlier this month — a clay-court event on Morocco's red clay. He then reached the Barcelona semifinal the following week. Madrid this week is his third consecutive clay-court event, and he is not showing any signs of fatigue.

He arrived in Madrid at a career-high ranking of No. 42 in the PIF ATP Rankings. As recently as April 2025, he was outside the top 650. That 600-place rise in 12 months is the kind of trajectory that happens once in a decade in men's tennis.

His game is built around the qualities that define Spanish clay-court tennis: heavy topspin from both flanks, exceptional court coverage, the ability to grind through long rallies without losing focus. He has been described as having "inspiration from Nadal" and earning direct praise from Alcaraz — a notable endorsement from the player who, until his wrist injury last month, stood as the world No. 2.

What the Madrid Win Means

The significance of becoming the third Spaniard under 20 to win a main draw match at this tournament is worth pausing on. Nadal and Alcaraz are the only two players to have done it before. Both went on to become multi-Grand Slam champions and the centrepiece of Spanish tennis for their respective eras.

Jódar is not being compared to them in terms of what he will ultimately achieve — that would be premature. But the milestone itself confirms that he belongs in the conversation about the next generation of players capable of competing at the highest level on clay.

The Washington Post ran a piece this week headlined "A new 'Rafa' rises in Spain." The comparison carries pressure. Jódar has handled it with apparent ease. He gave a post-match interview shouting out Real Madrid footballer Jude Bellingham, who was watching in the stands — a small window into how connected he is to the culture of the city.

What's Next

Jódar faces No. 5 seed Alex de Minaur in the second round — a significantly more difficult assignment. De Minaur is a top-10 player with years of Masters 1000 experience and excellent movement. It will be the biggest match of Jódar's career so far. A win would push him close to the top 30 in live rankings and cement his status as not just a rising prospect but a genuine threat at Roland Garros in May. For the tournament, for Spanish tennis, and for anyone who appreciates the rare sight of a player ascending in real time, the Jódar story is one to follow very closely.
