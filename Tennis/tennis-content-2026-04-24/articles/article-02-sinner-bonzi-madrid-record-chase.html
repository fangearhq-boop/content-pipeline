Sinner's Madrid Mission: Five Consecutive Masters 1000 Titles Starts Against Bonzi

By Marcus Cole | Tennis Fanrecap

April 24, 2026

All of the context — the streak, the record, the expectations — comes down to a single afternoon on the Manolo Santana stadium. Jannik Sinner steps onto the Madrid clay for the first time in 2026 on Friday, and waiting across the net is Benjamin Bonzi: a French qualifier who has already won three matches this week to earn this chance.

The prize for Sinner is history. Five consecutive ATP Masters 1000 titles would be an Open Era first. No player has ever done it — not Roger Federer, not Rafael Nadal, not Novak Djokovic. Sinner already has four in a row: Paris, Indian Wells, Miami, Monte Carlo. Madrid is the fifth step.

Bonzi's Road to This Moment

Bonzi, ranked No. 104 in the PIF ATP Rankings, did not receive a direct entry into the Madrid main draw. He had to earn his way through qualifying, winning three rounds — including a 6-7(4), 7-6(4), 6-4 victory over compatriot Titouan Droguet in the final qualifying round. The Droguet match was a two-hour contest decided in the third set. By the time Bonzi walked into Friday's assignment, he had already played more tennis than Sinner this week.

Their head-to-head history gives Sinner the clear edge: 3-0 in career meetings. But two of those matches went to a deciding set. Bonzi is a capable clay-court competitor with the ball-striking and competitive instincts to stay in points long enough to create trouble. On the slow Madrid altitude clay, he is not a non-factor.

The State of the Record Chase

Sinner enters his Madrid debut on a 17-match winning streak, a run that began after a quarterfinal loss in Doha back in February. He swept the North American hard-court swing with back-to-back titles at Indian Wells and Miami — winning both finals without dropping a set. Then he won the Monte Carlo Masters on clay, defeating Carlos Alcaraz in the final.

Paris came before that, in November 2025, on indoor hard. The sequence spans three different surfaces across four continents and six months. It is one of the most consistent stretches of men's tennis in the Open Era.

Madrid is the most complicated test yet. Sinner has never won this title — his best results here include a handful of quarterfinal exits. The clay in Madrid, played at altitude, plays faster than Monte Carlo's traditional red clay and rewards more aggressive, shorter-point tennis. Sinner's game is well-suited to it, but the unfamiliarity with winning here adds a psychological element.

An Unusually Open Draw

The field Sinner faces has been significantly thinned by withdrawals. Carlos Alcaraz — his principal rival and the player most likely to extend him over a match — is sidelined with a wrist injury. Novak Djokovic is not in the draw. Taylor Fritz and Jack Draper also withdrew before the tournament. The result is a draw that, while still competitive, has fewer elite obstacles than normal.

Alexander Zverev is the No. 2 seed and represents the most credible threat. The German has won Madrid twice (2018 and 2021) and is a strong clay-court player. Casper Ruud is the defending champion. Arthur Fils, who won the Barcelona title earlier this month, is unseeded but in form. Young Spanish sensation Rafael Jódar has already won his opening match and is the crowd favourite.

But none of them are Alcaraz. And Sinner's draw has opened in his favour.

What's Next

If Sinner defeats Bonzi on Friday, he will face a third-round opponent from the opposite side of the lower half of the draw, likely on Sunday April 26 or Monday April 27. The quarterfinals are expected around April 28-29, with the final scheduled for Saturday May 2 or Sunday May 3. Five consecutive Masters 1000 titles would put Sinner in territory no player has ever occupied — and confirm this stretch as one of the great runs in the history of men's tennis.
