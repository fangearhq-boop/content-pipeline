The Channel Slam: What It Is, Who Has Done It, and What a Zverev Win Would Mean

By Elena Voss | Tennis Fanrecap

July 12, 2026

If Alexander Zverev defeats Jannik Sinner in the 2026 Wimbledon men's final on Sunday, he will complete the Channel Slam — winning the French Open and Wimbledon in the same calendar year. It is one of the rarest feats in tennis. In the Open Era, only six men have done it. Zverev could become the seventh.

What Is the Channel Slam?

The Channel Slam refers to winning the French Open and Wimbledon in the same year — back-to-back Grand Slam titles on opposite ends of the surface spectrum. Roland Garros is played on clay: slow, high-bouncing, physically grinding, rewarding consistency and topspin. Wimbledon is played on grass: fast, low-bouncing, favoring aggressive serve-and-volley tactics and flat ball-striking. The two tournaments take place roughly three weeks apart, in late May/early June and late June/early July respectively.

Winning one requires mastery of one surface. Winning both in the same season requires the ability to dominate two environments that demand almost opposite technical and tactical profiles. The transition window is short. The physical and mental demands of deep Grand Slam runs compound. Across the history of the Open Era, most of the sport's all-time greats have never completed the double.

The Six Men Who Have Done It

In the Open Era — which began in 1968 — six men have completed the Channel Slam. Rod Laver did it first in 1969, as part of his second career Grand Slam. Björn Borg accomplished it three times: 1978, 1979, and 1980, during the most dominant stretch of his career. Roger Federer completed it in 2009. Rafael Nadal in 2008. Novak Djokovic in 2021. And Carlos Alcaraz in 2024 — the most recent, and the performance that reset the benchmark for the modern era.

The list carries weight. Every name on it belongs to a player who either is, or is widely considered, among the greatest the sport has produced. The Channel Slam has historically functioned as a marker of all-time relevance — not just an elite achievement, but a signal that a player operates at the very top tier of the sport's historical record.

Zverev's Moment

Zverev won the 2026 French Open in May, claiming his first Grand Slam title at the age of 29 after years of deep runs that fell short at the final step. The victory at Roland Garros was the culmination of a career-long pursuit — and within weeks, he had won his way through to the Wimbledon final, where one more match separates him from completing the Channel Slam.

The ATP Tour has noted an additional layer to what a Zverev victory would represent. He won Roland Garros in May 2026 as his maiden Grand Slam title. If he wins Wimbledon today, he becomes the first man in the Open Era to win a second Grand Slam at the very next major after his first. The two achievements — completing the Channel Slam and winning consecutive Slams to open an account — would arrive simultaneously.

His opponent is Jannik Sinner, the defending champion and world No. 1. Sinner is one of the best players in the world on any surface, and has been dominant throughout this fortnight. But Zverev has arrived at this final in the form of his career, and the Channel Slam has always required something beyond ordinary excellence.

What's Next
