Noskova Wins Wimbledon 6-2, 5-7, 6-3 — Youngest Champion Since Kvitova in 2011

By Elena Voss | Tennis Fanrecap

July 12, 2026

Linda Noskova is the 2026 Wimbledon women's champion. The 21-year-old Czech, seeded No. 9, defeated compatriot Karolina Muchova 6-2, 5-7, 6-3 on Saturday in just under two and a half hours — winning the first all-Czech Grand Slam final in history and becoming the youngest Wimbledon women's champion since Petra Kvitova in 2011.

A Dominant Start, Then a Fight

Noskova came out of the gates with precision and power. She won the first set 6-2 and led 5-2 in the second, where she appeared to be on course to close the match in straight sets. Then Muchova — who had reached the final by saving a match point in the semifinals against Coco Gauff — found another gear.

Muchova saved five match points in the second set, clawing all the way back from that 5-2 deficit to take it 7-5. Centre Court erupted. The match had changed entirely. Muchova, the veteran of finals pressure — she reached the 2023 French Open final — had found a way back in.

But Noskova did not waver. She won the third set 6-3 to claim the title and end the afternoon's extraordinary drama. Petra Kvitova, who won Wimbledon in 2011 as the last woman younger than Noskova to claim the title, watched it all from the Royal Box.

A Record-Setting Performance

At 21 years and seeded No. 9, Noskova's triumph continues an extraordinary chapter for Czech women's tennis. Marketa Vondrousova won Wimbledon in 2023, Barbora Krejcikova won it in 2024, and now Noskova in 2026. Czech women have claimed three of the last four Wimbledon women's titles. Noskova herself was playing in her first Grand Slam final.

The all-Czech nature of the final was its own piece of history. Noskova and Muchova, both from the small Czech city of Přerov, became the first two players of the same nationality to contest a women's Grand Slam singles final since Venus and Serena Williams met at Wimbledon in 2009. No all-Czech Grand Slam final — men's or women's — had ever been played before Saturday.

Noskova, in her on-court speech, acknowledged the weight of what she and Muchova had accomplished together. "I think we made history today," she said. "I believe that all our Czech fans at home are proud of us, so no matter the result today, I think it was a good day for both of us."

A Champion's Composure

The manner of the victory was perhaps as significant as the result. When Muchova saved those five match points and completed the second-set comeback, the psychological pressure on a 21-year-old in her first Grand Slam final was enormous. Noskova's response — a clean 6-3 third set — was the work of a player who had found something within herself that the scoresheet could not fully reflect.

In her victory speech, Noskova spoke of a journey that had demanded everything. "I have been enjoying these two weeks so much, through the sad tears and the happy tears, all the sweat and blood I put into this," she said. She also addressed Muchova directly: "Karolina, you really made me work for it. I'm so glad that I could play my first Grand Slam final with you."

What's Next

With her Wimbledon title secured, Noskova will climb to a career-high ranking and enter the North American hard-court swing as one of the sport's top names. Muchova, who showed remarkable resilience throughout this fortnight, returns to the tour after a finalist's performance that will have re-established her credentials at the highest level. The men's final between Jannik Sinner and Alexander Zverev follows on Centre Court on Sunday, July 12, not before 4:00 PM BST (11:00 AM ET).
