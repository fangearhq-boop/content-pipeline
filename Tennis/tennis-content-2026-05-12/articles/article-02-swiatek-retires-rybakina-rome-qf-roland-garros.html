Swiatek Retires Against Rybakina in Rome QF — Roland Garros Alarm Bells Ring

By Marcus Cole | May 12, 2026 | Italian Open, Rome

Iga Swiatek will not win the 2026 Internazionali BNL d'Italia. She won't reach the semifinals, or the final, or add another Rome title to her collection. Instead, she retired from her quarterfinal against Elena Rybakina at 2-2 in the deciding third set, leaving the Foro Italico under a cloud of concern with Roland Garros qualifying beginning in just six days.

The score read Rybakina 2-6, 7-6(3), 2-2 (ret.) — a match that Swiatek had won the opening set handily before Rybakina fought back to take the second in a tiebreak. Whatever prompted Swiatek to stop, it was enough to end her afternoon before the third set could produce a conclusion. The exact nature of her withdrawal was not immediately specified, though the timing — less than two weeks before Roland Garros — makes it the story of the day.

This is now the second consecutive tournament where Swiatek has exited without reaching the final. She was already dealing with the emotional disruption of Francisco Roig — her new coach and the man who guided Rafael Nadal for 17 years — tearing his Achilles courtside during the week. Now a retirement in the quarterfinals. The four-time Roland Garros champion enters Paris under genuine uncertainty for the first time in years.

Rybakina Rising

Elena Rybakina accepts her Rome semifinal berth with the calm efficiency of someone who has been building toward this moment all clay season. The world No. 2 from Kazakhstan has been one of the most consistent performers on the WTA Tour in 2026, and her record against the top players in the field speaks for itself.

On clay specifically, Rybakina has always been a more dangerous player than her surface ranking suggests. Her flat, heavy groundstrokes do not spin off clay courts the way a traditional clay specialist's shots do — they punch through low and hard, disrupting opponents who expect the high-bouncing exchanges the surface typically produces. Against Swiatek, that style creates genuine problems even at full health.

With Aryna Sabalenka already eliminated by Sorana Cirstea earlier in the week, and now Swiatek gone, Rybakina is the highest-ranked remaining WTA player in Rome. She has a clear path to a title — and a statement that carries significant weight heading into the French Open.

The Roland Garros Picture

Six days. That is how much time stands between Swiatek's Rome retirement and the start of Roland Garros qualifying. The main draw begins May 25. Swiatek has reached the Roland Garros final in four of the last five years, winning the title four times. She is the defining player of this generation on the Parisian clay.

But she is not invincible. Her form in 2026 has been more fragile than her dominant record suggested it might be, and a retirement in Rome — whatever the cause — raises questions that only she and her team can answer in the days that follow. If Swiatek is healthy in Paris, she remains among the most dangerous players in the draw. If she arrives compromised, the women's draw becomes anyone's tournament.

Coco Gauff defends her 2025 Roland Garros title. Rybakina enters in form. Mirra Andreeva, Jelena Ostapenko, and Sorana Cirstea are all still standing in Rome and making statements. The women's French Open draw has not looked this uncertain in a very long time.

What's Next

Rybakina advances to the Rome semifinals, where she will face the winner of Tuesday's other quarterfinal between Coco Gauff and Mirra Andreeva. Swiatek's team is expected to provide an update on her condition in the coming days. Roland Garros qualifying begins May 18; the main draw is May 25.
