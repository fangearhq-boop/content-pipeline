Sinner vs. Pellegrino: One Win Closer to the Career Golden Masters

By Elena Voss | May 12, 2026 | Italian Open, Rome

Jannik Sinner walks onto Campo Centrale on Tuesday afternoon carrying a number that almost defies comprehension: 25-0. That is his Masters 1000 record in 2026 — no losses, no blemishes, no close calls that mattered. His opponent is Italian wildcard Andrea Pellegrino, ranked No. 155 in the world, who defeated Frances Tiafoe in the third round to set up this meeting. No one gives Pellegrino much of a chance. But Sinner has turned every appearance at this tournament into something bigger than just a match, and today is no different.

The prize on the line is history. Sinner has won eight of the nine Masters 1000 events on tour. Rome — the Internazionali BNL d'Italia, played on the red clay of the Foro Italico — is the only one he has never claimed. Win this tournament and he becomes only the second man ever to complete the Career Golden Masters, joining Novak Djokovic on one of tennis's most exclusive lists.

Before worrying about that milestone, Sinner must first handle Pellegrino — and then likely face Casper Ruud in the quarterfinals. Ruud crushed Lorenzo Musetti 6-3, 6-1 in the morning session, with Musetti forced to take a medical timeout for a left thigh issue. Ruud has yet to drop a set at this tournament. Neither has Sinner.

The Numbers Behind the Dominance

Sinner's 30-match Masters 1000 winning streak — a run that stretches back to last year — ties for second in series history, behind only Djokovic's all-time record. He arrived in Rome having already won five consecutive Masters 1000 titles: Paris, Indian Wells, Miami, Monte Carlo, and Madrid. That run of five straight is itself an Open Era first, something no player had ever done before Sinner achieved it in April.

The 2026 season has been a masterclass in dominance. Sinner entered Rome 25-0 in Masters 1000 play, a figure that has no parallel in the modern era. His opponents have found no weakness in his game — not on serve, not on return, not in the tiebreaks. He has absorbed pressure on the biggest stages and answered with clean tennis.

Pellegrino represents a different kind of test. The 31-year-old Italian wildcard has spent most of his career outside the top 100, grinding through qualifying draws and Challenger events. He has never been ranked inside the top 60. Against Tiafoe in the third round, he played the match of his life. Against Sinner, the crowd will chant for him — and he will almost certainly lose.

Home Soil, Enormous Pressure

There is something layered about watching Sinner play Rome. He is Italy's No. 1 player, the country's most celebrated sports figure, a man who has delivered titles at every other major stop on the Masters calendar. Yet Rome has always been the one that got away. The last Italian man to win the Rome title was Adriano Panatta in 1976 — half a century ago. The crowd at the Foro Italico does not let Sinner forget.

None of that pressure appeared to affect him last Monday against Alexei Popyrin. Sinner dispatched Popyrin 6-2, 6-0 in 65 minutes, losing just two games in a performance that was closer to a practice session than a competitive match. He won 25 of his 34 first-serve points and did not face a single break point. Pellegrino will need to be significantly more threatening to extend the match.

What's Next

A victory over Pellegrino advances Sinner into the Rome quarterfinals, where Casper Ruud awaits. Ruud is in fine form and has been one of the most consistent players on clay this spring, but he has never beaten Sinner at a Masters 1000 event. The semifinal and final would follow on Thursday and Saturday, with the Career Golden Masters title in reach if Sinner keeps winning.

The match against Pellegrino is scheduled for Campo Centrale at 3:00 PM CEST. Results will follow shortly after.
