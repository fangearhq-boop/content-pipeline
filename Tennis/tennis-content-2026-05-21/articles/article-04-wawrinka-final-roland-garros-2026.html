Wawrinka's Final Roland Garros: "There Will Be a Lot of Emotion"

By Elena Voss | Tennis Fanrecap | May 21, 2026

Stan Wawrinka lost his final match at the Geneva Open this week — 7-6, 7-6 to rising American Alex Michelsen in a second-round tiebreak battle that sent the home crowd into a prolonged standing ovation. When the match ended, career highlights played on the big screen at the Parc des Eaux-Vives. The crowd roared. The display ended with two words: "Merci Stan."

Now Wawrinka turns his attention to Paris. His 21st Roland Garros. His last.

"There will be a lot of emotion because it's Paris and Roland-Garros," the 41-year-old Swiss veteran said ahead of his departure for France. "I grew up with Roland-Garros, it's part of me. I hope I can win a few matches and above all enjoy the crowd."

The Man Who Conquered Paris

Wawrinka's record at Roland Garros is defined by a single afternoon in June 2015 that stands as one of the most stunning performances in Grand Slam history. He faced Novak Djokovic in the final — a player who had won the Australian Open, Roland Garros, Wimbledon, and the US Open multiple times, and was at the peak of his powers. Wawrinka won 4-6, 6-4, 6-3, 6-4. The clay was his that day in a way it rarely belongs to any player.

That title joined the 2014 Australian Open and 2016 US Open in a collection of three Grand Slam trophies — each one won in circumstances that defied expectation, against the best players in the world, on days when Wawrinka was simply better than everyone else on the planet. His backhand, regarded by many as the finest single-handed backhand of his generation, produced some of the most beautiful shots ever struck at Roland Garros.

A Career Defined by Resilience

Wawrinka's career was never straightforward. He played through multiple knee surgeries that would have ended lesser careers, rebuilt his ranking from outside the top 200, and returned to competitive tennis at an age when most professionals have retired. He received the wild card for this final Roland Garros from tournament organisers who recognised that the 2015 champion deserved one last run on the clay he conquered.

Currently ranked No. 125, Wawrinka approaches this tournament with no expectation of winning five matches. The draw at 41, against opponents half his age, makes a deep run objectively unlikely. But no one who has watched Wawrinka's career would write off the possibility of a moment — a match, a set, an afternoon on Chatrier — that reminds everyone why he was one of the greatest clay-court players of his era.

One Last Farewell

Wawrinka will be honoured after his final match at Roland Garros — a ceremony that has also been planned for Gael Monfils, who plays his farewell event on Chatrier tonight. The two players represent different aspects of French Open tennis: Monfils the showman who lit up the court; Wawrinka the foreign champion who made the French Open his own most cherished achievement.

Both are in their last year. Both will hear crowds they have earned through careers spent at the highest level. For Wawrinka, the emotion he speaks of is not just about tennis. Roland Garros is the tournament that gave him his greatest moment. He is going back to say thank you.

What's Next

Wawrinka is in the main draw on a wild card and will play in the first round beginning Sunday, May 24. His first-round opponent will be released with the order of play. A post-match farewell ceremony is planned for after his final match at the tournament. The 2026 Roland Garros is expected to be the last Grand Slam of his career.
