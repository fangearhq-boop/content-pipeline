### STORY 1: Qualifying Final — Stephens Qualifies; Goffin Bids Farewell

**Long-Form Post**
Window: 8:00 AM ET

Two stories from the final day of Roland Garros qualifying — one that ends with a championship and one that ends with a speech.

David Goffin walked onto the qualifying court today for what turned out to be the last match of his Roland Garros career. The 35-year-old Belgian, who announced 2026 as his final year on tour, lost to Britain's Toby Samuel 5-7, 6-3, 6-3 in the final qualifying round. When it was over, he stayed on court and addressed the crowd on Suzanne-Lenglen with the grace that has defined his entire career.

"It's pure joy, nothing but emotion with some magnificent moments," Goffin said. "I've always been welcomed here like one of yours, like a champion, like a prince."

Nearly 15 years. 14 main draw appearances. Goffin's farewell to Roland Garros deserved this moment.

On the other side of the day's results: Sloane Stephens is back in the main draw. The 2017 US Open champion came back from dropping the first set against Spain's Leyre Romero Gormaz to secure her 14th Roland Garros main draw appearance. At 33, Stephens is 35-13 lifetime in Paris. She belongs here — and she proved it again today.

Bianca Andreescu and Karolina Pliskova were both eliminated in Wednesday's second qualifying round. The field for the 2026 main draw is now complete.

Main draw begins Sunday, May 24. Draw ceremony at 2 PM Paris today.

### STORY 4: Wawrinka's Final Roland Garros

**Long-Form Post**
Window: 9:00 PM ET

Stan Wawrinka is coming home one last time.

The 41-year-old Swiss legend lost in the second round of the Geneva Open to Alex Michelsen 7-6, 7-6 this week — an emotional farewell to a home tournament where he won back-to-back titles in 2016 and 2017. When the match ended, career highlights played on the big screen at the Parc des Eaux-Vives. The crowd gave him the send-off he deserved. The screen read: "Merci Stan."

Now he shifts focus to Paris — and the 21st and final Roland Garros of his career.

Wawrinka won the French Open in 2015, defeating Novak Djokovic in the final in one of the greatest upsets in Grand Slam history. He also holds the 2014 Australian Open and 2016 US Open titles. Three Grand Slams, three jaw-dropping performances, and a legacy that will endure long past the last ball he strikes on Tour.

"There will be a lot of emotion because it's Paris and Roland-Garros," Wawrinka said. "I grew up with Roland-Garros, it's part of me. I hope I can win a few matches and above all enjoy the crowd."

He receives a wild card. He will be honoured after his final match.

Some careers end with records. Wawrinka's ends with something rarer: a crowd that never stopped believing in him.
