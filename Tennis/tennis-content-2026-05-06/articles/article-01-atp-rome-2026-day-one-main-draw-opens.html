ATP Rome 2026: Main Draw Opens at the Foro Italico as First-Round Action Gets Underway

By Marcus Cole | May 6, 2026

The 82nd edition of the Internazionali BNL d'Italia has officially launched — and the Roman clay is ready for what could be the most historic edition in years.

The ATP main draw at the 2026 Internazionali BNL d'Italia began Wednesday at the Foro Italico in Rome, opening the first of ten days of men's tennis competition at one of the sport's most storied venues. The clay courts of the Stadio Centrale and surrounding show courts were ready for action, offering prize money of €7,135,905 across the 82nd edition of a tournament that has been played in Rome since 1930.

Wednesday's first-round matches set up the second-round entries for the seeded players, who each receive a bye in the opening round as per standard Masters 1000 format. Among the first-round matches on the schedule: Hubert Hurkacz vs. Yannick Hanfmann, Sebastian Baez vs. Jared Brooksby, and Pablo Carreño Busta vs. Alejandro Tabilo. The outcomes of these matches will determine who the seeds face when they enter the draw over the coming days.

What the Seeds Are Waiting For

World No. 1 Jannik Sinner holds the top seed in the draw and receives a bye through the first round. He will play his opening match in the second round against either Alex Michelsen or Sebastian Ofner — whoever wins their first-round contest. For Sinner, this tournament carries the weight of history: a victory in Rome would complete the Career Golden Masters, making him only the second player after Novak Djokovic to win all nine Masters 1000 events.

Djokovic himself enters as the No. 7 seed and will play his second-round opener against the winner of Marton Fucsovics vs. qualifier Dino Prizmic. Fucsovics is ranked No. 57 and carries a 0-2 clay record in 2026 coming into Rome. Prizmic, the qualifier, has been in considerably stronger form: 23-8 on the season and 16-5 on clay. Djokovic's path through the bottom half of the draw — which also includes Alexander Zverev at No. 2 — shapes up as a genuine test even before the later rounds.

The draw has Sinner in the top half and Zverev and Djokovic in the bottom. A projected final between Sinner and Zverev is entirely plausible, though the clay season has a habit of producing surprises that make projection an imprecise science.

The Stakes in Rome

This edition of the Italian Open arrives at a remarkable moment in men's tennis. Carlos Alcaraz, the two-time defending Roland Garros champion, has already withdrawn from both Rome and the French Open with a right wrist injury. His absence reshapes the entire clay season narrative, and it places Sinner in an almost unprecedented position: the dominant No. 1, on home clay, with history available if he can simply win the one tournament he has never won.

For Djokovic, the tournament is the opposite kind of pressure — not a milestone chase, but a fitness test. He has not competed since suffering a right shoulder injury at Indian Wells in March. Rome is his first match since then, and every win here builds his case for appearing at Roland Garros, where he is a three-time champion. The 38-year-old has said his goal is to "be ready at least for Roland Garros." Rome is where that preparation happens.

The WTA field is running alongside the ATP draw, with women's first-round action having begun May 5. The shared venue at the Foro Italico means that this week, Rome belongs to all of tennis — and the city that has hosted this tournament for nearly a century is hosting its most consequential edition in some time.

What's Next

The ATP second round begins Thursday, when the seeded players enter the draw for the first time. Sinner's match — against Michelsen or Ofner — is expected on Thursday or Friday. Djokovic's second-round contest follows shortly after the Fucsovics-Prizmic first-round result is confirmed. The tournament runs through May 17, when the men's final will be played on the Stadio Centrale. Roland Garros qualifying begins May 18, making Rome the last major test for every player in the field before Paris. The pressure, the history, and the occasion are all present. The tennis begins now.
