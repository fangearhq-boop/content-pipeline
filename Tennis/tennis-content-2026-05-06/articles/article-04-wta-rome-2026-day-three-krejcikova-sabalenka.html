WTA Rome Day 3: Krejcikova Sets Up Sabalenka Clash as Top Seeds Enter the Draw

By Elena Voss | May 6, 2026

The Italian Open's women's draw is sharpening quickly. Barbora Krejcikova is through and will face world No. 1 Aryna Sabalenka — who has never won in Rome.

Three days into the 2026 WTA Italian Open, the draw is beginning to show its teeth. Barbora Krejcikova, the 2021 Roland Garros champion, defeated Elsa Jacquemot 6-2, 6-4 on Tuesday to advance to the round of 32. Her reward is a second-round match against world No. 1 and top seed Aryna Sabalenka — a match that carries significant stakes for both players, though for very different reasons.

For Krejcikova, it is an opportunity to announce herself as a player capable of disrupting the top of the draw in Rome. She is one of the more technically gifted clay-court players in the WTA field — her Roland Garros title stands as evidence of that — and Sabalenka's flat, aggressive game can be susceptible to heavy-spinning clay-court retrievers with variety. Krejcikova will need to play near her best to threaten the world No. 1, but the matchup is not one-sided.

The Sabalenka Italian Open Quest

For Aryna Sabalenka, this week in Rome represents something specific: the Italian Open is one of the few major titles still missing from her resume. She arrives as the world's top-ranked player, having posted a dominant start to the 2026 season. She has never won the Internazionali BNL d'Italia, and the draw — filled with clay-court specialists and a defending champion in Jasmine Paolini — is not offering her an easy route to the trophy.

Sabalenka's first competitive match of the Rome tournament will come against Krejcikova, and how that match unfolds will be an early indicator of her clay-court sharpness. The Belarusian's game — built around heavy, flat groundstrokes and an aggressive serving pattern — translates well to clay when she is moving forward and dictating. On days when she is defending from deep and absorbing pace, clay-court specialists can create problems.

Seeds With Byes, Bracket Taking Shape

With the top 32 seeds receiving first-round byes in the WTA 1000 format, the tournament's top names are only now entering the draw as Day 3 progresses. Elena Rybakina (No. 2), Coco Gauff (No. 3), and Iga Swiatek (No. 4) are among the players who have not yet played and are preparing for their second-round opponents to be decided by the ongoing first-round matches.

Defending champion Jasmine Paolini enters the draw as the 9th seed, carrying both the weight of a title to defend and the energy of home crowd support at the Foro Italico. The Italian won here last year, defeating Gauff in the final to claim the biggest clay-court victory of her career. The draw has placed Gauff and Paolini in the same quarter — setting up a potential quarterfinal that would be a direct rematch of that 2025 final.

Mirra Andreeva, the 8th seed who reached the Madrid final before falling to Marta Kostyuk, is also in that quarter of the draw. The bottom half of the WTA Rome bracket may prove to be the most competitive section of the tournament.

What's Next

The WTA Italian Open runs through May 16, when the women's final takes place on the Stadio Centrale at the Foro Italico. Second-round matches for the seeded players are scheduled to begin as Wednesday and Thursday progress, with the draw's first major encounters including Sabalenka vs. Krejcikova. Roland Garros qualifying begins May 18 — just two days after the Rome women's final — meaning players have almost no margin between these two major clay-court events. The performance level and physical condition of the top seeds over the next ten days in Rome will have a direct bearing on what happens in Paris when the French Open main draw opens May 25.
