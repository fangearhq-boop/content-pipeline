Djokovic Returns to Rome: Six-Time Champion's Comeback Begins on Familiar Clay

By Ryan Calloway | May 6, 2026

Novak Djokovic missed four consecutive Masters 1000 tournaments with a shoulder injury. He chose the Foro Italico — where he has won six titles — for his return to competition.

When Novak Djokovic arrives at the Foro Italico this week, it will be his first competitive match since suffering a right shoulder injury at Indian Wells in late March. The injury forced him to withdraw from four consecutive Masters 1000 events — Miami, Monte-Carlo, Madrid, and originally Rome before he confirmed his entry — representing one of the longer injury absences of his career in the clay season era.

That he chose Rome as the venue for his return is not a coincidence. Djokovic has won the Italian Open six times. He knows the clay at the Foro Italico — its bounce, its pace, its particular demands on the sliding baseline game — better than almost any player who has ever competed here. Coming back on familiar ground, in a city where the crowd is enthusiastic for his opponent (the Italian fans will back Sinner loudly) but respectful of his legacy, gives him the best possible footing for a competitive return.

The Injury and the Absence

The right shoulder injury at Indian Wells was serious enough to sideline Djokovic for over six weeks. He missed the Miami Open — a tournament he has won six times — then sat out Monte-Carlo and Madrid. The length of the absence drew attention across the sport. At 38 years old, every significant injury period raises questions about the timeline of the comeback and the level of performance when it arrives.

Djokovic has been seen hitting practice sessions at the Foro Italico ahead of the main draw. Those sessions were described by ATP sources as positive. Whether he is at full competitive fitness — at the level required to make a deep run at a Masters 1000 event — is a question that only match play can answer. The first round of that answer comes in the second round, when he faces the winner of Marton Fucsovics against qualifier Dino Prizmic.

Fucsovics enters the first-round match ranked No. 57 with a 0-2 clay record in 2026. Prizmic, the qualifier, has been in strong form on clay at 16-5 on the surface this year. Neither opponent would be considered a routine match for a player who has not competed in weeks, particularly one recovering from an upper-body injury that directly affects serving mechanics.

The Roland Garros Target

Djokovic has been open about his priorities for this stretch of the season. Roland Garros — where he is a three-time champion — is the primary objective. He told media before Rome: "At least for Roland Garros, I'm ready." The conditional framing matters. Rome is not the destination; it is the preparation. Every match Djokovic wins here builds his match sharpness. Every time he serves under pressure, runs down a drop shot, stays focused through a tiebreak, he is banking competitive confidence for Paris.

The timing cuts close. Roland Garros qualifying begins May 18. The main draw starts May 25. There are less than three weeks between Djokovic's first match in Rome and the opening Sunday of the French Open. If he exits Rome early, he may have time for a brief practice block before Paris. If he makes a deep run, the matches themselves serve as preparation. Either outcome is workable. What would not be workable is another injury.

Djokovic's draw puts him in the bottom half with Alexander Zverev at No. 2, which means a potential semifinal against the German before any possible final with Sinner. Lorenzo Musetti, the Italian 8th seed who would have enormous home crowd support, is also projected into Djokovic's quarter. The path is not easy — but Djokovic's six Rome titles speak to how well he navigates this particular court.

What's Next

Djokovic enters the main draw in the second round, playing his first match once the Fucsovics-Prizmic first-round result is confirmed, likely on Thursday or Friday. His progress through the draw will be one of the most closely watched stories of the Rome tournament. A Djokovic who can move freely and serve without shoulder restriction is a genuine title contender — six Rome titles do not lie about a player's relationship with this tournament. A Djokovic who is clearly not yet at full fitness will need to balance Rome points against physical preservation for Roland Garros. The answer to which version shows up will come quickly on the red clay of the Foro Italico.
