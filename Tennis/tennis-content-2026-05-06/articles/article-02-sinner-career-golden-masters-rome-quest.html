The Last Piece: Sinner's Career Golden Masters Quest Reaches Home Clay in Rome

By Elena Voss | May 6, 2026

Jannik Sinner has won eight of nine Masters 1000 events. The only one left is the Italian Open — and it starts today in Rome, where the crowd will be deafening from the first ball.

There is a concept in tennis called the Career Golden Masters. It means winning every one of the nine Masters 1000 events — the tier of tournaments that sits just below the Grand Slams in prestige and point value. There are nine of them: Indian Wells, Miami, Monte-Carlo, Madrid, Rome, Toronto/Montreal, Cincinnati, Shanghai, and Paris. They span four continents and three surfaces. To win them all requires excellence over years, across varying conditions, on hard courts and clay and everything in between.

Only one man has ever done it: Novak Djokovic. Now Jannik Sinner has arrived at the Foro Italico in Rome needing exactly one more title to join him.

Sinner has won eight of the nine Masters 1000 events. He is also just the third player to reach that number, alongside Djokovic and Roger Federer. The tournament he has never won — the one that holds the final piece of the puzzle — is the Internazionali BNL d'Italia, which opened its 2026 edition on Wednesday in Rome. On home soil. In front of his compatriots.

Five in a Row and Counting

The context that surrounds Sinner's pursuit makes it even more extraordinary. He arrives in Rome not as a player chasing redemption or returning from injury, but as the most dominant force in men's tennis at this moment. His last five Masters 1000 tournaments have all ended with him lifting the trophy: Paris 2025, Indian Wells 2026, Miami 2026, Monte-Carlo 2026, and Madrid 2026. That run of five consecutive Masters 1000 titles is an Open Era first. No player — not Federer, not Nadal, not Djokovic — had ever done it before Sinner won Madrid four days ago.

The streak now stands at five, and Rome is where he tries to extend it to six while simultaneously closing out history. The dual nature of the achievement — record-extending streak plus Career Golden Masters completion — gives these two weeks at the Foro Italico a weight that is difficult to overstate.

What Rome Has Been

Sinner's Rome history is not without its promising moments. He reached the final at the Foro Italico in 2025, giving clear evidence that the city's clay courts suit his game. But he fell short in that final, unable to close out the last title in his collection. That near-miss adds another layer to the 2026 campaign: Sinner has been close before, and this year, on a five-title streak with the home crowd at full volume behind him, the opportunity feels different.

Rome's clay plays slower than Madrid, heavier than Monte-Carlo. The Foro Italico is a stadium steeped in history — it hosted the Rome Olympics in 1960 and has been the site of some of the most memorable clay-court battles in the sport's modern era. For an Italian player, there is no louder welcome anywhere on tour. Sinner will hear his name from the first serve.

The Djokovic Standard

The man who sets the benchmark for this achievement is also in the field this week. Novak Djokovic has won the Italian Open six times and holds the Career Golden Masters in full. His presence in Rome — returning from a shoulder injury for his first tournament since Indian Wells — adds a historical dimension to the week. Djokovic knows exactly what completing the Golden Masters means, having lived it. If Sinner is to equal that achievement, he must navigate a draw that includes Djokovic in the opposite half as a potential final opponent.

Sinner will enter the second round against either Alex Michelsen or Sebastian Ofner — the winner of their first-round match. From there, the path narrows toward a potential final that could bring him face-to-face with one of the titans who defined the era Sinner is now superseding.

What's Next

The Italian Open runs through May 17, with the men's final scheduled for that afternoon. Sinner enters in the second round, likely playing his first match Thursday or Friday. Every round he advances brings the history closer. Every match he wins on Italian clay is another step toward the achievement that only Djokovic has made before him. Should Sinner win the title, he would complete the Career Golden Masters on home soil in front of the fans who have followed his rise from a young clay-court talent from San Candido to the most dominant player in the world. After Rome, Roland Garros begins May 25 — where another piece of history, the Career Grand Slam, would be available if Sinner adds French Open glory to his collection. The clay season has never meant more.
