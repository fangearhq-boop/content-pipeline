Roland Garros 2026: The Men's Field Without Alcaraz — 12 Days to Qualifying

By Marcus Cole | May 6, 2026

Carlos Alcaraz will not defend his back-to-back Roland Garros titles. His wrist injury has reshaped the entire men's field — and put Jannik Sinner in a position to make history in Paris.

Roland Garros qualifying begins in 12 days, on May 18. The main draw opens May 25. The men's final is scheduled for June 7. And for the first time in three years, Carlos Alcaraz will not be competing at the tournament he has won in each of the past two editions.

Alcaraz, the two-time defending Roland Garros champion, announced in late April that a right wrist injury would prevent him from competing at both the Italian Open in Rome and the French Open in Paris. The diagnosis — tendon inflammation combined with cartilage damage — ruled him out until at least June. His statement was direct: "We have decided the most prudent thing to do is to be cautious and not participate in Rome or Roland-Garros as we wait to evaluate the progress."

The 2025 Roland Garros final between Alcaraz and Sinner was one of the great finals in the tournament's history — Alcaraz saved three championship points and won in five sets. That match will not be replicated in 2026. The two principals are separated by circumstance: one missing through injury, the other arriving as the clear favorite to lift the Coupe des Mousquetaires for the first time.

Sinner: The Overwhelming Favorite

Jannik Sinner enters Roland Garros 2026 as the overwhelming favorite, a designation that would have seemed implausible before Alcaraz's withdrawal. The Italian world No. 1 — currently in Rome chasing the Career Golden Masters — has won five consecutive Masters 1000 titles and is playing the best tennis of his career. Roland Garros is the only Grand Slam title missing from his collection. Should he win in Paris, Sinner would complete his Career Grand Slam.

The challenge of Roland Garros is that it rewards not just baseline power but clay-court patience, tactical variation, and the ability to construct points over longer rallies than any other Grand Slam surface demands. Sinner's game — driven by heavy topspin from the backhand, flat power from the forehand, and exceptional court coverage — translates well to Paris clay. He has reached deep in the tournament previously and lost matches he arguably could have won. This year, without Alcaraz, the bracket is more open than it has been in years.

The Other Contenders

Alexander Zverev is the strongest challenger in the men's draw. The German was the 2024 Roland Garros finalist, losing in four sets to Alcaraz. He is a natural clay-court player whose consistency from the baseline and high serve percentage make him a genuine title threat over the fortnight in Paris. Currently in Rome where he is seeded No. 2, Zverev will arrive at Roland Garros with match sharpness if he goes deep in the Italian capital.

Novak Djokovic's participation at Roland Garros remains contingent on his performance and health in Rome this week. The three-time Roland Garros champion has missed four consecutive Masters 1000 events with a shoulder injury and returned to competition Wednesday at the Foro Italico. If Djokovic is fit and moving well in Rome, his Roland Garros entry is a near-certainty. If the injury limits him further, Paris may come too soon. The Roland Garros entry list includes his name; the question is the body.

Daniil Medvedev remains a perennial finalist threat and a player whose game has improved significantly on clay in recent seasons. Lorenzo Musetti, the Italian No. 8 seed in Rome who will have enormous home crowd support, is also a genuine clay-court contender capable of causing trouble in any draw.

The WTA Picture

On the women's side, Coco Gauff defends her 2025 Roland Garros title. Gauff won in Paris last year on the clay that suits her powerful forehand and exceptional movement, and she arrives in Rome as the No. 3 seed still searching for her best clay-court form of the 2026 season. Her likely challengers in Paris include Aryna Sabalenka (No. 1), Elena Rybakina (No. 2), and Iga Swiatek (No. 4) — a four-time Roland Garros champion who will always be a clay-court factor at any tournament she enters.

What's Next

The shape of the Roland Garros field will become considerably clearer over the next 12 days as both the Italian Open in Rome and the remaining clay-season results filter in. Djokovic's fitness, Sinner's form in Rome, and the condition of the top WTA seeds after their Italian campaigns will all factor into how Paris shapes up. Roland Garros qualifying begins May 18. Main draw Day 1 is May 25. Whatever happens in Rome this week — and however the Career Golden Masters quest resolves — it all flows directly toward the clay courts of the Bois de Boulogne, where another piece of history is waiting to be written.
