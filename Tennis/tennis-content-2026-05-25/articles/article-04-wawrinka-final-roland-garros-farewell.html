Stan Wawrinka's Final Roland Garros: A Champion Says Goodbye to Paris

By Elena Voss | Tennis Fanrecap | May 25, 2026

Twenty-one Roland Garros appearances. One title. Three Grand Slam trophies. And today, at 41 years old, Stan Wawrinka walks onto a Paris clay court for the last time.

Wawrinka faces Dutch lucky loser Jesper de Jong on Court Simonne-Mathieu on Day 2 of the 2026 French Open — his 21st and final appearance at the tournament that stands as the defining achievement of his career. He entered as a wild card recipient, the same generous provision Paris extends to its returning legends. The occasion is one of the great sporting farewells of 2026.

The 2015 Final: Tennis History, Made in Paris

To understand why Roland Garros matters so deeply to Wawrinka, go back to June 7, 2015. Down two sets to love against Novak Djokovic — then the world No. 1 and favourite — Wawrinka pulled off one of the most extraordinary comebacks in Grand Slam final history. He won in four sets, claiming his second Grand Slam title and arguably the most dramatic match of his career.

That final featured a backhand winner down the line — played at full extension, on the run, under match pressure — that became one of the iconic shots of the Open Era. Djokovic raised his hands in disbelief at the net. Wawrinka fell to the clay. Paris had a new champion, and Swiss tennis had its second Grand Slam winner of the generation.

He returned to the final in 2017, losing to Rafael Nadal. But his name was already written into the Roland Garros record books. It always will be.

A Career of Late Peaks

Wawrinka's career is a study in the art of the late bloomer. He won his first Grand Slam — the Australian Open — at 28, in 2014. He won Roland Garros at 30. He won the US Open at 31. Three different Grand Slams, three different surfaces, all after the age most players begin to decline. He showed that the tennis record books were not closed to players who developed slowly, who struggled through early career frustrations, who found their peak later than expected.

That message has resonated with players and fans alike. Wawrinka's career trajectory is one of the most unique in the sport's modern history — a player who became one of the three best in the world only after turning 30, after decades of being in the shadow of his Swiss compatriot Roger Federer.

The Emotional Peg: Fils' Withdrawal

There is an unexpected poignancy to today's match. Wawrinka was originally scheduled to face French No. 1 Arthur Fils — a homecoming clash of old champion and new generation. Then Fils withdrew with a hip injury, and Dutch lucky loser Jesper de Jong stepped in. The romance of the original matchup is gone, but the emotional weight of the occasion is unchanged. Paris will still be watching. Paris will still be cheering.

"There will be a lot of emotion because it's Paris and Roland-Garros," Wawrinka said ahead of the match.

He does not need to win today to have earned his place in Roland Garros history. That was settled in 2015. But a final day on the clay, in front of a crowd that has always loved him, is a fitting way for one of the sport's great champions to close this chapter of his life.

What's Next

Whatever the result today, Wawrinka will complete his final professional season. He plans to retire at year's end. Roland Garros is the tournament that defined him — and today, Paris returns what he gave it in 2015: a proper send-off for a proper champion. Merci, Stan.
