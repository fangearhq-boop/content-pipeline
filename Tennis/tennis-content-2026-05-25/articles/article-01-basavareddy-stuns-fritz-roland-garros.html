Wild Card Basavareddy Stuns Fritz: First American Top-10 Win at Roland Garros Since 2000

By Marcus Cole | Tennis Fanrecap | May 25, 2026

Nobody told Nishesh Basavareddy that wild cards aren't supposed to beat No. 7 seeds at Roland Garros. On Day 1 of the 2026 French Open, the 21-year-old American — ranked No. 148 in the world and carrying a wild card earned through the USTA's Roland Garros Wild Card Challenge — walked onto Court Suzanne-Lenglen and dismantled Taylor Fritz 7-6(5), 7-6(5), 6-7(9), 6-1 in one of the most tactically inventive upsets of the early tournament.

It was the first American top-10 win at Roland Garros since 2000 — a 26-year drought ended by a Stanford alumnus from California. Basavareddy had entered the match with a career record of 0-3 against top-10 opponents. He left with something far more valuable: belief.

The Drop Shot as a Weapon

The tactical story of the match was the drop shot. Basavareddy deployed it 27 times during the four-set contest, repeatedly pulling Fritz — a player who thrives when he can dictate from the baseline — forward and away from his preferred position. The strategy disrupted Fritz's formidable groundstroke game and turned one of the tour's most physically imposing players into a reactive defender scrambling to reach shots short in the court.

The numbers validate the approach. Basavareddy won 31 of 37 net approaches (84%) while Fritz, usually comfortable at the net when he chooses to attack, won just 15 of 32 (47%). Fritz hit 71 winners to Basavareddy's 51 — a significant gap — but sheer hitting power was negated by the American's ability to control pace and placement. "He cooked me with the drop shots," Fritz said afterward (per ATP Tour).

The third set was the match's most dramatic. Fritz saved a potentially decisive break to take it to a tiebreak, then pushed it to 9-9 before forcing the set with some of his best tennis of the day. But Basavareddy had already done the psychological damage. The fourth set was a procession: 6-1, and it was over.

Who Is Nishesh Basavareddy?

Basavareddy grew up in Newport Beach, California, the son of Telugu immigrants who settled in Indiana before the family moved west. He picked up tennis following his older brother to courts as a child and developed through the American collegiate system at Stanford University — one of the country's most storied tennis programs. After turning professional, he had a breakthrough 2025 season that included his first ATP semifinal and his first main draw Grand Slam win, briefly cracking the top 100. A difficult Australian Open saw him slide to No. 242, but he worked his way back to No. 148 by Roland Garros.

His wild card came via the USTA's Roland Garros Wild Card Challenge — a selection process rewarding strong performances in USTA events. It was a second chance on the biggest clay court stage. He took full advantage.

What It Means for the Draw

Fritz's exit is significant beyond the result itself. As the No. 7 seed and one of America's best performers at Grand Slams in recent years, he was projected as a potential quarterfinalist on this side of the draw. His early departure opens the path for players in that quarter and gives Basavareddy — now very much the story of the early tournament — a genuine chance to advance further.

It is also the latest reminder that Roland Garros 2026 is playing out as one of the most open in recent memory. With No. 1 Jannik Sinner the heavy favourite and several top seeds already vulnerable, Basavareddy's win fits a pattern of unpredictability that could define the fortnight.

What's Next

Basavareddy advances to the second round of Roland Garros for what is becoming a defining moment in his early career. Fritz, who was one of the leading American hopes for the title, exits at the first hurdle. For Basavareddy, one of the sport's most creative young players made his case for belonging among the best — one drop shot at a time.
