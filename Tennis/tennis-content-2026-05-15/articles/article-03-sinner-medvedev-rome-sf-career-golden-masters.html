Sinner vs. Medvedev: One Win From the Career Golden Masters

By Ryan Calloway | Tennis Fanrecap | May 15, 2026

There is always a scoreline to follow in Jannik Sinner's matches, but on Friday at the Foro Italico, the subtext is larger than any single number. Sinner enters his Italian Open semifinal against Daniil Medvedev with 32 consecutive ATP Masters 1000 wins — a new all-time record. Win this semifinal, win the final on Sunday, and he completes the Career Golden Masters, holding all nine Masters 1000 titles simultaneously. Rome is the only one he has never won.

Sinner set the record on Thursday by defeating Andrey Rublev 6-2, 6-4 in the quarterfinals, surpassing Novak Djokovic's previous mark of 31 consecutive wins. The run has spanned multiple tournaments and included wins over players at every ranking level. It is, by any measure, the most sustained period of dominance the 36-year Masters 1000 series has ever seen.

The Medvedev Factor

Medvedev is not the opponent most players would choose to face at this point. The Russian is the 2023 Rome champion. He has beaten Sinner on clay before, which places him in a very short category of players who can claim that distinction in recent seasons. In the quarterfinals, Medvedev showed his characteristic resilience by falling 1-6 in the opening set against 20-year-old lucky loser Martin Landaluce before reasserting control to win 1-6, 6-4, 7-5.

That comeback — from a set and a break down against an opponent playing with nothing to lose — was a reminder of how dangerous Medvedev remains when he finds his tactical rhythm. He is not at the peak of his powers from 2021 and 2022, but he is still capable of producing high-level tennis in a three-set semifinal on the surface where he has won this title before.

Sinner's head-to-head record against Medvedev has swung heavily in the Italian's favor in recent years. Sinner has dominated most of their recent meetings and enters as the clear favorite. But on clay, against a champion who knows this court, no lead in a head-to-head is absolute.

What a Title Would Mean

The Career Golden Masters — winning all nine Masters 1000 events at least once — is one of the rarest achievements in men's tennis. Djokovic is the only player in history to hold all nine simultaneously. Sinner, at 24, already holds eight. He won his eighth Masters 1000 title at the Miami Open this year. Rome is the gap in his collection, and it has been a source of extra motivation throughout this week at the Foro Italico.

Beyond the personal milestone, a Rome title would also mean something for Italian tennis history. The last Italian man to win the Rome Masters was Adriano Panatta in 1976. No Italian player has won the home title in five decades. Sinner does not need that additional context to feel the weight of this week, but it adds to it.

In the other semifinal, Luciano Darderi faces Casper Ruud. Darderi — a 23-year-old wild card — reached his first career Masters 1000 semifinal after beating No. 2 seed Zverev and outlasting Rafael Jodar in a quarterfinal that finished past 2:00 AM. Ruud, a four-time Rome finalist, is the higher-pedigree opponent, but Darderi will have the full weight of the Italian crowd behind him.

What's Next

Both ATP semifinal matches take place Friday at the Foro Italico. The ATP final is scheduled for Sunday, May 17. Roland Garros qualifying starts the following day, Sunday May 18, with the main draw beginning May 24. For Sinner, a Rome title would cap the most dominant single clay swing in recent memory and send him to Paris as an overwhelming favorite for a first career Grand Slam title at Roland Garros.
