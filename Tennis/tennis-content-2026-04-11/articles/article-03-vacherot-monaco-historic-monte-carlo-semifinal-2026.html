Monaco's Own: Vacherot Makes History as First Monegasque MC Semifinalist in the Open Era

By Ryan Calloway | April 11, 2026

Valentin Vacherot, the No. 23 player in the world, has beaten Cerundolo, Musetti, Hurkacz, and De Minaur on his home courts. Alcaraz is next.

There are upsets, and there are stories. Valentin Vacherot's run at the 2026 Monte Carlo Masters is both — and it is one that will be remembered in Monaco for a very long time.

By defeating Alex de Minaur in the quarterfinals on Friday, Vacherot became the first Monegasque player to reach the Monte Carlo Masters semifinals in the Open Era. He is playing at home, on the courts he grew up watching, coached by his half-brother Balleret. And he is now two wins from lifting the trophy at the most famous clay event in the world.

Standing between him and the final is the defending champion and world No. 1, Carlos Alcaraz.

The Route: Four Quality Opponents, Zero Easy Matches

Vacherot's path through the draw reads like a greatest-hits list of current clay-court tennis. He began by defeating Juan Manuel Cerundolo, then edged Lorenzo Musetti in a tiebreak-heavy battle of 7-6(6), 7-5. In the round of 16, he toppled Hubert Hurkacz — the 2025 Monte Carlo finalist — 6-7(4), 6-3, 6-4 in a grueling two hours and 53 minutes.

Then on Friday he found another gear, saving 13 break points and outlasting Alex de Minaur — one of the most relentless defenders on the ATP Tour — in yet another three-set match.

His last two matches alone lasted a combined five hours and 17 minutes. He enters the semifinal against Alcaraz with no days of rest, a body that has been pushed to its limits, and an entire principality behind him.

The Meaning for Monaco

The Monte Carlo Masters has been held in Monaco since 1897. In all that time — through the Open Era, through the reigns of Laver, Borg, McEnroe, Agassi, Federer, Nadal, and Djokovic — no Monegasque player had ever reached the semifinal.

Vacherot is 27. He has spent his career navigating the challenger circuit, rising and falling in the rankings, always with Monte Carlo on the calendar as the one tournament where the crowd knows his name from the first point. This year, they are learning it in a different way.

"Playing at home, with your family in the crowd — this is something I dreamed of," Vacherot said after advancing to the semifinals. His ranking has already improved to No. 23 in the world — a career high that this run has secured regardless of Saturday's result.

The Challenge Ahead

Alcaraz is a different proposition from anyone Vacherot has beaten this week. The Spaniard is 20-2 in 2026, has dropped just one set in three Monte Carlo matches (to Etcheverry), and beat Bublik 6-3, 6-0 in the quarterfinal as if he were warming up. They have never played each other, which removes one source of psychological edge for Alcaraz — but also means Vacherot has no data on how to beat him.

The crowd will be the loudest variable. Court Rainier III is not a big stadium; it is intimate, dramatic, and will be overwhelmingly partisan for the local hero. In that environment, tired legs and depleted reserves can be compensated for by something harder to measure: will.

What's Next

Vacherot faces Carlos Alcaraz in the Monte Carlo semifinal today at approximately 3:30 PM CEST on Court Rainier III. A win sends the first Monegasque into a Monte Carlo final in the Open Era and sets up a Sunday championship match against either Sinner or Zverev. Whatever happens Saturday, Vacherot has already secured a career-high ranking and written his name into Monte Carlo history.
