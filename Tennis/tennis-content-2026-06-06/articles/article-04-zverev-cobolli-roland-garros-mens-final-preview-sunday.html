Zverev vs Cobolli: The Roland Garros Men's Final That Guarantees a First-Time Grand Slam Champion

By Elena Voss | June 6, 2026

Sunday's Roland Garros men's final is settled: Alexander Zverev of Germany against Flavio Cobolli of Italy. One of them wins a Grand Slam title for the first time. There is no other outcome available.

Zverev, No. 2 seed and 28 years old, is in his fourth Grand Slam final. Cobolli, No. 10 seed and 24 years old, is in his first. The match is scheduled not before 3:00 PM Paris time — 9:00 AM ET — on Court Philippe-Chatrier. It will be the last match played at Roland Garros 2026.

The Weight Zverev Carries

Three times before, Alexander Zverev has been in this position. Three times before — US Open 2020, Roland Garros 2024, Australian Open 2025 — he has lost. The conversation that surrounds him is inseparable from those results now: the player good enough to reach Grand Slam finals but, so far, unable to win one.

At Roland Garros 2026 he has been the most complete player in the draw. He dropped just two sets in six matches, beating Jakub Mensik in the semifinals with the kind of controlled, patient performance that characterises his best tennis. On clay — the surface he has dominated longest in his career — he is a difficult player to beat over five sets.

The question is not whether Zverev can play well enough to win a Grand Slam final. He can. The question is whether he can win one when the moment arrives. Sunday is one more opportunity to answer it.

Cobolli's Case

Flavio Cobolli's route to the final has been built on consistency rather than marquee scalps. He is the No. 10 seed, dropped just two sets in six matches, and beat No. 4 seed Felix Auger-Aliassime in the quarterfinals as the clearest statement of his quality on this surface in this fortnight.

He reached the final after Matteo Arnaldi withdrew from their all-Italian semifinal with a viral illness — a circumstance that required no play from Cobolli on Friday, which means he is rested and sharp going into Sunday. Whatever the manner of the semifinal, his campaign as a whole has been legitimate.

Cobolli beat Zverev in Munich earlier in 2026, which is the one data point that cuts against the notion that Sunday is simply a matter of Zverev closing out an obvious win. He won that match on clay. This match is also on clay. The head-to-head across their four meetings stands at 3-1 to Zverev, but Cobolli knows how to beat him.

Head-to-Head and Surface Context

Zverev and Cobolli have met four times. Zverev won three of them, including a straight-sets win against Cobolli at Roland Garros 2025 in the third round. Cobolli's victory came in Munich 2026, also on clay.

Their most recent meeting before Sunday was in Madrid this spring, where Zverev won. Going into Sunday's final, Zverev has the head-to-head advantage and the surface history. He also has the experience of three previous Grand Slam final appearances — however those ended — that Cobolli does not.

Experience cuts both ways. Zverev knows what a Grand Slam final feels like; he also knows what it feels like to lose three of them. Whether that history helps him or haunts him on Sunday is one of the more interesting psychological questions the match raises.

The Broader Significance

Sunday's final will produce the first first-time men's Grand Slam champion since Jannik Sinner at the Australian Open in 2024. The men's game has not had a new major winner in two and a half years. Whoever wins Sunday — whether it is Zverev finally getting his title or Cobolli arriving without expectation and taking it — there is a genuine historic dimension to the result.

For Italy, there is an additional layer. The last Italian man to reach a Grand Slam final was Adriano Panatta, who won Roland Garros in 1976. Cobolli standing across the net from Zverev on Sunday is the continuation of a week that, however it ends, has already produced the first all-Italian men's Grand Slam semifinal in history.
