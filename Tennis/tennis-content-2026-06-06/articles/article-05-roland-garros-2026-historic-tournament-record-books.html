Roland Garros 2026 Has Been One for the History Books — Here's Everything That Made It Extraordinary

By Marcus Cole | June 6, 2026

Before Sunday's men's final concludes Roland Garros 2026, it is worth stepping back and taking stock of everything this tournament has already delivered. There have been upsets, records, historical firsts, and the kind of unpredictability that no one fully predicted when the draws were announced two weeks ago.

The full picture is remarkable.

A Women's Draw That Produced Two First-Time Finalists

Mirra Andreeva and Maja Chwalinska meet in Saturday's women's final — and neither has ever appeared in a Grand Slam final before. That outcome, while not unprecedented, is rare.

Chwalinska's path is the more startling of the two. She entered the tournament as a qualifier, ranked No. 114 in the world. No qualifier had ever reached the Roland Garros women's final before her. She is only the second women's qualifier to reach a Grand Slam final in the Open Era, and just the third woman to reach the Roland Garros final on her main draw debut — joining Evonne Goolagong in 1971 and Chris Evert in 1973.

Andreeva is the fourth-youngest Roland Garros women's finalist in 30 years. At 19, she is in company alongside Martina Hingis, Kim Clijsters, and Coco Gauff — the only younger players to reach this stage of this tournament in the last three decades.

A Men's Draw Without Its Top Three

Jannik Sinner, the world No. 1, was eliminated in the second round by Francisco Cerundolo. Carlos Alcaraz withdrew before the tournament began due to injury. Novak Djokovic fell in the third round to 19-year-old João Fonseca of Brazil.

For the first time in years, Roland Garros's men's draw reached the business end of the tournament without any of the three names that have dominated men's tennis in the recent era. The draw was open in a way that it almost never is, and it produced accordingly unpredictable results.

Fonseca himself — who became the first Brazilian man in a Roland Garros quarterfinal since Gustavo Kuerten in 2004 — was the breakout story of the tournament's first week, beating Djokovic from two sets down and then defeating two-time finalist Casper Ruud in the fourth round before his run ended in the last eight.

The First All-Italian Men's Grand Slam Semifinal — and Its Unusual End

When the men's draw produced Flavio Cobolli against Matteo Arnaldi in the semifinals, it became the first all-Italian men's Grand Slam semifinal in history. That distinction survived the fortnight's most unfortunate subplot: Arnaldi withdrew with a viral illness on Friday morning before the match could be played.

The walkover — only the fourth in Open Era Grand Slam semifinal history — means the historic match never took place in a competitive sense. But Italy has its Grand Slam finalist regardless, and Cobolli will face Zverev on Sunday carrying his country's first appearance in a men's Grand Slam final since Adriano Panatta in 1976.

Both Finals Feature First-Time Finalists

In both the women's and men's draws, every player who reached the final is making their Grand Slam final debut. Andreeva and Chwalinska in the women's draw. Cobolli in the men's draw — and while Zverev has appeared in three previous GS finals, none of those ended in a title, making Sunday's result another "first win" if he achieves it.

The tournament has delivered on the promise of a wide-open draw. Roland Garros 2026 is not the story of dominance reaffirmed; it is the story of what happens when the expected order is disrupted and the next generation steps into the space that creates.

What's Next
