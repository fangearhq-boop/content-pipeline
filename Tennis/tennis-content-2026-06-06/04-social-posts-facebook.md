### STORY 1: Roland Garros Women's Final — Andreeva vs Chwalinska (TODAY)

#### Long-Form Post — 9:00 AM ET

Today at Roland Garros, tennis gets its first Grand Slam final of 2026 — and it features two players who have never been here before.

Mirra Andreeva, the 19-year-old Russian ranked No. 8 in the world, faces Maja Chwalinska, a Polish qualifier ranked No. 114. The match starts at 3 PM Paris time (9 AM ET) on Court Philippe-Chatrier.

Chwalinska's run here has been one of the stories of the year. She entered Roland Garros as a qualifier — meaning she had to win three matches just to reach the main draw — and has since beaten her way to the final. She is the first qualifier in the history of Roland Garros to reach the women's final. She is also just the third woman ever to reach the Roland Garros final in her first appearance in the main draw, joining Evonne Goolagong (1971) and Chris Evert (1973).

Andreeva has been no less impressive. At 19, she is the fourth-youngest Roland Garros women's finalist in 30 years, and she dismantled Marta Kostyuk 6-1, 6-3 in just 76 minutes in the semifinal — a performance that looked like the work of a much more experienced player.

Whoever wins today lifts the Coupe Suzanne-Lenglen for the first time.

Who are you backing?

### STORY 2: Zverev def. Mensik 7-5, 6-2, 3-6, 6-3

#### Long-Form Post — 8:00 AM ET

Alexander Zverev is back in a Grand Slam final.

The German No. 2 seed defeated 20-year-old Jakub Mensik 7-5, 6-2, 3-6, 6-3 on Friday in three hours and one minute to reach his fourth Grand Slam final — and his second at Roland Garros.

The match followed a familiar Zverev pattern: control, authority, a minor wobble, and then a firm re-assertion. He dominated the first two sets, allowing Mensik to win the third after the Czech youngster took a medical timeout midway through the set. But Zverev immediately answered in the fourth, jumping to 3-0 and never looking back.

Mensik, at 20 years old and in his first Grand Slam semifinal, showed why he belongs at this stage — but Zverev's experience proved decisive. The German now has four Grand Slam final appearances (US Open 2020, Roland Garros 2024, Australian Open 2025, and now this one) and has yet to win any of them.

Sunday gives him the chance to change that.

His opponent? Flavio Cobolli of Italy.

What do you think — does Zverev finally win the big one on Sunday?

### STORY 3: Arnaldi Withdraws — Cobolli Into First Grand Slam Final

#### Long-Form Post — 10:30 AM ET

Roland Garros 2026 will have its first Italian men's Grand Slam finalist since 1976 — but not quite in the way anyone expected.

Matteo Arnaldi withdrew from today's all-Italian Roland Garros semifinal against Flavio Cobolli before the match could begin. The 25-year-old Italian suffered from a viral illness — vomiting and fever overnight on Thursday — and despite seeing a doctor early Friday morning and taking medication, was unable to keep food or drinks down throughout the day.

Cobolli advances to his first Grand Slam final via walkover, a result that is — depending on your perspective — either deeply anticlimactic or an understandable end to a medical situation that gave Arnaldi no choice.

It is only the fourth time in the Open Era that a Grand Slam semifinal has ended in a walkover.

The historical facts remain: this was the first all-Italian men's Grand Slam semifinal in history, and Cobolli will now meet Alexander Zverev on Sunday for the title. Italy will have its first men's Grand Slam finalist since Adriano Panatta in 1976.

Cobolli has earned his spot here. He dropped only two sets in six matches, beating No. 4 seed Felix Auger-Aliassime in the quarter-finals and navigating a wide-open draw without stumbling. His path to the final is legitimate — even if the last step was handed to him by circumstances beyond anyone's control.

Feel for Arnaldi. But respect what Cobolli has built this fortnight.

### STORY 4: Men's Final Preview — Zverev vs Cobolli (Sunday)

#### Long-Form Post — 3:00 PM ET

Sunday's Roland Garros men's final has one guarantee written into it before a ball is struck: someone is going to win their first Grand Slam title.

Alexander Zverev has been here three times before (US Open 2020, Roland Garros 2024, Australian Open 2025) and lost each time. He is 28 years old. He has been one of the best players in the world for most of the past decade. The Grand Slam title remains the single gap in his résumé.

Flavio Cobolli has never been here before. The 24-year-old Italian No. 10 seed reached this final by going through one of the most open Roland Garros draws in recent memory — Sinner was out in the second round, Djokovic in the third, Alcaraz before the tournament began. Cobolli beat FAA in the quarter-finals, and has dropped just two sets across the fortnight.

Their head-to-head stands at 3-1 in Zverev's favour, though Cobolli beat Zverev in Munich earlier this year. Zverev won their Madrid meeting this spring. On clay, this year, they are split. That split continues on Sunday.

Both men want the same thing. One of them gets it.

Sunday. 3 PM Paris / 9 AM ET. Philippe-Chatrier.

### STORY 5: Roland Garros 2026 — A Tournament That Rewrote the Record Books

#### Long-Form Post — 9:00 PM ET

Before we get to Sunday's final, it's worth pausing to consider what Roland Garros 2026 has already given us.

Maja Chwalinska became the first qualifier in the history of the Roland Garros women's draw to reach the final. She arrived ranked No. 114, had to win three qualifying matches, and then won seven more to reach the championship round.

The men's draw produced the first all-Italian Grand Slam semifinal in history — even if it ended before a point was played when Matteo Arnaldi withdrew with a viral illness. That walkover was only the fourth of its kind in Open Era Grand Slam semifinal history.

Mirra Andreeva, at 19, is the fourth-youngest Roland Garros women's finalist in 30 years, joining a list that includes Martina Hingis, Kim Clijsters, and Coco Gauff.

On the men's side, Jannik Sinner was eliminated in the second round, Carlos Alcaraz withdrew before the tournament, and Novak Djokovic fell in the third round — leaving the draw as wide open as it has been in years, and producing the most unexpected men's final lineup since long before Sinner's era began.

Both men's finalists — Zverev and Cobolli — are seeking their first Grand Slam title. Both women's finalists — Andreeva and Chwalinska — are in their first Grand Slam final. In the Open Era, this has happened only a handful of times across a single Grand Slam.

Whatever happens on Sunday, Roland Garros 2026 has delivered.
