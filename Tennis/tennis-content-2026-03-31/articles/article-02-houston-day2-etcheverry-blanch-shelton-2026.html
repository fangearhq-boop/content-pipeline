Houston Open Day 2: Etcheverry Falls, Blanch Wins, Shelton Waits

March 31, 2026 · Tennis Fanrecap

Day 2 at the Fayez Sarofim & Co. U.S. Men's Clay Court Championship produced a notable clay-court lesson: a 6-0 first set doesn't guarantee anything.

Tomas Martin Etcheverry, one of the ATP's most reliable clay specialists, won the opening set against Federico Agustin Gomez with a bagel and then proceeded to lose the next two — 7-6(4), 6-2 — in a reversal that illustrated exactly why the clay swing remains the most unpredictable surface on tour. Gomez's win removes one of the dangerous early-round names from the bottom of the draw.

More First-Round Results

Liam Draxl defeated Nishesh Basavareddy in three sets, 3-6, 6-1, 7-6(2), continuing a strong clay showing from the Canadian. Darwin Blanch, the young American whose name keeps appearing in first-round results this clay season, won his match in three sets, 6-0, 5-7, 7-6(5). Clement Tabur won 6-2, 7-5 to advance.

The early-round results continue to shape a bracket where the seeded players — who mostly carry first-round byes — will face a mixed field of qualifiers and survivors when they enter Wednesday.

Shelton Draws Zhang Zhizhen on Wednesday

Top seed Ben Shelton, ranked world No. 9, has his first-round bye and is scheduled to face Zhang Zhizhen on Wednesday, April 1. Shelton is the first American to be seeded No. 1 at a major ATP clay event in recent memory, and the Houston week represents the clearest test of his clay game ahead of the European swing.

Zhang Zhizhen is a consistent baseliner who handles clay comfortably. The match will be an early data point on Shelton's clay form — his serve is always a weapon, but his movement and groundstroke depth on a slow clay surface are the questions that follow him from hard courts.

Frances Tiafoe, the No. 2 seed, is also expected to enter on Wednesday. Defending champion Jenson Brooksby faces Mackenzie McDonald in a first-round match that carries its own stakes: a Brooksby loss would remove last year's title holder from the field early and shift the spotlight entirely to Shelton and Tiafoe.

The American Clay Narrative

Houston has long been the annual referendum on American men's tennis and clay courts. The U.S. has historically been the weakest major tennis nation on red clay at the elite level. But Shelton and Tiafoe represent a shift — both are ranked in the world's top 25, both have improved their clay games measurably, and both are using Houston as a launching point for the Monte Carlo and Rome swings.

The prize money at River Oaks Country Club is $700,045 — an ATP 250 level event — but its value extends beyond the check. Houston is the proving ground. The results here establish momentum, or expose weaknesses, before the field moves to Europe.

What's Next

Wednesday, April 1 is the biggest day of the Houston week so far. Shelton vs. Zhang and Tiafoe's opener will dominate the River Oaks Country Club schedule. Brooksby's match vs. McDonald also plays out on the same day. By Wednesday evening, the second-round picture will be clear — and the real American clay story will have its first definitive chapter.
