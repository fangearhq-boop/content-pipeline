Wawrinka and Berrettini: Monte Carlo's Wild Cards Tell the Full Story of Men's Tennis

March 31, 2026 · Tennis Fanrecap

Every Grand Slam and Masters 1000 event distributes wildcards, and the spots are rarely more interesting than when they carry genuine narrative weight. The 2026 Rolex Monte-Carlo Masters has two such cases: Stan Wawrinka, 42 years old, returning to a court where he won in 2014; and Matteo Berrettini, the former No. 6 in the world, working through a comeback that has taken longer than either he or his supporters expected.

Together, they represent a kind of cross-section of men's tennis history — two players whose best work came in different eras, drawn into a bracket that may be defined by players 20 years younger than one of them.

Wawrinka: The Legend Returns

In 2014, Stan Wawrinka won Monte Carlo, then won Roland Garros. It was the beginning of his late-career Grand Slam phase — a run that produced three major titles in three years (Australian Open 2014, Roland Garros 2015, US Open 2016) and established him as one of the most dangerous clay-court players in the world at any given tournament.

Wawrinka is now 42. His ranking has fallen far from the No. 3 he once held. But the skills that made him extraordinary on clay — a massive one-handed backhand capable of generating violent angles, a heavy forehand that stays dangerous in long rallies, and a serve that remains a weapon — do not fully disappear with age. Clay is the surface that preserves veteran talents longest. Federer was competitive on clay deep into his 30s. Connors played it into his 40s. And Wawrinka, on the right day, is capable of dismantling anyone in a draw.

His Monte Carlo wildcard isn't sentiment. It's an acknowledgment from a tournament that knows its clay-court history.

Berrettini: The Comeback That Needs a Statement

Matteo Berrettini's career arc looks different from almost every other player in the top tier of men's tennis. The Italian reached No. 6 in the world and the 2021 Wimbledon final, where he lost to Novak Djokovic in four sets. He played the kind of tennis that looked like a No. 1 ranking was a realistic destination.

Then injuries arrived. Abdominal, hamstring, hip — a series of setbacks that have kept him from competing consistently since 2022. He has returned to action in stretches, shown flashes of his old form, but never sustained it over the length of a full tournament run at the highest level.

Monte Carlo's wildcard gives him the kind of draw that rewards quality over quantity — a single early upset against a top-10 player in a match-by-match format that suits a player still building match sharpness. Berrettini's serve-plus-forehand game is not purely a grass-court weapon. He has the groundstrokes to compete on clay, and the motivation of a player who knows exactly what he looked like at his best.

Three Generations in One Draw

What makes the 2026 Monte Carlo wildcards particularly striking is the generational contrast they create when placed alongside Joao Fonseca's debut in the same draw. Fonseca is 19, born in 2007. Wawrinka is 42, having won Grand Slams before Fonseca began secondary school. Berrettini is 30, a contemporary of Alcaraz's formative years who has spent the last three seasons fighting to reclaim the form of his own early 20s.

Tennis is one of the few individual sports where this kind of multi-generational field coexists on the same courts. The Monte Carlo draw accommodates all three, in a bracket already defined by the most dramatic No. 1 ranking battle in years.

What's Next

Wawrinka and Berrettini will enter the Monte Carlo draw in the first round — unlike Alcaraz and Sinner, who receive byes as top seeds. Their opponents will be determined by the draw bracket, and first-round clay-court matches at Masters 1000 level are exactly the kind of matches where both wildcards are dangerous: single-set pressure, unpredictable surface conditions, and the crowd at the Monte Carlo Country Club always responsive to a veteran presence.

Watch the wildcard first-round results as the Monte Carlo week begins April 5. A Wawrinka win over a top-30 opponent would be one of the most celebrated results of the clay season. And a Berrettini serving performance close to his 2021 peak would be the clearest sign yet that the comeback has found its footing.
