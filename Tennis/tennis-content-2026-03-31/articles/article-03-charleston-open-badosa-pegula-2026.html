Charleston Open: Badosa Returns as Wild Card, Pegula Defends on Green Clay

March 31, 2026 · Tennis Fanrecap

The Credit One Charleston Open is in full swing, and on a surface found nowhere else on the WTA calendar, it always generates the spring season's most distinctive first week of tennis.

Green clay — lighter, slightly faster, lower-bouncing than the red clay used across European events — rewards a particular kind of game: heavy topspin with patience, plus a serve that can work through the slower surface. Defending champion Jessica Pegula knows this court well. She won the title in 2025 and returns as the No. 1 seed, opening against Yulia Putintseva on Wednesday.

Badosa's Comeback Story

The most compelling narrative in the Charleston draw is Paula Badosa's return as a wild card. Two years ago, Badosa was ranked No. 2 in the world — one of the most explosive ball-strikers in women's tennis, a player who generated offense from both wings and handled clay as well as any European on tour. Since then, injury has eroded her ranking and kept her from competing consistently at the highest level.

She draws Kayla Day in the first round. The match gives her an opportunity to win on a surface she has always handled well and build match confidence heading into the European clay swing. A wild card at a $2.5 million event with this field is not a courtesy — it's an opportunity. Badosa is the kind of player who can go from wild card to quarterfinal in a single week if her game is clicking.

The Green Clay Advantage

Charleston's surface distinction matters for the opening days of the WTA clay season. Players who spent February and March competing on hard courts — the Australian Open swing, then Dubai, Indian Wells, Miami — arrive on green clay with game plans that don't always transfer cleanly. The ball sits up differently, rallies extend longer, and the court demands more patience than hard-court tennis punishes you for skipping.

The effect is particularly notable in early rounds, where hard-court specialists who made deep Miami runs can struggle on the green clay against players who have specifically prepared for this surface. It's why Charleston regularly produces first-round results that surprise.

First-round action continues with Bianca Andreescu vs. Dalma Galfi, Caty McNally vs. Ashlyn Krueger, Anna Kalinskaya vs. Viktoriya Tomova, and several other matches shaping the early bracket. The winners move into a second round that begins to separate the surface specialists from the broader field.

What's Next

Wednesday brings the top seeds into action. Pegula's first match of her title defense is the headline. Badosa's Tuesday result will be known by then — and if she wins, her second-round draw becomes the most-watched match on the green clay boards.

Charleston has a way of announcing itself. By Thursday, the field of contenders will be narrowed to the players who have found their clay legs early and the survivors who have adapted faster than their opponents expected. The first week of WTA clay season is the most truthful fortnight in the sport — no amount of hard-court form carries over unchanged when the surface changes this dramatically.
