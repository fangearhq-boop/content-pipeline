Monte Carlo Draw Is Set — Sinner and Alcaraz on a Collision Course for No. 1

March 31, 2026 · Tennis Fanrecap

The draw for the 2026 Rolex Monte-Carlo Masters is officially set, and it delivers exactly the framing the clay season deserved: Carlos Alcaraz as the No. 1 seed and defending champion, Jannik Sinner as the No. 2 seed with zero points to defend, and a bracket that could determine the world No. 1 ranking before the month of April is over.

The tournament runs April 5-12 at the Monte Carlo Country Club in Roquebrune-Cap-Martin, France. Absent from the draw are Novak Djokovic (shoulder injury) and Holger Rune (Achilles), which removes two of the most dangerous clay-court players in the world and opens the bracket for the men who remain.

The Points Pressure

The No. 1 race heading into Monte Carlo is not abstract math anymore — it's a bracket. Alcaraz enters at 13,590 ATP points; Sinner enters at 12,400. The gap is 1,190 points. That number was 3,150 before Sinner's Miami Open title. It has been cut by nearly two-thirds in a single tournament.

What makes Monte Carlo unusual is the asymmetry in defending points. Alcaraz won the title in 2025, earning 1,000 ranking points that are now attached to that result and must be defended over the next two weeks. If he loses in the first round, roughly 750 of those points fall off his total, dropping his ranking to approximately 12,590 — just 190 points above Sinner's current total. A Sinner title, combined with any early Alcaraz exit, returns Sinner to No. 1 for the first time since his 2025 doping ban.

Sinner, by contrast, missed Monte Carlo entirely in 2025. His three-month suspension ran through the spring clay swing. He arrives in Monaco with no defending points and nothing to lose in the rankings column.

The Draw Structure

Alcaraz and Sinner are seeded first and second respectively, which places them on opposite sides of the 56-player draw. A final between the two remains the most likely scenario if both players perform to their ranking. The bracket between them features a field that — while deep — lacks the two players most capable of consistently defeating the top seeds.

The seeded field includes Alexander Zverev (No. 3), Taylor Fritz, Casper Ruud, Jack Draper, Hubert Hurkacz, Alex de Minaur, Stefanos Tsitsipas, Daniil Medvedev, Arthur Fils, and Jiří Lehečka, among others. Sebastian Korda, Tommy Paul, and Joao Fonseca are also in the draw.

Wildcards Stan Wawrinka and Matteo Berrettini add star power to the opening rounds. Wawrinka won Monte Carlo in 2014 and remains capable of unseating any player in a clay-court match. Berrettini, the former No. 6 in the world and 2021 Wimbledon finalist, is working through an injury comeback that has yet to produce its defining moment.

Fonseca's Monte Carlo Debut

One of the subplots worth watching is Joao Fonseca's first appearance at a Masters 1000 on clay. The 19-year-old Brazilian won the 2024 Next Gen ATP Finals and has been among the most talked-about young players in tennis. Monte Carlo, on a surface that suits his aggressive baseline game, represents his first real test at this level of clay-court competition.

A deep Fonseca run — quarterfinal or beyond — would immediately alter the draw path for one of the top seeds and introduce a new name to the Monte Carlo conversation.

What's Next

The Rolex Monte-Carlo Masters begins April 5 with first-round matches. Alcaraz and Sinner, as the top two seeds, receive first-round byes and are expected to enter play on Sunday, April 6, at the earliest. The draw ceremony results set the full bracket for public viewing on the official Monte Carlo tournament website.

For the No. 1 ranking, the math is brutally simple. Win the title, gain 1,000 points. Lose early, defend nothing. Sinner's first-round opponent on clay will be the first concrete data point in a story that may not be fully told until the final Sunday. Alcaraz, playing in front of a crowd that saw him lift the trophy twelve months ago, carries both the advantage of experience at this venue and the weight of expectation that comes with defending a title.

Monte Carlo has hosted legendary clay-court battles for decades. It may be about to host another.
