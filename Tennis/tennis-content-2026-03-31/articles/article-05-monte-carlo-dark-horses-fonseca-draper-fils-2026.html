Monte Carlo Dark Horses: Fonseca, Draper, and Fils Could Derail the Expected Final

March 31, 2026 · Tennis Fanrecap

The storyline writing itself around the 2026 Rolex Monte-Carlo Masters is simple: Alcaraz and Sinner on a collision course for No. 1. But tennis is rarely that tidy. Novak Djokovic and Holger Rune — two of the players most capable of disrupting the top seeds' paths through a bracket — are absent. That absence creates real openings for the players ranked just outside the very top of the draw.

Three of them warrant specific attention before play begins April 5.

Joao Fonseca: The Debut That Could Matter

Joao Fonseca is 19 years old and making his first appearance at a Masters 1000 on clay. The Brazilian won the 2024 Next Gen ATP Finals — the ATP's showcase tournament for the sport's best under-21 players — and has steadily moved through the rankings with a style of play that is built for clay: heavy topspin, patient from the baseline, capable of constructing points over long rallies without losing the plot.

The Monte Carlo Country Club clay is among the most demanding on tour. It is slow, high-bouncing red clay where physical conditioning and mental patience matter as much as technical ability. Fonseca has the game for this surface, and he has the youth to recover between rounds at a rate that older players cannot match.

His debut at this level will be watched closely. A first-round win against a top-30 opponent would be a meaningful result. A quarterfinal appearance would be one of the clay season's defining early stories — and would reshape at least one of the top seeds' path to the final.

Jack Draper: The Serve-and-Backhand Threat

Jack Draper has been British tennis's most consistent source of optimism in 2026. The left-hander brings a serve that works on any surface and a backhand capable of generating deep, heavy shots that stay dangerous through long clay-court exchanges. He has steadily improved on the surface that once exposed his physical limitations most clearly.

The absence of Rune from the Monte Carlo draw is particularly relevant for Draper. Rune is the kind of player who would likely be seeded above Draper and in his section — a removal that opens a potential path to the semifinal for a player who has been building toward exactly this kind of opportunity.

Draper is not a favorite. But he is the kind of left-handed serve-dominant player who creates problems for any opponent in a best-of-three clay-court match, and his current form suggests he's competing at a level that can produce a deep result.

Arthur Fils: The Rebound Play

Arthur Fils lost the Miami Open semifinal 6-2, 6-2 to Jiří Lehečka — a flat, comprehensive defeat that ended the French player's otherwise productive Miami campaign abruptly. The result was the kind of loss that carries into the next tournament if a player lets it. Fils has enough experience now to understand that one bad match on hard courts does not define a clay season.

Clay is where Fils is most comfortable. His baseline game — heavy from both wings, strong on the cross-court patterns — is built for the surface. He has the physicality to stay in long matches and the competitive temperament to grind through difficult draws. Monte Carlo, where he arrives as a seeded player, represents the exact kind of reset that a player needs after a disappointing semifinal.

A Fils run to the Monte Carlo final would be one of the spring's genuine surprises. A quarterfinal appearance would be a confirmation that the Miami loss was an outlier, not a form indicator.

What's Next

All three players will enter Monte Carlo's first round when the draw's first-round matches begin April 5. Their sections of the bracket, and who they face in the early rounds, will determine how much of a factor each can be. The full draw is available through the official Monte Carlo tournament website and the ATP Tour draws page.

The No. 1 race between Alcaraz and Sinner gets the headlines. But the players who shape the bracket around them — who they face, how difficult their path to the final turns out to be — are determined by what Fonseca, Draper, Fils, and their peers do in the first week. Dark horses don't always win. But they always matter.
