Alcaraz, the defending champion, carries 1,000 ranking points into the draw that he must defend from his 2025 title. ### Long-Form Post ``` Two years ago, Paula Badosa was ranked No. 2 in the world. Today she's playing Charleston as a wild card. The comeback is very much still in progress — but Badosa is playing again, competing in the Credit One Charleston Open after a grinding injury period. She draws Kayla Day in the first round, a match she'll need to win on green clay, a surface she's always handled well. Charleston is the only green clay event on the WTA calendar, and that distinction matters. The surface is lighter and faster than European red clay, more familiar to American hard-court players, and produces a different kind of tennis than what you'll see in Rome or Paris. Defending champion Jessica Pegula knows this well — she held the title last year and plays her opener on Wednesday against Yulia Putintseva. The rest of the field is active Tuesday: Bianca Andreescu plays Dalma Galfi. Caty McNally, Anna Kalinskaya, Ashlyn Krueger, and Oleksandra Oliynykova all have first-round matches. Charleston always generates early upsets because green clay neutralizes some of the form advantages that players carry in from the hard-court swing. For Badosa, every match she wins at this stage of her career is a statement. And on green clay, she has the game to make the second week. Do you think Badosa can make a deep run at Charleston? ``` ### Image Post Caption #### Image Caption -- 3:30 PM ET ``` Paula Badosa is back — playing Charleston as a wild card. Monte Carlo Wildcards — Wawrinka & Berrettini Bring Star Power to Monaco [TIER 2]
NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post ``` Two names that dominated men's tennis across two different eras will be in the Monte Carlo draw next week: Stan Wawrinka and Matteo Berrettini. Wawrinka is 42 years old. He won the Monte Carlo title in 2014 — the same spring he took Roland Garros — and went on to win three Grand Slams in total (Australian Open 2014, Roland Garros 2015, US Open 2016). He arrives as a wildcard, carrying decades of clay-court intelligence into a draw populated by players born after his first ATP title. That's not a sentimental narrative. That's a genuine threat. On clay, Wawrinka's one-handed backhand and heavy forehand are weapons at any age. Berrettini presents a different kind of story. The Italian former No. 6 — 2021 Wimbledon finalist — has spent much of the past two years managing injury. He's working his way back through the levels with a wildcard entry in Monaco that may be his best chance to rediscover the form that made him one of the most dangerous servers in the game. His serve-plus-forehand game plays on clay. Three generations of men's tennis will be at Monte Carlo: Wawrinka near the end of a Hall of Fame career, Berrettini in the uncertain middle of a comeback, and Joao Fonseca at the absolute beginning of what could be something special. That's the human texture behind the No. 1 ranking battle. Which wildcard performance at Monte Carlo are you most looking forward to? ``` ### Image Post Caption #### Image Caption -- 6:30 PM ET ``` Wawrinka (age 42, 2014 champion) and Berrettini (comeback) both get wildcards at Monte Carlo. ```

### Long-Form Post ``` Sinner and Alcaraz are the obvious story at Monte Carlo. But the names that could derail the expected final deserve attention. Joao Fonseca is 19 years old and making his first appearance at a Masters 1000 on clay. The Brazilian won the 2024 Next Gen ATP Finals and has been one of the most talked-about young players in the sport. Monte Carlo is his kind of stage — and clay is his best surface. Jack Draper is coming off his best sustained stretch of professional tennis. The British No. 1 has improved dramatically on clay and carries one of the game's better left-handed serves into a draw that suddenly lacks two of its most dangerous players (Djokovic and Rune are absent). Arthur Fils knows what it's like to be close. He lost the Miami Open semifinal badly — 6-2, 6-2 to Lehecka — and will want to use Monte Carlo as the place where he resets his clay-court season. His baseline game is built for red clay. None of these players are likely to win Monte Carlo. But one of them could reach the quarterfinal and alter the bracket in ways that change the whole No. 1 conversation. In a draw this open, the dark horses matter.
