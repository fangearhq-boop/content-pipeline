Svitolina Arrives at Roland Garros as a Genuine Contender — and She Knows It

By Elena Voss | Tennis Fanrecap | May 19, 2026

Ask Elina Svitolina whether she can win Roland Garros this year and she does not deflect the question. "The Rome victory gives me a lot of confidence," the Ukrainian said ahead of the tournament. "I come to Paris feeling good about my game."

There is substance behind the confidence. Svitolina arrives in Paris as the No. 7 seed — elevated after her Rome title — having produced arguably the best week of clay-court tennis by any woman in the 2026 season. She is 31 years old, has never won a Grand Slam, and this may be her most realistic opportunity yet.

The Rome Title That Changed the Conversation

Svitolina's path through the Rome draw would have been remarkable for any player. She beat Elena Rybakina in the quarterfinals. She beat Iga Swiatek in the semifinals. She beat Coco Gauff in the final, 6-4, 6-7(3), 6-2, in a match that required everything she had.

That run made her the first woman in the Italian Open's history to defeat three top-4 players in a single tournament. It was not a lucky bracket or a soft draw — she played through the world No. 4, No. 3, and No. 2 in succession. And she did it on clay, the surface where Swiatek has traditionally been untouchable.

It was her third Italian Open title (she also won in 2017 and 2021), her fourth WTA 1000 title, and her 20th career singles title overall. More relevant to Paris, it also sent a clear message to the rest of the draw.

The Seeding That Matters

Svitolina enters Roland Garros ranked No. 7 in the world — up from No. 10 before Rome — and seeded No. 7 in the draw. The practical implication of that seeding is significant: she cannot be drawn against another top-8 seed until the quarterfinals.

In a draw where Sabalenka, Swiatek, Gauff, and Rybakina are all present, a protected path through the first week gives Svitolina the opportunity to build momentum and confidence before the tournament's defining moments. Any top-8 clash will come only when both players have already proven they belong in the second week.

"She will not face a top-eight ranked player until the quarter-finals, therefore significantly increasing her chances of winning her first Grand Slam title," noted Tennishead ahead of the tournament.

The Grand Slam Question

Svitolina has never won a Grand Slam. The closest she came was the Wimbledon 2019 semifinal and the same stage at the 2021 Tokyo Olympics, where she won the bronze medal. She returned to the tour in 2023 after having a child and has been building steadily toward this kind of form ever since.

In recent years, Svitolina has spoken openly about her identity beyond tennis — a Ukrainian competing while her country is at war, dedicating her Rome title to "Ukrainians from bomb shelters." The emotional context does not diminish the tennis; it seems, if anything, to sharpen her focus.

Roland Garros is not a tournament she has won before. But in 2026, with the Rome title fresh, the seeding protective, and the form genuine, the case for Svitolina has never been stronger.

What's Next

The Roland Garros draw ceremony is Thursday, May 21 at 2:00 PM Paris time. Svitolina will learn her first-round opponent that afternoon. As the No. 7 seed, she will be placed in one of the quarters of the draw that avoids the No. 1, 2, 3, and 4 seeds until the quarterfinals. The main draw begins Sunday, May 24, with the women's final scheduled for Saturday, June 6.
