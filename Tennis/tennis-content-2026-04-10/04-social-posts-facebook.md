### STORY 2: Vacherot Makes Monegasque History

#### Long-Form Post — 12:30 PM ET

There is a particular kind of moment in tennis when a local player does something extraordinary at their home tournament. Thursday afternoon in Monaco produced one of those moments.

Valentin Vacherot, 28 years old, born and raised in Monaco, became the first Monegasque player in the history of the Open Era to reach the quarterfinals at the Monte Carlo Masters. He did it by defeating Hubert Hurkacz — the 2025 finalist — 6-7(4), 6-3, 6-4 in two hours and 53 minutes.

The match demanded everything he had. He lost the first set in a tiebreak, fought back through a physical second set, and won the third with growing conviction. The crowd, which had been with him throughout, understood what was happening by the time the match reached its final stages.

The context makes the result even more meaningful. Vacherot's half-brother, Benjamin Balleret, also Monegasque and also Vacherot's coach, had previously held the tournament's best-ever result by a player from Monaco. Vacherot surpassed it — first by beating Musetti in the previous round to reach the Round of 16, and then by dismantling Hurkacz to reach the last eight.

He now faces fifth seed Alex de Minaur in Friday's quarterfinals.

There are not many moments in sport like this one. A player achieving something unprecedented — in history, in the Open Era — on clay courts he has walked beside since childhood. Vacherot earned this.

### STORY 3: Sinner's 37-Set Streak Ends — He Wins Anyway

#### Long-Form Post — 8:00 AM ET

For 186 days and 37 consecutive Masters 1000 sets, nobody could take a set from Jannik Sinner. The streak began at the Paris Masters last November, survived Indian Wells without dropping a single set, and survived Miami in the same fashion. By the time the clay season opened at Monte Carlo, it had taken on a life of its own.

Tomas Machac ended it in a tiebreak.

Sinner won the first set against the Czech 6-1 — making it 37 consecutive sets. Then Machac found something in the second set, produced cleaner tennis, and took the tiebreak 7-3. The streak was over at 37.

Then Sinner won the third set 6-3.

That is the part of this story worth emphasizing. Machac had done something no player at the Masters 1000 level had managed in over six months. Sinner, rather than letting the moment destabilize him, responded with calm, clean, purposeful tennis. He won the third set without drama and advanced to the quarterfinals 6-1, 6-7(3), 6-3.

He faces Félix Auger-Aliassime in the Monte Carlo quarterfinals on Friday. The No. 1 race with Alcaraz continues. Winning the Monte Carlo title would close the gap to Alcaraz dramatically.

The streak is over. The run continues.

### STORY 5: WTA Linz — Ostapenko Outlasts Eala

#### Long-Form Post — 6:00 PM ET

The match that everyone at the Upper Austria Ladies Linz will be talking about happened on Thursday, and it ended in the most dramatic way possible.

Alexandra Eala led Jelena Ostapenko 5-1 in the second set. She held a set point. Ostapenko had come from behind in the first set too, falling to a deficit before finding her level. At 5-1 in the second, most players would have closed it out.

Ostapenko did not close. Eala's level dropped. The Latvian won six consecutive games to take the second set 7-5 and the match 6-4, 7-5 in one hour and 43 minutes.

The result was significant on multiple levels. Ostapenko had not beaten Eala in their two previous WTA meetings — this was her first win. And for Eala, who had been magnificent through 5-1, the collapse will sting for some time.

Ostapenko advances to face Elena-Gabriela Ruse in the Linz quarterfinals. Also in the QF draw: Donna Vekic vs. Karolina Pliskova. The Linz QFs are today, April 10.

And then attention turns to Stuttgart. The Porsche Tennis Grand Prix begins Monday, April 13, on indoor clay. Aryna Sabalenka (No. 1 seed), Elena Rybakina (No. 2), Coco Gauff (No. 3), and Iga Swiatek (No. 4) are all in the draw. Defending champion is Jelena Ostapenko — who is currently still competing in Linz.

The women's clay season is well and truly alive.
