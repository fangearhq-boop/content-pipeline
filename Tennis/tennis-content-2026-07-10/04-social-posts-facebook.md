### STORY 1: Muchova Saves Match Point, Beats Gauff

#### Long-Form Post — 8:00 AM ET

Coco Gauff was one point away from her first Wimbledon final.

She led 9-8 in the third-set tiebreak. She had match point. The shot was a forehand drop shot — a risky choice in a big moment. It went into the net.

Karolina Muchova exhaled. Then she played the next point like she was born for it — a forehand lob winner at 9-9 that turned the tiebreak around. She won 12-10. Match score: 6-2, 1-6, 7-6(10) in 2 hours and 35 minutes.

It was one of the most dramatic Wimbledon semifinals in years. Gauff had dominated the second set 6-1, setting up a third set that should have been hers. But Wimbledon has a way of finding the player who wants it most in the final moments. Thursday, that was Muchova.

In the other semifinal, Linda Noskova made it look almost unfairly easy — 6-4, 6-4 in 79 minutes against Marta Kostyuk. The Ukrainian barely knew what hit her.

The result: the first all-Czech women's Wimbledon final in history. The first same-nationality women's Grand Slam final since Venus and Serena Williams in the 2017 US Open.

Saturday. Muchova vs. Noskova. Centre Court.

### STORY 2: Women's Final Preview — Muchova vs. Noskova

#### Long-Form Post — 12:30 PM ET

This Saturday, two women will walk onto Centre Court for the Wimbledon women's final. Both are Czech. Both are from Přerov — a city of 43,000 people in the Moravia region of the Czech Republic.

That detail is almost too good to be true. But it is true. Karolina Muchova (29) and Linda Noskova (21) grew up in the same city — and now they're playing for the most famous trophy in women's tennis.

Muchova has been here before. She reached the 2023 French Open final, where she took a set from Iga Swiatek before losing. She's a seasoned veteran of the biggest moments. She got here Thursday by saving a match point in the third-set tiebreak. That is the kind of mental strength it takes to win a Slam.

Noskova, 21, has never been here. But she played like she has — 79 minutes, 6-4, 6-4, Kostyuk never in it. Noskova is the youngest woman in the Wimbledon final since Emma Raducanu won at 18 in 2021. She hits big and doesn't panic.

Czech women have won 12 Wimbledon titles. Navratilova, Novotna, Kvitova, Vondrousova in 2023, Krejcikova in 2024. If a Czech player wins Saturday, it will be three Czech champions in four years.

Who are you rooting for Saturday — the experienced Muchova or the breakthrough Noskova?

### STORY 3: Fery vs. Zverev

#### Long-Form Post — 9:00 AM ET

Right now on Centre Court at Wimbledon, Arthur Fery is playing Alexander Zverev for a place in the final.

Fery is 23 years old. He is ranked No. 114. He was handed a wildcard to enter the tournament. He has six career ATP-level wins — and four of them have come at this Wimbledon. He is in his fifth career Grand Slam main draw appearance. And right now, he is a semifinalist.

He is the fourth men's wildcard in Open Era history to reach a Grand Slam semifinal. The third wildcard to reach the Wimbledon semifinal specifically. The last one was Goran Ivanisevic. In 2001. Ivanisevic won the title.

On the other side: Alexander Zverev. No. 2 seed. 2026 Roland Garros champion. He won Paris by beating Flavio Cobolli in the final. Cobolli is also the player that Fery dismantled 6-4, 7-6(4), 6-0 in the quarterfinals. Wimbledon has given this tournament a shape that feels almost scripted.

Zverev is chasing something called the Channel Slam — winning Roland Garros and Wimbledon in the same calendar year. Only five men have done it in the Open Era: Laver, Borg, Federer, Nadal, and Djokovic.

Fery stands between him and history. Centre Court has seen bigger underdog stories. This tournament already has one of them.

What do you think happens today — does Fery keep the fairy tale going?

### STORY 4: Sinner vs. Djokovic

#### Long-Form Post — 6:00 PM ET

Three days ago, Novak Djokovic played Felix Auger-Aliassime for 5 hours and 15 minutes. It was the longest men's quarterfinal in Wimbledon history. He won 7-6(10), 3-6, 6-3, 6-7(4), 7-6(4).

He played with a left ankle and calf injury from the first set. He took a medical timeout and went back out there. He is 39 years old. He has seven Wimbledon titles. He is not done.

Today he plays Jannik Sinner.

Sinner is the defending champion. He has not been seriously tested this fortnight — after a five-set first-round scare against Kecmanovic, he has won 12 consecutive sets. He reached the Wimbledon semifinal for the third time in his career, and last year he beat Djokovic here in straight sets, 6-3, 6-4, 6-4.

Their head-to-head record stands at 6-5 in Sinner's favor. Their last meeting was the 2026 Australian Open semifinal, where Djokovic came back from a set down to win in five. Sinner won at Wimbledon last year. Djokovic won in Melbourne this January.

The form sheet says Sinner. The precedent says never rule out Djokovic on this surface.

At 39 years and 46 days, Djokovic is the oldest Wimbledon men's semifinalist in the Open Era. If he wins today and Sunday, he will have done something no one has done before.

Today's match is on Centre Court after Fery vs. Zverev. You won't want to miss it.

### STORY 5: Czech Women's Tennis Dynasty

#### Long-Form Post — 9:00 PM ET

Saturday's Wimbledon women's final is all-Czech. To understand why, you have to go back almost 50 years.

Martina Navratilova defected from Czechoslovakia to the United States in 1975. Over the next 15 years, she won nine Wimbledon singles titles — a record that stands to this day. She left behind a generation of young Czech girls who watched her win and decided they wanted the same thing.

Jana Novotna won Wimbledon in 1998 — she had famously cried on the Duchess of Kent's shoulder after losing the 1993 final, and she finally got her title five years later. Petra Kvitova won in 2011, beating Sharapova, and again in 2014. Marketa Vondrousova won in 2023, the first unseeded Wimbledon champion since 1963. Barbora Krejcikova won in 2024.

Czech women: 12 Wimbledon singles titles.

And now Karolina Muchova and Linda Noskova — both from the Czech city of Přerov, population 43,000 — are playing each other in Saturday's final. One of them will add title No. 13 to a dynasty that began before most of today's top players were born.

This isn't an accident. Czech tennis has spent decades building an infrastructure — the right coaching, the right courts, the right competitive environment — to turn young talent into Wimbledon champions. The dynasty is structural.

The question Saturday is only which part of the pipeline claims the trophy.
