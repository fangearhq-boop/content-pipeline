Fery vs. Zverev: British Wild Card Meets Channel Slam Chaser in Wimbledon Semifinal

By Marcus Cole | Tennis Fanrecap | July 10, 2026

Wimbledon, London

The 2001 men's wildcard at Wimbledon was Goran Ivanisevic. He was ranked No. 125 when the draw was made. He reached the final. He won the title. He cried on the grass. It is one of the most replayed moments in tennis history.

Today on Centre Court, Arthur Fery — 23 years old, ranked No. 114, also a wildcard — plays Alexander Zverev for a place in the 2026 Wimbledon final. The parallel is not subtle. Fery has not claimed it publicly. The sport does not care whether he claims it.

The Wildcard Who Keeps Winning

Fery is in his fifth career Grand Slam main draw appearance. He had six career ATP tour-level wins before this fortnight. He needed a wildcard to enter the draw. He is the fourth men's wildcard to reach a Grand Slam semifinal in the Open Era — after Jimmy Connors at the 1991 US Open, Henri Leconte at the 1992 French Open, and Ivanisevic at the 2001 Wimbledon.

His path here has not been soft. He defeated Flavio Cobolli — the No. 9 seed and a player ranked in the top 10 of the world — 6-4, 7-6(4), 6-0 in the quarterfinals, including a 6-0 third set that was one of the most dominant displays of the fortnight. Before that, he beat players he was not supposed to beat in rounds he was not supposed to reach.

Fery is 23. He has broad shoulders and a serve that works on grass and a game that was built — partly through the resources of his family, his father Loic Fery is a hedge fund manager and president of French football club FC Lorient — for exactly these conditions. The "posh wildcard" narrative has followed him all fortnight. The results do not fit the dismissal.

Zverev and the Channel Slam

On the other side of the net is the Roland Garros champion. Alexander Zverev won the French Open on June 7, defeating Flavio Cobolli — yes, the same player — in the final. Now he is at Wimbledon, in his first career semifinal here, with a chance to complete what tennis calls the Channel Slam: winning Roland Garros and Wimbledon in the same calendar year.

Five men have done it in the Open Era. Rod Laver. Björn Borg. Roger Federer. Rafael Nadal. Novak Djokovic. If Zverev wins today and Sunday, he becomes the sixth. The company he would join is not a list that needs introduction.

Zverev reached this semifinal by beating Taylor Fritz 6-4, 6-4, 6-2 in the quarterfinals, a result that reversed a losing streak against the American that had stretched to seven consecutive matches since Rome 2024. He is playing well. He serves big, his forehand is a consistent weapon on grass, and he has reached a level of composure in major tournaments that eluded him for much of his career.

The Match

Zverev is the heavy favorite. He is the higher-ranked player, the more experienced Grand Slam competitor, and a player whose serve and groundstroke power match up well against the surfaces and conditions at Wimbledon.

But Fery has beaten everyone put in front of him this fortnight. He did not play a bad match. He has not been broken in situations where being broken would have mattered. And he is playing at home, at Wimbledon, in front of a crowd that wills him into every point.

The match begins today at 1:30 PM BST on Centre Court. The last wildcard to play on Centre Court at Wimbledon in a semifinal won the title. History does not repeat. But it sometimes rhymes.

What's Next

The winner of today's match plays either Jannik Sinner or Novak Djokovic in the Wimbledon men's singles final on Sunday, July 12. If Zverev wins today and Sunday, he completes the Channel Slam and joins five men in Open Era history. If Fery wins today, he becomes the story of the 2026 summer — a wildcard whose run mirrors the one Ivanisevic made 25 years ago on the same courts.
