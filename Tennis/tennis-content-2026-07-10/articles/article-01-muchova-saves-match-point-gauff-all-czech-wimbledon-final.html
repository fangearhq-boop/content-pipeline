Muchova Saves Match Point, Outlasts Gauff in Tiebreak Thriller — All-Czech Wimbledon Final Set

By Elena Voss | Tennis Fanrecap | July 10, 2026

Wimbledon, London

Coco Gauff was a forehand drop shot away from the Wimbledon final. She led 9-8 in the third-set tiebreak. The match point was there. She went for the drop shot. It went into the net.

Karolina Muchova took a breath and played the next three points better than Gauff played the last one. She won the tiebreak 12-10. She won the match 6-2, 1-6, 7-6(10) in 2 hours and 35 minutes. And she is in her first Wimbledon final.

In the other semifinal, Linda Noskova made the afternoon feel almost routine — 6-4, 6-4 in 79 minutes against Marta Kostyuk. Two Czech women in the semifinals of Wimbledon. Two Czech women through to the final. The first all-Czech women's Wimbledon final in history is set for Saturday.

The Tiebreak That Decided Everything

The first set belonged to Muchova entirely. She moved well, dictated on the forehand, and Gauff never settled into the sort of baseline rhythm that defines her at her best. Muchova won it 6-2 without a great deal of drama.

The second set was a different match. Gauff found her timing and Muchova's level dropped — 6-1 to the American in a set that lasted barely twenty minutes. The first player to take the third set would win, and by the time it reached the tiebreak, neither had given it away.

At 9-8 in the tiebreak, Gauff was serving and held match point. The drop shot was ambitious. In a different moment, it might have worked. This one caught the tape and died in the net.

Muchova answered immediately. At 9-9, she hit a forehand lob over a charging Gauff — clean, precise, exactly what the moment required. It put her up 10-9 with the match on her racket. Gauff put a forehand into the net on the next point. The title was Muchova's to fight for on Saturday.

"I can't describe how I feel," Muchova said afterwards. "I knew I had to stay in every point. She's a great champion. I'm just really happy to be in the final."

Noskova's Demolition

The second semifinal offered no such drama. Linda Noskova, 21, the No. 9 seed, was simply better than Marta Kostyuk from the first game. She served with authority, attacked from the back of the court with pace and depth, and gave Kostyuk no angles to work with.

Noskova won 79% of her first-serve points. She hit 22 winners against 8 unforced errors. The 6-4, 6-4 scoreline flatters Kostyuk slightly — the Ukrainian was never genuinely in the match after Noskova broke for 3-1 in the first set.

Noskova is 21 years old and this is her first Grand Slam final. She showed no signs of nerves on Thursday. Saturday will be another story — but if her semifinal is any indication, she arrived at Wimbledon ready for this.

Historic Final

The all-Czech final is the first of its kind in Wimbledon women's singles history. It is also the first same-nationality Grand Slam women's final since Venus and Serena Williams met in the 2017 US Open final — a nine-year gap that ends on Centre Court this Saturday.

Wimbledon 2026 is guaranteed to produce a first-time women's Grand Slam champion. Neither Muchova nor Noskova has won a major. Noskova has never been in a Slam final before. Muchova lost the 2023 French Open final to Iga Swiatek. One of them gets their title Saturday.

What's Next

The Wimbledon women's singles final takes place on Saturday, July 11, on Centre Court. Muchova vs. Noskova. Two women from the same Czech city of Přerov. Two players building on a tennis legacy that stretches from Martina Navratilova through Petra Kvitova, Marketa Vondrousova, and Barbora Krejcikova. One of them adds to it Saturday.
