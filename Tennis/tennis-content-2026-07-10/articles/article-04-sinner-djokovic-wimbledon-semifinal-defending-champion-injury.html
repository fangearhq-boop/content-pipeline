Sinner vs. Djokovic: Defending Champion Faces a 39-Year-Old Wimbledon Legend Playing Through Injury

By Elena Voss | Tennis Fanrecap | July 10, 2026

Wimbledon, London

Novak Djokovic played 5 hours and 15 minutes in his quarterfinal against Felix Auger-Aliassime three days ago. He injured his left ankle and calf in the first set, took a medical timeout, and kept playing. He won 7-6(10), 3-6, 6-3, 6-7(4), 7-6(4). It was the longest men's quarterfinal in Wimbledon history.

Today he plays Jannik Sinner.

Sinner is the defending champion. He has not lost a set since the first round of this tournament — a five-set match against Miomir Kecmanovic that he came through to start the fortnight. Since then he has won 12 consecutive sets. He has reached his third Wimbledon semifinal and has not been seriously tested in ten days.

The contrast between the two men walking onto Centre Court today is as sharp as anything this Wimbledon has offered.

Djokovic's Body, Djokovic's Will

Djokovic is 39 years old and 46 days — the oldest Wimbledon men's singles semifinalist in the Open Era. The only player older in a Wimbledon semifinal in any era was Ken Rosewall, who was 39 years and 243 days old when he reached the 1974 last four.

The ankle and calf injury from the FAA quarterfinal remains the central question around Djokovic's semifinal. Reports from Thursday's practice suggested the ankle was sore but manageable. In the past, Djokovic has played through worse at Wimbledon. But there is a difference between managing an injury through a five-set war and managing it again three days later against the No. 1 player in the world, on the same tired legs.

The 5-hour quarterfinal cost something. How much is what today answers.

Sinner's Case

The argument for Sinner is simple: he is the best player in the world, he has been the best player in the world for over a year, and he is playing the best tennis of his career on grass at the venue where he is the defending champion.

His path to the semifinal included wins over Kecmanovic, Nuno Borges, Jenson Brooksby, Shintaro Mochizuki, and Jan-Lennard Struff — the last of whom he beat in the quarterfinals in comfortable straight sets. He has dropped just one set in five matches and that came in the very first round. He arrives here as fresh as anyone left in the draw.

Last year at Wimbledon, Sinner beat Djokovic 6-3, 6-4, 6-4 in this same stage of the tournament. Sinner leads their head-to-head 6-5 overall. The statistics favor him. The form chart favors him.

The Case for Djokovic

The argument against ruling out Djokovic begins and ends with the surface. He has seven Wimbledon titles. The last one came in 2022. He won on this grass when it mattered most for years. His movement has changed with age, but his serve remains elite, his sliced backhand is still one of the hardest shots in tennis to attack on grass, and his capacity to raise his game in a crisis — as the FAA quarterfinal proved — has not left him.

Their last meeting was the 2026 Australian Open semifinal, where Djokovic came back from a set down to win 3-6, 6-3, 4-6, 6-4, 6-4 in just over four hours. That result was a reminder that the head-to-head stat is not the whole story. Djokovic knows how to beat Sinner when the moment demands it.

If his ankle holds, this match goes the distance. If it does not, Sinner wins comfortably and the story writes itself. The variable no one can fully know walking onto Centre Court today is Djokovic's body.

What's Next

The winner of today's match plays in the Wimbledon men's singles final on Sunday, July 12, against the winner of Fery vs. Zverev. For Sinner, a title defense. For Djokovic, a chance at an eighth Wimbledon title — and his 25th Grand Slam overall — that would set new boundaries for what is possible at 39. The match begins today on Centre Court following Fery vs. Zverev.
