From Navratilova to Noskova: How Czech Women Built Tennis's Greatest Wimbledon Dynasty

By Ryan Calloway | Tennis Fanrecap | July 10, 2026

Wimbledon, London

Saturday's Wimbledon women's final features two Czech players from the same city. Karolina Muchova and Linda Noskova both grew up in Přerov, a Moravian city of 43,000. It sounds improbable. It is. And yet within the context of Czech women's tennis, it makes a kind of perfect, cumulative sense.

Czech women have won 12 Wimbledon singles titles. The list of countries with more is short, and most entries on it are called "United States." This is not a country that stumbled into tennis excellence. It built it over half a century.

The Foundation: Navratilova

Martina Navratilova was born in Prague in 1956. She defected to the United States in 1975, at 18, when she could already see the limits that Czechoslovakia's communist government would place on her career. She became an American citizen, but her roots are Czech, and the girls who grew up watching her win at Wimbledon grew up in Czech towns just like Přerov.

Navratilova won nine Wimbledon singles titles between 1978 and 1990. Nine. The number is so large it sounds like a misprint. She won six in a row between 1982 and 1987. On the surface that caused other players to slip, her footwork never failed. On the type of grass that rewarded net play, her serve-and-volley game was devastating. She is the greatest Wimbledon women's singles champion in history, and she did it as a player who once held a Czech passport.

The girls who followed her — through state television, through coaches who had watched her compete, through a national mythology that formed around what she had done — grew up knowing that the All England Club was a place where a Czech woman could win.

The Lineage

Jana Novotna won in 1998, her third Wimbledon final after losing famously in 1993 — she cried on the shoulder of the Duchess of Kent in the Royal Box — and again in 1997. Novotna was elegant, serve-and-volley oriented, and a player who made Wimbledon her spiritual home despite needing years to close out a title on it.

Petra Kvitova won in 2011, beating Maria Sharapova in a display of left-handed power that made the grass look like a weapon. She won again in 2014 against Eugenie Bouchard. Two titles in four years from a player who, at her best on grass, had no peers.

Then the gap. Czech women were visible but not dominant at SW19 through the middle 2010s. Krejcikova and Vondrousova were developing. The pipeline was refilling.

Marketa Vondrousova won in 2023 as an unseeded player — the first unseeded women's champion at Wimbledon since 1963 — and she did it without a single moment of panic in the final against Ons Jabeur. Barbora Krejcikova won in 2024, beating Jasmine Paolini.

Přerov, Population 43,000

It is worth sitting with the detail that both Saturday's finalists come from the same city. Přerov sits in eastern Moravia, between Olomouc and Ostrava. It is not a large city by any measure. It has produced two women who are, right now, two of the best grass-court tennis players in the world.

The explanation is partly structural — Czech tennis clubs have access to the kind of developmental infrastructure that only decades of sustained investment can build. The explanation is also cultural: winning at Wimbledon is not abstract for young Czech players. They have names on the wall. They have stories to follow. Navratilova. Novotna. Kvitova. Vondrousova. Krejcikova.

Muchova and Noskova are the next two names.

What's Next

One of them wins Saturday, July 11, on Centre Court. The winner becomes the Czech Republic's 13th Wimbledon singles champion, and the third Czech women's champion in four years. The other returns to the tour with a Wimbledon final on her resume that she did not have two weeks ago. Either way, the pipeline continues. From Navratilova to Noskova, Czech women at Wimbledon are not finished.
