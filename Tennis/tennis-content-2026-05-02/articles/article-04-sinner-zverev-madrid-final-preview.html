Sinner vs Zverev: Sunday's Madrid Final Has History on the Line

By Ryan Calloway | Tennis Fanrecap

May 2, 2026 — Madrid, Spain

Sunday's Mutua Madrid Open ATP final brings together two players who have spent the last 18 months writing chapters into the sport's record books. Jannik Sinner will chase something that has never been done. Alexander Zverev will try to stop him — and in doing so, end one of the longest losing streaks of his career.

The final is scheduled not before 5 PM CEST on the Manolo Santana court. For context: no player in 36 years of Masters 1000 tennis has won five consecutive events at this level. Sinner has four in a row.

What Sinner Is Chasing

The four consecutive Masters 1000 titles Sinner currently holds came in succession — Paris (October 2025), Indian Wells, Miami, and Monte-Carlo in 2026. A fifth would give him a record that has eluded Djokovic, Nadal, and Federer — the three men who defined the Masters 1000 era before him.

Djokovic and Nadal each managed four consecutive titles at various points in their careers. Nobody got to five. The closest anyone came was Djokovic in 2015 and Nadal in his clay-dominant years, and both came up short. Sinner now stands one match from the mark they never reached.

His overall Masters 1000 winning streak stands at 27 matches heading into Sunday. He enters having not faced a break point in his entire semifinal. He has not lost a Masters 1000 match since October 2025.

Zverev's Case

Alexander Zverev has won the last match between these two players exactly zero times in eight attempts. That streak runs back to Cincinnati in August 2024. In that period, Zverev has been a top-3 player in the world for much of it — this is not a case of losing to someone far superior in ranking. Sinner simply finds a way.

And yet Zverev arrives at this final playing some of the best tennis of the clay season. He won 85 percent of first-serve points in his semifinal against Blockx. He hit 35 winners. He never faced a break point. Against the kind of form Zverev showed on Friday night, many opponents would have lost badly. Blockx competed well in the second set and still could not hold back the tide.

Zverev is also a two-time Madrid champion (2018 and 2021). He knows this court, this crowd, this altitude. He has won here before and he will arrive Sunday with the belief that he can win here again.

The Tactical Chess Match

Both players serve well. Both can construct points with patient baseline play and accelerate when the chance arrives. The player who dominates the tiebreaks may well decide the final — and both have shown under pressure this week that their nerves hold when the moment demands it.

Sinner's key advantage is his return. He converts break point opportunities at a high rate and makes opponents feel permanent pressure on their second serve. Zverev will need to win more free points on first serve than he has in recent meetings to limit those opportunities.

What's Next

Sunday's Madrid final closes the Mutua Madrid Open and marks the penultimate Masters 1000 event before Roland Garros. The French Open begins May 18 — two weeks from now. The form, momentum, and ranking points from this final will carry directly into Paris. A Sinner win writes the story of the pre-Roland Garros season in one sentence. A Zverev win reopens conversations that have been largely closed for eight months.
