Roland Garros 2026: Alcaraz Out, Djokovic Doubtful, Sinner Arrives on a Historic Streak

By Marcus Cole | Tennis Fanrecap

May 2, 2026 — Paris, France (preview)

Roland Garros begins May 18 — sixteen days from today — and the draw for the French Open has never looked more open heading into the clay Grand Slam. Two of the top contenders are missing before the first ball is struck, and the player who arrives with the most momentum is a 23-year-old Italian who has not lost a Masters 1000 match since October.

The Absences That Change Everything

Carlos Alcaraz, the defending Roland Garros champion, is almost certain to miss Paris. The Spaniard suffered a right wrist injury that has sidelined him since withdrawing from the Barcelona Open in mid-April. He has not played since, and there has been no confirmed timeline for his return. Missing Roland Garros would represent the biggest injury blow of Alcaraz's career to date.

Novak Djokovic has been absent from the entire clay season with a shoulder problem. He skipped Monte-Carlo, Madrid, and has not confirmed he will be ready for Paris. At 38 years old, Djokovic's approach to his schedule has changed significantly, and the chances of him making the main draw at Roland Garros appear more uncertain with each passing week.

Their combined absence removes two of the most credible challengers to Sinner from the men's draw before practice rounds even begin.

Sinner: The Favorite

Jannik Sinner arrives at Roland Garros — whatever happens in Sunday's Madrid final — as the clear No. 1 seed and the clearest favorite. His Masters 1000 winning streak stands at 27 or more. He has won four consecutive events at that level. He has not dropped a set in the Madrid semifinal against Fils. He is operating at a level of sustained excellence that has not been seen at the Masters 1000 tier in the history of the series.

Sinner has never won Roland Garros. He reached the quarterfinals last year before falling in four sets. But he arrives in 2026 as a different player — more experienced, with a serve that has taken steps forward, and a return game that is arguably the best in the world.

The Women's Draw: Five Credible Champions

The women's Roland Garros field may be stronger than the men's in terms of competitive depth. Aryna Sabalenka — last year's finalist and current world No. 1 — is the top seed. Coco Gauff, the defending champion, arrives having recovered from an illness that disrupted her Madrid campaign. Iga Swiatek has won Roland Garros four times and remains a threat every time the red clay of Paris comes around.

Elena Rybakina won Stuttgart in April and is finding form at the right time. And then there is Mirra Andreeva — 19 years old, 12-1 on clay in 2026, in the Madrid final today. Andreeva may arrive at Roland Garros as the most in-form player in the entire women's draw, which makes her the most compelling story heading into the clay major.

What's Next

The Roland Garros entry lists have been released and the tournament begins May 18 with qualifying matches starting May 11. Between now and then, the Italian Open in Rome (May 6-18) represents the final clay tune-up for most of the leading contenders. Sinner may choose to skip Rome to rest before Paris — a decision that will be closely watched. Full draw details and seedings will be confirmed once the ATP and WTA rankings are locked in on the Monday before the tournament begins.
