Serena Williams' Wimbledon Return Ends in the First Round — Maya Joint Beats Seven-Time Champion in Three Sets

By Ryan Calloway | July 1, 2026

The 44-year-old seven-time Wimbledon champion lost to 20-year-old Australian qualifier Maya Joint 6-3, 6-7(6), 6-3 on Centre Court on Tuesday — her first Grand Slam singles match in nearly four years.

Centre Court was as full and as loud as it ever is before a match that carries real weight. Serena Williams walked out on Tuesday afternoon for her first singles appearance at a Grand Slam in close to four years, and the All England Club greeted her with the kind of reception that belongs to someone who has done what she has done here. Seven titles. Twenty-three Grand Slam singles championships. An entire era of women's tennis that bore her imprint.

Maya Joint walked out the other side of the net. She is 20 years old. She is Australian. She was ranked No. 87 in the world coming into the match, and she had lost her last 11 matches on tour. You could have been forgiven, in the noise and the occasion of the moment, for barely noticing she was there.

Joint won the match 6-3, 6-7(6), 6-3.

The Match

The first set went to Joint without drama or difficulty. Williams played with the urgency of a competitor who has not forgotten how to compete. But Joint was consistently a step ahead — holding her serve, reading Williams's patterns, and accepting the moment without visibly feeling the weight of it. She took the first set 6-3 in the manner of a player who understood her task and executed it.

The second set changed the emotional register of the afternoon. Williams found more of her game. The exchanges grew longer. Centre Court found a different gear. And when the set reached a tiebreak, Williams — serving at 44 years old, in her first competitive singles match since the 2022 US Open, against an opponent half her age — won it 7-6(6). The crowd response was not polite. It was genuine and loud and full of what tennis crowds feel when something unexpected pushes back against the expected ending.

It lasted one more set.

Joint absorbed the second set loss and came out for the third exactly as she had played the first. The match ended 6-3 to Joint. It was not a close third set. Williams had offered everything she had, and what she had was not enough to win three sets on Tuesday.

What Joint Did

The temptation with this match is to focus entirely on Serena Williams — the return, the age, the trophies, the emotion of the occasion. But Joint did something real here, and it deserves the same attention.

She was on an 11-match losing streak when she walked onto Centre Court. Eleven consecutive defeats, the kind of run that erodes confidence and makes every court feel hostile. Against that backdrop, against this opponent, in front of this crowd, she played two complete sets of composed, high-level tennis. She lost one set. She found a way through. She did not panic in the second when Williams won the tiebreak, and she did not overthink the third.

That is not nothing. That is, in fact, a considerable achievement for a 20-year-old who had not won a match since January.

The Larger Picture

Williams's return to singles was always going to be an event shaped by uncertainty. She had not played a Grand Slam singles match since losing to Ajla Tomljanovic at the 2022 US Open in the third round — a match after which she suggested it might be her last. She then went quiet on the singles circuit. The wild card request, when it came, was genuinely surprising.

At 44, the physical demands of a three-set match are different from what they were at 34 or 24. Williams showed in the second set that she can still produce moments of genuine quality. But moments — even beautiful ones — are not matches, and matches are what Grand Slams require.

She is still entered in the doubles draw alongside Venus Williams. The sisters in doubles is its own story, separate from what happened on Tuesday afternoon, and one that the All England Club will follow closely.

What's Next

Maya Joint advances to the second round of Wimbledon 2026. She will enter it as a qualifier who has already won the match that nobody expected her to win. Whatever happens next, the win over Serena Williams on Centre Court will define her tournament regardless of how far she goes.

For Serena Williams, the singles chapter at Wimbledon 2026 ends here. She came back. She won a set tiebreak on Centre Court at 44 years old. She lost in three sets to a 20-year-old who was on an 11-match losing streak. Tennis, as a sport, does not arrange endings cleanly. Tuesday was proof of that.
