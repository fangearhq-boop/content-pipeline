Sinner Chases History in Madrid: Five Consecutive Masters 1000 Titles Has Never Been Done

By Elena Voss | Tennis Fanrecap

April 23, 2026

Paris. Indian Wells. Miami. Monte Carlo. Four consecutive Masters 1000 titles, and Jannik Sinner arrives at the 2026 Mutua Madrid Open needing just one more to do something that has never been done in the Open Era. Not by Federer. Not by Nadal. Not by Djokovic.

Five consecutive Masters 1000 titles. The record sits there, unclaimed, waiting for the right player at the right moment. Sinner, 24, enters as the No. 1 seed at the Caja Mágica carrying a 17-match winning streak and one of the most dominant stretches of form men's tennis has seen in years.

The Streak That Put Him Here

Since a quarterfinal loss in Doha in February, Sinner has not lost a match. He won the Paris Masters last November — an indoor hard-court title — then swept the North American hard-court swing, taking Indian Wells and Miami without dropping a set in either final. He followed that by winning the Monte Carlo Masters on clay, defeating Alcaraz in the final 7-6(5), 6-3.

The streak spans surfaces. It spans months. It spans four of the biggest events in tennis outside of the Grand Slams. And it now arrives in Madrid, a city where Sinner has never claimed the title.

"I know it's a challenge," Sinner said ahead of the tournament. "But I'm in good shape, I'm feeling good, and I want to keep going."

Why This Record Is So Hard to Break

The Masters 1000 series — now comprising nine events per year played on three surfaces across four continents — was designed precisely to resist dominance. Winning two in a row is remarkable. Three is a dynasty moment. Four, as Sinner has done, enters elite historical company.

Djokovic came closest to five consecutive Masters titles in 2015 and again in 2016, but never managed the run. Nadal dominated the clay Masters calendar at his peak but never strung five across all surfaces. The sheer breadth of the series — Paris indoor, Indian Wells desert hard, Miami outdoor hard, Monte Carlo clay, Madrid altitude clay — makes a consecutive streak across different conditions an extraordinary ask.

Sinner's 2026 run has done exactly that. He has adapted to every surface and every context.

An Open Draw at the Perfect Time

Five major withdrawals have reshaped the Madrid field before the first match was played. Carlos Alcaraz — the player who would represent Sinner's most dangerous rival — is at home with a wrist injury. Novak Djokovic is out. Taylor Fritz withdrew. Jack Draper is gone. Frances Tiafoe pulled out.

The result is a draw that, while still formidable, has fewer landmines than any comparable Masters event. Sinner opens in the second round against Benjamin Bonzi or qualifier Titouan Droguet. He leads Bonzi 3-0 in their career head-to-head, though he has needed a deciding set against the Frenchman twice. He has never played Droguet.

Alexander Zverev is the No. 2 seed and the most credible obstacle. The German won Madrid in 2018 and 2021 and is dangerous on clay. Arthur Fils — who just won Barcelona — is unseeded but dangerous. Casper Ruud, the defending champion, defends 1,000 ranking points.

What's Next

Sinner is expected to play his second round on Friday, April 24, or Saturday, April 25, depending on the schedule. If he advances through the early rounds cleanly, the quarterfinals arrive around April 28-29, with the final set for May 3. Five consecutive Masters 1000 titles would be an Open Era first — and would cement Sinner's 2025-2026 stretch as one of the most remarkable runs of results the men's game has seen in the modern era.
