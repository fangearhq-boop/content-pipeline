Alcaraz Awaits Wrist Test Results With Five Weeks Until Roland Garros

By Ryan Calloway | Tennis Fanrecap

April 23, 2026

Carlos Alcaraz is waiting. He sits at home with a wrist splint on his right hand, waiting on medical test results that will tell him whether he can attempt to defend his French Open title on May 25. And right now, everyone who follows tennis is waiting with him.

The injury came fast and without much warning. In the opening set of his Barcelona Open first-round match against Finland's Otto Virtanen on April 14, Alcaraz felt something wrong in his right wrist. He finished the match. He withdrew from the tournament the next day. He then withdrew from Madrid — his home country's biggest clay event. And now the question that matters is whether Roland Garros is even possible.

The Injury and What It Means

Alcaraz has not disclosed the precise nature of his wrist injury beyond confirming it affected his right wrist — his dominant hand — during match play at Barcelona. He is wearing a wrist splint and awaiting imaging or specialist results that will clarify the extent of the damage.

In the immediate aftermath of the withdrawal from Madrid, Alcaraz addressed the French Open question head-on. His words were measured — the words of a player who has thought carefully about the risk and doesn't want to compound the problem.

"If I push to play Roland-Garros, it could harm me for the tournaments that follow," Alcaraz said. "I'd rather come back maybe a little later, but in great shape, than come back quickly and risk making this injury worse."

He added: "I would rather come back later and in top condition than return too soon and not be ready." And crucially: "I can't give a timeline for my return."

The Math Is Difficult

Roland Garros 2026 begins on May 25. That is roughly five weeks from now. Even in the most optimistic scenario — test results come back clean this week, the injury heals quickly, Alcaraz resumes practice within ten days — he would arrive in Paris without a competitive match on clay since his abbreviated Barcelona R1.

The reigning champion, defending 2,000 ranking points on the clay of Roland Garros, without a competitive warm-up. The stakes are enormous on multiple levels.

From a ranking standpoint, Alcaraz has already lost ground. He withdrew from Barcelona (missing potential points) and now Madrid (missing the chance to gain from a favorable draw). Meanwhile, Jannik Sinner is competing in Madrid, playing every match, and adding to his points tally. Sinner's lead at No. 1 grows with each day Alcaraz is sidelined.

The French Open Title Is on the Line

This is not simply an injury story. It is the potential unraveling of something extraordinary. Alcaraz won Roland Garros in 2024, then defended it in 2025 — adding to his collection of four Grand Slam titles, all won before his 23rd birthday. A second consecutive French Open title would put him in company that only the sport's all-time greats have occupied.

The test results, expected within days, will provide the next chapter. If the news is good, he will have a tight but manageable window to recover, return to practice, and prepare for Paris. If the news is bad, the entire clay season could be lost — and so could the defense of one of the most prestigious titles in tennis.

What's Next

Alcaraz is expected to receive and share his medical results within the coming days. The final decision on his Roland Garros entry would need to be made well before May 25 — players typically need to confirm entries approximately two weeks in advance. Until the tests speak, all timelines remain open. The tennis world is holding its breath.
