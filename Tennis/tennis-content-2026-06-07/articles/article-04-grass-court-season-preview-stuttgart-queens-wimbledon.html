Clay Is Done. Wimbledon Season Begins Monday — A Preview of the 2026 Grass Court Swing

By Elena Voss | June 7, 2026

The men's final at Roland Garros takes place today, and when the last ball is struck on Court Philippe-Chatrier, the calendar flips. Monday morning the Stuttgart ATP 250 begins, and the grass-court season — culminating at Wimbledon — is underway. The transition happens faster than it might seem: from slow, high-bouncing red clay to low, skidding green grass in six days.

For players who have spent six weeks adjusting their games for clay, the pivot to grass demands an immediate and comprehensive reset.

Stuttgart: The First Grass Test

The ATP 250 event in Stuttgart, Germany, runs from June 8-14 and serves as the traditional opening week of the grass-court season on the men's side. The confirmed field includes Taylor Fritz, Matteo Berrettini, Nick Kyrgios, and Jakub Mensik — players whose games translate well to fast surfaces, where the serve is the dominant weapon and net approaches earn points that would be impossible on clay.

Berrettini, in particular, is a player whose career has been defined by his grass-court ability. When healthy, he is one of the most dangerous players in the world on this surface — a 2021 Wimbledon finalist whose serve and forehand were built for exactly this moment in the calendar. Stuttgart has historically been where players find their grass-court feet before the more prestigious events that follow.

For Mensik, coming off a Roland Garros semifinal run that announced him to a global audience, the Stuttgart week represents an immediate test of adaptability. Going from a clay semifinal to a grass 250 in days is precisely the kind of transition that separates players who are technically broad from those who are specialists.

Queen's Club and Beyond

Following Stuttgart, the HSBC Championships at Queen's Club in London — one of the oldest and most prestigious grass events on the calendar — follows for the men, alongside the ongoing WTA grass swing at s-Hertogenbosch. These events serve as the final preparation ground before Wimbledon, where the grass-court season reaches its peak.

Queen's Club is a particular proving ground. Historically, players who win Queen's carry genuine momentum into Wimbledon; the surface and conditions are similar, the mind is in grass-court mode, and the grass-specific footwork and timing are properly calibrated. The opposite is also true — players who struggle at Queen's often find the transition isn't working, and must find their game fast.

The Surface Challenge

Moving from clay to grass isn't simply a change of footwear. Every element of the game must be recalibrated. The ball stays lower after the bounce, reducing the time to set up groundstrokes. The topspin that works on clay — generating high-kicking balls that sit up for the opponent — becomes less effective when the ball slides through the surface rather than bouncing up. Serve-and-volley tactics, largely extinct on clay, become viable again.

Players with flat, attacking games — big servers and aggressive baseliners who like to shorten the point — typically thrive in the transition. Players whose games are built on high-bouncing topspin and physical endurance often need more time to adapt.

How the Roland Garros finalists adjust will be one of the most interesting stories of the next three weeks.

What's Next

The 2026 grass court season opens with the ATP 250 in Stuttgart (June 8-14) and the WTA event in s-Hertogenbosch. The HSBC Championships at Queen's Club and the Eastbourne International follow, with Wimbledon as the season's centrepiece. Full grass season schedules are available at atptour.com and wtatennis.com.
