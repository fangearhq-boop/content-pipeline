Zverev vs Cobolli: The Roland Garros Men's Final Is Today — One of Them Wins Their First Major

By Elena Voss | June 7, 2026

Court Philippe-Chatrier hosts the Roland Garros men's singles final on Sunday afternoon, and the outcome is guaranteed to produce a first-time Grand Slam champion. Alexander Zverev and Flavio Cobolli are both playing in their first Roland Garros final, and only one of them has been in a Grand Slam final before — and he's yet to win one.

The match begins not before 3:00 PM Paris time — 9:00 AM ET — following the conclusion of the men's doubles final. It will be played on the tournament's centrepiece court, in front of a packed crowd, with the Coupe des Mousquetaires waiting for whoever breaks through.

The Man Who Has Been Here Before

Alexander Zverev stands at perhaps the most significant crossroads of his career. At 28, he has lost three Grand Slam finals — the 2020 US Open to Dominic Thiem in five sets, the 2024 Roland Garros final to Carlos Alcaraz, and the 2025 Australian Open final to Jannik Sinner. Each time, a first major title has arrived within reach and then slipped away.

His route to this final has been commanding. He dismissed Jakub Mensik in the semifinals, 7-5, 6-2, 3-6, 6-3, over three hours and one minute — a match that Mensik complicated with a medical timeout in the third set before Zverev reasserted himself to go up 3-0 in the fourth and close it out. On clay, in the conditions he was built for, Zverev's serve and forehand have been consistently dominant throughout the fortnight.

He leads the head-to-head against Cobolli 3-1. He beat the Italian in straight sets at last year's Roland Garros. The numbers favour him.

The Man Who Is Here for the First Time

Flavio Cobolli's path to Sunday's final is one of the more unusual in recent Grand Slam history. The No. 10 seed reached the title match after Matteo Arnaldi — his opponent in the first all-Italian Grand Slam semifinal in history — withdrew on Friday morning with a viral illness. Cobolli advanced via walkover, one of only four in Open Era Grand Slam semifinal history, to earn his place in today's final without hitting a ball in the last round.

That does not diminish what he achieved to get there. Cobolli defeated Lorenzo Musetti and Felix Auger-Aliassime among others in reaching the semifinal stage. He is ranked inside the world's top 15. And critically, he beat Zverev in Munich earlier in the 2026 clay season — the most recent installment in their head-to-head.

If Cobolli wins today, he becomes the first Italian man to win a Grand Slam title since Adriano Panatta won Roland Garros in 1976. The weight of that context is on both players — though it falls more squarely on the Italian.

What the Match Could Turn On

Zverev's serve will be the decisive weapon. He generates free points consistently from his first delivery and can neutralise the returner early in rallies. On clay, that service advantage is somewhat reduced — the surface slows the ball and gives returners more time — but Zverev's movement and groundstroke consistency have both improved significantly from the versions of himself that lost previous finals.

Cobolli's best path runs through the Zverev forehand, which under sustained pressure can become erratic. The Italian has the ability to construct rallies on clay, vary pace, and use the cross-court angle effectively. His Munich win over Zverev demonstrated that the German's game is not without vulnerabilities — particularly when Cobolli gets him on the run wide to his backhand.

The mental dimension will matter enormously. Zverev has navigated final stages of Grand Slams before and knows what the noise sounds like. Cobolli is entering territory he has never experienced. That experience gap could be the difference — or Cobolli's clean slate could liberate him.
