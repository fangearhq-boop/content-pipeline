Roland Garros 2026 Was One for the History Books — Every Record That Fell This Fortnight

By Ryan Calloway | June 7, 2026

The 2026 Roland Garros is not yet complete — the men's final between Alexander Zverev and Flavio Cobolli takes place this afternoon — but the fortnight has already secured its place as one of the most historically dense Grand Slam tournaments in recent memory. From the first round to today's final, the tournament produced milestones that haven't been seen in decades, or in some cases, ever.

The First Qualifier in Roland Garros Women's Final History

Maja Chwalinska entered Roland Garros 2026 ranked No. 114 in the world, needing to win three qualifying matches before the main draw had even begun. She then won seven more. When she defeated Diana Shnaider in the semifinals, Chwalinska became the first qualifier in the history of the Roland Garros women's singles to reach the final.

She is also only the second women's qualifier to reach a Grand Slam singles final in the Open Era. She joins Evonne Goolagong (1971) and Chris Evert (1973) as the only women to reach the Roland Garros final on their tournament debut. No qualifier — in any Grand Slam, on either tour — has done more than Chwalinska did these past two weeks.

She lost the final 6-3, 6-3 to Mirra Andreeva on Saturday. But to frame her result as a loss is to miss the story entirely.

The Youngest Roland Garros Champion Since 1992

Andreeva's title on Saturday made her the youngest Roland Garros women's champion since Monica Seles won the title for the third consecutive time in 1992. At 19 years and 38 days, Andreeva joins a very short list of teenage Roland Garros champions — and she is the first Russian woman to hold the Suzanne Lenglen trophy since Sharapova won it in 2014.

The manner of the victory matters too: trailing 2-3 in the first set against a left-handed opponent whose style was causing real problems, Andreeva reoriented herself and won 10 straight games. That is not the performance of a player who got lucky. That is the performance of a champion in full control of her game when the stakes were highest.

The First Italian Men's Grand Slam Finalist Since Panatta

Flavio Cobolli stands in today's men's final as the first Italian man in a Grand Slam singles final since Adriano Panatta won this very tournament in 1976 — fifty years ago. The Italy men's tennis history at the major level ran dry after Panatta, through the careers of Barazzutti, Bertolucci, Berrettini, Fognini, and Musetti, none of whom reached a Grand Slam final. Cobolli, 22, gets there first.

And he did so in the most unusual way imaginable. His all-Italian semifinal against Matteo Arnaldi — the first all-Italian men's Grand Slam semifinal in history — never took place. Arnaldi was struck by a viral illness overnight Thursday that left him unable to eat or drink, and he withdrew Friday morning. Cobolli advanced to his first Grand Slam final via walkover.

The Fourth Walkover in Open Era Grand Slam Semifinal History

Arnaldi's withdrawal was only the fourth walkover in Open Era Grand Slam semifinal history. Walkovers at this stage are extraordinarily rare: the moment typically comes after a competitor has played six rounds over nearly two weeks, close enough to the title that withdrawing carries enormous personal cost. Arnaldi had no choice — the illness was too severe. But the rarity of the circumstance underlines how unusual this tournament has been from start to finish.

The Absent Champions

One final piece of context: the top three players entering 2026 — Jannik Sinner, Carlos Alcaraz, and Novak Djokovic — were all absent from the final stages of the men's draw. The tournament that played out at Roland Garros 2026 was a legitimately open competition, producing finalists and results that the traditional power structure of men's tennis would not have predicted. That is not a diminishment — it is one of the things that makes this fortnight worth remembering.
