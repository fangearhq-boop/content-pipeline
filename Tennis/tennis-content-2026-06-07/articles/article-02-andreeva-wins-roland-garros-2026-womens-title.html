Mirra Andreeva Wins Roland Garros 2026 — Youngest Champion Here Since Monica Seles

By Marcus Cole | June 7, 2026

Mirra Andreeva is a Grand Slam champion. The Russian teenager defeated Maja Chwalinska 6-3, 6-3 on Saturday in the Roland Garros women's final, claiming the Coupe Suzanne Lenglen in her first appearance at this stage. At 19 years and 38 days old, she became the youngest Roland Garros women's champion since Monica Seles won the title in 1992.

It is also the first time a Russian woman has won Roland Garros since Maria Sharapova lifted the trophy in 2014. Twelve years of waiting ends with a 19-year-old who barely existed when Sharapova last stood in the champion's circle at Philippe-Chatrier.

The Match

Andreeva did not have an easy opening. Chwalinska, the Polish qualifier ranked No. 114 in the world entering the tournament, took a 3-2 lead in the first set with a style that was consistently disruptive — left-handed serves pulling wide, drop shots at unexpected moments, a pace variation that Andreeva took several games to read.

Then the teenager from Krasnoyarsk shifted. She stepped inside the baseline, started taking the ball earlier, and stopped allowing Chwalinska's variety to dictate the rally. From 2-3 down in the first set, Andreeva won 10 consecutive games to close out both sets and the match. The final sequence — comfortable, controlled, almost clinical — bore no resemblance to the uncertainty of the match's opening stages.

Chwalinska, in her first Grand Slam main draw appearance and her first Slam final, fought as well as a qualifier with nothing to lose can fight. But once Andreeva settled, the result was rarely in doubt.

The Historic Weight

Andreeva's place in the history books at Roland Garros is already substantial. She is one of only three women in the Open Era to have won the Roland Garros title at 19 or younger — joining Seles (1990, 1991, 1992) and Arantxa Sanchez (1989). The lineage of teenage clay-court champions is a short and remarkable one.

The Russian connection matters too. Sharapova's 2014 title came in a golden era of Russian women's tennis. In the years since, the next generation has taken time to emerge. Andreeva is that next generation — and she arrived at Roland Garros having already been a top-10 player for the better part of a year, with a semifinal appearance here in 2025 behind her.

She arrived at this tournament, in her own words, prepared. She departed it as a champion.

The Coach in the Corner

Andreeva is coached by Conchita Martinez, the Spanish former world No. 2 who reached the Roland Garros final herself in 2000. Martinez never lifted the Suzanne Lenglen trophy at Philippe-Chatrier during her playing career. On Saturday, she watched the player she coaches do what she could not.

The coaching relationship has visibly strengthened Andreeva's tactical maturity. The decision to step inside the baseline when Chwalinska had the upper hand in the first set — to take the fight to her opponent rather than waiting — is the kind of adjustment that reflects good coaching as much as good instinct. Andreeva made it at the right moment.

What's Next

Andreeva begins her post-Roland Garros life as a Grand Slam champion. The grass season opens immediately — Stuttgart starts Monday — and Wimbledon follows. The women's ranking picture at the top has shifted considerably with this title: Andreeva, the No. 8 seed entering the tournament, moves up significantly with the Roland Garros champion's points added to her total.
