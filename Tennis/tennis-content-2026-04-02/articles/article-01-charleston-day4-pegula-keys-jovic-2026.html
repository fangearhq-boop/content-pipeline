Charleston Day 4: Pegula Survives, Keys Cruises, Jovic Charms the Crowd

By Elena Voss | Tennis Fanrecap | April 2, 2026

The Credit One Charleston Open produced three very different stories on Thursday — a defending champion under pressure, a title contender in full flight, and a teenage sensation stealing the spotlight.

Pegula Survives

Jessica Pegula did not have an easy Thursday. The defending champion and No. 1 seed at Charleston fought through a marathon match against Elisabetta Cocciaretto to advance to the quarterfinals. Local reports described the match as one of survival — Pegula scratching for every point against an Italian opponent who gave her no free points on the green clay.

Survival, in this context, is not a criticism. Green clay — the surface unique to Charleston among WTA events — has a habit of producing longer, grittier rallies than traditional red clay. The bounce is lower and faster, the slide more limited. Cocciaretto, who has been improving steadily on the WTA circuit, was not going to hand Pegula an easy win, and she didn't.

Pegula is through to the quarterfinals and still on track to defend her 2025 Charleston title. But Thursday reminded the field — and any fans who thought the defending champion would coast — that Charleston's green clay respects no one's ranking.

Keys in Command

Madison Keys told a different story entirely. The American veteran cruised past Anna Bondar in the round of 16, advancing to the quarterfinals without the drama that defined Pegula's day. Keys on a surface she is comfortable with, in clean early-season form, is a different proposition from most players in the draw.

Keys has never won Charleston, but she has the kind of flat-strike groundstroke game that the green clay rewards. The low bounce on the surface suits her aggressive, take-the-ball-early approach better than slower red clay. Thursday's win was a statement of intent: Madison Keys is a legitimate title contender this week.

Jovic Takes the Stage

The headline from the Post and Courier said it directly: "Teen tennis star charms Charleston Open crowd." That player is Iva Jovic — the 18-year-old who has been generating buzz with her big game and bigger personality all week in South Carolina.

Jovic's advance through the Charleston draw represents exactly the kind of next-generation moment the WTA has been working to cultivate. Young players from France, the United States, and across Europe have been arriving at the top of the women's game with increasing regularity over the past two years — but making fans fall in love with you in front of a home crowd in South Carolina takes something extra. Jovic, apparently, has it.

Her run at Charleston is still young, but the crowd energy around her matches suggests she has already become this tournament's breakout story.

The QF Picture

With Pegula and Keys both through, the Charleston quarterfinal field is taking shape around a mix of established names and emerging challengers. The round of 16 also included Peyton Stearns against Sofia Kessler — a matchup confirmed on WTA's official draw page — as the green-clay drama continued across all courts.

Belinda Bencic, the 2016 Charleston champion and Paris 2024 Olympic gold medalist, also advanced to the round of 16 earlier in the week, adding another compelling subplot to what is shaping up as one of the best Charleston fields in recent years.

What's Next

The quarterfinals at the Credit One Charleston Open are set for Friday and Saturday. Pegula, the defending champion, will look to recover from Thursday's exhausting marathon and bring her best green-clay game. Keys will enter as one of the favorites. Jovic's continued run would make her the tournament's story of the week — and potentially the WTA's story of the young clay season.

For Bencic, reaching the quarterfinals of a tournament she won ten years ago would be an extraordinary milestone on her comeback journey. Charleston's green clay continues to deliver the unexpected, and the quarterfinals promise more of the same.
