Madison Keys Wins Eastbourne Third Title and Joins Evert and Navratilova in Elite Company

By Elena Voss | June 28, 2026

Keys defeated Tatjana Maria 7-5, 6-3 to become only the third woman in history to win Eastbourne three times — and she did it without ever losing a final at the tournament.

Madison Keys is now in exclusive company. Her 7-5, 6-3 victory over Tatjana Maria in the Lexus Eastbourne Open final on Saturday made her only the third woman ever to win this tournament three times, joining Chris Evert and Martina Navratilova. Beyond the count, there is a further distinction that sets Keys apart from everyone who has come before her: she has never lost an Eastbourne final. Three appearances. Three victories. It is not a record; it is a pattern of dominance on a specific surface at a specific time of year that speaks to how well her game translates to grass.

Maria, 38 years old and competing in the biggest final of her career, was not an easy opponent. The German has built one of professional tennis's most compelling late-career stories, extending her playing time while raising a family and continuing to compete at the highest levels of the tour. She fought for everything on Saturday, and there were moments in the first set — particularly around the midpoint — where the match genuinely hung in the balance. But Keys found her level when it mattered, broke at the crucial moment in the first set, and carried that momentum through the second to close out the title in straight sets.

How Keys Wins on Grass

The tennis Keys plays is well-suited to grass in a way that can be easy to take for granted. Her serve is one of the most reliable weapons in the women's game — on a surface that rewards pace and bounce, a big first serve behind which she can dictate the opening shot becomes an even more decisive asset than it is on hard courts. Her groundstrokes, particularly her forehand, are capable of generating the kind of flat, driving pace that skids through the turf and hurries opponents.

Beyond the technical fit, there is something about the grass-court swing that seems to bring out Keys's most composed tennis. She has won at Eastbourne with a consistency no other player in the tournament's modern history has matched, and the three-title sequence — with zero losses — suggests this is not coincidence. Keys understands how to construct points on grass, how to use her serve to create space, and how to close out matches when they move into the tighter moments. All three of those qualities were on display against Maria.

The Wimbledon Implication

Wimbledon begins Monday June 29. The timing of Keys's Eastbourne victory is close to ideal from a form and confidence perspective. She arrives in SW19 having won her last grass-court event, having dropped no sets in the tournament's final stages, and having beaten an experienced opponent in a final where she was expected to win but still had to perform.

That combination — form, confidence, and a completed win — is what players are looking for heading into a Grand Slam. The draw at Wimbledon is deeper and more unpredictable than Eastbourne, and a tournament run of seven matches tests resources that a five-match week does not. But Keys enters the Championships in the best possible position: healthy, winning, and knowing exactly what her grass-court game can do.

What's Next

Madison Keys enters Wimbledon 2026 as one of the most credible sleeper threats in the women's draw. Her Eastbourne title and the company she now keeps on its winners' list will follow her through the first week of the Championships. She plays her opening match at the All England Club beginning Monday June 29.

Tatjana Maria, having reached her first WTA 500 singles final at 38, returns to the tour with the knowledge that her game remains capable of reaching the final of a significant event. She, too, will be in the Wimbledon draw next week.
