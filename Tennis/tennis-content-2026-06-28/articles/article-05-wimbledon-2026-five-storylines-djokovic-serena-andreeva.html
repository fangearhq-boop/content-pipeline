Five Wimbledon 2026 Storylines That Will Define the Next Two Weeks

By Marcus Cole | June 28, 2026

Djokovic's record chase, Serena's comeback, Andreeva's double attempt, Raducanu's fitness, and a first-round blockbuster that has been circled since draw day.

Wimbledon 2026 begins Monday June 29. The draw is set, the order of play is announced, and across two weeks of grass-court tennis the sport will answer questions that have been building through an entire grass-court swing. Here are the five storylines that will matter most.

1. Djokovic at the Crossroads

Novak Djokovic is 39 years old, seeded No. 7, and has played approximately 13 matches in all of 2026. His participation in the season has been limited by shoulder concerns, and the question that has surrounded him through the entire grass-court swing is whether he can sustain the physical demands of a seven-match Grand Slam run.

What is not in question is what winning would mean. A record-tying eighth Wimbledon title would equal Roger Federer's mark at the Championships he dominated for a generation. Combined with the French Open wins already on Djokovic's record, an eighth Wimbledon would bring him to 25 Grand Slam titles — the all-time record, which he currently shares with Serena Williams at 24. He opens against Wu Yibing on Centre Court, Day 1.

2. Serena at 44

The last time Serena Williams played Grand Slam singles, it was September 2022 at the US Open. She announced that tournament as a farewell of sorts, using the word "evolving" rather than "retiring" — and then did not play singles in a major for nearly four years. She is back now, at 44, with a wildcard into the Wimbledon singles draw and a doubles wildcard alongside Venus.

Her first-round opponent is Maya Joint, a 20-year-old ranked outside the top 100. The contrast is almost theatrical: 44 years, seven Wimbledon titles, 24 Grand Slams on one side of the net; 20 years old, first Grand Slam match, nothing to lose on the other. Serena has not won Wimbledon since 2016. She won her last slam at the 2017 Australian Open, at which point she was already pregnant. The match will be one of the most-watched in the first week of the Championships regardless of the outcome.

3. Andreeva's Historic Double

Mirra Andreeva, 19 years old and ranked No. 5, won the Roland Garros title this year and arrives at Wimbledon with more clay-court titles than grass-court points. The last woman to win both Roland Garros and Wimbledon in the same year was Justine Henin in 2006. Andreeva has the talent, the competitive instincts, and the ranking to make a deep run — but grass is a genuinely different test, and arriving at the All England Club as the player everyone is watching is a new kind of pressure for a teenager.

Her first-round match is against Magda Linette. Everything after that is unwritten.

4. Raducanu's Fitness

Emma Raducanu is the British No. 1, seeded No. 32, and carrying the expectations of a home nation that wants desperately to believe this could be her Wimbledon. She missed a practice session last week with a reported fitness concern — not a retirement from an event, not a confirmed injury, just a missed practice — but at Wimbledon, where the home crowd and media scrutiny are unlike anywhere else in tennis, even a missed practice becomes a storyline. She opens against Antonia Ruzic on Monday.

5. Fritz vs. Draper in Round 1

No. 6 seed Taylor Fritz was last year's Wimbledon semi-finalist. Jack Draper is the British wildcard who has been among the most improved players on tour and who plays on grass with the kind of aggression that makes him dangerous regardless of draw position. Fritz withdrew from Eastbourne specifically to protect his fitness and be fresh for Wimbledon. Draper played Eastbourne and lost in the semi-finals to Humbert in three sets.

They meet in Round 1. One of them goes home after one match. It is the kind of first-round matchup that, in any other tournament, would be a quarter-final feature. At Wimbledon 2026, it happens Day 1.

What's Next

Wimbledon 2026 runs June 29 through July 12 at the All England Club. Day 1 begins with outside courts at 11:00 AM BST, Court 1 at 1:00 PM BST, and Centre Court at 1:30 PM BST. Full scheduling and results are available at wimbledon.com.
