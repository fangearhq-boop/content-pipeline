Djokovic Defeats Auger-Aliassime in the Longest Quarterfinal in Wimbledon History to Reach Record Eighth Consecutive Semifinal

By Ryan Calloway | Tennis Fanrecap | July 8, 2026

Wimbledon, London

Five hours and 15 minutes. That is how long Novak Djokovic and Felix Auger-Aliassime spent on Centre Court at the All England Club on Tuesday — the longest quarterfinal in Wimbledon history — before Djokovic closed out a 7-6(10), 3-6, 6-3, 6-7(4), 7-6(4) victory that was as close to a match as any tennis match could be without going into a sixth set.

It was Djokovic's 15th Wimbledon semifinal. It was his eighth consecutive, a record he now holds alone, moving past Roger Federer's seven. He is 39 years old, was playing through an ankle and calf injury that required a medical timeout in the opening set, and walked off Centre Court having beaten No. 3 seed Auger-Aliassime in a match that each man was entitled to win.

"I was telling the kids to go to sleep after the fourth but they didn't want to listen and I am glad they stayed," Djokovic said afterward. "It was honestly one of the best matches I have been part of on this court in my career."

A Match That Could Have Ended Three Times

The first set required a 22-point tiebreak. Djokovic won it 12-10. The score understates the tension of those points — each moment in a tiebreak of that length carries the weight of everything that came before it, and by the time Djokovic closed it out, both players had demonstrated exactly what the next four sets would look like.

Auger-Aliassime won the second and was the better player for stretches of the third and fourth. When the Canadian forced a fifth set, the match had well passed the three-hour mark. In the fifth-set tiebreak, each player held three set points. Djokovic converted his on the fourth opportunity.

The match ended close to the All England Club's 11 p.m. curfew — the same boundary that had suspended a different match the previous night. It ended in Djokovic's favor. It just as easily could have ended in Auger-Aliassime's.

A Record That Belongs to Djokovic Alone

The number eight means something specific here. Federer reached the Wimbledon men's singles semifinals in seven consecutive editions — a mark that stood as the benchmark for sustained excellence at the All England Club. Djokovic matched it before Tuesday. Now he stands alone.

He is also the second-oldest man in the Open Era to reach a Wimbledon semifinal, behind only Ken Rosewall, who reached the last four in 1974. Those are the historical coordinates of Tuesday's win: one of the biggest, one of the hardest, one of the most closely contested matches he has ever played on this surface, arriving at the precise moment when a new record became possible.

What's Next

On Friday, Djokovic faces Jannik Sinner on Centre Court in the Wimbledon semifinals. It is the second consecutive year they have met at this stage, the third time in four years. Last year, Sinner defeated Djokovic in straight sets in this exact match — and then won the title. Djokovic arrives having played through five hours of quarter-final tennis. Sinner arrives having not dropped a set all tournament.

Whether fatigue or momentum matters more on Friday, only the match will determine. What Tuesday proved, again, is that Djokovic at 39 can produce tennis capable of winning any match at this tournament. The question now is whether he can do it one more time.
