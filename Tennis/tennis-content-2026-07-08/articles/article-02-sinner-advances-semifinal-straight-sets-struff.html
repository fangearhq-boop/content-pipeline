Sinner Ends Struff's Fairy Tale in Straight Sets to Reach Wimbledon Semifinals Without Dropping a Set

By Marcus Cole | Tennis Fanrecap | July 8, 2026

Wimbledon, London

The fairy tale ran out at No. 1 Court on Tuesday. Jan-Lennard Struff — 36 years old, 47th Grand Slam, first-ever quarterfinal — met Jannik Sinner, and the match went about as you might expect when you put the oldest Open Era first-time Grand Slam quarterfinalist against the world No. 1 and defending champion.

Sinner won 7-5, 7-6(4), 6-3. He hit 16 aces. He converted all three of his break points. He committed 16 unforced errors — half of Struff's 29. He has not dropped a set in the entire 2026 Wimbledon Championships. He is into his 10th Grand Slam semifinal.

The numbers describe a performance of quiet authority. Sinner is not the kind of player who overwhelms opponents with chaos. He takes it away from them, one exchange at a time, until the result is inevitable.

What Struff Had Already Done

The full context of Tuesday's match requires acknowledging what Struff had already accomplished. He entered the 2026 Wimbledon draw as an unseeded player and defeated No. 8 seed Daniil Medvedev in the round of 16 — one of the bigger upsets of the first week — to reach the quarterfinals for the first time in his career, in his 47th major appearance.

At 36 years and 78 days old, he became the oldest man in the Open Era to reach his first Grand Slam quarterfinal, a mark that had previously belonged to Fabrice Santoro. Getting there was an achievement that stands regardless of what happened Tuesday. Struff found a level at Wimbledon 2026 that he had not reached in the 16 years he had been on the ATP Tour.

He found Sinner at the quarterfinal stage. That is where the story ended.

A Tournament Still to Win

Sinner's position is close to ideal. He arrives at the final four of Wimbledon with no sets dropped, a schedule that has been efficient without being easy, and a serve that has been among the most reliable weapons in the draw. Sixteen aces against Struff — a server in his own right — with only two double faults is a strong indicator of the form he is carrying into the back half of the tournament.

The numbers also confirm something the eye already suggests: Sinner is not winning ugly or surviving close calls. He is controlling his matches at a level that leaves opponents without the leverage they need to create problems.

What's Next

On Friday, the defending champion faces Novak Djokovic in the Wimbledon semifinals on Centre Court — a rematch of last year's semifinal, which Sinner won in straight sets before lifting the trophy. Djokovic arrives off a five-hour-15-minute quarterfinal victory over Felix Auger-Aliassime, the longest quarterfinal in Wimbledon history. Sinner arrives off two hours of straight-sets tennis on No. 1 Court.

The contrast in physical wear heading into Friday is obvious. Whether it matters is a different question. Djokovic has played through worse before.
