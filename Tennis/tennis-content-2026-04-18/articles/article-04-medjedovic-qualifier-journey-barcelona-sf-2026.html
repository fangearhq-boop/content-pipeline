From Qualifying to Semifinals: Hamad Medjedovic's Improbable Barcelona Run

By Elena Voss | Tennis Fanrecap | April 18, 2026

Hamad Medjedovic did not enter the Barcelona Open Banc Sabadell as a main draw player. He entered as a qualifier — which means he first had to beat two opponents just to earn a place at the starting line. When the qualifying rounds were over and his name appeared on the main draw, he had already done more work than most players at an ATP 500 ever have to do.

Then he kept winning.

The Qualifying Gauntlet

Qualifying for an ATP 500 event requires two wins. Medjedovic started with Vilius Gaubas, then faced Quentin Halys — and had to take that match to a third-set tiebreaker to survive. A small moment, easy to overlook in retrospect. But tiebreakers are often where close qualifying runs end. Medjedovic won it, earned his main draw spot, and walked into the bracket alongside players who had not had to earn their way in the same way.

Five Wins, Each More Significant Than the Last

In the main draw, the opponents got harder with every round, and Medjedovic beat all of them. First was Marco Trungelliti, the Argentine veteran who had just won the Marrakech title the previous week — a player in form and with nothing to prove. Medjedovic dismissed him.

Then came the match that changed the narrative entirely. In the Round of 16, Medjedovic was drawn against Alex de Minaur — the Australian who was ranked No. 7 in the world at the time and one of the more reliable clay-court performers on tour. De Minaur had the seeding, the experience, and the ranking. Medjedovic had won his first ATP 500 main draw match the round before. The result was one of the week's most significant upsets, and it put Medjedovic's name in conversations it had rarely entered before.

He followed it with a quarterfinal victory over Nuno Borges, the Portuguese No. 5 seed — 7-6(6), 6-2 in straight sets. That was five main draw wins. Seven total in Barcelona, counting qualifying.

The Semifinal — and What It Means

Medjedovic is 21 years old, born in 2004. He is Serbian, and his rise through the rankings has been steady rather than sudden — until this week in Barcelona, which has been anything but quiet. His semifinal opponent on Saturday is Andrey Rublev, the No. 5 seed and one of the most experienced clay-court players in the draw. Rublev will be the favorite. He always is, at this level, against this kind of opponent.

But Medjedovic was supposed to be the underdog every single round this week, and it has not meant what it usually means. He arrived in Barcelona needing to win two qualifying matches to get in the door. By Saturday afternoon, he will be one win away from an ATP 500 final.

What's Next

If Medjedovic beats Rublev on Saturday, he reaches the Barcelona final — and the first ATP 500 final of his career. His ranking will jump significantly regardless of the outcome. The Madrid Open (April 26) and the French Open (May 24) are next on the calendar, and whatever happens this week, Medjedovic will arrive at both events as a different kind of player than the one who started qualifying in Barcelona.
