Rome Is the Final RG Exam: Ranking the WTA's Five Biggest Roland Garros Contenders

By Elena Voss | May 7, 2026 | Tennis Fanrecap

The Roland Garros main draw opens May 25. Qualifying begins May 18. The WTA Italian Open — which runs through May 17 in Rome — is the last WTA 1000 event before Paris, which makes every match on the Foro Italico clay a meaningful data point in the most important form guide of the clay season.

Five players enter Rome as genuine Roland Garros contenders. Each carries a different story, a different momentum level, and a different set of questions to answer between now and the first round in Paris.

No. 1 — Aryna Sabalenka: Dominant and Hungry

Aryna Sabalenka is the best player in women's tennis right now, and the numbers make the case without needing decoration. At 27-1 for the 2026 season, she has won nearly everything she has entered. She is the WTA No. 1 seed in Rome. She was the Roland Garros finalist in 2025. She is the heaviest favorite for the women's title in Paris.

What Sabalenka does not have is a Rome title or a Roland Garros trophy. Both could change before June arrives. The player who has defined consistency in 2026 needs only to keep doing what she has been doing. Rome is the opportunity to sharpen the tool that breaks through in Paris.

No. 2 — Mirra Andreeva: The Form Player

Mirra Andreeva enters Roland Garros as a seeded player for the first time in her career. At 19 years old and ranked No. 7 in the world, she has become the most in-form clay player on the WTA Tour — a distinction confirmed Thursday when she demolished Antonia Ruzic 6-1, 6-0 in her Italian Open second-round match.

Andreeva's 13 clay wins in 2026 lead the WTA Tour. Her clay season has included a Stuttgart upset of Iga Swiatek, a Linz title, a Madrid final, and now a dominant Rome second-round victory. The question is not whether she is ready for Roland Garros. The question is how far she can go.

No. 3 — Coco Gauff: The Defending Champion With Everything to Prove

Defending a Roland Garros title is one of the most psychologically demanding tasks in tennis. Coco Gauff must do it while also coming off a season where she has not played clay before this week in Rome — her first clay event of 2026.

Gauff's clay credentials are not in question. She won Paris. She is ranked No. 3 in the world. Her game translates exceptionally well to the red surface. But the gear change from hardcourt to clay, compressed into a week at the WTA Italian Open before the world's greatest clay-court Grand Slam, leaves no margin for slow starts.

Thursday's second-round match against Tereza Valentova is Gauff's first real statement of where her clay game is in 2026. Watch how she moves, how she constructs points, and how she manages the pressure of being the defending champion at a tournament that is essentially a two-week Roland Garros rehearsal.

No. 4 — Iga Swiatek: The Champion Who Needs Rome

Iga Swiatek has won Roland Garros four times. No active player has a deeper relationship with the Paris clay. But her 2026 clay season has not gone to plan — she retired from her Madrid match against Ann Li due to a virus and will arrive in Rome needing to rebuild momentum before the tournament that has defined her career.

Swiatek's clay game is still elite. Her footwork, her topspin forehand, and her ability to construct points from behind the baseline have not disappeared. But she needs match time, and she needs the confidence that comes from winning matches on clay in the weeks before Paris. Rome is where that process begins.

No. 5 — Elena Rybakina: The Silent Threat

Elena Rybakina, the world No. 2, often gets overlooked in Roland Garros conversations because her most celebrated titles — the 2022 Wimbledon championship and her 2026 Australian Open run — came on grass and hardcourt. But Rybakina's serve-and-groundstroke game is genuinely dangerous on clay, and she arrived at the Italian Open in sharp form after winning Stuttgart earlier this month.

Her target — an Australian Open and Roland Garros double in 2026 — is ambitious but not unrealistic. Rybakina rarely says much, but she showed at Stuttgart that her clay game is tournament-winning level. Rome, and then Paris, will reveal whether that level is Grand Slam-winning level.

What's Next

All five contenders are in the WTA Italian Open draw through the week of May 7-17. The WTA Italian Open final is scheduled for Saturday, May 17 at the Foro Italico in Rome. Roland Garros qualifying begins May 18. The Roland Garros main draw opens Sunday, May 25 in Paris.
