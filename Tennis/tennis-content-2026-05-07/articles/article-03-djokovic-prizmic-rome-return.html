Djokovic Faces Qualifier Prizmic in Rome Return as Sinner Also Eyes R2 Debut

By Marcus Cole | May 7, 2026 | Tennis Fanrecap

When Novak Djokovic accepted a wild card to the 2026 Italian Open and returned to Rome after weeks away from the tour, the expectation was a manageable opening-round matchup to ease the six-time champion back into competitive tennis. He gets no such luxury.

Croatian qualifier Dino Prizmic, 21, beat Marton Fucsovics in the first round to claim his spot in the Round of 32. His reward: Novak Djokovic, the greatest clay-court player in the sport's modern era, in what will be Djokovic's first competitive clay-court match since a shoulder injury ended his Indian Wells campaign on March 12.

The Comeback After Eight Weeks Out

Djokovic has been away from the tour since mid-March. He skipped Monte Carlo. He skipped Madrid. He has been a presence at Roland Garros conversations without being a presence on tour, a strange position for a player who has shaped the men's draw at every major event for two decades.

Rome is where the return begins. Djokovic is a six-time Italian Open champion — among the most decorated records in the tournament's history — and has spoken clearly about his goals: get healthy, find his game, and arrive at Roland Garros ready to compete. "I hope to be ready at least for Paris," he said ahead of the tournament.

The shoulder injury has not been elaborated on in clinical detail, but the length of the absence suggests a recovery that required patience rather than a quick fix. Coming back at Rome, on a surface he has mastered over 20 years of professional tennis, is logical. The Foro Italico feels like home for Djokovic in a way few clay venues do.

Prizmic Is Not Just a Name in the Draw

Dino Prizmic earned his place in this draw by beating Fucsovics. He is 21 years old and has shown throughout this clay season that he is not intimidated by the occasion. His game is built around attacking groundstrokes and an ability to hit through pressure — qualities that travel reasonably well from qualifier matches to main draw encounters.

Djokovic's injury absence is the key uncertainty. Eight weeks off the tour means no match sharpness, no recent competitive rhythm, and — critically — no recent measure of what the shoulder actually permits under match conditions rather than practice conditions. Those unknowns favor Prizmic more than the world No. 6 (or wherever Djokovic's ranking has settled after months of inactivity) would prefer.

The match sets up as a genuine test rather than a formality. How Djokovic responds to that pressure will answer the Roland Garros questions that have been building since March.

Sinner Also Enters Round 2

Djokovic is not the only top player making his tournament debut in the Round of 32 this week. World No. 1 Jannik Sinner, who holds a first-round bye as the top seed, faces the winner of the Alex Michelsen vs. Sebastian Ofner first-round match. Sinner enters Rome carrying a 28-match winning streak at the Masters 1000 level — an unprecedented run that includes five consecutive titles dating back to last November.

The prospect of both Sinner and Djokovic on court within the same 24-hour window, both opening their Rome campaigns, creates the kind of week-turning moment that the Italian Open's later rounds need to build toward. Sinner's path to a Career Golden Masters — winning all nine Masters 1000 events, something only Djokovic has achieved — runs through the Foro Italico this week.

What's Next

Djokovic vs. Prizmic is scheduled in the Round of 32, expected Thursday or Friday at the Foro Italico. Sinner's debut match against Michelsen or Ofner is scheduled in the same Round of 32 window. Results will shape the ATP Italian Open quarterfinal picture. The tournament runs through May 17, with Roland Garros main draw opening May 25.
