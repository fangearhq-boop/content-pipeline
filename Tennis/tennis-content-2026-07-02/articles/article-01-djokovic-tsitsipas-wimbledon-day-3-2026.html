Djokovic Flattens Tsitsipas in 98 Minutes at Wimbledon — Ball Girl Prank Becomes the Story Within the Story

By Elena Voss | July 2, 2026

The suspended second-round match resumed on Day 3 and was over almost before it started: 6-3, 6-4, 6-2 in under two hours. Then Djokovic decided to make it funny.

The match had been stopped by darkness on Tuesday evening, with Djokovic leading by two sets to none. He came back out on Wednesday and finished the job in 56 additional minutes. The final tally was 6-3, 6-4, 6-2 against Stefanos Tsitsipas, in a total match time of 1 hour 38 minutes. Thirty-three winners. Seven unforced errors.

That last number is the one that gets absorbed slowly. Seven. In a full three-set match at a Grand Slam, against a former world No. 3 and two-time Australian Open finalist. For most players, seven unforced errors would represent the first game and a half. For Djokovic on Centre Court grass on Wednesday afternoon, it was the entire match.

The Match

The third set — the portion actually played on Day 3 — had all of Djokovic's best qualities. He won 46 of 52 points on his first serve across the full match, which meant that every time he got his first delivery in, the point was effectively his to lose. He rarely lost it. Tsitsipas, ranked around No. 87 in the world at this tournament, had the pedigree to give Djokovic problems — the two have produced memorable matches, including Tsitsipas winning a French Open semifinal in four sets in 2021. Wednesday's version of Tsitsipas, however, was not that version.

The head-to-head between the two now stands at 13-2 in Djokovic's favor. For all of Tsitsipas's talent, he has not found an answer to Djokovic's variety, speed, and ability to defuse pace. On grass, that problem is compounded. Djokovic's low, skidding slice backhand — one of the most effective shots in the history of the game on this surface — caused consistent trouble off the Tsitsipas backhand wing throughout.

Djokovic moves into the third round without dropping a set in the tournament.

The Prank

Between the second and third sets, Tsitsipas briefly left Centre Court to change. Djokovic, with time on his hands and a match firmly in hand, noticed a loose piece of tape on his right shoulder. A ball girl was nearby with a pair of scissors. He asked her to trim it.

She carefully did exactly that. And when she finished, Djokovic suddenly recoiled, crying out in theatrical fake pain — as if the scissors had found something other than tape. The ball girl's expression went from focused concentration to startled shock in an instant. Centre Court erupted.

He immediately apologized and began laughing. In his post-match press conference, Djokovic addressed it with characteristic self-awareness: "Stefanos went to change so I had some spare time. I'm sorry if I scared her. I guess these kind of things surface when you're 2 sets to 0 up rather than 2 sets to 0 down."

The video was widely shared before the match had even finished. It captured something that the serious business of the match did not: the degree to which Djokovic, at 39 years old, remains at ease in the environment of a Grand Slam. He is not grinding through Wimbledon as an aging champion trying to hold on. He looks like someone who finds the place relaxing.

What's Next

Djokovic faces a third-round opponent at Wimbledon. With Shelton gone and the American men's draw thinned, the remaining challengers in his section of the draw will become clearer over the next day of play. He is the favorite to go deep, and Wednesday did nothing to complicate that assessment.

For Tsitsipas, the Wimbledon chapter closes early once more. He is a clay-court player at his best, and grass has never produced his finest tennis. He will move on to the hard-court season. Djokovic, meanwhile, will stay right where he is — until someone finds a way to make his matches harder than Wednesday's seven unforced errors suggest they need to be.
