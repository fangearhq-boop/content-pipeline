Krejcikova Stuns No. 5 Seed Andreeva at Wimbledon — Defending Champion Needs Seven Match Points but Advances in Three Sets

By Ryan Calloway | July 2, 2026

Barbora Krejcikova won Wimbledon in 2024. On Wednesday she backed it up, eliminating French Open champion Mirra Andreeva 4-6, 7-5, 6-4 in the biggest women's upset of the 2026 tournament so far.

The women's draw at Wimbledon 2026 had been losing seeds at a pace the All England Club was still absorbing. Zheng Qinwen gone on Day 1. Svitolina gone on Day 2. And now, on Day 3, Mirra Andreeva — the No. 5 seed and the player who lifted the Roland Garros trophy just a few weeks ago — exits in the second round.

Barbora Krejcikova beat her 4-6, 7-5, 6-4. The defending Wimbledon champion, not seeded in this year's draw, has advanced to the third round and reminded the field that grass is a different world from clay.

The Match

Andreeva took the first set and had reason to believe her game was translating. She is an aggressive, high-tempo player with a flat game that can be devastating on fast surfaces when everything is landing. In the first set, it was. Krejcikova dropped it 4-6 and faced the prospect of needing to win two consecutive sets against the in-form player of the clay swing.

The second set was where the match changed character. Krejcikova found her serve and her length on the baseline. The set went long, deep into the second half, and eventually Krejcikova broke late to take it 7-5. The first set was Andreeva's. The second told a different story.

Krejcikova controlled the third set better — taking it to within reach of closing at multiple points before Andreeva found resistance. Seven match points passed before Krejcikova finally converted. That number captures how competitive the match remained even when Krejcikova was serving for it. But close counts only in some sports, and Krejcikova has been in high-pressure moments at Wimbledon before. She closed the door.

Why This Matters

The headline version of this match — defending Wimbledon champion beats reigning French Open champion — sells it cleanly. But the deeper significance is what it says about the surface itself. Andreeva arrived here as the clear form player of the last month of tennis. She had beaten some of the best players in the world on clay. The transition to grass is not automatic, and the record of French Open champions struggling at Wimbledon the following fortnight is well established.

Krejcikova understood her own advantage. She has won here before. She knows how to play the angles, the slice, the low bounce, and the way grass rewards attacking the net at the right moment. She used all of it on Wednesday.

The result also means the top half of the women's draw — which had already lost Svitolina in Round 1 — is now missing Andreeva as well. That section of the bracket is open in ways it was not expected to be before the tournament began.

What's Next

Krejcikova moves into the third round, where the remaining field is consolidating. Sabalenka, Gauff, and Osaka all advanced on Wednesday, with Swiatek facing Pliskova on Thursday. The path to a Wimbledon final is clearing, and Krejcikova — who knows exactly what that path looks like, having walked it in 2024 — is still walking it in 2026.

For Andreeva, this is an early exit from what many had expected to be a deep run. She will carry the Roland Garros title into the hard-court season, where her game may once again find its highest level. But Wimbledon, for another year, belongs to someone else.
