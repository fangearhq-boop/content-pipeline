Sinner Survives Two Tiebreaks Against Borges to Reach Wimbledon Third Round

By Marcus Cole | July 2, 2026

The defending Wimbledon champion had to earn every set on Day 3, eventually beating Nuno Borges 7-6(4), 7-6(2), 6-4 to continue his title defense.

Jannik Sinner needed two tiebreaks, 47 winners, and the better part of an afternoon before he finally put away Nuno Borges on Day 3. The final score — 7-6(4), 7-6(2), 6-4 — does not tell you that the first two sets were close throughout, that Borges kept pushing and pushing until Sinner found a way to outpace him in the tiebreaks, and that the third set was the only part that felt like business as usual for the defending champion.

Sinner is through to the third round of Wimbledon 2026. He is building match by match, and Wednesday's performance was a step forward from Monday's five-set grind against Kecmanovic — even if it didn't look entirely fluent at the start.

What Made Borges Dangerous

Nuno Borges is not a name that generates fear on a seedings list, but he is a controlled, intelligent player who knows how to build pressure. On grass, his flat, fast game kept Sinner from settling into rallies early. He tested the Sinner backhand, pressed the net at the right moments, and competed for long enough that each tiebreak represented a genuine threat.

Sinner won the first tiebreak 7-4 and the second 7-2. The gap between those two scores tells a partial story — the first was closer than it looks by the end, while the second was more comfortable once it started. Sinner's serve, which can be a genuine weapon when landing at pace and in the right corners, showed up better in the second tiebreak than the first.

The Trajectory

Context matters here. Sinner skipped every ATP grass warm-up event before Wimbledon — no Halle, no Queen's Club, nothing. He came into the tournament cold. His opening match against Kecmanovic was a five-set battle that stretched over three-plus hours. Against Borges, he needed three sets and two tiebreaks but closed the third set more cleanly.

If that trajectory continues — each match slightly more controlled than the one before — then Sinner arriving at the second week of Wimbledon in his best grass-court form is not an unreasonable expectation. Forty-seven winners in one match suggests the firepower is there. What's been developing is the consistency in the difficult moments.

What's Next

Sinner advances to the third round at Wimbledon, where the field is thinning and opponents will carry more weight. With Shelton, Ruud, and Rublev already gone, the path through the draw has adjusted. Djokovic — who beat Tsitsipas in 98 minutes on Wednesday — is the most obvious threat in the other half of the men's draw.

Sinner will take whatever he learned from the Borges match, adjust, and come back out for the third round. He has the title. He has the experience of defending it. The question on Wednesday was whether the match sharpness was there. It is arriving, set by set.
