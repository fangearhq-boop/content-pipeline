Ruud and Rublev Exit Wimbledon on Day 1 — Four Seeds Fall as Draw Opens Up

By Marcus Cole | June 30, 2026

No. 11 seed Casper Ruud was beaten in straight sets by Hubert Hurkacz. No. 12 seed Andrey Rublev fell to compatriot Roman Safiullin in a five-set thriller that reached a 12-10 deciding tiebreak. Four men's seeds total exited the tournament on Day 1.

Four men's seeds fell at Wimbledon on the opening day. Two of them are significant enough to reshape the middle section of the draw. Casper Ruud — seeded No. 11 — lost to Hubert Hurkacz in straight sets. Andrey Rublev — seeded No. 12 — lost to compatriot Roman Safiullin in a five-set match that ran to a tiebreak in the final set and needed 12-10 to separate them.

By the end of Monday, two seeded players who might have been expected to last into the second week were gone. The players who beat them are now in the draw with considerably more space than they expected when the week began.

Ruud Falls to Hurkacz in Straight Sets

Casper Ruud's Wimbledon record has been a long-running subplot of his career. He is, by almost any measure, among the best clay-court players on the tour — multiple French Open finals, consistent results on the red dirt throughout his career. On grass he is a different player. The surface does not suit his baseline game, his movement, or the margins at which he operates.

Hubert Hurkacz, meanwhile, is one of the most effective grass-court players outside the top five. His serve is a weapon that neutralises the first strike and wins cheap points. His net approach is confident. His movement, always reliable, suits the quick court well. Against Ruud, he was able to do all of this from the first set and maintain it throughout. Ruud had no apparent route into the match and was beaten in straight sets without finding a meaningful foothold.

The Rublev-Safiullin Thriller

Andrey Rublev's match against Roman Safiullin was something different. Two Russian players. Five sets. A deciding tiebreak that ran to 12-10. The kind of match that defines a Tuesday evening conversation rather than a simple headline.

Rublev went into the match as the clear favourite — ranked No. 12 in the world, seeded, and with considerably more Grand Slam experience than his opponent. Safiullin, a qualifier whose ranking sits outside the top 50, had the profile of a first-round exit. What he had instead was the quality to compete for five sets and the nerve to win the final tiebreak when it mattered.

The decisive tiebreak ran to 12-10. At a point when either player might reasonably have collapsed or converted, the match kept producing points. Safiullin eventually got the one that ended it. Rublev — who had been in the match throughout, had led sets, had opportunities — was out.

The 12-10 tiebreak number is one of those scorelines that will be referenced for the rest of the tournament. It is not just a close match — it is a match that found the absolute limit of both players over the course of a tiebreak and then produced a winner from inside that limit.

The Draw Implications

Four seeds gone on Day 1 is not routine. It is not unprecedented either — Wimbledon's grass surface has always been capable of producing first-round exits that other Grand Slams would not — but four in one day creates unusual space in the draw.

Hurkacz now has a path through the draw that does not require him to beat a seed until potentially the quarterfinals. Safiullin has the profile of a player who could continue upsetting the established order if he has another match like Monday in him. The players in the lower half of the men's seedings — the players who were supposed to have clear runs but now face either Hurkacz or Safiullin — will have adjusted their expectations accordingly.

What's Next

Day 2 of Wimbledon 2026 continues Tuesday with the remaining first-round matches, including No. 6 seed Taylor Fritz against lucky loser Dusan Lajovic following Jack Draper's withdrawal. The men's draw, already reshaped by Monday's exits, will take on further definition before the week is over.
