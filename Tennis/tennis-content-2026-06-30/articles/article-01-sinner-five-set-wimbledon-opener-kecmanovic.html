Defending Champion Sinner Survives Kecmanovic in Five Sets to Open Wimbledon Title Defense

By Marcus Cole | June 30, 2026

Jannik Sinner needed three hours and 30 minutes to defeat Miomir Kecmanovic 4-6, 6-3, 6-7(6), 6-2, 6-3 on Centre Court on Monday — a match that served as both a survival story and a question mark about his preparation.

Jannik Sinner came to Wimbledon having not played a single ATP grass-court match in 2026. No Queen's Club, no Halle, no Eastbourne. The defending champion arrived at the All England Club with no grass form to speak of, only the title he won here twelve months ago. On Monday, that decision nearly cost him.

Miomir Kecmanovic, ranked No. 50 in the world and seeded nowhere in the draw, walked onto Centre Court as a very heavy underdog. He left it — after three hours and 30 minutes — as the player who pushed the defending champion to five sets and had him wobbling in the third. Sinner won 4-6, 6-3, 6-7(6), 6-2, 6-3. The title defence is alive, but it did not look uncomplicated.

How the Match Unfolded

Kecmanovic took the first set 6-4, playing with the energy and looseness of a player who has nothing to lose. Sinner, by contrast, looked like a player who had not played a competitive grass match in over a year. The first set went with serve until Kecmanovic broke late and converted the set without needing a tiebreak.

Sinner steadied in the second, winning 6-3 with more authority — beginning to find his length and take time away from Kecmanovic's groundstrokes. The match appeared to be turning. Then the third set arrived and it turned back. Kecmanovic stayed level, the set went to a tiebreak, and Sinner — who had begun to look comfortable — fell behind before fighting back to take it 7-6. The momentum swung one way, then the other, and the match hung in a balance that felt genuinely uncertain.

The fourth and fifth sets told a different story. Sinner, now fully switched on in a way that the earlier sets had not demanded, took the fourth 6-2 and the fifth 6-3. Kecmanovic's energy, which had been the engine of the opening set, had limits. Sinner's did not appear to.

The Preparation Question

Sinner's choice to skip the entire grass warm-up season attracted commentary in the build-up to Wimbledon. It was a calculated decision — arguably the same one Rafael Nadal used to make in certain years — and it carries the logic that a top player can adjust to the surface within a few matches at the tournament itself. But it carries a risk: the first match is always the hardest, and first matches can be lost.

Against Kecmanovic, Sinner nearly demonstrated both the risk and the recovery. The first two sets showed a player finding his way. The tiebreak showed him under pressure. The final two sets showed what he is capable of when fully engaged. Whether the five-set effort leaves any residual fatigue ahead of the second round will be one of the small questions worth watching.

What's Next

Sinner faces Portugal's Nuno Borges in the second round on Wednesday. Borges is a reliable baseline player who has performed solidly on the tour throughout 2026, but he does not carry the unseeded menace of Kecmanovic on a day when the draw produces surprises. Sinner will have a day to rest and reset before that match.

The bigger picture is that the title defence is intact. Sinner showed, at least in the fourth and fifth sets, that his grass game is not far away. The question is whether it will be near enough, against harder opponents than Kecmanovic, as the tournament progresses into the second week.
