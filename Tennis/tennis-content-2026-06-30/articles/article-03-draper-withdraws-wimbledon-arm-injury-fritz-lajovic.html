Draper Withdraws from Wimbledon with Arm Injury Recurrence — Lucky Loser Lajovic Steps In for Fritz Clash

By Ryan Calloway | June 30, 2026

Jack Draper announced his withdrawal from Wimbledon 2026 on Tuesday after the arm injury that has haunted his season returned — ending his participation before he faced No. 6 seed Taylor Fritz. Lucky loser Dusan Lajovic takes his place.

There was a version of this week in which Jack Draper walked onto Centre Court as Britain's best hope at its home Grand Slam, off the back of a run to the Eastbourne semi-finals that suggested his game was close to where it needed to be. That version ended on Tuesday morning, when Draper posted on social media: "Devastated to share that I have had to withdraw from my first round match due to a recurrence of my arm injury."

It is the same arm. The same injury that kept him out of the Australian Open and Roland-Garros in 2026. The injury that has refused to disappear through a season in which Draper has managed to compete in brief windows and then been forced out again.

The Injury History

Draper's arm problem is not a new story in 2026 — it is the story that has defined most of his year. He missed the Australian Open in January. He missed Roland-Garros in May. Both absences were attributed to the same problem in the same part of his body: an arm injury that has been difficult to fully resolve and that resurfaces under the physical demands of match play.

The Eastbourne run — four matches on grass in the week before Wimbledon — had given grounds for cautious optimism. Draper reached the semi-finals, lost there, and left Eastbourne having demonstrated that the arm was at least capable of sustaining a week of competition. Whether the Eastbourne week then contributed to what happened Tuesday is not clear from the available information. What is clear is that the arm did not hold.

Lajovic Steps In

Dusan Lajovic, who entered Wimbledon qualifying as a lucky loser after failing to advance through the qualifying rounds, receives the call to take Draper's place in the main draw. He will face sixth seed Taylor Fritz in the first round on Tuesday.

Fritz is the clear favourite for the match regardless of who he faces. He is one of the most effective grass-court players in the top ten, with a serve that becomes a weapon on the surface and groundstrokes that suit its low-bounce characteristics. Lajovic is ranked outside the top 50 and has no surface record that suggests he will trouble Fritz significantly. The match has effectively become a free first round for the American seed.

From Fritz's perspective, the draw has opened slightly. A match against Draper — seeded or not, British at home, motivated to prove himself after months of injury — would have carried different pressures than a match against Lajovic.

What This Means for British Tennis at Wimbledon

Draper was not the only British player at Wimbledon — Katie Boulter is competing on the women's side, and a number of British wildcards and qualifiers are in the draw. But Draper was the player around whom the narrative of British Wimbledon participation was built in 2026. His absence removes the main storyline the tournament had for domestic fans.

This is also the third consecutive Grand Slam at which Draper has been absent or withdrawn. That pattern raises questions about the durability of the recovery he has been attempting, and about what the timeline looks like for the remainder of the 2026 season. Whether the US Open in late August provides enough time for the arm to heal properly is a question that will not be answered on Tuesday.

What's Next

For Draper, the next steps are medical rather than competitive. The question is whether this latest recurrence requires additional rest, additional treatment, or additional intervention — and whether 2026 has any further competitive opportunities available to him at the major level. His talent is not in doubt. His availability has been the year's defining limitation.

For Wimbledon, the draw continues without him. The Fritz-Lajovic match will proceed, and Fritz will almost certainly advance. The bigger story is what British tennis looks like in 2026 when its most promising men's player cannot get through the first two days of Wimbledon without an injury ending his participation.
