Swiatek Returns to Clay in Rome, Dominates McNally 6-2, 6-3

By Ryan Calloway | May 8, 2026 | Tennis Fanrecap

Iga Swiatek returned to clay Friday at the Foro Italico and immediately looked like herself. The four-time Roland Garros champion defeated American Caty McNally 6-2, 6-3 in the Italian Open's second round, removing any concern about lingering effects from the illness that forced her retirement at the Madrid Open two weeks ago.

The scoreline was clean, the movement was sharp, and the point construction was decisive. There was no hesitation from the world No. 4 seed. She played the kind of tennis that has made her the most dominant player in Roland Garros history, and she did it in a tournament where she has not yet won the title.

Answering the Madrid Questions

Swiatek's 2026 clay season had not followed the expected script heading into Rome. At the Stuttgart Porsche Tennis Grand Prix, she fell to a resurgent Mirra Andreeva in the quarterfinals — still a respectable result, but not the title run that would have confirmed her form heading to Rome. At the Madrid Open, she retired mid-match against Ann Li with a gastrointestinal illness that appeared to be circulating at the venue, ending her tournament participation before she could show what she was capable of.

Friday's 6-2, 6-3 win over McNally did not come with the narrative weight of a major win, but it delivered something more immediately useful: confirmation that the four-time champion is healthy, moving well, and competing with intent on the surface where she has been most dangerous.

McNally is an improving American player who can be difficult to manage from the baseline with her two-handed backhand and ability to absorb pace. Swiatek did not let her settle into her preferred game, using heavy topspin and precise placement to control the baseline exchange from the opening game. The 6-2 first set was not flattering to McNally. The 6-3 second set confirmed that Friday's performance was genuine rather than a result of an unusually poor opponent day.

What Rome Means for the Roland Garros Calculus

Rome is the final WTA 1000 before Roland Garros, where qualifying begins May 18 and the main draw opens May 25. That gives every player competing at the Foro Italico fewer than three weeks to finalize form, recover physically, and arrive at Roland Garros with the kind of confidence that carries through a 14-day Grand Slam.

For Swiatek, every additional win in Rome carries compounding value. The French Open has been the defining event of her career — four titles, a first-name recognition level in Paris, a playing style calibrated precisely for the slow red clay of Philippe-Chatrier. But 2026 has tested that legacy narrative. While Sabalenka has gone 28-1 and Andreeva has built a 13-win clay streak, Swiatek has been playing catch-up.

Friday did not erase those questions. It began to answer them.

The Field Around Her

Swiatek advances in a WTA Italian Open draw that features Sabalenka as the dominant No. 1 seed, Rybakina as the Stuttgart champion and No. 2 seed, and Andreeva as a No. 5 seed with clay-court momentum. Gauff, the defending Roland Garros champion, is also in the draw. Paolini is defending the Rome title on her home clay.

In previous years, Swiatek would have been the consensus favorite entering any clay-court event. In 2026, she is one of several credible contenders — a position that may ultimately make her more dangerous, not less. Players forced to compete rather than coast often find something extra in their game.

The third round in Rome will provide a clearer picture of where she stands.

What's Next

Swiatek advances to the Italian Open third round. The WTA Italian Open runs through May 17 at the Foro Italico in Rome. Roland Garros qualifying begins May 18 with the main draw opening May 25.
