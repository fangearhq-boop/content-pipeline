The Last Piece: Sinner Needs Rome to Complete the Career Golden Masters

By Marcus Cole | May 8, 2026 | Tennis Fanrecap

In 36 years of ATP Masters 1000 tennis, only one player has won at least one title at every single event on the calendar: Novak Djokovic. The Career Golden Masters — nine titles, nine different cities — remains one of the most exclusive individual records in the sport.

Jannik Sinner is one tournament away from joining him.

The world No. 1 has won Masters 1000 titles at eight of the nine events. He has won in Montreal, Cincinnati, Shanghai, Paris, Indian Wells, Miami, Monte-Carlo, and Madrid. The one remaining: Rome, the Italian Open, the tournament being played in his home country at the Foro Italico this week.

What the Career Golden Masters Actually Means

The ATP Masters 1000 series was established in its current form in 1990 with nine mandatory events. Winning all nine titles in a career requires not just excellence, but diversity — indoor and outdoor courts, hard courts and clay, North American and European conditions, different opponents and different pressures at different points in a career.

Djokovic completed the set in 2018. Before him, Roger Federer came close but never won Monte-Carlo. Rafael Nadal, despite his unmatched clay dominance, never won the Paris Masters. Andy Murray reached 11 Masters finals without completing the full set.

Sinner's eight-of-nine completion rate at age 24 places him in historically extraordinary company. The rate at which he has collected these titles — including five consecutive Masters 1000 wins from Paris 2025 through Madrid 2026 — makes what he is attempting more remarkable still.

The Streak Within the Quest

Sinner's 28-match Masters 1000 win streak is already among the longest in the history of the series. Should he reach the Italian Open semifinal, his streak would extend to 32 consecutive Masters 1000 victories — surpassing Djokovic's record of 31 set during the Serbian's dominant 2011 season.

That record sat undisturbed for 15 years until Sinner began his current run last November in Paris. He has not lost a Masters 1000 match since. Not in Paris. Not in Indian Wells. Not in Miami. Not in Monte-Carlo. Not in Madrid. Five consecutive titles, 28 wins, no losses at the highest level of men's tennis below the Grand Slams.

In 2026, he is 30-2 overall, with both losses coming at Grand Slam level — a semifinal exit at the Australian Open against Djokovic and a quarterfinal loss in Doha to Jakub Mensik.

The Italian Factor

No Italian player has won the Rome singles title since Adriano Panatta in 1976. Half a century of Italian tennis history has passed without a home champion at the Foro Italico. Panatta remains a national sports hero in Italy in part because of that 1976 title, one he won at a time when clay-court tennis and Italian identity were tightly interwoven.

Sinner would carry that history with him if he wins. He has already won three Grand Slams, ending Italy's Grand Slam drought in men's singles. He has won Masters 1000 titles on multiple surfaces. The Foro Italico would give him the Career Golden Masters and the Italian Open in the same week — a combination of historical weight that is difficult to overstate in the context of Italian sports culture.

The crowd at Rome will know what is on the line. It will feel different from any tournament Sinner has played before, even with the pressure of the five consecutive Masters titles he is already carrying.

The Competition

Completing the Career Golden Masters is not a formality. Novak Djokovic enters his own second-round match Friday with a champion's motivation in his home city — not his home country, but the city where he has won the title six times. Alexander Zverev, the No. 2 seed, represents a major threat in the bottom half of the draw. The draw is not easy, and Sinner's path to the final will require winning five more matches at the highest level.

But Sinner has not lost a Masters 1000 match in five months. The record and the quest are there because he has earned them. Rome is the last piece — and he is here to complete the picture.

What's Next
