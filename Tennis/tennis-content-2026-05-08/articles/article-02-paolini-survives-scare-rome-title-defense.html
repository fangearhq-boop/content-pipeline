Paolini Survives Scare to Begin Rome Title Defense: 57 Errors, 54 Winners, and Through to R3

By Marcus Cole | May 8, 2026 | Tennis Fanrecap

Jasmine Paolini's Italian Open title defense opened Thursday at the Foro Italico the way most of her best matches do: with drama, with errors, and ultimately with a win that left no one certain how she had managed it. The defending champion defeated qualifier Leolia Jeanjean 6-7(4), 6-2, 6-4 in just under three hours, overcoming a first-set tiebreak loss and a staggering 57 unforced errors to advance to the third round.

The 54 winners she produced in the same match may explain how the scoreline looked the way it did. Paolini did not play clean tennis on Thursday. She played Italian tennis in Rome — which is something different.

The First-Set Alarm

Jeanjean, a French qualifier, played her best tennis of the day when it mattered most: in the first-set tiebreak. The Frenchwoman seized control at 4-4 in the breaker, winning three straight points with her most aggressive serving and baseline work of the match to take the set 7-4.

A lesser player — or one less experienced in high-pressure moments on clay — might have felt the crowd's nerves feeding into her own. Paolini, who has won this title before and understands what Rome means in Italy, came back out for the second set with different intent.

She went up 4-1, then 5-1 in the second set, and closed it out 6-2 in a manner that suggested the tiebreak had been a brief detour rather than a genuine warning. In the third set, a seven-deuce game for the 4-3 break proved decisive — a messy, grinding game that captured the entire match in a single extended point.

The Numbers That Tell Two Stories

Fifty-seven unforced errors would be a disaster in almost any professional tennis match. The WTA official site used it as a headline. What the same statistics board also showed was that Paolini hit 54 winners — nearly point-for-point offsetting the errors with outright winners that Jeanjean had no answer for.

That is the Paolini paradox on clay. She generates extreme levels of both winners and errors from the same fearless shot-making. On a good day, the winners outnumber the errors and she overwhelms opponents. On a mixed day, like Thursday, she nearly loses a set before finding her level and winning anyway.

The crowd at the Foro Italico never left her. They roared through the multi-deuce games, groaned at the missed forehands, and erupted when she converted the third-set break. Home atmosphere is a real variable in Paolini's game, and Thursday's match had it in abundance.

Defending With Everything Available

Paolini is seeded No. 9 at this year's Italian Open — not the No. 1 seed she might have received at peak form, but a position that reflects a 2026 season in which she has remained a consistent presence near the top of the WTA rankings while others have risen faster. She arrives as the defender of a title she won here in a performance that made her a national sporting figure in Italy.

The third-round opponent is Elise Mertens, an experienced Belgian who has made deep runs at major events throughout her career and knows how to construct points on clay. Paolini will need cleaner ball-striking than she showed Thursday if she wants to continue defending past the third round.

But she is through. Defending champions find ways to advance even when nothing is working cleanly. Thursday's match confirmed that Paolini still knows how to do that in Rome.

What's Next

Paolini faces Elise Mertens in the Italian Open third round. The WTA Italian Open runs through May 17 at the Foro Italico in Rome. Roland Garros qualifying begins May 18 with the main draw opening May 25.
