WTA Stuttgart 2026 Preview: Can Sabalenka Finally Win the Porsche Tennis Grand Prix?

By Elena Voss | April 9, 2026

The 48th Porsche Tennis Grand Prix starts April 13 in Stuttgart with the world's top four players entered — and a storyline about Aryna Sabalenka's four-final, zero-title relationship with the tournament.

The Porsche Tennis Grand Prix opens Monday in Stuttgart. It runs through April 19. It is a WTA 500 event played on indoor clay at the Porsche Arena — the first significant clay stop on the women's calendar before the clay-court swing reaches Roland Garros in late May.

The draw features Aryna Sabalenka as No. 1 seed, Elena Rybakina as No. 2, Coco Gauff as No. 3, and Iga Swiatek as No. 4. Jelena Ostapenko is the defending champion, having beaten Sabalenka in the 2025 final. It is the 48th edition of the event.

The headline story, though, is about Sabalenka.

Four Finals, Zero Titles

Aryna Sabalenka has reached the Stuttgart final four times. She has not won once. In 2022, Iga Swiatek beat her. In 2023, Swiatek beat her again. In 2025, Jelena Ostapenko claimed the title in a final that once again ended without Sabalenka lifting the trophy.

This is not a tournament where Sabalenka lacks for results. She reaches the final regularly. But Stuttgart has become a specific pressure point in an otherwise dominant career — a place where the trophy has been visible and unavailable.

She arrives this year as the world's best player. Her 2026 season record through the Miami clay opener stood at 23-1. She won the Miami title in March. She is playing with the form of someone who wins most of the tournaments she enters. Stuttgart, so far, has been the exception.

The Competition

Rybakina, the No. 2 seed, is a powerful player on any surface and has shown she can be decisive on clay when her serve and forehand click. Gauff, at No. 3, brings athleticism and an improving baseline game. Swiatek, seeded fourth this year rather than at the top, carries two previous Stuttgart titles — both won against Sabalenka in the final — and plays indoor clay as well as anyone in the draw.

Ostapenko, as defending champion, occupies a different kind of psychological position. She plays a high-risk, high-reward style that can be devastating and unpredictable. Her title defense adds texture to an already loaded field.

What's Next

The Porsche Tennis Grand Prix runs April 13–19 at the Porsche Arena in Stuttgart, Germany. For players preparing for Roland Garros, Stuttgart is a critical early indicator of clay-court form — and for Sabalenka, it represents one more opportunity to solve a tournament that has repeatedly denied her at the final stage.

She is No. 1 in the world. She has never won in Stuttgart. That context arrives with her to every match she plays this week.
