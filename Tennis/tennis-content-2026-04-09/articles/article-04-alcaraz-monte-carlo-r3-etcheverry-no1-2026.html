Alcaraz Defends Monte Carlo Title with Honest Admission: "I Expect to Lose No. 1 to Sinner"

By Ryan Calloway | April 9, 2026

Facing Etcheverry in the Round of 16, the defending champion is playing with 1,000 points on the line and a self-awareness about the rankings race that is rare at the top of the sport.

World No. 1 players do not typically arrive at the tournament they are defending and announce that they expect to lose their ranking. Carlos Alcaraz did exactly that after his opening win over Sebastián Báez at the Rolex Monte-Carlo Masters.

The quote, reported by Eurosport, was a candid appraisal of where the rankings stand: Alcaraz enters Monte Carlo with 13,590 points and is defending 1,000 of them as last year's champion. Jannik Sinner enters with 12,400 points and zero to defend — he was absent from Monte Carlo in 2025 due to his doping ban. Every match Sinner wins here without Alcaraz matching him closes the gap by 45 to 90 points at a time.

The math favors Sinner's return to the top. Alcaraz is simply being honest about it.

The Etcheverry Match

Thursday's Round of 16 opponent, Tomás Martín Etcheverry, is an Argentine clay-court specialist who reached this stage by defeating Grigor Dimitrov 6-4, 2-6, 6-3. That win came with significance attached: the loss dropped Dimitrov outside the ATP top 100 for the first time in 14 years.

Etcheverry arrives in form and confident. Alcaraz holds a 1-0 career head-to-head advantage over the Argentine, with their only meeting coming at the 2024 Argentina Open. Clay, however, is Etcheverry's best surface — he is a baseliner who grinds, retrieves, and can extend rallies to lengths that test even top-five players.

Alcaraz is 18-2 for the 2026 season, with the Australian Open and Doha titles already in his column. He is not playing poorly. He is playing one of the most complete seasons of his career. The issue is simply that Sinner — with zero points to defend — has an unusual structural advantage in the Monte Carlo rankings race.

The Bigger Picture

Alcaraz's public comment about the ranking reflects a maturity that has developed in his game off the court as well as on it. The Spanish teenager who burst onto the scene in 2021 is now 22, a three-time Grand Slam champion, and a player who understands that honest assessment of competitive situations is not a weakness — it is simply an accurate read.

He is defending his Monte Carlo title against a field that includes a Sinner on arguably the best run of his career, a Fonseca already in the quarterfinals, and a Zverev playing his home event. Alcaraz has every reason to compete hard here. He also has every reason to be clear-eyed about the rankings math that surrounds him.

What's Next

Alcaraz faces Etcheverry in the Round of 16 Thursday on Court Rainier III. A win advances him to the quarterfinals, where the draw could deliver a meeting with a player from the top half of the bracket. A loss eliminates him from title contention and accelerates the timeline on Sinner's return to the No. 1 ranking.

Every match from here carries that weight. Alcaraz knows it. He said it out loud. Now he plays.
