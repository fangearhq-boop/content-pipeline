Sakkari Wins Athens Open in Historic Home Final — First WTA Title in the City in 35 Years

By Marcus Cole | Tennis Fanrecap

July 19, 2026 — Athens, Greece

Maria Sakkari won the Vanda Pharmaceuticals Athens Open on Sunday, defeating No. 1 seed Barbora Krejcikova in the final of a tournament that had not existed in Athens for 35 years. She won it in front of her home crowd, at the Olympic Tennis Centre, ending a drought of more than two years without a WTA title match appearance.

The result placed a particular stamp on July 19, 2026 for Greek tennis. While Sakkari was competing in Athens, Stefanos Tsitsipas was in the EFG Swiss Open Gstaad final against Raphael Collignon. Two Greek players in ATP and WTA finals on the same day is not something that happens on a typical Sunday. It happened today.

Sakkari's last WTA final was Indian Wells in March 2024, where she was runner-up to Iga Swiatek. That was more than two years ago. The road back to this stage has been its own story. She chose Athens to return to one.

How She Got Here

Sakkari's path through the Athens draw was commanding from the start. She dropped just three games in her first two matches at the Olympic Tennis Centre, building momentum and feeding off a crowd that came each day in growing numbers.

In the semifinal, she defeated Alina Korneeva 6-1, 7-5, continuing the pattern. Krejcikova, the No. 1 seed and 2021 Roland Garros champion, reached the final with her own three-set win over Clara Tauson (6-1, 4-6, 6-3). Both players earned their spots.

In the final, Sakkari carried the crowd with her from the first game. Playing in Athens, against a strong field, with the full weight of 35 years without a WTA event in this city behind the moment — she converted.

What This Week Meant for Athens

The Vanda Pharmaceuticals Athens Open replaced the Jiangxi Open on the 2026 WTA calendar. It was the first WTA event held in Athens since 1991 — a gap of 35 years during which the city hosted the Olympic Games and the sport changed entirely, but the tournament itself was absent.

Its return coincided, almost improbably, with a moment where the home player was the No. 4 seed and a genuine title contender. The draw set it up. Sakkari won it. By the time Sunday's final ended, Athens had a WTA champion and a story to build the tournament's future on.

Krejcikova, for her part, had a strong week. She is one of the better clay-court players on the WTA tour and pushed deep into the draw of a tournament she could have won. In the final, though, the crowd and the occasion belonged entirely to Sakkari.

A Day Greek Tennis Will Remember

Tsitsipas had called it "historic" before either match was played. He was right. Greek City Times reported his celebration of Sakkari's run ahead of the Athens final, even as he prepared for his own title match in Gstaad.

Both finals ended in different ways — Collignon beat Tsitsipas in Gstaad, and Sakkari won in Athens — but the fact of two Greek players competing for titles on the same day is the kind of thing that gets remembered in national sporting history regardless of individual outcomes.

For Sakkari specifically, the win ends a run of time away from title matches that lasted long enough to raise questions. On Sunday in Athens, she answered them.

What's Next

The WTA tour transitions to North American hardcourts following the clay swing. The National Bank Open in Toronto begins August 1, with 72 of the top 75 WTA players confirmed. Sakkari will enter the hardcourt season with a title in hand and momentum from one of the more emotionally charged wins of her career.

Athens now has a WTA event to build on — a home champion in its first year back, a crowd that showed up, and a narrative that will bring fans back next time. That's how tournaments earn a long-term place on the calendar.
