Sinner, Djokovic, Zverev Head Record Canadian Open Field — Alcaraz Out Again

By Marcus Cole | Tennis Fanrecap

July 19, 2026 — Montreal, Canada

Tennis Canada has confirmed 71 of the ATP Tour's top 72 players for the 2026 National Bank Open in Montreal, running August 1–13. The organization called it the strongest entry list in the tournament's history. The numbers support the claim: with the exception of one player, the entire top tier of men's professional tennis is committed to showing up in Montreal.

That one player is Carlos Alcaraz. The No. 2 player in the world has not competed since injuring his right wrist at the Barcelona Open in April. He missed the French Open. He missed Wimbledon. He is not traveling to Montreal. His return timeline has not been confirmed by his team, and the US Open — scheduled for late August — becomes the next realistic target with each passing week.

The rest of the field is exceptional. World No. 1 Jannik Sinner, the 2023 Montreal champion, is confirmed. Novak Djokovic is confirmed. Alexander Zverev, who won Roland Garros in May, is confirmed. Canada's Félix Auger-Aliassime makes his ninth home appearance.

Sinner's Position Heading In

Sinner is undefeated in ATP Masters 1000 events so far in 2026. He won his second consecutive Wimbledon title in July, defeating Alexander Zverev in the final. His form entering the North American hardcourt season is as strong as any player on tour has been in recent memory at this point in the calendar.

Montreal in 2023 was where Sinner won what was then his biggest hardcourt result. He returns as the No. 1 seed and the defending points holder from that event. How he performs on North American hardcourts after the clay and grass stretch will define whether he maintains his lead over the rest of the field heading into the US Open.

Djokovic, at 39, has targeted this event as part of his preparation for what he has called one final US Open push. He reached the Wimbledon semifinal before falling to Sinner. He arrives in Canada without a title this season but with form that suggests he remains competitive at the highest level.

Zverev's Hardcourt Opportunity

Zverev won his first Grand Slam at Roland Garros in May, ending years of speculation about whether he could win the biggest events. He has now won a major and reached the Wimbledon final in the same calendar year. His hardcourt results have historically been strong — he won the Canadian Open in 2017 — and Montreal represents his first chance to collect points on the surface that will define the last portion of his 2026 season.

The conversation around Zverev going into Canada is different from what it was a year ago. A slam champion who has reached back-to-back major finals has a different weight in any draw. Montreal will test whether that weight translates to results on a fast hardcourt away from European clay.

Canadian Interest

Félix Auger-Aliassime, described by Tennis Canada as "the winningest Canadian singles player in history," makes his ninth home appearance. The local wild card for the main draw goes to Gabriel Diallo, adding Canadian representation from a younger generation. Gaël Monfils, the veteran Frenchman with a large North American fan base, also received a wild card.

On the WTA side, 72 of the top 75 players are confirmed for Toronto, which runs simultaneously with the men's event in Montreal. That means the week of August 1–13 delivers two Masters-level fields at once in Canada — a week that functions as one of the sport's major pre-US Open appointments.

What's Next

The clay swing finishes this weekend with finals in Bastad, Gstaad, Athens, and Iasi. When those results are in, the tour takes roughly two weeks before the Montreal and Toronto draws are released on July 29–30. Play begins August 1.

The Alcaraz situation remains the story that could change the entire narrative of the North American hardcourt season. If he returns fit and in form at the US Open, the draw becomes a different event. If he is unable to compete, Sinner's path to another major becomes significantly clearer. Each week without an update from the Alcaraz camp adds to the uncertainty.
