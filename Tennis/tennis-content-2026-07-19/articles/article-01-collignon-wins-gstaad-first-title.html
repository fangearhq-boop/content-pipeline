Collignon Beats Tsitsipas in Two Tiebreaks for First ATP Title at Gstaad

By Elena Voss | Tennis Fanrecap

July 19, 2026 — Gstaad, Switzerland

Raphael Collignon is an ATP Tour champion. The Belgian player defeated No. 3 seed Stefanos Tsitsipas 7-6(2), 7-6(5) in Sunday's EFG Swiss Open Gstaad final, winning the first title of his career on a clay court in the Swiss Alps.

Getting to that final required something most players on tour never have to do. In Saturday's semifinal, Collignon faced match point against Juan Manuel Cerundolo — down a set and trailing 3-5 in the second — and erased it. He closed the set in a tiebreak and reeled off five games in a row from 2-5 down in the decider, winning 1-6, 7-6(5), 7-5 to advance to his first ATP Tour final.

On Sunday, he showed no sign of carrying the weight of the day before. Two tiebreaks against Tsitsipas, both won cleanly, no dropped serve in the final that required defending. Collignon converted the title without letting the moment turn against him.

What the Final Looked Like

Tsitsipas was returning to an ATP Tour final for the first time in 17 months. He had earned it with consecutive three-set wins through the week, including a 6-4, 3-6, 6-3 semifinal defeat of Aleksandr Shevchenko. He arrived in Sunday's final as the more experienced man, the more decorated name, the player with more final appearances.

None of that decided the match. Collignon served well enough to stay with him in every game that mattered, and in the tiebreaks — the places where final margins are settled — he was the more decisive player. The first tiebreak went 7-2. The second went 7-5. Neither was close enough to feel like a coin flip.

For Tsitsipas, the loss stings in a particular way. He waited a long time to get back to a final and played well enough across the week to be holding a trophy. Collignon simply played better on Sunday.

What Collignon's Title Means

Collignon reached a career-high ATP ranking of No. 42 earlier in July and is only the second Belgian player to reach the Gstaad final, following David Goffin in 2015. Where Goffin came up short that year, Collignon converted.

Belgian tennis does not produce ATP Tour champions regularly. At the level of a clay-court ATP 250 final, against a player of Tsitsipas's record, winning it in straight-set tiebreaks is a result worth noting on its own. Combined with the semifinal comeback — a match that could have ended Collignon's week in the second set — this week in Gstaad tells a complete story.

He will rise higher in the rankings when the new ATP numbers are released. Where that takes him heading into the North American hardcourt season remains to be seen. But in Gstaad, he is a champion, and that fact is not changing.

Context: The Post-Wimbledon Clay Week

The EFG Swiss Open is part of the brief clay swing that follows Wimbledon — a compressed stretch of European clay events before the tour pivots to North American hardcourts. Gstaad, at altitude in the Swiss Alps, tends to produce results that can catch the field off guard.

This week did exactly that. Cerundolo, one of the better clay players in the draw, was a match point from ending Collignon's run. He didn't. The player who came back from that position and then beat the highest-ranked opponent in the draw won the tournament.

Collignon now travels forward with a title, a higher ranking, and the knowledge that he has won a professional tennis championship on the ATP Tour. The week in Gstaad established something about who he is as a competitor.

What's Next

The post-Wimbledon clay season wraps up this weekend across Bastad, Gstaad, Athens, and Iasi. From here, the tour moves to North American hardcourts with the Canadian Open beginning August 1. Collignon will enter that stretch as an ATP Tour champion for the first time in his career, with a ranking that opens new doors in qualifying and direct entry for bigger events.

For Tsitsipas, the road continues toward Canada and Cincinnati before the US Open. He showed enough in Gstaad to suggest another deep run is possible. Sunday just wasn't his day.
