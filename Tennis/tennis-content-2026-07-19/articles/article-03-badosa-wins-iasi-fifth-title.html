Badosa Claims Fifth Career WTA Title at Iasi, Returns to Top 100

By Ryan Calloway | Tennis Fanrecap

July 19, 2026 — Iasi, Romania

Paula Badosa won the UniCredit Iasi Open on Sunday, defeating Mayar Sherif in the final to claim her fifth career WTA title. She has now entered five WTA title matches as a professional and won every one of them. That is a record that is difficult to explain other than as a defining characteristic of how she approaches the biggest moments in a tournament.

The win moves Badosa back into the top 100 in the WTA rankings — as high as No. 75 — and gives her direct entry into the US Open main draw. After spending time outside the top 100 earlier this year, the turnaround is now complete. She is back.

Badosa entered the final on a six-match winning streak and needed all of it heading in. Her semifinal against Tamara Zidansek was the kind of match that defines a run: she lost the first set, fell a break behind in the second, and then won seven of the final nine games to advance 3-6, 7-5, 7-6. A player who wasn't mentally sharp would not have made that recovery. Badosa made it look like the expected outcome.

Why Iasi Is Personal

The city matters to Badosa in a way that is not just about the ranking points or the draw. Growing up, she followed Simona Halep, Romania's most celebrated tennis champion, with the attentiveness of someone who wanted to understand what greatness looked like on a tennis court. Winning in Romania, in the city of Iasi — "Iasi feels like home after my winning debut here," she said — carries meaning that a result sheet cannot fully capture.

Halep's name looms over Romanian tennis in a way few athletes define a sport in a country. Badosa winning at Iasi is, in its own way, a tribute to the influence that watching Halep had on her development. She acknowledged it plainly and without exaggeration.

Sherif reached her first WTA final since 2024 with a dominant semifinal performance, defeating Oleksandra Oliynykova 6-3, 6-1. She was not outmatched in the final — she was beaten by a player who has not lost a title match in her career. That context softens the result without diminishing what she achieved in reaching Sunday.

The Comeback Nobody Made a Fuss About

Badosa's 2026 story does not have a dramatic headline moment. She did not come back from a long injury layoff or make a flashy return at a major. She came back match by match, tournament by tournament, through the grinding middle of the season where most ranking recoveries happen without much notice.

Outside the top 100 is a difficult place for a player of her ability. The seedings disappear, the draws are harder from Round 1, the protected ranking window closes. She navigated all of it and arrived in Iasi playing the best tennis she had played in months.

Five titles, zero losses in WTA finals, and a return to the top 100: this is a record that belongs to someone who does not let the biggest stage in a tournament affect her game. Whether that is temperament, preparation, or something harder to categorize, it is real and it is the clearest fact about Badosa's career in title matches.

What's Next

The North American hardcourt swing begins in Montreal and Toronto the first week of August. Badosa will enter that stretch ranked back inside the top 100 with momentum from the Iasi run and, critically, direct access to main draws at the biggest events.

The US Open follows in late August. With direct entry now secured, Badosa has no qualifying hurdles before the year's final Grand Slam. Where she goes from Iasi is no longer a question about whether she belongs in main draws. She is already there.
