### STORY 1: Collignon Wins Gstaad

**Long-Form Post (8:00 AM ET)**

Raphael Collignon is an ATP Tour champion. The Belgian walked off the clay in Gstaad on Sunday with his first career title, defeating No. 3 seed Stefanos Tsitsipas 7-6(2), 7-6(5) in the EFG Swiss Open final.

Getting to that final required something most players never have to do. Collignon faced match point against Juan Manuel Cerundolo in the semifinals — down a set and 3-5 — and turned it around to win 1-6, 7-6(5), 7-5. Then he turned around and beat one of the tour's most experienced finalists in two tiebreaks without looking rattled for a moment.

For Tsitsipas, this was his first ATP final in 17 months. He played well enough to win it. Collignon simply played better.

Collignon reached a career-high ranking of No. 42 earlier this month and is the only second Belgian to reach the Gstaad final since David Goffin in 2015. Sunday, he did what Goffin didn't — he won it.

### STORY 2: Sakkari Wins Athens

**Long-Form Post (10:30 AM ET)**

Sunday, July 19 may be the best day in recent Greek tennis history.

Maria Sakkari won the Vanda Pharmaceuticals Athens Open, defeating No. 1 seed Barbora Krejcikova in the final of the first WTA event in Athens in 35 years. Playing in front of her home crowd, Sakkari ended a drought that had lasted more than two years — her last WTA final was Indian Wells in March 2024. She's been close. Today she got there.

At the same time Sakkari was winning in Athens, Stefanos Tsitsipas was in the Gstaad final against Collignon. Two Greek players competing in ATP and WTA finals simultaneously. That does not happen very often, and it happened in Athens for the first time in decades.

Krejcikova, the 2021 Roland Garros champion, reached the Athens final via a comeback win of her own over Clara Tauson (6-1, 4-6, 6-3 in the SF). She played well this week. But the crowd — and the moment — belonged to Sakkari.

Were you watching the Athens final today? What a stage for Greek tennis to reclaim.

### STORY 3: Badosa Wins Iasi

**Long-Form Post (12:30 PM ET)**

Paula Badosa won the UniCredit Iasi Open today, defeating Mayar Sherif in the final to claim her fifth career WTA title. She has now entered five WTA title matches and won five. That is a record worth paying attention to.

This week said a lot about where Badosa is right now. She came back from a set and a break down against Tamara Zidansek in the semifinals, winning 3-6, 7-5, 7-6. She brought a six-match winning streak into Sunday's final. She has been playing through the draw like someone who expected to be there.

The ranking picture makes this win even more meaningful. Badosa spent time outside the top 100 earlier in the year. Her Iasi title moves her back as high as No. 75 in the world — enough to enter the US Open directly.

She also has a personal connection to this tournament. "I grew up following Simona," she said after the match, referencing Simona Halep, Romania's most celebrated player. Winning in Romania, at the same tournament where Halep's story looms large, clearly meant something beyond the ranking points.

What are your expectations for Badosa heading into the North American hardcourt swing?

### STORY 4: Bastad Final — Rublev vs. Darderi

**Long-Form Post (3:00 PM ET)**

The Nordea Open Bastad final has a storyline that writes itself: two former Bastad champions across the net from each other.

No. 1 seed Andrey Rublev, the 2023 Bastad champion, faces No. 2 seed Luciano Darderi, who won the title in 2025 and is defending it today. Neither player had an easy path through the semifinals. Rublev needed three sets to beat Alejandro Tabilo (6-4, 4-6, 6-4), while Darderi survived a marathon against Adolfo Daniel Vallejo that stretched to a second-set tiebreak that went to 11 games before Darderi finally closed it out 6-4, 6-7(11), 6-3.

Worth noting: the July 18 pipeline previewed this final as Rublev vs. Vallejo. Darderi had other plans, fighting through that punishing second set to earn his place in today's final.

One former champion is going to add a second Bastad title. The other is going home with a runner-up result at the event he used to own.

Who's your pick — Rublev or Darderi for the Bastad title?

### STORY 5: Canadian Open Preview

**Long-Form Post (6:00 PM ET)**

The clay swing is wrapping up this weekend. What comes next is the North American hardcourt summer — and it starts in Montreal.

Tennis Canada just confirmed 71 of the ATP Tour's top 72 players for the National Bank Open (Aug. 1–13). World No. 1 Jannik Sinner, the 2023 Montreal champion who has not lost a Masters 1000 event all season, is in. Novak Djokovic is in. Alexander Zverev, the Roland Garros champion, is in. Félix Auger-Aliassime is making his ninth home appearance.

The one name missing from the top 72: Carlos Alcaraz. His right wrist injury, which has kept him off the tour since April, has followed him through the French Open, Wimbledon, and now Montreal. His return timeline remains uncertain — every week that passes raises questions about whether he makes the US Open.

On the WTA side, 72 of the top 75 players are set for Toronto on the same week.

"Strongest entry list in tournament history" is how Tennis Canada described the field. With Alcaraz absent and Sinner in perfect form, that's a statement worth watching play out.

Who do you think challenges Sinner for the Montreal title?
