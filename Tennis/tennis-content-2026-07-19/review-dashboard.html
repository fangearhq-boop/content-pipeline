NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post Raphael Collignon is an ATP Tour champion. The Belgian walked off the clay in Gstaad on Sunday with his first career title, defeating No. 3 seed Stefanos Tsitsipas 7-6(2), 7-6(5) in the EFG Swiss Open final. Getting to that final required something most players never have to do. Collignon faced match point against Juan Manuel Cerundolo in the semifinals — down a set and 3-5 — and turned it around to win 1-6, 7-6(5), 7-5. Then he turned around and beat one of the tour's most experienced finalists in two tiebreaks without looking rattled for a moment. For Tsitsipas, this was his first ATP final in 17 months. He played well enough to win it. Collignon simply played better. Collignon reached a career-high ranking of No. 42 earlier this month and is the only second Belgian to reach the Gstaad final since David Goffin in 2015. Sunday, he did what Goffin didn't — he won it. What do you make of this week's clay-court first-title stories — Merida at Umag earlier in the week, now Collignon at Gstaad? ### Image Post Caption Collignon beats Tsitsipas in two tiebreaks. ### Long-Form Post Sunday, July 19 may be the best day in recent Greek tennis history. Maria Sakkari won the Vanda Pharmaceuticals Athens Open, defeating No. 1 seed Barbora Krejcikova in the final of the first WTA event in Athens in 35 years. Playing in front of her home crowd, Sakkari ended a drought that had lasted more than two years — her last WTA final was Indian Wells in March 2024. She's been close. Today she got there. At the same time Sakkari was winning in Athens, Stefanos Tsitsipas was in the Gstaad final against Collignon. Two Greek players competing in ATP and WTA finals simultaneously. That does not happen very often, and it happened in Athens for the first time in decades. Krejcikova, the 2021 Roland Garros champion, reached the Athens final via a comeback win of her own over Clara Tauson (6-1, 4-6, 6-3 in the SF). She played well this week. But the crowd — and the moment — belonged to Sakkari. Were you watching the Athens final today? What a stage for Greek tennis to reclaim. ### Image Post Caption Sakkari wins it in Athens. ### Long-Form Post Paula Badosa won the UniCredit Iasi Open today, defeating Mayar Sherif in the final to claim her fifth career WTA title. She has now entered five WTA title matches and won five. That is a record worth paying attention to. This week said a lot about where Badosa is right now. She came back from a set and a break down against Tamara Zidansek in the semifinals, winning 3-6, 7-5, 7-6. She brought a six-match winning streak into Sunday's final. She has been playing through the draw like someone who expected to be there. The ranking picture makes this win even more meaningful. Badosa spent time outside the top 100 earlier in the year. Her Iasi title moves her back as high as No. 75 in the world — enough to enter the US Open directly. She also has a personal connection to this tournament. "I grew up following Simona," she said after the match, referencing Simona Halep, Romania's most celebrated player. Winning in Romania, at the same tournament where Halep's story looms large, clearly meant something beyond the ranking points.
