Djokovic Makes History With 106th Wimbledon Win, Surpassing Federer's All-Time Record

By Marcus Cole | Tennis Fanrecap | July 6, 2026

Wimbledon, London

Novak Djokovic did not play his best tennis on Sunday. He admitted as much afterward. But at 39 years old, on the grass courts he has mastered like no other, he did something no man in history has done: win for the 106th time at Wimbledon.

Djokovic defeated qualifier Roman Safiullin 7-6(4), 6-3, 3-6, 6-3 in the fourth round on Day 7, claiming sole possession of the all-time record for men's singles victories at the All England Club. The previous mark of 105 belonged to Roger Federer, who won the title eight times across his career. Djokovic, a seven-time champion at Wimbledon, now stands alone.

"A win is a win, even if you win ugly," Djokovic said afterward — and the quote captured everything about his performance. He dropped a set to Safiullin, a qualifier who had done nothing wrong. He never found the clean, dominant rhythm that has defined his best Wimbledon days. He simply ground through it, because that is what Djokovic does.

The Record That May Never Be Broken

To understand how significant 106 wins at a single Grand Slam is, consider what it required. Djokovic first won Wimbledon in 2011. He has now played 15 years of top-level tennis at the All England Club, navigating injuries, retirements, and the relentless rise of younger generations, and he is still here — still advancing, still collecting victories on the surface that suited him best.

Federer's 105 wins came across a career that produced five consecutive titles from 2003 to 2007, and an eighth title in 2017. No player aside from those two has come close to that level of sustained Wimbledon dominance. For Djokovic to surpass it at 39, in a year when many questioned whether he could still compete at the top level, makes the achievement all the more remarkable.

It is the kind of record that analysts project will stand for decades. To even challenge it, a player would need to start winning at Wimbledon in their early 20s and never stop for the better part of two decades.

Getting Ugly Against Safiullin

The path to 106 was not straightforward. Safiullin, a qualifier who had navigated his way to the fourth round without beating any seed of note, was a dangerous opponent — loose, with no pressure to perform, capable of big serving and aggressive groundstrokes. Djokovic dropped the third set 3-6 after winning the first two, and for a stretch, the four-set conclusion was not guaranteed.

But Djokovic broke back in the fourth set and closed out 6-3 to finish. The match lasted approximately three hours and 25 minutes — longer than the score suggests, longer than a first-seed match against a qualifier has any right to be. Djokovic's ability to close it out when it mattered, on a day when his best was not available, is itself a form of greatness.

He now advances to his 17th Wimbledon quarterfinal, where he will face No. 3 seed Felix Auger-Aliassime — a matchup with all the narrative weight of a generational chess match. Twenty-four Grand Slam titles versus zero. The record-holder versus the contender.

What's Next

Djokovic versus Auger-Aliassime is scheduled for the Wimbledon quarterfinals on Tuesday or Wednesday (Day 9 or 10). FAA, who defeated Alejandro Davidovich Fokina in a grinding five-set battle on Day 7, is one of the most dangerous players in the draw and will enter the quarterfinal with considerable momentum. Their head-to-head history and the stakes of the match — a potential eighth Wimbledon title for Djokovic, a first major for FAA — make this one of the most compelling matches of the fortnight.

For now, Djokovic holds the record. 106 wins. Sole possession. The Wimbledon history books have been rewritten.
