Barnes and Barrett Drop 33 Each as Raptors Destroy Cavaliers 126-104, Cut Series to 2-1

By Jake Torres | Hoop Heroes | April 24, 2026

Down 2-0 and facing elimination pressure at home, the Toronto Raptors did not fold. They exploded.

Scottie Barnes and RJ Barrett each scored 33 points — combining to shoot 23-for-36 from the field and 9-for-13 from beyond the arc — as the Raptors dismantled the Cleveland Cavaliers 126-104 in Game 3 on Thursday night in Toronto. The series still belongs to Cleveland, but the Cavaliers now head home with a 2-1 lead and a team that just reminded them it will not go quietly.

The signature sequence of the night came spanning the third and fourth quarters, when Toronto scored on 19 consecutive possessions — a sustained offensive onslaught that produced a 47-23 run and put the game out of reach. It was the kind of offensive performance that coaches dream about: efficient, relentless, and perfectly timed.

Stars Delivered When Needed Most

Barnes and Barrett have been Toronto's franchise cornerstones through a difficult rebuilding stretch, and Game 3 was their statement. Their combined shooting lines were as efficient as they get in a playoff setting — nearly 64 percent from the field and a scalding 69 percent from three-point range. For two young stars asked to carry a team against a Cleveland squad that had won the first two games away from home, this was the answer the Raptors needed.

The contributions did not stop with the starting two. Off the bench, Collin Murray-Boyles posted 22 points and 8 rebounds, providing the Raptors with a crucial third scoring option that Cleveland's defense struggled to account for. Jamison Battle went a perfect 5-for-5 from the field for 14 points, adding to a Toronto bench performance that outclassed Cleveland's reserves in every measure.

Cleveland's Defense Crumbles

The Cavaliers entered Game 3 having dropped just two regular season home games after the All-Star break, but Toronto turned Scotiabank Arena into their house. Cleveland's typically reliable half-court defense had no answer for Toronto's ball movement, and once Barnes and Barrett found their rhythm, the Cavaliers could not string together stops.

The 47-23 run that decided the game was not a product of Cavaliers turnovers or lucky bounces — it was pure offensive basketball. Toronto attacked mismatches, moved the ball ahead of the defense, and made open shots when they were available. It is a blueprint Cleveland's coaches will spend the next 48 hours trying to counter.

What's Next

Game 4 takes place Sunday, with Cleveland returning home to host a Raptors team that just regained its confidence. The Cavaliers still hold the series lead and the home court advantage for Game 4, but Toronto's performance on Thursday demonstrated that this matchup is far from settled. Barnes and Barrett are capable of this level on any given night, and Cleveland knows it now in a way they perhaps did not after winning the first two games. If the Raptors can steal one on the road and bring the series back to Toronto tied at 2-2, the complexion of this first-round matchup changes entirely.
