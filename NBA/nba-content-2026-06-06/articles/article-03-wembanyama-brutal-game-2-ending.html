29 Points and Still Heartbreak: Breaking Down Wembanyama's Brutal Game 2 Finale

By Damon Pierce | June 6, 2026 | Hoop Heroes

Victor Wembanyama scored 29 points on 11-of-21 shooting in Game 2 of the NBA Finals. Those are numbers a franchise player puts up. Those are numbers that should win games.

They didn't. And the reason why is a 30-second sequence so painful that it will define Wembanyama's first Finals appearance long after the series ends — for better or for worse.

The Sequence

With 13 seconds left and the Spurs trailing by one, 104-103, Wembanyama secured what should have been a routine defensive rebound. The Spurs were right there. One possession to either tie or take the lead.

He turned and fired a pass toward Stephon Castle. Castle wasn't looking back. The ball hit Castle squarely in the back and bounced loose. Jalen Brunson — who had been struggling all night, going 7-of-25 from the field — was in the right place. He pounced on the ball, drove hard to the baseline, and drew a foul as he stumbled out of bounds.

Brunson stepped to the free throw line. He made one of two, pushing New York's lead to two. 105-103 with 2 seconds left.

The Spurs inbounded quickly. Wembanyama caught, turned, and took an open 20-foot jumper as time expired. The shot was long. Knicks 105, Spurs 104.

The Context Around the Numbers

What makes those 30 seconds so agonizing is the quality of Wembanyama's overall performance. He was outstanding for the vast majority of Game 2 — aggressive in the post, creative from the perimeter, and disruptive on defense. A 29-point night on efficient shooting should produce a win in a one-possession game.

Instead, two moments — the errant pass and the missed buzzer shot — overshadowed everything that came before them. That is the cruelty of close games in the Finals. There is no context. There is no partial credit. There is only the final score.

Wembanyama is 22 years old. He is playing in his first NBA Finals after a remarkable run through the Western Conference playoffs, including a 41-point, 24-rebound double-overtime performance in the series. The Spurs would not be here without him.

The Burden of Being the Guy

Part of what makes Wembanyama's position so difficult is exactly what makes it extraordinary. When the Spurs need a basket, the ball finds him. When the game is on the line, the moment belongs to him. That is the life of a franchise superstar.

But with 13 seconds left and a chance to potentially steal a win at home, the sequence went sideways in a way that will haunt him. A pass that should have been routine. A buzzer shot that had to be perfect — and wasn't.

The Spurs are now down 2-0 and facing a hostile crowd at Madison Square Garden on Monday. History is not on their side. But Wembanyama, the unanimous Defensive Player of the Year, has the talent to flip this series with one dominant performance. He has done it before in these playoffs — most memorably in that double-overtime epic — and the Spurs need him to do it again.

What's Next

Game 3 is Monday, June 8 at 8:30 PM ET at Madison Square Garden. It will be the first NBA Finals game played at MSG in 27 years. For Wembanyama, it is the first road Finals game of his career. The Spurs need a win to avoid the near-impossible 3-0 hole that no team in Finals history has ever escaped. Game 4 is Wednesday, June 10, also at MSG.
