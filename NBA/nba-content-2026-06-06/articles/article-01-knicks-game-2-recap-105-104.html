KAT Carries, Brunson Clutches: Knicks Survive Spurs to Take 2-0 Series Lead

By Jake Torres | June 6, 2026 | Hoop Heroes

Jalen Brunson was a disaster from the field. He went 7-for-25 — the worst shooting performance of his entire postseason run. In any normal situation, that number buries a team's championship hopes.

The New York Knicks still won 105-104 over the San Antonio Spurs in Game 2 of the NBA Finals, and they still lead the series 2-0. They did it because Karl-Anthony Towns refused to let one bad night end their season, and because Brunson was just clutch enough when it truly mattered.

KAT Steps Into the Void

When Brunson's jumpers stopped falling, Karl-Anthony Towns picked up the slack in a massive way. The veteran big man finished with 21 points, 13 rebounds, and 4 assists, shooting an efficient 8-of-12 from the field with three made three-pointers.

Towns set the tone early, scoring 17 of his points before halftime and keeping the Knicks ahead during the stretches when Brunson was misfiring. His physical play in the post against Wembanyama created mismatches all night, and his rebounding on both ends helped stabilize the Knicks' defensive possessions.

Without Towns performing at that level, the Knicks lose Game 2 going away. He was the most important player on the floor for long stretches, and his efficiency made the difference in a one-point game.

The 13 Seconds That Decided Everything

The Spurs were furious in the fourth quarter. San Antonio went on a 14-0 run to close within one point, and the Knicks' offense had gone completely cold. The building at Frost Bank Center was alive with belief that a comeback was coming.

Then came 13 of the strangest seconds in recent Finals history.

Victor Wembanyama pulled down a defensive rebound and looked to initiate the offense. He fired a pass toward Stephon Castle — but Castle wasn't looking. The ball hit Castle in the back and caromed away. Brunson swooped in for the steal, drove hard toward the baseline, and drew a foul as he went out of bounds.

Brunson stepped to the line with 105-103 on the scoreboard and the Knicks' season on the line. He made one of two free throws, pushing the lead to two with just 2 seconds remaining.

The Spurs inbounded quickly. Wembanyama caught, turned, and launched a 20-foot jumper as time expired. It sailed long. New York 105, San Antonio 104.

Brunson's Night in Full

The statistics for Brunson read like a cautionary tale: 7-of-25 from the field, 2-of-8 from three, 20 points on enormous volume. By his standards — he averaged 26.9 points on quality shooting throughout the playoffs — it was a genuine off night.

But Brunson still contributed 6 assists and came up with 5 steals, including the biggest one of the series so far. The Knicks' point guard has proven throughout this postseason that he can win ugly when his shot isn't falling, and Friday night in San Antonio was the ultimate test of that resilience.

For the Spurs, the fact that the Knicks won despite Brunson's shooting struggles has to be alarming. New York's depth and versatility mean there is no single player to stop — and if Brunson gets right in Game 3, the Spurs will face a Knicks team firing on all cylinders.

What's Next

The series shifts to New York for Game 3 on Monday, June 8 at 8:30 PM ET at Madison Square Garden. It will be the first NBA Finals game played at MSG since 1999 — the last time these two franchises met in the championship round, a series the Spurs won 4-1.

The Knicks are now -490 series favorites after opening as +164 underdogs. San Antonio has never rallied from a 3-0 series deficit in NBA Finals history, making Monday's game as important as any the Spurs will play this year. With Wembanyama still capable of changing a series with one dominant performance, the Spurs are not done — but their margin for error has all but disappeared.
