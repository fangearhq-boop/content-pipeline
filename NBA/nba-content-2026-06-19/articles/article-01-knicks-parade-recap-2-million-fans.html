2 Million Strong: New York Celebrates the Knicks' First Championship Parade in 53 Years

By Marcus Cole | Hoop Heroes | June 19, 2026

New York came out in force Thursday morning, and the numbers confirmed what anyone standing in that crowd already knew: this was a day unlike any other in the city's recent memory.

An estimated two million fans packed Broadway from Battery Park to City Hall for the New York Knicks' first championship parade in 53 years. NYPD Commissioner Jessica Tisch called it the department's largest single-event deployment ever, with more than 10,000 officers on duty throughout Lower Manhattan. The 2026 Knicks didn't just win a title — they moved an entire city.

The 1970 and 1973 championship teams never got this. No Canyon of Heroes. No ticker-tape raining down on Willis Reed, no crowd roaring for Walt Frazier. Both of those teams won titles, but neither was celebrated on Broadway. Thursday was, in every sense, the first time the franchise had ever done this. For Knicks fans who grew up hearing about those championships from parents and grandparents, the weight of the moment was something else entirely.

Brunson's Moment With the Crowd

Jalen Brunson, the unanimous Finals MVP after his 45-point performance in the Game 5 clincher against the San Antonio Spurs, did what he always does: made it about the fans.

Along the parade route, Brunson hoisted the Larry O'Brien Trophy above the barrier and held it out toward the crowd so fans could reach up and touch it. It was the kind of spontaneous, unscripted moment that defines a champion's relationship with a city. For a player who left $113 million on the table to give New York the roster room to build this championship team, the gesture felt entirely in character.

At the City Hall celebration that followed, Mayor Zohran Mamdani presented the Knicks with Keys to the City. Brunson addressed the crowd alongside coach Mike Brown. Walt Frazier and Patrick Ewing — legends who never got this moment themselves — were in attendance.

Alicia Keys and the Emotional Crescendo

The ceremony at City Hall reached its emotional peak when Alicia Keys performed Empire State of Mind live — the Jay-Z anthem that has become as much a part of New York's sports identity as the Knicks themselves. The crowd, already emotionally raw from the parade, erupted.

For a franchise that spent decades as a national punchline, and for a fan base that endured some of the most painful losing seasons in professional sports, the combination of the championship, the parade, and that song in that setting was nearly too much to process. Several fans interviewed by local news described it as the greatest day of their lives.

What Made This Season Special

The Knicks went 4-1 over the San Antonio Spurs in the Finals, losing just three games total throughout the entire postseason. Brunson averaged 32.6 points, 4.6 assists, and 4.2 rebounds per game across the playoffs. Towns provided the interior presence that past Knicks teams lacked. The roster, built in part by Brunson's salary sacrifice, proved to be exactly the right combination of talent, toughness, and chemistry.

It was the kind of championship run that doesn't come along often — decisive, dominant, and deeply satisfying for a market that had been waiting since Richard Nixon was in the White House.

What's Next

The confetti has settled on Broadway, but the Knicks' front office doesn't have much time to enjoy the moment. The NBA Draft is just four days away, the Giannis Antetokounmpo trade deadline is June 23, and free agency opens June 30. The window to build on this championship is wide open — but so is the competition.

For one morning, though, New York stood still. Two million people on Broadway. Alicia Keys at City Hall. A franchise, a city, and a moment 53 years in the making.
