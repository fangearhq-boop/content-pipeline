No Team Has Ever Entered the Finals This Dominant — Can the Knicks Prove It's Real?

By Marcus Cole | Hoop Heroes | June 3, 2026

The numbers are not subtle.

No team in NBA history has ever entered the Finals with a better combined point differential than the 2026 New York Knicks. Their +271 mark across this postseason is the best on record for a team arriving at the championship round. Their net rating of +19.4 over the playoffs is historically elite. They are shooting 40.0% from three-point range — the best mark in this postseason field.

They have won 11 straight games. They swept the defending Eastern Conference powers in Boston 4-0 without a moment of doubt.

Tonight, they find out if it's enough to beat Victor Wembanyama.

The Path That Built This Streak

The Knicks' dominance didn't happen overnight. The process began in the regular season, where Jalen Brunson averaged 28.4 points and 6.1 assists per game — one of the best seasons by a Knicks guard in the franchise's modern era. Head coach Tom Thibodeau built a system that maximized Brunson's creation ability while surrounding him with elite floor spacers and connective players.

Karl-Anthony Towns and OG Anunoby emerged as the perfect complements. Towns, in particular, has been an offensive force in the playoffs — shooting 48.9% from three while also giving the Knicks a dominant interior presence on both ends. Anunoby's 48.3% three-point mark in the playoffs is equally remarkable for a player whose reputation was built primarily on defense.

In the Eastern Conference Finals sweep of Boston, Brunson averaged 27.8 points and 6.7 assists en route to ECF MVP honors. The Knicks held the Celtics under 107 points in three of four games. It was not competitive.

The Asterisk That Doesn't Disappear

Here is the honest accounting: the Knicks did not play Wembanyama in the East. They did not face a seven-game series in this playoff run. Their dominant numbers were built against the conference that produced zero Wembanyama-caliber players.

The Spurs went through seven grueling games against Shai Gilgeous-Alexander and Oklahoma City. Wembanyama was asked to perform at an elite level against the Western Conference's best for a month straight. San Antonio arrived at the Finals tested, steeled, and confident in their ability to win in adversity.

New York has not needed to find another gear yet. Tonight may ask them to find one they haven't shown.

Why the Numbers Still Matter

The skepticism is fair — but so is the evidence. Teams that enter the Finals with historic offensive numbers tend to perform historically in the Finals, too. The +271 point differential is not a mirage; it reflects consistent execution across three series against good defensive teams.

The Knicks also have nine days of rest heading into tonight's Game 1, against a Spurs team finishing a Game 7 less than four days ago. Recovery matters in a series that will run until at least June 13 if it goes to five games.

Most importantly: the Knicks believe. No team has lost 11 in a row without eventually facing a moment where belief wavers. This group has not found that moment yet.

What's Next

Game 1 tonight at 8:30 PM ET is the first real test of whether the Knicks' dominance translates to a Finals floor. A win would extend the streak to 12 and put immediate pressure on the Spurs' home-court advantage. A loss would raise reasonable questions about whether Wembanyama is the one matchup this Knicks team cannot overcome.

The streak will end eventually — all streaks do. The only question is whether it ends before or after the Knicks hold a trophy. Tonight is the first answer.
