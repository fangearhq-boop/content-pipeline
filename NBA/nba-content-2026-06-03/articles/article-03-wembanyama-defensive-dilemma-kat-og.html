Guard KAT or Guard the Rim? Wembanyama Faces His Toughest Defensive Test in the Finals

By Damon Pierce | Hoop Heroes | June 3, 2026

The New York Knicks have spent this entire playoff run setting a trap. In the NBA Finals, they're about to spring it on the best defensive player in the world.

The dilemma is elegant in its cruelty: Karl-Anthony Towns is shooting 48.9% from three-point range in these playoffs. OG Anunoby is shooting 48.3%. Both are legitimate offensive threats at the perimeter. Both demand attention. And both are capable of punishing any defensive breakdown from the arc.

Victor Wembanyama can guard the rim or guard the shooters. The Knicks have built a system that forces him to choose — and penalizes him for either answer.

How the Knicks' System Works

New York's offense operates from a five-out floor spacing concept. By placing five players capable of shooting from deep on the court simultaneously, the Knicks stretch defensive coverage to its limits. There is no comfortable spot for a rim protector when every player on the floor is a genuine three-point threat.

When Wembanyama stays near the basket, Towns and Anunoby fire open looks at rates approaching 49% — historically elite numbers that would punish any team. When Wembanyama steps out to contest, Jalen Brunson has a clear driving lane with no help defender behind him. Brunson, one of the best at finishing off the glass and drawing fouls, becomes unstoppable in those scenarios.

The Knicks are shooting a playoff-best 40.0% from three-point range this postseason. Their entire offensive architecture is designed to make this exact problem unsolvable.

Wembanyama's Record This Postseason

The degree of difficulty here is worth putting in context. Wembanyama averaged 27.3 points, 10.9 rebounds, 3.1 assists, 2.7 blocks, and 1.4 steals per game in the Western Conference Finals. He is the unanimous Defensive Player of the Year. He has, at 22, already redefined what an NBA center is capable of defensively.

Against Oklahoma City in the WCF, he anchored a defense that neutralized Shai Gilgeous-Alexander in Games 4 through 7 after SGA's dominant start to the series. The Thunder's offense ran through the paint — a matchup that plays directly into Wembanyama's strengths as a deterrent and shot-blocker.

The Knicks are not the Thunder. They do not want to drive into Wembanyama. They want to pull him away from everything he's best at.

San Antonio's Possible Solutions

Head coach Mitch Johnson and his staff have had time to study the problem. The most obvious counter is switching — assigning Wembanyama a specific player to track regardless of where the ball moves, rather than anchoring him at the basket. If he can stay attached to Towns without giving up driving lanes elsewhere, the Spurs limit damage at the arc.

The problem with switching is that Towns has the footwork and motor to post Wembanyama in isolation — another scoring avenue the Knicks would welcome. The Spurs may also look to use Harrison Barnes or Chris Paul as a drop coverage defender while Wembanyama plays higher up the paint, meeting Brunson at the point of attack rather than waiting at the rim.

There is no perfect answer. San Antonio's coaching staff knows that. The goal is not to solve the problem — it is to make the problem less catastrophic than the Knicks intend.

What's Next

Game 1 tonight at 8:30 PM ET on ABC is the first real-time test of how Mitch Johnson schemes around this challenge. Watch Wembanyama's positioning in the first three possessions — that will telegraph San Antonio's defensive game plan before any analyst has a chance to explain it.

If the Spurs hold the Knicks under 110 points in Game 1, expect the series to go deep. If New York exploits the spacing early and posts a 115-plus total, the pressure on Wembanyama to be perfect on both ends will intensify quickly. Either way, the tactical battle between these two teams is unlike anything the Finals has seen in years.
