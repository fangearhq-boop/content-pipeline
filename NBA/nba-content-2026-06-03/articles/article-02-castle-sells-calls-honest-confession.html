'I Sell Calls Too': Stephon Castle's Disarmingly Honest Finals Confession

By Marcus Cole | Hoop Heroes | June 3, 2026

Most players deny it. Stephon Castle leaned in.

At NBA Finals Media Day on Tuesday afternoon, the San Antonio Spurs' second-year guard was asked about flopping — the art of embellishing contact to draw fouls. It's a question designed to provoke denial, defensiveness, or a careful non-answer. Castle gave none of those things.

"I sell calls too sometimes. I mean, I can't lie," he said.

In a league where players and coaches carefully curate their public image, the answer landed like a splash of cold water. Simple. Direct. Honest.

The Full Context

Castle wasn't confessing to anything reckless. He was explaining his basketball philosophy with the clarity of someone who has thought deeply about every edge available to him on the floor. Asked whether not embellishing for calls might be leaving points on the table, he elaborated.

"It's really just a feel thing, especially in the playoffs," Castle said. "If it's too egregious, the refs are not going to bail you out... I think we talk to the refs a lot, especially me in particular, but most of the time they're right."

The statement reveals a sophisticated understanding of officiating dynamics that most players don't articulate publicly. Working officials — building a reputation, establishing contact early, communicating after whistles — is a craft. Castle is telling you he practices it.

The Assignment He's Been Given

This candor matters because of what Castle is about to attempt: stopping Jalen Brunson in the NBA Finals.

Brunson is one of the most decorated guards of his generation — ECF MVP, the engine behind New York's historic 11-game playoff win streak, a player who averaged 27.8 points and 6.7 assists per game in the Eastern Conference Finals. He creates in isolation, off screens, in the midrange, and at the rim. He plays under control when defenders give him space and attacks without hesitation when they overplay.

Castle has been San Antonio's answer to elite offensive guards all postseason. He is long, athletic, and quick enough to stay in front of guards smaller than him without getting burned. In WCF Game 7, he contributed 16 points, six rebounds, and six assists — showing his two-way impact matters far beyond the defensive end.

Why This Changes the Dynamic

In a Finals already loaded with tactical intrigue, Castle's confession adds a layer that few observers expected. The Spurs' primary perimeter defender has just told the world — and by extension the officiating crews — exactly how he operates.

Whether that transparency is calculated or purely authentic may not matter. What it does is establish Castle as someone who plays every angle, not just the ones that look good in a highlight. For a young player in his first NBA Finals, that kind of self-awareness is rare and potentially decisive.

Brunson has seen every kind of defender. He hasn't seen one who announced exactly what he was going to do to beat him — and meant it as a compliment to his own craft.

What's Next

The Castle-Brunson matchup begins tonight at 8:30 PM ET on ABC. Every possession will be a case study in what Castle described — feel, communication, and the fine art of working within the game's margins. Watch for early foul trouble on either side; it will reshape the series strategy for both coaches immediately.

Game 2 is Thursday, June 5 at Frost Bank Center. If Castle can limit Brunson to under 24 points tonight, expect the Spurs to run back the same scheme in Game 2 with minor adjustments. If Brunson explodes past 35, expect the Spurs to chase answers they may not have.

Either way, Stephon Castle told you everything. He's just a 21-year-old playing in his first Finals, guarding an All-Star, and working the refs — because that's how you win.
