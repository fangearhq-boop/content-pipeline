The Shot That Broke OKC: Wembanyama's 40-Foot Buzzer Beater Is Already a Playoff Legend

By Damon Pierce | Hoop Heroes | May 25, 2026

There are shots in NBA playoff history that become shorthand — a name, a team, a moment that anyone who watched remembers exactly where they were. Victor Wembanyama added one to that list on Sunday night in San Antonio.

With the first-half clock running down in Game 4 of the Western Conference Finals, Wembanyama caught the ball near the Spurs bench, took three deliberate dribbles to center court, and launched from 40 feet. The ball traced a perfect arc and splashed through the net as the buzzer sounded. Frost Bank Center went into a frenzy. Oklahoma City's bench stared. The Spurs led 50-38 going into halftime and never wavered.

According to GeniusIQ tracking data, it was the longest field goal by a Spurs player in the playoffs since the tracking service began in 2014. It was also the longest made shot of Wembanyama's career.

More Than Just a Shot

Context matters with plays like this. Wembanyama did not make a desperate heave from half court and get lucky. He arrived at that moment on the back of a dominant first half in which he had already shown every weapon in his arsenal.

He scored on a 27-footer just 24 seconds into the game. Within the game's first two minutes, he blocked a Chet Holmgren two-handed dunk attempt — a rejection that set the defensive tone for everything that followed. By the time the halftime buzzer sounded, Wembanyama had already scored 22 points. The 40-foot make was the exclamation point on a half that Oklahoma City could not have scripted worse for itself.

The Context the Stats Don't Capture

Wembanyama's final line — 33 points, 8 rebounds, 5 assists, 3 blocks — is remarkable for any player in any game. For a 22-year-old in the Conference Finals, it represents something approaching a generational statement. The shot and the block at the game's beginning and the halftime buzzer all share the same thread: a player operating with a confidence and skill set that simply does not match his age or experience level.

The psychological damage of the buzzer beater extended beyond the halftime scoreboard. Oklahoma City's offense never recovered, finishing the night at 33 percent from the field and 18 percent from three. SGA, averaging more than 30 points per game in this series before Sunday, was held to 19 on 6-of-15 shooting. The Spurs' defense disrupted every system OKC tried to run. But that buzzer beater — the moment — removed any belief OKC had left.

What's Next

The Western Conference Finals is now tied at 2-2 with Game 5 scheduled for Tuesday, May 26, at Paycom Center in Oklahoma City at 8:30 PM ET on NBC/Peacock. OKC enters as 5.5-point home favorites. The Thunder will be motivated by a home crowd and the memory of Sunday's blowout. But the Spurs now hold something money cannot buy — the momentum of a performance in which Wembanyama reminded the basketball world exactly who he is.
