New York's Draft Night Trade Chain: What the Knicks Got and Why It Matters

By Marcus Cole June 24, 2026

The Trade Chain, Explained

The New York Knicks entered June 23 holding the No. 24 pick and the No. 31 pick in the 2026 NBA Draft. They exited with five selections — zero in the first round and five in the second — and not a single addition to the rotation. That's not a failure. That's exactly the plan for a team that already has its core locked in and needs assets, not a 25th pick trying to crack a championship roster.

Here's how the chain worked. New York swapped No. 24 with the Los Angeles Lakers, who moved up to No. 25 and selected Sergio De Larrea, a Spanish guard. The Knicks then took De Larrea and immediately sent him to the Dallas Mavericks for No. 30 (Koa Peat) plus two second-round picks. They then packaged Peat to the Phoenix Suns for three additional second-rounders. Final haul: picks at No. 31, No. 47, and No. 55, with the flexibility that comes from holding five picks in a single draft class.

Why Second-Round Picks Matter for a Champion

The conventional wisdom is that second-round picks are filler. For rebuilding teams, that's sometimes true. For a championship team under a luxury-tax crunch, second-round picks are currency. They can be packaged together to acquire a veteran at the trade deadline when a team needs a specific skill set. They can be sent out to clear a contract. They give general managers Tom Thibodeau and Leon Rose something to negotiate with when the midseason market opens.

New York won the 2026 championship in June. They aren't drafting their way to another title. But holding five picks heading into trade season is considerably more useful than holding one first-rounder at No. 24 who wasn't going to see meaningful minutes on a team with Jalen Brunson, OG Anunoby, Karl-Anthony Towns, and a proven rotation already in place.

The Real June 30 Agenda: Brunson, OG, KAT

Free agency officially opens June 30, and that's when the Knicks' offseason actually begins. Brunson's extension is the priority — the point guard who carried New York to its first championship since 1973 won't be allowed to walk, and the two sides are expected to begin serious talks immediately after the moratorium lifts. OG Anunoby and Karl-Anthony Towns both have deals running through 2026-27, but securing extensions now would prevent any uncertainty heading into what could be a repeat run.

Repeating as NBA champion is hard. Since 2000, only five teams have done it. The Knicks' draft night strategy shows they understand the formula: keep the core intact, accumulate flexible assets, and don't reach for draft picks you don't need. The second-round chips are insurance. The championship blueprint is already hanging in the rafters.

What's Next

Free agency opens June 30. Brunson's extension talks are the first domino. If New York locks up Brunson early in July, expect OG and KAT conversations to follow quickly. The second-round picks from draft night will come up at February's trade deadline — keep an eye on what New York targets when the market opens and those picks become available sweeteners in a bigger deal.
