Larry Bird in 1986. Josh Hart in 2026. That's the Company He Keeps.

By Jake Torres | Hoop Heroes | June 4, 2026

When the final box score from Game 1 of the 2026 NBA Finals went up Wednesday night, Josh Hart's line read: 3 points on 1-of-5 shooting.

Stop there, and you have missed the entire story.

In 27 minutes of play, Hart grabbed 15 rebounds, dished out 6 assists, recorded 4 steals, and added a block. He became only the second player in NBA Finals history to post at least 15 rebounds, 5 assists, and 4 steals in a single game. The other player on that list is Larry Bird, who did it in 1986 for the Boston Celtics.

The New York Knicks won Game 1, 105-95. Hart was the biggest reason why.

What 3 Points Can't Capture

There is a running joke in New York that Josh Hart is the most important Knick who never leads the box score. Game 1 of the Finals made that case in the most emphatic way possible.

Hart grabbed second-chance rebounds on possessions the Spurs thought were over. He found cutters in traffic and delivered assists that opened up the offense at critical moments. His four steals disrupted San Antonio's rhythm in the half court, forcing turnovers that flipped momentum in a game the Knicks trailed by 14 points in the third quarter.

In the final minute, with the game still theoretically alive for San Antonio, Hart corralled a Victor Wembanyama turnover — the Spurs' star had dribbled the ball off his own foot — and held the ball while advancing the fastbreak to drain the clock. The game was effectively over from that moment.

The Knicks outscored the Spurs by 22 points during the minutes Hart was on the court.

The Larry Bird Parallel

Bird's 1986 Finals performance came in a series where the Celtics beat the Houston Rockets for the NBA title. It was the last championship of Bird's career, and the stat line Hart matched was built in a postseason defined by all-around play rather than pure scoring.

The parallel to Hart is more than statistical. Both players understand — and embrace — the idea that winning requires more than points. Bird's Celtics needed him to rebound, facilitate, and defend in that series. The Knicks need the same from Hart in this one.

For New York, Hart's presence solves problems that no other player on the roster addresses. He absorbs the physical work of guarding bigger wings. He vacuums up offensive rebounds against longer, more athletic defenders. He makes the plays that do not show up in highlight reels but absolutely show up in the win column.

The Bigger Picture

The Knicks did not get here on the back of Jalen Brunson alone, though Brunson's 30-point clutch performance in Game 1 rightly drew the headlines. Karl-Anthony Towns' 18 points and 12 rebounds mattered. OG Anunoby's defensive presence mattered. The coaching adjustments Knicks head coach Tom Thibodeau made in the third quarter mattered.

But Hart's 27 minutes were the connective tissue. He was everywhere the Spurs did not want him to be, on both ends of the floor, for the entirety of his time on the court.

What's Next

Game 2 is Friday at 8:30 PM ET on ABC from Frost Bank Center. The Knicks lead the 2026 NBA Finals 1-0. San Antonio will adjust — they always do — but one of their primary adjustments in Game 2 is likely to assign Wembanyama to guard Hart early, daring the low-scoring wing to beat them with the ball in his hands.

If the strategy works and Hart struggles offensively, the Spurs can shift their defensive attention elsewhere. If Hart responds — with another line of 15 rebounds and six assists regardless of his point total — then the Knicks probably win Game 2 as well.

Larry Bird company is not a small thing. And if Hart keeps playing like this, the Knicks are going to be very hard to stop.
