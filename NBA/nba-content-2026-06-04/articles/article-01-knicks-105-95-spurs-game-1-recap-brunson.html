Down 14, Then Dominant: Brunson's Clutch 30 Gives Knicks Game 1 on the Road

By Damon Pierce | Hoop Heroes | June 4, 2026

The San Antonio Spurs led the New York Knicks by 14 points in the third quarter of Game 1. They had home court. They had Victor Wembanyama. They had the loudest building in the Western Conference Finals behind them.

It did not matter.

Jalen Brunson scored 30 points — 13 of them in the fourth quarter — as the Knicks came back to beat the Spurs 105-95 on Wednesday night at Frost Bank Center, taking a 1-0 series lead in the 2026 NBA Finals. New York extended its postseason winning streak to 12 consecutive games and grabbed home-court advantage in a series they were not supposed to control from the jump.

How the Knicks Came Back

Trailing by double digits with the Spurs crowd energized, the Knicks did not panic. That has been their calling card all postseason — an unshakeable composure that has seen them through every difficult moment since the first round began.

Brunson was the anchor. When San Antonio extended its lead in the third, Brunson made individual plays in traffic that kept New York within striking distance. And when the fourth quarter opened and the Knicks needed someone to take over, Brunson did exactly what he has done in every big moment this postseason: scored efficiently, made the right decisions under pressure, and willed his team forward.

Karl-Anthony Towns added 18 points and 12 rebounds, providing the big-body presence that forced Wembanyama into difficult defensive decisions all night. Towns shot efficiently from mid-range and from three, giving the Knicks the floor spacing to let Brunson operate in the paint on key possessions.

New York ultimately closed the game with a 12-0 run, turning a tight contest into a convincing final margin. Brunson's corner three-pointer with two minutes remaining gave the Knicks a lead they would not surrender.

The Streak That Keeps Growing

The win was the Knicks' 12th consecutive playoff victory, the second longest single-season postseason winning streak in NBA history. The 2017 Golden State Warriors hold the record at 15 straight. The Knicks now sit two games back of that mark.

Over those 12 wins, New York has outscored its opponents by 272 combined points — 30 more than the Warriors managed during their historic 2017 run. The Knicks are not just winning. They are dismantling teams.

San Antonio had home court entering this series as the Western Conference champion. Winning Game 1 in San Antonio shifts the series dynamic entirely — the Knicks can now afford to split in San Antonio and return to Madison Square Garden with a series lead.

What Wembanyama Showed — and Did Not

Victor Wembanyama finished with 26 points and 12 rebounds, but the numbers obscure a difficult night. He shot 6-of-21 from the floor and 2-of-9 from three — a performance well below his postseason standard. In the final six minutes, fatigue appeared to be a real factor, and a limping exit in the third quarter had Spurs fans briefly holding their breath. The Spurs will need significantly more from their franchise cornerstone in Game 2. When Wembanyama is locked in — as he was during the Western Conference Finals against Oklahoma City — San Antonio is a different team entirely. The question heading into Friday is whether he can find that form again.

What's Next

Game 2 takes place Friday, June 5 at 8:30 PM ET on ABC from Frost Bank Center in San Antonio. The Spurs remain home-court favorites at -218 on the moneyline heading into Game 2, with the Knicks listed as +180 underdogs. San Antonio will need to make significant adjustments — particularly around Wembanyama's efficiency and fourth-quarter stamina — to even the series before it moves to Madison Square Garden.

For the Knicks, the goal is simple: do what they have been doing for the last 12 games. Stay composed. Play through Brunson. Let Towns and OG Anunoby space the floor. And trust that Josh Hart will make the plays nobody else in the league makes.

Twelve wins. One series lead. The Knicks look every bit like a team that intends to add an exclamation point to what has already been the most dominant postseason run in recent memory.
