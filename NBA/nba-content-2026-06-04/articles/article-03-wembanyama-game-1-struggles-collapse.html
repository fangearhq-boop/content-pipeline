Wemby's Nightmare Game 1: 6-of-21, Gassed in the Fourth, and a Limping Exit

By Marcus Cole | Hoop Heroes | June 4, 2026

Victor Wembanyama had 26 points and 12 rebounds in his NBA Finals debut. He also added 3 blocks and, at moments, looked every bit like the generational talent who just won his second Western Conference Finals MVP award.

And yet the Spurs lost by 10. And the reason why is a story that starts and ends with Wembanyama himself.

The 22-year-old shot 6-of-21 from the floor and 2-of-9 from three-point range in Game 1. He traveled in the fourth quarter. He dribbled the ball off his own foot on a critical possession late in the game. And in the third quarter, he limped off toward the bench after tangling with Josh Hart, sending Frost Bank Center into silence. An MRI later cleared him — no ligament damage — but the image of Wembanyama hobbling off the floor was the defining visual of Wednesday's loss.

The Shooting Collapse

Wembanyama's 28.6% shooting mark in Game 1 was the lowest of his 2026 postseason. Through the Western Conference Finals against Oklahoma City, he shot better than 45% from the floor in four of seven games. Game 1 was an outlier — and the Knicks deserve significant credit for forcing it.

Karl-Anthony Towns and OG Anunoby present a defensive nightmare for any center. Both shot better than 48% from three in these playoffs, which means Wembanyama cannot simply retreat toward the rim and protect his natural habitat. He must venture to the perimeter to guard shooters, leaving him exposed to pump fakes, isolations, and Brunson drives that bypassed him entirely.

The result was a scattered performance. Wembanyama looked uncomfortable in ways he has not looked since his rookie year — uncertain of which threat to prioritize, late on rotations, and slightly off-balance on the offensive end when he finally got the ball in spots he normally converts.

Gassed in the Closing Minutes

The final six minutes of Game 1 told the most important story. With roughly 40 minutes on his legs from a game that had pushed him defensively on every possession, Wembanyama went 1-for-5 from the floor in the closing stretch. He committed a travel. Then, on a possession where the Spurs desperately needed a stop, he dribbled the ball off his foot — a turnover that Hart converted into a fastbreak clock drain that effectively ended the game.

The Spurs' defensive system asks Wembanyama to do more than any other center in the league. He is the primary rim protector, the perimeter defensive anchor, and the focal point of San Antonio's offensive sets, all in the same night. Against a team as physical and relentless as the Knicks, that load appeared to finally catch up with him in the game's final act.

The Limp Scare

The third-quarter limping episode — Wembanyama moving slowly toward the San Antonio bench after a physical exchange with Hart — had the Frost Bank Center crowd on edge. He waved off the training staff, returned to the game, and the post-game MRI revealed no structural damage to his left knee.

But the moment was a reminder of how fragile the Spurs' title window becomes if Wembanyama is compromised. San Antonio built this entire run around his unique combination of skills. Without him healthy and efficient, they are a good team. With him, they are a genuine contender. The limping image, however brief, made that precarious balance visible.

What's Next

Game 2 is Friday at 8:30 PM ET on ABC at Frost Bank Center. The question for Spurs head coach Mitch Johnson is how to manage Wembanyama's minutes and defensive assignments differently than he did in Game 1. Limiting the defensive space Wemby must cover — perhaps by playing more help defense on Towns and daring the Knicks to beat them with someone other than Brunson — could preserve Wembanyama's energy for the fourth quarter when it matters most.

Wembanyama is too good to repeat a 6-of-21 performance. His bounce-back games after poor outings have been some of his best this postseason. The bigger concern for San Antonio is not whether Wemby will improve — he will — but whether the Knicks can force a similar workload on him in Game 2 and get similar results.

If so, New York could go up 2-0 in San Antonio before the series ever shifts to Madison Square Garden.
