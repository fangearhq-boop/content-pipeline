Wembanyama's Historic 39/15/5 Night Lifts Spurs Past Timberwolves 115-108 in Game 3

By Jake Torres | May 9, 2026 | Hoop Heroes

There are nights in the NBA when you watch something and understand immediately that you'll be telling people about it for years. Thursday night in Minnesota was one of those nights. Victor Wembanyama scored 39 points, grabbed 15 rebounds, and blocked 5 shots — putting his name alongside Kareem Abdul-Jabbar, Hakeem Olajuwon, and Shaquille O'Neal as the only players in NBA playoff history to record those numbers in a single game.

The San Antonio Spurs won 115-108 to take a 2-1 series lead over the Minnesota Timberwolves, and the final score underplays how thoroughly Wembanyama dominated the game's most critical moments.

The Fourth Quarter Belonged to Wemby

San Antonio led by varying margins throughout the game, but it was the final period that revealed everything about who Wembanyama has become as a playoff performer. He scored 18 of his 39 points in the fourth quarter — a superhuman response to the Timberwolves' push to take the game back on their home floor.

Every time Minnesota threatened to close the gap, Wembanyama had an answer. A mid-range jumper here. A drive through contact there. A catch-and-finish that looked impossible given the angle and the defender's position. He shot 13-of-18 from the field in total, a display of efficiency that would be remarkable for any player but borders on alien when combined with the defensive impact he had on the other end.

Five blocks in a playoff game matters differently than five blocks in a regular season matchup. Every blocked shot in the playoffs carries a weight of finality — the Wolves couldn't find clean looks, couldn't predict where Wembanyama would arrive from, and couldn't run actions that scouted favorably against him.

Joining the Legends

The statistical threshold — 35 points, 15 rebounds, and 5 blocks in a single playoff game — has only been cleared three times before in NBA history. Kareem Abdul-Jabbar did it. Hakeem Olajuwon did it. Shaquille O'Neal did it. Those are three of the greatest big men to ever play the game, and now a 22-year-old from France has joined that list in just his second playoff run.

Wembanyama came into this postseason with something to prove after the Spurs' first-round experience last year. He has delivered emphatically. His combination of size (7-foot-4 wingspan that defies measurement), skill (a shooting range that extends to the three-point line and beyond), and basketball IQ (he reads passing lanes, anticipates screens, and understands positioning at a level that takes most players a decade to reach) has no historical parallel. The comparisons to past players feel inadequate because he is, quite literally, a different kind of basketball player.

Edwards Was Magnificent — And Still Couldn't Win

Anthony Edwards deserves significant credit in any honest account of this game. He scored 32 points, pulled down 14 rebounds, and distributed 6 assists — a triple-double-adjacent performance from a player fighting with everything he had for a Minnesota win. On most nights, against most opponents, that kind of line wins basketball games.

Stephon Castle supplied 13 points and 12 assists for San Antonio, ensuring the Spurs had secondary creation to complement Wembanyama's scoring. The ball moved cleanly. The offense didn't stagnate.

What's Next

The Spurs have stolen home court from the Timberwolves and now lead the series 2-1. Game 4 returns to San Antonio, where a win would give the Spurs a near-insurmountable 3-1 series lead.

For Minnesota, the challenge is existential: how do you guard a player who can score from anywhere, protect the rim from everywhere, and impact the game in ways that don't show up in any single box score category? The Timberwolves don't have an easy answer. But Anthony Edwards is not the kind of competitor who concedes quietly. Game 4 in San Antonio will tell us a great deal about whether this series has another chapter or whether Wembanyama is about to drag his team to the conference finals.
