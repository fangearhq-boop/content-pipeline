Embiid Returns, 76ers Still Fall — Philadelphia Down 0-3 and Facing the Impossible

By Marcus Cole | May 9, 2026 | Hoop Heroes

Joel Embiid ran out of the tunnel in Philadelphia on Thursday night and the building erupted. For a few seconds, everything felt possible again — the 76ers' star was back, their chance for a miracle was alive, and the team that had been humiliated in Games 1 and 2 in New York might finally have its moment.

Embiid played 35 minutes. He scored 18 points. The 76ers lost 108-94 and now trail the Knicks 0-3 in the Eastern Conference Semifinals. No NBA team has ever come back from a 0-3 deficit to win a series. Not once, in the history of the league.

Philadelphia's season is over in every meaningful way except the final buzzer.

The Embiid Question

Embiid returned after missing Game 2 with a right ankle sprain and hip soreness that kept him from playing at all. His 18-point, 35-minute performance wasn't a disaster, but it confirmed what many suspected: he isn't fully healthy, he isn't able to impose his will the way he can at his peak, and the Knicks have schemed well enough against him that even a limited version of Embiid can't change the series' trajectory.

The Sixers needed 30-point Embiid. They got a diminished version working through pain, and the result was the same as the games he missed. That reality will fuel enormous debate in Philadelphia this summer about whether his long-term health is compatible with the team's championship window — or whether that window has already closed.

No OG, No Problem for New York

The Knicks played Game 3 without OG Anunoby, who sat out with a hamstring strain. Their best perimeter defender — the player most responsible for making Embiid's nights miserable — wasn't on the floor, and the Knicks still won by 14 points. That's the part that should most alarm Philadelphia's front office.

Jalen Brunson (33 points, 9 assists), Mikal Bridges (23 points), Landry Shamet (15 off the bench, +20), and Josh Hart (12 points, 11 rebounds) were all better than anything the Sixers could produce collectively. Karl-Anthony Towns managed 12 rebounds and 7 assists while in foul trouble. This New York team doesn't need everything to go perfectly — it just needs enough people to execute, and there are always enough people willing to do it.

What Went Wrong in Philadelphia

The 76ers are talented. Tyrese Maxey had 26 points in Game 2. Paul George and Kelly Oubre Jr. can contribute. VJ Edgecombe has flashed genuine upside. But the roster was built around the assumption of a healthy, dominant Embiid — and that version hasn't been consistently available all season.

Philadelphia's inability to create off the dribble without Embiid as the primary option, combined with the Knicks' suffocating switching defense and transition discipline, made for a predictable outcome across three games. The 76ers could never string together enough possessions to build and sustain leads. The Knicks could.

What's Next

Game 4 is Monday, May 10, at 3:30 PM ET on ABC. It will be played in Philadelphia before a crowd that will either witness a miraculous beginning of a comeback or the end of their team's season.

History says it's the latter. But the 76ers will play, because that's what teams do — and because Embiid hasn't gotten to this stage of his career by accepting defeat easily. Monday's game will matter regardless of the outcome, if only because it will tell us how these players handle facing the hardest truth in professional basketball: that some years, the better team wins before you're ready to go home.
