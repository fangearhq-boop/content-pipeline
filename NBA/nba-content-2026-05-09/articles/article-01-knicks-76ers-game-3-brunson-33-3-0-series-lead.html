Knicks 108-94 76ers Game 3 — Brunson 33, Bridges 23 Put New York One Win From Sweep

By Damon Pierce | May 9, 2026 | Hoop Heroes

The New York Knicks are playing the best basketball of the Tom Thibodeau era, and they're doing it with multiple key contributors sitting on the bench in street clothes. Thursday night's 108-94 Game 3 victory in Philadelphia — a win that pushes the Knicks to a commanding 3-0 series lead — wasn't just convincing. It was historic. New York is now one win from its first best-of-seven playoff sweep since 1999.

Without OG Anunoby, who sat out with a hamstring strain, and with Karl-Anthony Towns managing foul trouble through much of the night, the Knicks leaned on two players who have become the identity of this postseason run: Jalen Brunson and Mikal Bridges.

Brunson and Bridges Are Unstoppable Together

Brunson finished with 33 points and 9 assists, operating with the calm efficiency of a player who has been in this moment a hundred times in his mind. He attacked mismatches, ran the pick-and-roll with Town and Bridges, and never allowed Philadelphia's defensive adjustments to slow him for more than a possession or two before finding a new crease to exploit.

Bridges was the perfect complement, scoring 23 points on 8-of-14 shooting. He's been the most improved performer of this postseason run — a player who seemed to disappear in stretches earlier in the year but has arrived at exactly the right time. His ability to score off movement, catch-and-shoot, and attack off the dribble gives New York a true second option when defenses key on Brunson.

Together, they combined for 56 points. The 76ers had no answer for either of them individually, let alone both at the same time.

The Supporting Cast Stepped Up Again

Landry Shamet is fast becoming the unlikely hero of this postseason. Off the bench, the veteran guard dropped 15 points in 26 minutes and finished a stunning +20. He hit shots when New York needed them, played within the offense, and gave Thibodeau another trusted option to go to when the starters needed rest.

Karl-Anthony Towns was disrupted by early foul trouble but still managed 8 points, 12 rebounds, and 7 assists — a quietly excellent all-around performance that didn't show up loudly in the box score but kept New York's offense flowing and Philadelphia's big men occupied. Josh Hart logged a vintage effort as well: 12 points and 11 rebounds of the gritty, relentless variety that Hart has made his trademark.

Embiid Returned — It Didn't Matter

Joel Embiid made his return after missing Game 2 with a right ankle sprain and hip soreness. He played 35 minutes and scored 18 points — a reasonable output from a player who clearly wasn't at full physical capacity. The building went electric when he took the floor. But the Knicks treated his return like any other wrinkle to account for and adjust to, not a turning-point moment.

That speaks to where this series is: New York has solved Philadelphia's best player. When Embiid has the ball in the post, the Knicks make things difficult. When he catches in space, they have the personnel to contest. And when Embiid rests, the Sixers don't have the secondary creators to maintain offensive pressure.

What's Next

Game 4 is Monday, May 10, at 3:30 PM ET on ABC. The game will be played in Philadelphia, where the 76ers have a crowd that will be desperate, loud, and emotionally invested in extending the season.

The Knicks don't care about any of that. They've already taken Games 1, 2, and 3 by a combined margin of more than 50 points. No team in NBA history has ever overcome a 0-3 series deficit to win. New York will not be taking its foot off the pedal. The sweep is within reach, and this group of Knicks looks like the kind of team that closes things out.
