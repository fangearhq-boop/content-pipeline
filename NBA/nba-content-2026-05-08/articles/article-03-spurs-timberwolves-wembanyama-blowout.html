Wembanyama and Spurs Obliterate Wolves 133-95 — Minnesota's Worst Playoff Loss in Franchise History

By Damon Pierce | May 8, 2026 | Hoop Heroes

It was supposed to be a competitive series. The Timberwolves had home-court advantage in Game 1, an All-Star lineup, and enough talent to push the San Antonio Spurs. But on Wednesday night, Game 2 became a humiliation that will be remembered in Minnesota for a long time: the Spurs won 133-95, and that 38-point margin stands as the worst postseason defeat in Timberwolves franchise history.

Victor Wembanyama needed just 26 minutes to post 19 points and 15 rebounds. The scariest part? San Antonio won this game so comfortably that they could afford to rest him before it was over.

Wembanyama Barely Needed to Try

When the best player in a blowout win is described as subdued — and he still finishes with 19 points, 15 rebounds, and 2 blocks in 26 minutes — that tells you everything about how one-sided this game was. Wembanyama was efficient and dominant without ever appearing to exert himself. He shot 7-for-15, knocked down a pair of threes, and controlled the paint on both ends while San Antonio's supporting cast made the result academic well before the fourth quarter began.

Stephon Castle led all scorers with 21 points, picking up the offensive slack and allowing Wembanyama to focus energy on defense. De'Aaron Fox added 16. The Spurs shot 50% from the field and 41% from three — clinical numbers that left Minnesota with no avenue back into the game.

Minnesota Was Never in It

The Timberwolves shot 29.8% from the field in the first half. They went 2-for-15 from three before the break. They trailed by 25 at halftime. Those aren't numbers from a team that was close and simply ran out of gas — those are numbers from a team that was outclassed from the opening tip and never found a way to compete.

Anthony Edwards, Julius Randle, Jaden McDaniels, and Terrence Shannon Jr. each finished with 12 points, but none of them could generate any offensive rhythm against San Antonio's disciplined and versatile defense. The Wolves held to just 35 points in the first half while the Spurs pushed the lead to a margin that rendered the second half meaningless.

A Historic Embarrassment Demands a Response

The Timberwolves organization has been building toward a deep playoff run for years. Anthony Edwards is one of the most exciting young stars in the game. Julius Randle was acquired specifically to help Minnesota compete at this level. And yet here they stand, down 0-2 after suffering the most lopsided loss in their playoff history.

There's no sugarcoating it. The Spurs didn't just beat Minnesota in Game 2 — they exposed the gap between where this Wolves team currently is and what it takes to beat a San Antonio roster that has absorbed all of Wembanyama's growth and built everything else around him. The speed, the size, the shooting, and the system the Spurs play with are a different challenge than anything Minnesota faced in the first round.

What's Next

Game 3 is tonight in Minneapolis at 9:30 PM ET on Amazon Prime Video. The Timberwolves get to play in front of their home crowd, and that environment will matter — Target Center can be a genuinely hostile arena when the fans are engaged and the team is playing well.

But the Wolves need more than a crowd. They need Edwards to recapture the aggressive, attacking form that made him so dangerous all season. They need their three-point shooters to find their range. And they need to find a way to slow down Wembanyama — or at least make him work harder than 26 minutes to dominate them. The series is tied 1-1, but the momentum belongs entirely to San Antonio.
