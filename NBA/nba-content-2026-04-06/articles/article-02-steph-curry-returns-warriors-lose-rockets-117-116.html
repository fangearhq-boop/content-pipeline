Curry Returns With 29 Points Off the Bench — Warriors Lose 117-116 Heartbreaker to Rockets

By Marcus Cole April 6, 2026

The Return Everyone Was Waiting For

Steph Curry hadn't played a basketball game in 27 games — nearly two full months away from the court with a right knee injury. When he re-entered Golden State's lineup Saturday night against the Houston Rockets, he did something he hadn't done in 14 years: he came off the bench. In 26 minutes, he scored 29 points, went 5-for-10 from beyond the arc, and looked entirely like himself. The Warriors still lost, 117-116, in one of the more bittersweet final-week games in recent memory.

Alperen Sengun delivered the dagger, hitting a go-ahead interior bucket with 11.1 seconds remaining to seal Houston's narrow win. Kevin Durant paced the Rockets with 31 points — the game-high. Curry's 29 came in an efficient, electrifying performance that showed two things clearly: he didn't lose anything sitting out those 27 games, and the Warriors are going to be a problem in the Play-In Tournament.

Off the Bench for the First Time in 14 Years

Steve Kerr handled Curry's return with caution, bringing him off the bench to manage his minutes after a lengthy layoff. The decision meant the Chase Center crowd had to wait slightly longer than expected, building anticipation that paid off when Curry entered in the second quarter. Alongside him on the floor at one point: Seth Curry, his brother, in what NBC Bay Area noted was the first time the two had played together in Warriors franchise history.

The bench debut was more procedural than symbolic — Kerr wants to ease the greatest shooter in basketball history back into full game shape before the Play-In on April 14. What the performance showed, though, is that the process isn't going to take long. Curry hit his first three-pointer, ran Golden State's offense with the fluidity of someone who'd played all season, and finished with only the narrow loss to show for it.

Houston Makes a Statement, Golden State Gets a Glimpse

For the Rockets, the win was a meaningful statement. Houston has been one of the West's most consistent teams since Sengun's return in mid-March, and surviving Curry's return against the Warriors demonstrated playoff-level toughness. Durant's 31-point performance alongside Sengun's 24-point, go-ahead basket signals that Houston has the offensive firepower to compete with any team in the West.

For Golden State, the loss doesn't change the fundamental story. The Warriors locked into the No. 8 West Play-In seed well before Saturday. What changes now is the calculus everyone else has to do. A Curry-led Warriors team in a one-game Play-In against Phoenix is a genuinely dangerous thing. He put up 29 in his first game back. What happens with a week of practice and full game conditioning heading into April 14?

What's Next

Golden State faces the No. 7 Phoenix Suns in the West Play-In Tournament on April 14, exclusively on Prime Video. The winner advances directly to the NBA Playoffs; the loser gets one more chance in the elimination game. With Curry back and a week of full-team practices ahead of them, Golden State enters the Play-In as one of the most dangerous teams in the field. The Suns will have to be ready — a fully locked-in Curry is a different challenge than anything they've seen this season.
