Block Party, Wrong Result: Wembanyama Shatters NBA Playoff Blocks Record but Wolves Steal Game 1

By Damon Pierce | Hoop Heroes | May 5, 2026

Victor Wembanyama recorded 12 blocks — the most in a single playoff game in NBA history — and San Antonio still lost 104-102 as Anthony Edwards returned early to close out the Timberwolves' road win.

Victor Wembanyama did something Monday night that may never be replicated in the NBA Playoffs. He blocked 12 shots — shattering the all-time postseason record for blocks in a single game. He also had 11 points and 15 rebounds, completing one of the rarest triple-doubles in basketball history.

And the San Antonio Spurs lost anyway.

The Minnesota Timberwolves escaped Frost Bank Center with a 104-102 victory in Game 1 of the Western Conference Semifinals, stealing home-court advantage on the back of an unexpected hero: Anthony Edwards, who was not supposed to be on the floor at all.

Wembanyama's Historic Night

The numbers defy comprehension. The previous NBA playoff record for blocks in a single game was 10. Wembanyama matched that mark in three quarters. By the time the final buzzer sounded, he had added two more.

His 12 blocks came in all shapes: help-side rejections, one-on-one denials at the rim, and impossibly timed recoveries that should not be physically possible for a player of any size, let alone one standing 7-foot-4. He finished with 11 points, 15 rebounds and the record-breaking block total — a triple-double by the most unusual accounting imaginable.

"More blocks than points" became a refrain across social media, and while that fact is statistically unusual, it almost undersells just how dominant his defensive performance was. Wembanyama was a one-man zone for stretches of this game, altering shots the Wolves thought were clean and forcing Minnesota into contested floaters from angles they did not want.

Edwards Changes the Equation

The Spurs did not lose because of poor defense or poor effort. They lost because Anthony Edwards returned from injury ahead of any reasonable schedule.

Edwards hyperextended his left knee and was expected to miss at least the first two games of this series. Instead, he was in the lineup for Game 1, coming off the bench after Minnesota's medical staff cleared him with some caution. He played 25 minutes, shooting 8-for-12 from the field and finishing with 18 points.

Eleven of those came in the fourth quarter, when the Spurs were trying to protect a slim advantage and close the game at home. Edwards arrived and made sure that did not happen. His aggressive drives and mid-range pull-ups in the final 10 minutes were decisive, helping Minnesota overcome Wembanyama's historic defensive showcase and escape with the two-point win.

Julius Randle Steadies Minnesota

Before Edwards delivered his fourth-quarter statement, Julius Randle was the engine keeping the Timberwolves competitive. Randle finished with 21 points and 10 rebounds, providing the kind of sturdy baseline scoring that Minnesota needed when Wembanyama was blocking everything around the basket and forcing adjustments.

Randle's ability to operate in mid-range and on the perimeter gave Minnesota scoring options that did not require driving into the paint, which Wembanyama was effectively closing all night. That spacing ultimately proved crucial in keeping the Wolves close enough for Edwards to finish the job.

The Spurs' Uncomfortable Reality

For San Antonio, this loss stings in a specific way. The Spurs entered this game as the No. 2 seed in the Western Conference, playing at home, with one of the most extraordinary players in basketball history performing at a level that should guarantee victory.

The Spurs have now lost their home opener for just the second time in their last 17 such games — a sharp contrast with their regular-season dominance at Frost Bank Center. Deriving comfort from Wembanyama's individual performance is valid, but it is also incomplete. San Antonio's offensive support did not match the defensive effort he provided.

Game 2 remains in San Antonio. The Spurs have every reason to believe they can recalibrate. But on a night when their best player was historically great and they still came up short, the margin for error in this series now belongs to Minnesota.

What's Next

Game 2 of the Timberwolves-Spurs series returns to Frost Bank Center in San Antonio. The central question: Can San Antonio generate enough offense to complement Wembanyama's defense, or will Edwards continue to make the Spurs pay for any late-game lapses? Minnesota has shown it can win on the road with or without its best player fully healthy. That is a dangerous thing to demonstrate in Game 1.
