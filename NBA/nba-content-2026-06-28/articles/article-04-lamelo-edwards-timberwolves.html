LaMelo Ball + Anthony Edwards: The Timberwolves Just Built Something Special

By Jake Torres | Hoop Heroes

June 28, 2026

Three days ago, the Minnesota Timberwolves made one of the most significant trades of the entire offseason. On June 25, the deal was completed: LaMelo Ball and Josh Green are heading to Minnesota in exchange for Naz Reid, a 2033 unprotected first-round pick, three pick swaps (2028, 2029, 2030), and three second-round picks (2029, 2032, 2033).

The trade officially executes July 6. But the picture is already clear. Anthony Edwards and LaMelo Ball are going to be teammates. The Western Conference should be paying attention.

What LaMelo Brings

LaMelo Ball is 24 years old and just completed a season in which he averaged 20.1 points and 7.1 assists per game while earning $40.8 million annually. His passing vision is genuinely elite — the kind of playmaking ability that forces defenses to account for him even when he does not have the ball, because when he does, something creative is about to happen.

His shooting from deep has been a polarizing topic throughout his career, but his ability to create offense for others and push pace gives Minnesota a dimension the team was missing. Edwards has been spectacular as a scorer and the team's clear alpha, but he has been doing it largely without a co-creator who could take real pressure off him in the half court. LaMelo changes that equation.

The Edwards Dynamic

Anthony Edwards is one of the most talented players in the league. His combination of scoring ability, athleticism, and competitive intensity makes him a building block for any franchise. The Timberwolves have built around him with that in mind.

The addition of LaMelo raises an interesting question about how roles will be defined. Two players who both want the ball, both want to create, and both have legitimate claims to lead-guard minutes will need to find a rhythm. On paper, the skill sets are complementary — Edwards' off-ball scoring and finishing at the rim pair with LaMelo's passing and pace-setting. In practice, the fit will need to be developed, and the early stretch of next season will be the real test.

It is a high-quality problem to have. Teams that are managing backcourt dynamics because they have too much talent are in a better position than teams wondering where their next star is coming from.

The Price Minnesota Paid

This trade was not cheap. The Timberwolves gave up Naz Reid — a capable big man who had carved out a significant role in Minnesota's rotation — plus a 2033 unprotected first-round pick and a bundle of pick swaps and second-rounders. That is a substantial haul of future assets heading to Charlotte.

The bet Minnesota is making is that the window to compete in the West is open right now, and filling it with a star-level backcourt partner for Edwards is worth depleting future flexibility. That is an aggressive posture — and it is exactly the kind of move that separates franchises that are willing to commit to contention from those that perpetually hedge.

Charlotte Begins Its Rebuild

For the Charlotte Hornets, this trade is the beginning of a new chapter. LaMelo Ball was the centerpiece of the franchise and the player around whom Charlotte had been trying to build. Moving him, even for the package Minnesota sent, represents an acknowledgment that the current construction was not working.

Naz Reid gives Charlotte a proven NBA contributor. The picks — particularly the 2033 unprotected first — give the front office future assets to work with. This is a foundational reset, not an instant turnaround, and Charlotte fans should expect the rebuild to take time before the fruits of this deal become apparent.

What's Next

July 6 is when the trade officially clears. After that, Minnesota will turn its attention to surrounding Edwards and LaMelo with the right complementary pieces — whether through free agency, the trade market, or internal development. The Timberwolves have made their statement. The Western Conference is deep and competitive, and the question of how this backcourt fits together against elite competition will not be answered until real games are played. But the foundation is undeniably intriguing. Watch for Summer League and early training camp coverage to reveal how Edwards and LaMelo are actually coexisting before the season tips off.
