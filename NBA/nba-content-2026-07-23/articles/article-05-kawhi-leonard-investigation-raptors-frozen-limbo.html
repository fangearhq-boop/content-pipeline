A Year Later, the Kawhi Investigation Has No End in Sight — and the Raptors Are Paying for It

By Damon Pierce | Hoop Heroes | July 23, 2026

Approximately one year ago, the NBA opened an investigation into whether the Los Angeles Clippers had provided Kawhi Leonard with impermissible benefits in violation of league salary cap rules. As of July 23, that investigation remains open. No conclusion. No timeline. No trade.

The team bearing the cost most directly is the Toronto Raptors — who agreed in principle to receive Kawhi Leonard from the Clippers in exchange for Brandon Ingram, Gradey Dick, and multiple draft picks — and who have been frozen in place ever since.

What the Investigation Actually Covers

The NBA retained law firm Wachtell Lipton to conduct the probe. The original allegation: Aspiration Fund Adviser LLC, a financial services company, paid Leonard a $28 million endorsement deal that functioned as a form of disguised compensation — essentially a violation of the salary cap's restrictions on non-uniform player benefits. Aspiration has since filed for bankruptcy. Alex Sanberg, who was connected to the probe, has been sentenced to 14 years in prison.

The investigation has since expanded. HoopsHype reported in mid-July that the scope now includes a second, previously undisclosed endorsement deal with an unnamed company, as well as personal expense coverage. What began as a single-deal inquiry has grown substantially in both complexity and duration.

Silver's Deadline and the Reality

Commissioner Adam Silver has not been quiet about his expectations. At his Summer League media availability, Silver said the investigation "needs to be wrapped up before next season." His language has been consistent: this must be resolved before the 2026-27 season begins.

But also as of mid-July, HoopsHype reported the conclusion remains "weeks away." A Silver deadline and a Wachtell Lipton timeline are two different things. The probe's complexity — now spanning multiple deals, a bankrupt company, and a fraud conviction — has made a clean resolution elusive.

What the Raptors Are Left With

Toronto declined to assume the legal risk of completing the trade while the investigation was pending — a defensible decision, but one that leaves them in an unusual position. They cannot build their roster around a player they may or may not receive. They cannot officially move forward without clarity. They are operating in a holding pattern that has lasted a full year.

For a franchise that won the 2019 NBA championship — and has been in varying stages of rebuild since — this is a particularly costly form of uncertainty. Every summer week that passes without resolution is a week of planning lost. Free agents have been signed, re-signed, and waived. Roster decisions have been made across the league. The Raptors are watching from the sideline of their own offseason.

What's Next

The NBA's options when the investigation concludes run a spectrum: no action against the Clippers, fines and draft pick forfeitures, suspension of Leonard, or voiding of the contract. That last possibility is the nuclear option — it would have enormous ripple effects on Leonard's career and the trade's viability. The Clippers have denied any wrongdoing throughout.

At 35, Kawhi Leonard is not getting younger. Every month of investigation is a month of his remaining peak that passes without clarity. The Raptors need an answer. The league needs a conclusion. Right now, neither has one.
