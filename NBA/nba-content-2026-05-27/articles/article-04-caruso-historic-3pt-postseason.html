On Pace for NBA History: Alex Caruso's Playoff Three-Point Surge

By Jake Torres | May 27, 2026 | Hoop Heroes

Alex Caruso is shooting 47.7 percent from three in the 2026 NBA playoffs. During the regular season, he shot 29.3 percent from beyond the arc. That 18.4 percentage-point jump from the regular season to the postseason is on pace to be the largest such improvement in NBA history — and it is happening in the Western Conference Finals.

In Game 5 against the San Antonio Spurs on Monday night, Caruso underscored the point with a 22-point performance in just 22 minutes off the bench. He went 4-of-8 from three, 8-of-8 from the free-throw line, added 6 assists and 3 steals, and was a significant factor in Oklahoma City's 127-114 victory that pushed the Thunder to a 3-2 series lead.

How the Jump Happened

Caruso's postseason shooting improvement is not random variance — it reflects the specific conditions that the Thunder's offensive system creates for him. During the regular season, Caruso was often asked to handle the ball in pick-and-roll situations, run point guard minutes, and take a wider variety of shots. The result was a catch-all role that produced inconsistent looks from three.

In the playoffs, Caruso operates in a more defined capacity alongside Shai Gilgeous-Alexander. He moves off the ball almost exclusively — using screens, sliding to the corners, and relocating to the arc while SGA commands defensive attention at the elbows and mid-range zones. The three-pointers he receives are clean catch-and-shoot opportunities, the highest-efficiency type in modern basketball. His shot selection has not changed; the quality of those shots has dramatically improved.

That improvement compounds across a full postseason. The WCF series average for Caruso — 17.0 points per game on 56.8 percent shooting from the field and over 58 percent from three — reflects a player operating in an environment perfectly tailored to his strengths.

The Historical Context

The 18.4 percentage-point jump between his regular-season and playoff three-point marks puts Caruso in historic territory. While individual playoff sample sizes are smaller than a regular season, the trend has been consistent enough across multiple rounds to suggest it is real rather than a hot streak.

Caruso is 32 years old and in his ninth NBA season. He was undrafted, cut from two teams early in his career, and has been primarily known as an energy defender and connective player throughout his career. The fact that his defining statistical moment is occurring on the biggest stage — in the WCF, potentially heading into the NBA Finals — is a storyline that transcends the numbers.

What It Means for the Thunder

Oklahoma City's Finals case rests heavily on SGA's production, but the Thunder's depth is what separates them from one-star teams. Chet Holmgren, Jared McCain, Cason Wallace, and Caruso all contribute in ways that extend possessions, win defensive sequences, and punish opponents who focus their attention on the two-time MVP.

Caruso's three-point shooting, specifically, has made OKC's offense nearly impossible to guard in half-court sets. Teams cannot hedge too far off him without surrendering an open arc shot. They cannot fight through screens too aggressively without losing track of SGA coming off the same action. The result is a spacing advantage that manifests in SGA getting the ball in ideal positions on nearly every possession.

The Thunder are one win from the NBA Finals. If Caruso maintains even a fraction of his playoff efficiency in that series, Oklahoma City's offense will pose a challenge no defense has fully solved in the 2026 postseason.
