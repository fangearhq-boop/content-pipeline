Two Wins Away: The Knicks Haven't Won a Title Since 1973 — That Could Change This Week

By Marcus Cole | June 8, 2026 | Hoop Heroes

The New York Knicks won their last NBA championship on May 10, 1973. The roster featured Willis Reed, Walt Frazier, Dave DeBusschere, and Bill Bradley — names that belong to a different era of basketball, a different era of New York, a different world entirely. Most Knicks fans alive today have never seen their team raise the Larry O'Brien Trophy.

After tonight's Game 3 at Madison Square Garden, they could be two wins away from changing that forever.

What This Team Has Built

The road here was not sudden. This Knicks team has been methodically assembled over years of difficult decisions, near-misses, and small steps forward. What they have produced in the 2026 playoffs is something the franchise has never seen in five decades.

Entering tonight, the Knicks have won 13 consecutive playoff games. Their point differential across this entire postseason run stands at plus-270. They swept their Eastern Conference Finals opponent in four games, then traveled to San Antonio — one of the league's most intimidating venues — and won both road games to open the NBA Finals.

They are only the third team in NBA Finals history to win the first two games on the road.

ESPN Analytics gives them a 58.2% win probability for tonight's Game 3. The Spurs are +380 to win the series.

A History of Near-Misses

Knicks fans understand the weight of "close" better than most fan bases.

The 1994 Finals: Patrick Ewing and John Starks took the Houston Rockets to seven games. Starks went 2-for-18 in Game 7. Houston won. Ewing never got a ring.

The 1999 Finals: The franchise reached the championship round as the No. 8 seed — improbable and electric. But San Antonio, led by Tim Duncan, was simply too good. The Spurs won in five games, including the clincher at MSG on June 25.

After 1999: the post-Ewing years, the Isiah Thomas era, years of lottery picks and dysfunction, the rebuild, and finally — starting around 2021 — the slow, patient construction of something real.

The 1994 and 1999 runs are still talked about with pride in New York. But they ended without championships. The drought has lasted 53 years.

Why This Team Might Actually Finish It

What separates this Knicks team from those near-misses is depth and balance. Jalen Brunson does not need to be perfect every night — he proved that in Game 2 when he shot 7-for-25 and the Knicks still won. Karl-Anthony Towns, Josh Hart, O.G. Anunoby, and a roster full of contributors mean no single bad performance can sink the team.

That depth is what has powered the 13-game winning streak. It is what produced the plus-270 point differential. And it is why, even against a generationally talented player in Victor Wembanyama, the Knicks have found ways to win every time the Spurs threatened to turn the series.

What's Next

Game 3 tips off tonight at 8:30 PM ET at Madison Square Garden on ABC. A Knicks win puts them one game from the sweep — and, more significantly, one game from a situation where even a series loss would require San Antonio to win four straight against a team that has not lost a playoff game in over a month.

For the generation of Knicks fans who inherited the drought — who grew up hearing about Willis Reed and Walt Frazier but never experienced a title themselves — tonight is the most important home game since 1999. The difference is that this time, New York has the lead.

Fifty-three years is a long time. The end might be this week.
