29 Points Wasn't Enough. Now Wembanyama Has to Face MSG in Game 3.

By Jake Torres | June 8, 2026 | Hoop Heroes

For 47 minutes and 47 seconds of Game 2, Victor Wembanyama was everything the San Antonio Spurs needed.

He scored 29 points on 11-of-21 shooting. He defended the paint. He kept his team in a game where Jalen Brunson shot 7-for-25 from the field. With Wembanyama carrying the offense and the Spurs clinging to a lead in the fourth quarter, a series-tying victory felt not just possible but earned.

Then came the final 13 seconds.

The Moment That Defined the Series

Wembanyama's errant pass became a turnover. Brunson drew a foul. Two free throws fell. Final score: Knicks 105, Spurs 104.

It was a devastating sequence for a 22-year-old in his first NBA Finals — the kind of moment that tests character and composure in equal measure. After the game, Wembanyama met the media with the poise of a veteran twice his age.

"We can't change the past," he said. "We're already thinking about Game 3."

That sentence encapsulates something remarkable about Wembanyama. He is not defined by the moment that just happened. He is already recalibrating, already focused on the next opportunity. For a player who has been under the brightest spotlight in professional basketball since before he was drafted, the emotional composure is a feature, not a surprise.

The Challenge Tonight

But composure will only take the Spurs so far tonight. Madison Square Garden in an NBA Finals setting is one of the most hostile environments in professional sports. The crowd that awaits Wembanyama has been waiting 27 years for this moment. They watched their team lose this same series in 1999 against this same franchise. They are not going to be quiet, and they are not going to be forgiving.

What Wembanyama needs in Game 3 is not just production — he delivered that in Game 2 — but production without catastrophic error. His scoring ability is not in question. His turnovers have been. In Game 1, he had six turnovers to go with a difficult 6-for-21 shooting performance. In Game 2, the shooting improved dramatically, but the late turnover loomed over everything else.

To win tonight and stay alive, the Spurs need their franchise player to give them 48 clean, complete minutes. They also need their supporting cast to show up around him in a way they have not managed in Games 1 or 2.

What the Knicks Are Prepared For

New York has been preparing for the best version of Wembanyama all series. The Knicks reportedly use what some players have called the "Wemby Drill" in practice — a coach holding a broom at rim level to simulate the shot-blocking threat he presents. The preparation is a compliment to his dominance and a tactical acknowledgment of his uniqueness.

But the Knicks also know his tendencies. They know he can be rattled into turnovers under pressure. They know his free-throw percentages late in games and his tendencies when fatigued. The Josh Hart performance in Game 1 — 15 rebounds, 6 assists, 4 steals — was built on specific preparation and the willingness to crash into every possession as if his career depended on it.

What's Next

Game 3 tips off tonight at 8:30 PM ET on ABC at Madison Square Garden. For Wembanyama, the moment is as large as any he has faced in his career. He has already proven he belongs — two Finals games with production levels that would define most players' postseason careers. Now he needs to prove he can win in an environment that wants nothing more than to see him fail.

The 22-year-old who said "we can't change the past" is right. He cannot. But he can change what comes next — if he delivers the 48 minutes the Spurs need, and if his supporting cast finally shows up alongside him.
