Jalen Brunson's Legacy Night: One Win from Joining New York's Greatest Champions

By Damon Pierce | Hoop Heroes | June 13, 2026

The summer of 2022, Jalen Brunson left the Dallas Mavericks and signed with the New York Knicks. The contract was large. The expectations were enormous. The pressure in New York is always enormous. And the critics — there were plenty of them — suggested that a backup point guard getting a max contract from a franchise that hadn't won since 1973 was just another chapter in a long story of Knicks dysfunction.

Tonight, at 8:30 PM ET in San Antonio, Jalen Brunson has the chance to end that story permanently.

What He Has Done

The Knicks are in Game 5 of the 2026 NBA Finals largely because of Jalen Brunson. He scored 30 points in Game 1 of this series, setting the tone in enemy territory. He scored 36 in Game 4 — a performance that helped engineer the largest comeback in NBA Finals history, a 29-point second-half deficit erased in real time with the series on the line.

But the points are only part of the story. It is the steadiness that makes Brunson extraordinary. When the Knicks trailed by 27 in the third quarter of Game 4, there was no visible panic from the point guard at the center of their offense. Just the next play. Just the next basket. Just the next defensive stop. The Knicks clawed back because their leader never suggested that clawing back was impossible.

He has been the engine of a 13-game win streak heading into these Finals. He has been the player San Antonio's defense has focused on most, and he has beaten it consistently enough to put his team on the doorstep of the championship.

The Historical Weight

The last time the New York Knicks won an NBA Championship was May 10, 1973. Willis Reed, playing through an injury that has become one of the defining images in sports history, walked out onto the Madison Square Garden floor and inspired his team to beat the Los Angeles Lakers. Walt Frazier had one of the greatest Games 7 performances in basketball history: 36 points, 19 assists. New York celebrated in the streets.

For 53 years, no Knick has put on a championship ring. The franchise has passed through Patrick Ewing's near-misses, the Sprewell-Houston Finals run in 1999, and decades of rebuilding cycles that seemed to go nowhere. Every year that passed added weight to the drought.

Tonight, Brunson has the chance to end all of that weight with one more road win in San Antonio — the kind of win he and his teammates have already delivered three times this postseason.

The Road Closer

There is something fitting about the Knicks potentially clinching a championship on the road. They have been the ultimate road warriors in these playoffs — perfect in three elimination games away from MSG, winning each by an average of 39.3 points. They opened this very series by winning Games 1 and 2 in San Antonio. This team does not need home-court advantage to close.

If anything, Brunson and the Knicks have been more dangerous when the building is hostile. As if the noise from opposing fans only sharpens their focus.

What's Next

If Brunson delivers tonight — and the Knicks win their first championship since 1973 — his place in New York sports history is secured permanently. He joins Frazier and Reed in the conversation as the men who brought a championship to the most demanding sports city in the world. The city will celebrate like it hasn't in over half a century. The wait ends tonight in San Antonio. Tip-off is at 8:30 PM ET.
