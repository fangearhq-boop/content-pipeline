Wembanyama's Road Back: What 'Playing Normal' Must Look Like in Game 2

By Damon Pierce | Hoop Heroes | June 5, 2026

Victor Wembanyama is 22 years old, and he is remarkably calm for a player who just went 6-of-21 in the most important game of his life so far.

"I just have to play normal," he said Thursday. "I'm not worried. We just need to approach the game with a better mental state."

It is the right attitude. It is probably the only correct thing to say. Whether it translates to the floor tonight — when Game 2 of the 2026 NBA Finals tips at 8:30 PM ET on ABC — is the most important question in the sport right now.

What Went Wrong in Game 1

Wembanyama's Game 1 numbers — 26 points, 12 rebounds, three blocks — look fine on first glance. They are not fine. He took 21 shots to score those 26 points, made only two of nine from three, and turned the ball over six times. In the final six minutes of the game, he was visibly fatigued: a travel, a dribble off his own foot, contested jumpers when he had no legs underneath him.

The Knicks made him work on both ends all night. Karl-Anthony Towns and OG Anunoby — two players Wemby has to track to the three-point line defensively — shot a combined 48-plus percent from three during this postseason. Every possession Wemby spent chasing those two to the arc was a possession that drained his legs. By the time the Knicks needed a comeback in the fourth quarter, Wemby was a half-step slow.

That is the trap the Knicks set. And the Spurs walked right into it.

Shaq's Prescription

Shaquille O'Neal's analysis on Thursday cut to the core of the problem: Wembanyama is settling for jump shots. He needs to attack the paint.

It sounds simple. It is not. The Knicks defend Wemby's perimeter shooting with their length — Josh Hart, OG Anunoby, and Mikal Bridges all have the wingspan to contest without fouling when Wemby operates outside the paint. But when Wemby uses his footwork to get inside — where his combination of height, touch, and reach creates impossible defensive angles — the Knicks have to help. They have to rotate. They have to foul. And all of those outcomes open the game up for the rest of San Antonio's offense.

The six turnovers in Game 1 are the other problem. Wemby needs to make simpler decisions when he is doubled — find the outlet, move the ball, reset the possession. The Spurs had only 16 team assists in Game 1. That is the number of a team that stopped moving the ball under defensive pressure instead of working through it.

The Mental Reset

There is a version of tonight where Wembanyama comes out attacking — straight to the rim, drawing fouls, finishing at the glass, generating the rhythm that unlocks the Spurs offense and forces the Knicks defense to scramble. There is a version where Devin Vassell hits three or four threes, the assists total reaches 25, and San Antonio's movement offense looks like the machine that dismantled Oklahoma City in seven games.

Wemby's composure suggests he believes that version is possible. The Spurs would not be here if they could not get back to themselves. San Antonio won 60 games this regular season, won a brutal Western Conference Finals, and has a 22-year-old who has only scratched the surface of what he can do.

One bad game is one bad game. Wemby knows it. Now he has to show it.

What's Next

Game 2 is tonight at 8:30 PM ET on ABC from Frost Bank Center in San Antonio, where the Spurs are favored by 5.5 points. A Spurs win ties the series and sends it to New York for Games 3 and 4 on even terms. A Knicks win puts San Antonio in a desperate situation — down 2-0, with the next two games at Madison Square Garden, in a building that has been the loudest arena in the NBA all postseason.

Wembanyama says he just needs to play normal. Tonight is his chance to prove it.
