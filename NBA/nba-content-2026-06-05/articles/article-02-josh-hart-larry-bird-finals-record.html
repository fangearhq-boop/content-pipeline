The Hart of It All: Josh Hart Joins Larry Bird in the NBA Finals History Books

By Marcus Cole | Hoop Heroes | June 5, 2026

Larry Bird did it in the 1986 NBA Finals, Game 3 against the Houston Rockets: 25 points, 15 rebounds, 11 assists, four steals. A masterpiece from the greatest Boston Celtic of all time, in the biggest games of that era.

Forty years later, Josh Hart matched him.

Not in points — Hart scored three. But in the combination of rebounds, assists, and steals that makes a Finals game line historically unusual, Hart was right there with Bird: 15 rebounds, six assists, four steals, and a block in 27 minutes during the New York Knicks' 105-95 Game 1 victory over the San Antonio Spurs on Wednesday night. It is the first time any player has posted 15-plus rebounds, six-plus assists, and four-plus steals in an NBA Finals game since Bird's legendary 1986 effort.

The Knicks were plus-22 in Hart's 27 minutes.

A Different Kind of Greatness

The basketball public has spent years trying to contextualize what Josh Hart is, because his game defies easy categorization. He is not a superstar. He is not a sixth man. He is not a role player in any traditional sense, because "role player" implies limited impact — and Hart's impact is not limited at all. It is just invisible to people who only look at scoring lines.

Wednesday night made it visible.

Hart spent Game 1 being everywhere. He boxed out Victor Wembanyama on possessions where nobody else could have won the battle. He found teammates cutting to the basket for clean looks that did not show up in anyone else's stat line. He picked pockets, ran the floor, and contested shots by players six inches taller than him with a fearlessness that has become his calling card.

When Wembanyama turned the ball over in the final minutes of Game 1 with the Knicks nursing a late lead, it was Hart who corralled the loose ball and made sure the Spurs had no chance to extend the possession. It was a play that did not decide the game — the Knicks were already ahead — but it was the kind of play that separates teams that win from teams that almost win.

What Brunson Saw

After the game, Jalen Brunson was asked about his teammate's performance. He called Hart "a kid." It was the most Brunson possible response — part affectionate, part knowing, entirely accurate.

Hart plays like a kid. He plays with the kind of joy and relentless effort that looks effortless from the outside, but is actually the product of years of understanding exactly what his team needs from him in every moment. There is no ego involved. There is no concern about whether the box score reflects what he contributed. He knows. His teammates know. And on Wednesday night, the entire country got to watch the explanation unfold in real time.

Brunson has been New York's offensive engine all postseason. Karl-Anthony Towns has provided the stretch-big presence that opened the floor. OG Anunoby has locked down opposing wings. Mitchell Robinson has provided the physical counter to Wembanyama inside.

But in Game 1, on the road, in the NBA Finals, it was Josh Hart who made the Knicks plus-22 without scoring.

The Streak Behind the Streak

The Knicks' 12-game playoff winning streak is the second-longest single-season postseason run in NBA history. The larger story, if you want to find it, is that Hart has been a constant in every one of those wins — not as the scorer, but as the player who made the winning possible.

Players like Hart are easy to appreciate in retrospect and easy to undervalue in the moment. Right now, in the moment, his Game 1 line is extraordinary. And in forty years, someone will look up who was the last player to match those Finals numbers — and find a name most of the sports world has not heard of yet.

That is probably fine with Josh Hart.

What's Next

Hart and the Knicks return for Game 2 tonight at 8:30 PM ET on ABC from Frost Bank Center in San Antonio. The Spurs will have made adjustments. The Knicks will be tested differently. The question is whether Hart's impact can survive a defense that now knows exactly what to expect — and has to guard it anyway, because nobody has figured out how to stop someone who does not need to score to ruin your night.

Forty years. Larry Bird. Josh Hart. Company that tells you everything about what kind of player gets to that level of Finals impact without needing a single highlight.
