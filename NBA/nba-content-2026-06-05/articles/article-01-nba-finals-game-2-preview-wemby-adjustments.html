Can the Spurs Bounce Back? What Has to Change in Game 2 of the NBA Finals

By Jake Torres | Hoop Heroes | June 5, 2026

The San Antonio Spurs will take the floor tonight at Frost Bank Center knowing one thing clearly: what they showed in Game 1 is not going to be enough.

Game 2 of the 2026 NBA Finals tips off at 8:30 PM ET on ABC, with San Antonio entering as 5.5-point home favorites despite trailing the series 0-1. The Knicks took Game 1 on the road by 10 — coming back from 14 down in the third quarter — and they now arrive at Game 2 riding a 12-game postseason winning streak. The pressure, plainly, is on San Antonio.

Victor Wembanyama is calm. That much was evident in his postgame comments after Game 1, and in his media availability on Thursday. "I just have to play normal," he said when asked about his 6-of-21 shooting performance. "I'm not worried. We just need to approach the game with a better mental state."

The composure is genuine. But composure alone will not solve what ailed the Spurs on Wednesday night.

The Offensive Breakdown

San Antonio's offense fell apart against the Knicks' switching, length-heavy defense. The Spurs shot just 25.6 percent from three-point range in Game 1 and managed only 16 assists as a team — both figures well below their postseason averages coming in. The ball stopped moving in critical moments, isolations replaced action, and Victor Wembanyama — who averaged more than 30 points per game in the Western Conference Finals — was neutralized by a combination of contested look denial and late-game fatigue.

Shaquille O'Neal identified the key adjustment on Thursday: Wembanyama has to stop settling for jump shots over outstretched arms and attack the paint instead. The Knicks are most comfortable defending Wemby when he is operating at the perimeter, where their long-armed wings can contest without fouling. When Wemby gets into the paint — where his footwork, touch, and length create impossible angles for defenders — the entire Knicks defense is forced to collapse and help. That opens shooters. That generates fouls. That is the offense San Antonio needs.

Devin Vassell, who shot 39.7 percent from three during the Western Conference Finals, was quiet in Game 1. The Spurs will need him to threaten from the perimeter in Game 2, drawing attention away from Wemby's drives and creating the one-on-one opportunities that define San Antonio's offense at its best.

The Physical Question

There is an element of this series that nobody is saying out loud but everybody is thinking about. Wembanyama spent enormous defensive energy in Game 1 — covering ground from three-point line to paint, contesting Karl-Anthony Towns and OG Anunoby from the perimeter while also protecting the rim. He limped in the third quarter. He was visibly gassed in the final six minutes, struggling with turnovers and inefficient shots when his legs were clearly heavy.

An MRI after Game 1 came back clean — no ligament damage. Wembanyama is expected to play. But his workload on both ends of the floor is a conversation the Spurs coaching staff needs to have before tip-off. San Antonio may need to be more selective about how much defensive ground Wemby covers, particularly late in close games.

Mitchell Robinson, who has played through a broken pinkie this postseason, gives the Knicks a physical presence at center that forces the Spurs to think carefully about any physical approach to limiting Wemby. The Knicks will not be bullied.

The Knicks' Formula

New York does not need to change a thing. Jalen Brunson — who scored 30 points in Game 1, including 13 in the fourth quarter — will continue to operate as the engine of everything the Knicks do. Karl-Anthony Towns stretched Wemby to the perimeter with his shooting, opened the lane for Brunson drives, and finished with 18 points and 12 rebounds.

And Josh Hart? Hart finished Game 1 with 15 rebounds, six assists, four steals, and a block in 27 minutes. He controlled the glass, created chaos on the defensive end, and made the Knicks plus-22 in his minutes despite scoring just three points. That kind of impact is extraordinarily difficult to game-plan against.

The Knicks' formula entering Game 2 is simple: play the same way, trust the same process, and let the streak grow.

What's Next

Game 2 tips off tonight at 8:30 PM ET on ABC from Frost Bank Center in San Antonio. If the Spurs win, the series returns to New York tied 1-1. If the Knicks take Game 2, they would return to Madison Square Garden with a commanding 2-0 lead and the ability to close the series at home.

The Spurs are favored. Frost Bank Center will be electric. Wembanyama says he is not worried.

Tonight, we find out if he's right.
