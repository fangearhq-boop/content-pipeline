Silver Says Kawhi Investigation Must End Before Next Season. It Has Been Running 11 Months.

By Jake Torres

July 20, 2026

The NBA's investigation into the LA Clippers and Kawhi Leonard is still running — and it just grew larger.

The probe, launched nearly a year ago and conducted by law firm Wachtell Lipton, has now expanded to include a second previously undisclosed endorsement arrangement involving Leonard, as well as additional personal expenses that the Clippers organization may have covered for the star forward. That scope expansion comes on top of the original inquiry into whether the team circumvented the salary cap through a $28 million endorsement deal with Aspiration, a green banking company that simultaneously held a $300 million, 23-year naming rights agreement with the Clippers' arena before going bankrupt.

Commissioner Adam Silver addressed the investigation publicly and directly: "I would say this can be wrapped up — and needs to be wrapped up — before next season."

It was the most pointed statement Silver has made about the probe since it began. The implication was clear: nearly a year is long enough, and the league will not enter the 2026-27 season with this unresolved.

A Trade in Limbo

The practical consequence of the investigation is a trade that cannot be finalized. On June 30, the Clippers and Toronto Raptors agreed to send Kawhi Leonard north — Toronto would receive the former Finals MVP in exchange for Brandon Ingram, Gradey Dick, multiple first-round picks (including unprotected selections in 2031 and 2033), a 2027 pick swap, and two second-round picks.

That trade exists on paper only. The NBA placed it on hold pending the investigation's conclusion. The Raptors, who gave up genuine assets to acquire Leonard, cannot officially add him to their roster, integrate him into their offseason planning, or even guarantee his participation in training camp. Every day the investigation continues is another day Toronto cannot move forward.

The Clippers maintain they are innocent. The organization says it was defrauded by Aspiration co-founder Joe Sanberg, who was subsequently convicted on unrelated fraud charges and sentenced to 14 years in federal prison. The Clippers argue any money routed through Aspiration was the result of Sanberg's misconduct, not their own cap circumvention.

What the Expansion Means

When an investigation expands rather than contracts, it typically signals one of two things: either investigators are finding evidence they need to follow, or they are being thorough enough to withstand a legal challenge from whichever side loses. In either case, the Raptors should not expect a fast resolution simply because Silver issued a public deadline.

The second endorsement deal that has now entered the probe's scope has not been publicly identified. The nature of the personal expenses under scrutiny has also not been disclosed. Both will need to be examined, documented, and adjudicated before Wachtell Lipton can submit a final report to the league.

Silver's statement may create pressure, but investigations of this complexity rarely accelerate on a commissioner's timetable. The league's last major cap circumvention case — the league's investigation into a different team's endorsement arrangement — took 14 months from launch to resolution.

What's Next

The Raptors will continue building their roster around the uncertainty. Toronto is not helpless — they retain their own picks and players, and they can sign and develop talent in the interim. But they cannot fully commit to a direction that depends on Kawhi Leonard's presence until the investigation closes and the trade is either cleared or voided.

Silver's public pressure is the most significant development in the case in months. Whether it translates into a faster resolution depends entirely on what Wachtell Lipton finds and how quickly it can be formally submitted. The Raptors, and the rest of the league, are watching.
