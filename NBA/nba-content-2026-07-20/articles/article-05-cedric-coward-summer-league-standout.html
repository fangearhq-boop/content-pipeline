Cedric Coward's Summer League: 28 Points in the Semis, 19 in the Final, and a Statement Made

By Damon Pierce

July 20, 2026

Cedric Coward already had an NBA career before this summer. That context matters when measuring what he accomplished in Las Vegas over the past two weeks.

The Memphis Grizzlies wing, who starred at Washington State and Eastern Washington before entering the league, started 47 of 62 regular-season games in 2025-26 and averaged 13.6 points, 5.8 rebounds, and 2.8 assists per game. He was not a project or a fringe player trying to earn a roster spot. He was an established NBA contributor returning to the Summer League to sharpen his game.

What he did with that platform was worth watching.

A Tournament to Remember

Coward's Las Vegas Summer League unfolded in escalating chapters. He posted a double-double in the middle portion of the tournament — 17 points, 12 rebounds, 4 assists, 1 block, 1 steal — as a demonstration of his two-way range. His average through the field rounds was 14.7 points per game, above what most second-year players produce in the expanded competition environment.

Then came the semifinal against the Houston Rockets. Coward went off for 28 points, 6 rebounds, 5 assists, and 4 three-pointers on 9-for-19 shooting from the field. He led all scorers in a game Memphis needed to win to advance to the championship. He delivered.

In the championship game itself — a 94-90 loss to Golden State after the Warriors rallied from 12 down in the fourth quarter — Coward added 19 more. He did not fade under the spotlight. He competed in the most important moment of the Summer League and scored 19 points in a defeat that came down to the final possessions.

What He Said About Boozer

After the semifinal win, reporters asked Coward about Cameron Boozer, the Grizzlies' highly anticipated young prospect who had also played well throughout Vegas. Coward's answer was brief and direct: "He's special."

That kind of endorsement from a veteran contributor — a player who has already established himself in the regular season — carries weight. Boozer matched Coward's championship game scoring total with 19 points of his own, reinforcing the idea that Memphis may have two ascending pieces in the same front-court conversation.

What's Next

For Coward, the Summer League closes a chapter and opens a question. Memphis is in the middle of a significant roster reconstruction, and the decisions made this offseason will shape what the next few seasons look like for the franchise. Coward's Summer League performance makes a strong case that he should be a fixture in whatever system the Grizzlies build going forward.

He has already shown he can produce at the NBA level during the regular season. He has now shown he can elevate in high-pressure moments in a competitive tournament setting. The combination suggests a player on a real developmental arc — not one who is plateauing, but one who is still climbing.

The Summer League is over. For Coward, the summer is just getting started.
