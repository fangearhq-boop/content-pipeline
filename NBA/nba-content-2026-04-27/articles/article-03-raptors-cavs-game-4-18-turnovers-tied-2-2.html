18 Turnovers Doom Cavaliers as Raptors Win 93-89 to Tie Series at 2-2

By Marcus Cole | April 27, 2026

Cleveland had the series lead. Cleveland had the experience. Cleveland had Donovan Mitchell dropping 12 points in the fourth quarter. None of it was enough. The Toronto Raptors beat the Cavaliers 93-89 in Game 4 to tie the first-round series at two games apiece, turning a Cleveland turnover nightmare into a Toronto opportunity.

The Cavaliers committed 18 turnovers — a self-inflicted wound that gave the Raptors easy possessions throughout the afternoon and allowed Toronto to stay competitive despite Cleveland's late offensive push.

Barnes and Ingram Deliver

Toronto's Scottie Barnes and Brandon Ingram each scored 23 points to pace the Raptors offensively. Barnes was at his best in the clutch, hitting the go-ahead free throws in the final minute to push Toronto in front. It was the kind of composed play that playoff moments demand, and Barnes delivered without hesitation.

Ingram provided a complementary scoring punch throughout the game, giving the Raptors a two-headed offensive attack that Cleveland's defense couldn't contain. When the Raptors needed buckets late, both stars were available.

Mitchell's Fourth Quarter Wasn't Enough

Donovan Mitchell had 20 points for Cleveland, with 12 of them coming in the fourth quarter in a furious attempt to rescue the Cavaliers. Mitchell's late production was a reminder of the star power Cleveland has, but the turnover damage had already been done.

James Harden added 19 points for the Cavaliers, but Cleveland's collective effort in the turnover column undermined everything else. Eighteen giveaways against a team that plays as physically as Toronto leads to exactly this kind of result — a winnable game that slips away.

A Series Flipped on Its Head

After Cleveland took a 2-1 series lead following Game 3, the momentum appeared to be firmly in the Cavaliers' favor. Games 4 and a potential Game 5 in Cleveland looked like opportunities to close it out. Now the series is even, the home-court advantage Cleveland had built is gone, and Toronto has the confidence that comes from winning on the road in a hostile environment.

The Raptors entered this playoff as a No. 5 seed and were considered underdogs against the Cavaliers. Barnes' emergence as a clutch performer — after his 33-point Game 3 effort in the Raptors' Game 3 win — has been the defining storyline of the series.

What's Next

Game 5 returns to Cleveland, where the Cavaliers will play in front of their home crowd with a chance to reclaim the series advantage. For Cleveland, the correctable issue is ball security — eliminating turnovers and forcing the Raptors to beat them in a clean game. For Toronto, the challenge is proving Sunday was no fluke. This series is a genuine coin flip heading into the most important game of the year for both franchises.
