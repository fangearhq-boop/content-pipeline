Cade Cunningham's Comeback: How the Pistons Went From 14-68 to the No. 1 East Seed

By Jake Torres | Hoop Heroes | April 14, 2026

Detroit's 60-22 finish is one of the most extraordinary franchise turnarounds in recent NBA history — and it all runs through a point guard who played through a collapsed lung to get here.

The Long Way Back

Just two seasons ago, the Detroit Pistons finished 14-68 — one of the worst records in modern NBA history. They were a franchise in free fall, a cautionary tale about what happens when a rebuilding timeline goes wrong. The No. 1 seed in the Eastern Conference wasn't a realistic dream. It was barely a thought.

This season, the Pistons finished 60-22. No. 1 East. First 60-win campaign since 2005-06. Only the third 60-win season in franchise history. The transformation is stunning by any measure.

And in the regular-season finale against the Indiana Pacers — the game that clinched that 60th win — Cade Cunningham was back on the floor after missing 11 games with a collapsed lung, handing out 14 assists, pulling down eight rebounds, and scoring seven points in the closing chapter of one of the great individual acts of resilience this season has produced.

Cade Cunningham's Ascent

Cunningham was the No. 1 overall pick in 2021, and the expectations that followed him to Detroit were enormous. For a period, the fit between player and franchise seemed to be going nowhere — the Pistons bottomed out around him while he developed, and the question of whether he could carry a team to relevance remained open.

This season closed that question. Cunningham has grown into one of the most complete guards in the NBA — a player who can score, distribute, rebound at his position, and make the players around him better. His 14-assist performance in the season finale wasn't a fluke or a padding-the-stats cameo. It was a point guard taking over an important game and making sure his team ended the regular season the right way.

The collapsed lung diagnosis mid-season threatened to derail everything. Instead, his return gave the team a final push that confirmed the Pistons' identity as a legitimate No. 1 seed — not just by record but by competitive quality.

Home Court and the Awaited Opponent

Detroit now sits and watches as the Play-In Tournament determines their first-round opponent. The No. 8 seed — whoever emerges from tonight's games and Wednesday's games — visits Little Caesars Arena in the first round, where the Pistons will have home-court advantage for the entire series.

For context: two seasons ago, Detroit was playing in front of half-empty arenas during a lost season, watching lottery balls bounce with hopes of another high pick. Now they're the No. 1 seed, waiting with home court. The arc of this franchise has bent sharply upward in a very short time.

The Celtics Are Watching

The Boston Celtics finished as the No. 2 seed in the East at 60-22, and they'll be watching Detroit closely. Tatum, Brown, and a team that is consistently one of the top two or three contenders in the conference will eventually have to navigate through the Pistons' bracket side if both teams advance. The Pistons denied Boston the top seed in the final days of the regular season — a detail that won't be forgotten when the bracket inevitably produces a potential matchup.

What's Next

The Pistons open the first round on April 18 against the No. 8 Play-In winner. After that, the bracket holds the potential for a meeting with the No. 4 Cavaliers in the second round, and eventually the Celtics if both advance. Detroit hasn't won an NBA championship since 2004. The current team has the talent, the record, and the momentum to make a genuine run. Cade Cunningham's moment — and the franchise's — is right now.
