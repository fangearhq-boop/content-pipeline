Wembanyama's Dominant Season: Unanimous DPOY and the Spurs' Historic 24-3 Surge

By Damon Pierce | Hoop Heroes | April 14, 2026

Victor Wembanyama finished the regular season as the most dominant defensive player the league has seen in years — and the Spurs' 24-3 post-All-Star run suggests his era is just beginning.

Numbers Nobody Has Posted Since Shaq

Victor Wembanyama's 2025-26 season produced a stat line that required a search through decades of NBA history to find a comparison: 25 points, 11.5 rebounds, and 3.1 blocks per game. The last player to average numbers in that territory over a full season was Shaquille O'Neal in 1999-2000.

He did it while shooting 51.2 percent from the field, 34.9 percent from three-point range, and 82.7 percent from the free-throw line. At 22 years old. In his third season. These are not incremental improvement numbers — they're the statistical profile of a player who is already operating at an all-time level and hasn't reached his peak.

When asked about the Defensive Player of the Year award, Wembanyama said he would be "surprised" if the vote weren't unanimous — the first truly unanimous DPOY in NBA history. The oddsmakers have him at -5000, reflecting a 98 percent-plus implied probability. His surprise would be well-founded; there's no credible second-place candidate.

San Antonio's Remarkable Stretch Run

Individual greatness is one story. What the Spurs did as a team since the All-Star break is another, and together they've made San Antonio the team that the rest of the Western Conference doesn't want to face.

San Antonio went 24-3 from the All-Star break through the end of the regular season — the best record of any team in the NBA over that stretch. They finished at 62-20, locked up the No. 2 seed in the West, and won their first Southwest Division title since 2017.

For long-suffering Spurs fans, this season also ends a playoff drought that began in 2019. The drought is over, and this isn't a team sneaking into the postseason — this is a 62-win team with the second-best record in the entire conference, entering the playoffs with genuine championship aspirations.

Managing the Moment

Wembanyama appeared in 65 games this season — exactly the minimum required for postseason award eligibility. The Spurs were careful with him, managing his minutes and rest schedule with an eye toward the playoffs. The decision to let him play in that 65th game carried some risk; a pre-playoff injury would have been catastrophic. But the organization and the player made that call together, and it paid off.

Heading into the first round, San Antonio waits for tonight's Suns-Blazers Play-In game to determine their first-round opponent. Whether it's Phoenix or Portland, the Spurs will have home-court advantage, a healthy Wembanyama, and a team that has been playing its best basketball since February.

A New Era in San Antonio

The Spurs dynasty of the Tim Duncan era was built on defense, fundamentals, and system basketball. Wembanyama brings all of those things plus a once-in-a-generation physical profile. His wingspan, shot-blocking instincts, and ability to anchor a defensive scheme while contributing on offense at a high level is something the franchise has never quite seen before — not even from Duncan at his peak.

That's not hyperbole. It's the careful observation of a league watching a player who may be in the process of becoming the most dominant big man it has ever seen.

What's Next

San Antonio's first-round opponent is determined tonight. Regardless of whether it's the Suns or Blazers, the Spurs open the playoffs April 18 at home with full confidence and full health. The DPOY announcement will come sometime in late April. By then, Wembanyama will be in the middle of a postseason run that the entire basketball world will be watching. His first playoffs have arrived — and if this regular season is any indication, they won't be his last deep into May.
