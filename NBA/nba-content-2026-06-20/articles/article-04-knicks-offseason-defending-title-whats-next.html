After the Parade: What the Knicks' Championship Offseason Actually Looks Like

By Jake Torres | Hoop Heroes | June 20, 2026

Two million people packed Broadway two days ago. Alicia Keys sang Empire State of Mind at City Hall. The Larry O'Brien Trophy got touched by fans from Battery Park to Chambers Street. And now, the New York Knicks have to figure out how to do it again.

The offseason machine started the moment the confetti settled. For a champion, there is no extended celebration — just an immediate pivot to the next task. For the Knicks, that task is as challenging as any they've faced: keeping together the roster that just won the franchise's first title in 53 years.

The calendar is compressed. The NBA Draft is Monday (June 23). Free agency opens June 30. The Knicks hold the No. 17 pick and have a handful of critical decisions to make before summer ends.

The Giannis Decision — Easy One First

Let's start with what the Knicks are not doing: chasing Giannis Antetokounmpo. New York is officially out of the sweepstakes that has consumed the league's attention. It's a choice that reflects a deliberate philosophy — don't blow up a championship team to roll the dice on a different star, even one as talented as Giannis.

The logic is sound. Jalen Brunson is a unanimous Finals MVP and the unquestioned face of the franchise. Karl-Anthony Towns provided exactly what the Knicks needed in the post. OG Anunoby was an All-Defense selection and a critical two-way piece. The core is excellent. Why dismantle it?

The Roster Priorities

The most critical item on the Knicks' offseason checklist is Brunson. He averaged 32.6 points, 4.6 assists, and 4.2 rebounds per game through the playoffs — numbers that justify whatever extension the front office puts in front of him. His salary sacrifice before this season (he left $113 million on the table to give the team cap room) makes him a particularly loyal figure, but loyalty runs both ways. The Knicks need to pay him.

Behind Brunson, OG Anunoby is the most important retention target. He is one of the best perimeter defenders in the league and a reliable secondary scorer. Losing him would fundamentally change the team's identity on the defensive end.

Towns is also a priority, though his situation is more complex. He is coming off one of the best seasons of his career and will command a max offer. The Knicks want him back, but the financial gymnastics required to retain everyone will test front office creativity.

The Draft Pick (No. 17)

New York holds the No. 17 selection in Monday's draft — a decent mid-first pick on a deep draft night. The Knicks could use it to add a young developmental piece, package it in a trade for a proven rotation player, or hold it as future leverage. For a championship team, the pick is more likely a trade asset than a foundation piece.

What's Next

The Knicks have won. The question now is whether they can sustain it. Championship teams face a brutal reality: the league adjusts, players get paid, and the margins that produced a title can narrow quickly.

The next 30 days will tell us whether New York's front office is capable of doing what so few teams have managed in the modern NBA — keeping a championship team intact long enough to make a real run at a repeat.

Broadway was full Thursday. The goal is to fill it again in 12 months.
