LaMelo Ball Traded to Minnesota Timberwolves to Join Anthony Edwards

By Damon Pierce July 3, 2026 Hoop Heroes

The Minnesota Timberwolves just made themselves dangerous. The Charlotte Hornets traded LaMelo Ball — a two-time All-Star, the No. 3 overall pick in the 2020 NBA Draft, and widely considered the best player in franchise history — to Minnesota on Thursday in a blockbuster move that pairs him with Anthony Edwards. The Western Conference just got more interesting.

Ball arrived in Charlotte as a 19-year-old phenom with a handle like no one else in his class, a shooting stroke that could heat up without warning, and a passing vision that made teammates look like they were in on some secret only he knew. He delivered on every ounce of that promise. He earned two All-Star appearances, broke the franchise scoring records, and turned Charlotte into a must-watch product for the first time in years. Now he takes all of that to Minneapolis and teams up with one of the most explosive players in basketball.

What Ball Brings to Minnesota

Anthony Edwards is one of the most physically gifted players in the NBA — a relentless scorer who can play through contact, shoot off the dribble, and dominate games on both ends of the floor. But the Timberwolves have needed a playmaker who can create for others, ease the burden on Edwards as a primary initiator, and stretch the floor in a different way. LaMelo Ball is that player.

Ball's passing ability is genuinely elite. He sees angles that other players do not see, makes decisions at a pace that borders on reckless but somehow works, and elevates the production of the teammates around him. His three-point shooting gives opposing defenses a genuine headache. In Minnesota's offensive system, with Edwards pulling defenders into the paint and Ball operating from the perimeter, the Timberwolves have the personnel to generate high-quality looks all night long.

A Charlotte Farewell

For the Hornets faithful, this trade lands like a gut punch. LaMelo Ball was not just a player — he was a promise. He was the best player the franchise had ever seen, and the hope was always that he would be the cornerstone of something lasting in Charlotte. Watching him leave for a bigger market and a better team stings, even if the basketball logic of the trade makes sense from a franchise-building perspective.

Charlotte now enters a transition period. Whatever the Hornets received in return for Ball, they will need to deploy those assets wisely. A franchise that trades its best player ever needs to have a vision for what comes next. The front office has work to do, and the fanbase deserves transparency about the plan. The LaMelo era was special. What comes next needs to be worthy of following it.

The Western Conference Just Got Tighter

The NBA's Western Conference was already one of the deepest in recent history, with Oklahoma City, Denver, Golden State, and others all capable of winning a playoff series. Now add a Timberwolves team with Anthony Edwards and LaMelo Ball to that mix, and the competition for seeding and home court advantage becomes an even more compelling storyline heading into the 2026-27 season.

Minnesota's front office has been building toward a championship window, and this trade signals that they believe that window is open right now. Edwards has shown he can be a No. 1 option on a contending team. Ball has shown he can be a difference-maker when surrounded by the right pieces. Together, with Minnesota's existing defensive infrastructure, the Timberwolves have the look of a genuine threat.

What's Next

LaMelo Ball will report to Minnesota as one of the team's two highest-profile players. The Timberwolves' preseason and early regular season will generate enormous national attention as fans and analysts evaluate whether the Edwards-Ball partnership lives up to its billing. If the chemistry is there — and there is every reason to believe it will be — the Minnesota Timberwolves could be one of the most entertaining and effective teams in the NBA for years to come. Charlotte, meanwhile, begins the process of figuring out who they are without their best player. Both journeys are worth watching closely.
