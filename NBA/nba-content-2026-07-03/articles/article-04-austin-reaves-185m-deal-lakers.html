Austin Reaves Signs $185M Lakers Deal — The Most Historic Undrafted Contract in NBA History

By Jake Torres July 3, 2026 Hoop Heroes

No team called Austin Reaves' name on NBA Draft night in 2021. Five years later, the Los Angeles Lakers handed him $185 million and the keys to their franchise. Reaves re-signed with Los Angeles on a 4-year, $185 million deal on Thursday — the largest contract ever awarded to an undrafted player in the history of the NBA. It is the kind of number that would have seemed absurd on that quiet draft night when Reaves slipped through the cracks, unnoticed and unpicked, and found his own way to the NBA's biggest stage.

The deal signals more than a contract extension. It signals a new identity for the Los Angeles Lakers. With LeBron James departing after informing the organization he would not return for the 2026-27 season, the Lakers needed to make a statement about the future. Signing Reaves to the largest undrafted contract in league history is exactly that kind of statement. This is who the Lakers are building around now. This is who wears the purple and gold going forward.

The Road from Arkansas to $185 Million

Reaves played his college basketball at the University of Arkansas, where he carved out a reputation as a tough, intelligent, two-way player who made winning plays. He was not projected as a lottery pick. He was not even projected as a late-round pick. When the 2021 draft came and went without his name being called, Reaves signed as an undrafted free agent and went to work proving everyone wrong.

Season by season, Reaves built his game and his reputation in Los Angeles. He became the team's most reliable wing-level contributor, the player coaches trusted in clutch moments, and the fan favorite who represented everything the franchise wanted to stand for: hard work, versatility, and consistency. There is no shortcut to $185 million. Reaves earned every dollar of it the hard way, on the floor, over five years, in one of the most scrutinized markets in professional sports.

Building the Post-LeBron Lakers

The Lakers do not yet know who LeBron James will choose to play for in 2026-27. What they do know is that their future is being built without him. Reaves will form the offensive foundation of that future, playing alongside center Walker Kessler, who brings rim protection, rebounding, and shot-blocking to complement Reaves' scoring and playmaking ability.

The Reaves-Kessler pairing is not a flashy combination — it is a functional, complementary one. Kessler protects the paint and controls the glass. Reaves handles creation, shooting, and ball movement on the perimeter. Building outward from that core, the Lakers will look to add pieces in the draft and free agency that can make them relevant again in the Western Conference. The rebuild starts now, and it starts with a $185 million bet on Austin Reaves.

What This Means for Undrafted Players Everywhere

Beyond what this deal means for the Lakers, it carries a broader message for every player who was passed over on draft night. The NBA has always had examples of undrafted players who carved out long, productive careers — but none of them had ever been handed this kind of contract. The $185 million figure is a new landmark, a number that tells every undrafted free agent who signs a two-way contract or sits on the end of a bench that the ceiling is higher than it has ever been.

Austin Reaves did not need the draft to validate him. He did not need a lottery pick designation or a high slot to prove his worth. He needed the floor, time, and the conviction that hard work compounds over five years into something nobody saw coming on draft night in 2021. The Lakers saw it. And now they are paying for it at a scale the league has never seen from an undrafted player.

What's Next

The contract is signed and the direction is set. Austin Reaves will report to Lakers training camp this fall as the undisputed face of the franchise, carrying a $185 million deal and the expectations of one of basketball's most passionate fan bases. The burden is real, but so is the talent. The basketball world will be watching to see if the kid from Arkansas who nobody drafted can lead the Los Angeles Lakers back to relevance. If his career so far is any indication, betting against him is a mistake.
