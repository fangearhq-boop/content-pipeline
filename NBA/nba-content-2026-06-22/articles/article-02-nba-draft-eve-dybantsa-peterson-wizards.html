NBA Draft Eve 2026: Wizards Still "Torn" Between Dybantsa and Peterson at No. 1

By Damon Pierce | Hoop Heroes | June 22, 2026

Twenty-four hours. That's all the Washington Wizards have left to make one of the most consequential decisions in franchise history — and according to ESPN insiders, they still haven't made up their minds.

The 2026 NBA Draft tips off tomorrow night at 8 PM ET at Barclays Center in Brooklyn, broadcasting live on ABC and ESPN. At the top of the board sits a "tough call" — to borrow the exact language used by ESPN insiders — between two extraordinary prospects: AJ Dybantsa and Darryn Peterson.

Dybantsa: Confidence as a Strategy

If AJ Dybantsa is nervous about draft night, he has a very effective way of hiding it.

The 6-foot-9 BYU forward didn't work out for a single NBA team in the lead-up to the draft. His explanation was characteristically blunt: "I don't gotta work out. They know what I can do."

And the betting markets agree. Dybantsa carries -450 odds to be the No. 1 overall pick — making him a heavy favorite despite the Wizards' stated indecision. His college resume backs up the swagger: 25.5 points per game, 51.0% from the field, and an elite physical profile that scouts have compared to some of the game's all-time playmakers.

The argument for Dybantsa is straightforward — his combination of size, athleticism, and scoring instinct represents a once-in-a-generation prospect. The Wizards passed on players like him before. They cannot afford to pass again.

Peterson: The Chess Move

Darryn Peterson is playing a different kind of game — one that has nothing to do with on-court performance and everything to do with leverage.

Peterson visited only one team before the draft: Washington. He refused meetings with every other franchise. The message couldn't be clearer — Peterson believes he's the No. 1 pick, and he's willing to back that belief with action. If the Wizards pass on him, the expectation across the league is that the Utah Jazz will eagerly scoop him up at No. 2.

Peterson's skill set offers something different from Dybantsa — elite 3-point shooting and perimeter shot creation that could translate immediately in today's NBA. Multiple scouts have called the margin between the two prospects "razor-thin," and Peterson's technical shooting mechanics give some evaluators confidence that his floor is higher than Dybantsa's.

The Wizards' Historic Moment

Washington hasn't held a No. 1 overall pick since selecting John Wall in 2010. That selection began a promising era that was ultimately derailed by injuries and roster instability. The Wizards are desperate not to repeat those mistakes.

This year's team context is more promising than it has been in years. Washington added Trae Young from Atlanta in January and Anthony Davis from Dallas at the trade deadline. With Alex Sarr, Bilal Coulibaly, and Kyshawn George already in place, the Wizards have a legitimate young core — and the No. 1 pick could be the centerpiece of something special.

Dybantsa himself said: "If I go to DC I'll make playoffs." That's not just confidence — it's a read on the roster Washington has quietly assembled.

What's Next

By this time tomorrow, the "tough call" will be history. The Wizards will have made their pick, Peterson or Dybantsa will have his franchise, and the 2026 draft class will officially be underway.

Whatever Washington decides, both players are expected to make an immediate impact. The real question for Wizards fans is which path to contention they're choosing: Dybantsa's upside or Peterson's floor. In 24 hours, they'll have their answer.
