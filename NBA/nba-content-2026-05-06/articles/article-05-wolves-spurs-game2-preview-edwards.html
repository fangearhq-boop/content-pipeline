Wolves-Spurs Game 2 Preview: Wembanyama's Record-Breaking Performance Wasn't Enough — Now What?

By Marcus Cole | Hoop Heroes | May 6, 2026

Victor Wembanyama set the all-time NBA playoff blocks record with 12 in Game 1. The Spurs still lost. Tonight's game at AT&T Center is about whether San Antonio can find offensive answers — fast.

Victor Wembanyama blocked 12 shots in the first game of his playoff career against the Minnesota Timberwolves. He shattered the all-time NBA postseason record for blocks in a single game. He was magnificent, historic, and dominant on the defensive end.

San Antonio lost 104-102.

That sentence captures everything the Spurs need to fix before tonight's Game 2, which tips off at 9:30 PM ET at AT&T Center in San Antonio on ESPN. Minnesota leads the series 1-0 after surviving a game-winning 3-point attempt from Julian Champagnie in the final seconds. The Timberwolves escaped. The Spurs are the ones left answering questions.

Edwards' Return Shifts the Momentum

The biggest development from Game 1 was not Wembanyama's record. It was Anthony Edwards proving his hyperextended knee is no longer an issue.

Edwards returned from injury 10 days ahead of schedule and came off the bench in Game 1, scoring 18 points — including 11 in the fourth quarter. His clutch playmaking was the difference. When Minnesota needed a bucket in the closing minutes of a two-point game, Edwards delivered. He was 8-of-12 from the field in limited minutes, and his aggression on drives and in the pick-and-roll created constant problems for San Antonio's interior defense.

The Spurs had a defensive answer for most things in Game 1. They did not have a consistent answer for Edwards.

San Antonio's Offensive Problem

Wembanyama's 12 blocks and 15 rebounds were extraordinary. But the Spurs need him to do more on offense in Game 2. In Game 1, his scoring was limited while he focused heavily on rim protection. San Antonio came agonizingly close to stealing a game despite the offensive struggles — but they cannot build a series strategy around nearly winning.

The Spurs need to attack Minnesota's defense more aggressively in the half court. They need to find secondary scorers who can take pressure off Wembanyama. And they need to limit second-chance opportunities, because the Timberwolves punished them in transition and on offensive rebounds in Game 1's closing stretch.

Minnesota's Advantage Is Real

The Timberwolves are the more battle-tested team. They won the Western Conference title last season and know how to manage close games. Anthony Edwards — when fully healthy — is one of the five best players in the NBA. Julius Randle added 21 points and 10 rebounds in Game 1, providing the second punch Minnesota needed.

The Wolves won Game 1 on the road at full intensity. A 1-0 series lead with home court intact — Games 3 and 4 are in Minneapolis — gives Minnesota a significant structural advantage.

What's Next

Game 2 tips off tonight at 9:30 PM ET at AT&T Center in San Antonio, televised on ESPN. If San Antonio wins and ties the series, it breathes massive life into a Spurs team that is still figuring out what its ceiling looks like in Wembanyama's first deep playoff run. If Minnesota wins and goes up 2-0, the series is almost certainly over — no team has ever come back from a 0-2 deficit in a best-of-seven when the other team holds home court advantage and an Edwards who is fully operational. Game 3 in Minnesota is scheduled for Friday, May 8.
