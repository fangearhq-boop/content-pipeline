Wizards Shut Down AJ Dybantsa After Two Dominant Outings — No. 1 Pick Made His Statement

By Marcus Cole | July 15, 2026

Washington protected their most important asset after Dybantsa's 27-point debut and 23-point follow-up left little doubt about what the 2026 No. 1 pick can do at this level.

The Washington Wizards have seen enough — and what they've seen is very, very good.

The organization shut down AJ Dybantsa for the remainder of NBA Summer League after just two games. The reasoning is simple: the No. 1 overall pick in the 2026 NBA Draft scored 27 points in his debut and 23 more in his second appearance. There was nothing left to prove in Las Vegas, and everything to protect heading into his first professional season.

No injury was reported. This is precautionary handling of a player whose long-term value to the franchise is immeasurable — the kind of conservative, asset-protecting decision that keeps front offices employed.

A Debut Worth Remembering

Dybantsa's 27-point Summer League debut reportedly tied Blake Griffin's mark for the most points scored by a No. 1 overall pick in a Las Vegas Summer League opener. That is rarefied company, and Dybantsa's performance suggested he belongs in it — not just because of the scoring total but because of how he got there.

The combination of size, skill, and basketball IQ that made Dybantsa the consensus top pick was on display immediately. Then he came back for Game 2 and scored 23 more. By any measure, the Wizards received the opening statement they were hoping for from their franchise cornerstone.

Smart Management of a Franchise Asset

The decision to shut Dybantsa down after two games reflects how Washington views him: not as a Summer League player, but as the centerpiece of a rebuild that will define the franchise for the next decade. The risk of a freak injury in a meaningless game far outweighs any additional development value from playing him out.

Some fans will wish they could watch him for a few more games. That feeling is understandable. But the Wizards are playing a longer game, and every decision they make about Dybantsa this summer will be evaluated through that lens.

Protecting him is the right call. The Summer League is a showcase, not a measuring stick. Dybantsa used his two games to check every box he needed to check.

What's Next

Dybantsa's next steps involve training camp preparations, working with Washington's coaching staff, and beginning the integration process that every rookie goes through when the real work starts. The Summer League chapter is closed. The regular season chapter — the one that matters — opens in October. Based on what Washington saw in two games in Las Vegas, they have every reason to feel optimistic about what comes next.
