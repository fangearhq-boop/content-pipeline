27 Years Later: The Knicks Are One Win From the NBA Finals

By Marcus Cole | Hoop Heroes | May 24, 2026

The last time the New York Knicks appeared in the NBA Finals, Bill Clinton was in the White House and "The Sopranos" had just premiered its first season. It was 1999, and the Spurs beat New York in five games of a lockout-shortened series that many casual fans barely remember.

Twenty-seven years later, with a 3-0 lead in the Eastern Conference Finals, the Knicks are one victory away from going back.

This team has won 10 straight playoff games. Their offensive rating during that run — 130.5 points per 100 possessions — is the best among all 2026 playoff teams. They have swept the Philadelphia 76ers and dominated the Cleveland Cavaliers in a series where, after three games, every opponent has been left searching for adjustments that don't exist.

How They Got Here

This Knicks team is not built around one transcendent player. Jalen Brunson is the engine — he has averaged 26 points through three ECF games and makes every critical decision correctly — but what separates New York from most contenders is that Brunson does not have to be a one-man show.

Mikal Bridges has played at the level his acquisition demanded when the Knicks traded for him. OG Anunoby, despite arriving via trade amid injury concerns, has been elite on both ends of the floor. Karl-Anthony Towns provides a playmaking center who doesn't just score inside but orchestrates the offense at a level that traditional big men rarely achieve. And Josh Hart — the second-round pick who everyone counted out early in his career — is the soul of this team, the player willing to do whatever the moment demands without needing his name in the highlight reel afterward.

They shot 60% from the field and nearly 40% from three-point range during their 10-game win streak. Those numbers don't happen by accident. They happen because a team has found the version of itself that maximizes every player's strengths at the right moment in the right games.

What It Would Mean

For a franchise that has cycled through coaches, rebuilds, star free agents who left, and seasons that seemed to end before they started, reaching the NBA Finals would represent a moment of genuine redemption. The Garden — Madison Square Garden, the most famous arena in basketball — has been waiting for this.

There will be time to celebrate if and when it happens. Tomorrow night in Cleveland, the Knicks have a job to finish. They have shown in these playoffs that they don't need extra motivation, don't need rivalry storylines, don't need anything beyond preparation and execution. But for the fans who have watched this team for the past 27 years, Game 4 is not just a basketball game. It is the threshold of something they were beginning to wonder if they would ever see again.
