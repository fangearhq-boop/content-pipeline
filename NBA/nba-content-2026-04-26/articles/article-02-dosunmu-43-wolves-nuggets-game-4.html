Dosunmu Scores 43 Off the Bench — Best Reserve Playoff Performance in 50 Years — as Wolves Beat Nuggets 112-96

By Marcus Cole | Hoop Heroes | April 26, 2026

Ayo Dosunmu was not supposed to be the player who saved Minnesota's season. He was a reserve, a rotation piece, a name that rarely appeared in the biggest Game 4 conversations. But with Anthony Edwards sidelined by a knee injury and Donte DiVincenzo also unavailable, the Minnesota Timberwolves desperately needed someone to step forward against the defending champion Denver Nuggets. Dosunmu answered in historic fashion.

He scored 43 points off the bench — shooting 13-of-17 from the field, converting all five of his 3-point attempts, and making all 12 of his free throws in 42 minutes — as Minnesota won 112-96 to take a commanding 3-1 series lead. It was, according to NBA records, the highest-scoring playoff game by a reserve in 50 years, since Fred Brown erupted for 45 points for the Seattle SuperSonics against Phoenix on April 15, 1976.

Naz Reid contributed 17 points and nine rebounds as Minnesota's depth once again proved to be its most dangerous weapon. The Nuggets, without a reliable answer for the Timberwolves' collective assault, fell to the brink of elimination.

A Performance Nobody Saw Coming

Dosunmu's 43 points were not the product of luck or a favorable matchup. He was aggressive from his first minutes, attacking the Denver defense with a purposefulness that looked nothing like a backup player filling time. He shot it when it was there, drove when the lane opened, and made shot after shot from 3-point range with no hesitation.

The efficiency — 13-of-17 from the floor — is the mark of a player completely locked in. He went to the line 12 times, a testament to how often he put pressure on the defense to foul him rather than give up layups. When the 43 points were done, he had become part of a historical conversation that includes names like Brown, Michael Cooper, and other legendary reserve performers in NBA postseason history.

Minnesota needed every one of those points. Without Edwards, who had become one of the most electric performers in these playoffs, the Timberwolves leaned on structure and depth. Dosunmu provided the explosion that held Denver at bay whenever the Nuggets threatened to make a run.

Nuggets' Dynasty on the Brink

Denver now faces an almost impossible task. The Nuggets must win three consecutive games to survive, starting with Game 5. Nikola Jokic has not been at his most dominant in this series, and the supporting cast has struggled to generate the kind of consistent offense that characterized Denver's championship run.

The larger question for the Nuggets is whether this is a first-round upset or the beginning of a transition — from a team defined by Jokic's greatness to one that must figure out what comes next as Minnesota's younger, deeper roster begins to dominate the conference.

What's Next

Game 5 returns to Denver, where the Nuggets are desperate and dangerous and have Jokic as their lifeline. For Minnesota, the challenge is managing the health of Edwards and DiVincenzo while keeping Dosunmu ready to contribute at whatever level is needed. One more win ends the series. The Timberwolves are three quarters of the way there.
