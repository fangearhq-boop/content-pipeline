SGA's Back-to-Back MVP and Wemby's Historic DPOY Chase: The 2026 NBA Awards Conversation

By Marcus Cole | Hoop Heroes | April 15, 2026

The regular season is over and the ballots are being finalized. Two dominant performances this season stand above the rest — and one of them could rewrite NBA history.

SGA: The Case for Back-to-Back

Shai Gilgeous-Alexander finished the 2025-26 season averaging 31.6 points, 6.5 assists, 4.4 rebounds, and 1.4 steals per game while shooting 55 percent from the field. His Oklahoma City Thunder went 64-16 — the best record in the NBA by a substantial margin. He received 88 of 100 first-place votes in ESPN's straw poll of media voters.

The argument for a second consecutive MVP is simple: nobody was better. No player carried a team to more wins at a higher level of individual efficiency. The debate about whether SGA or Victor Wembanyama deserved this year's award — and it was a genuine debate, argued thoughtfully on both sides — ultimately appears to be resolving in SGA's favor based on where the voter consensus has landed.

At 27 years old, SGA is entering what should be the absolute prime of his career. If he takes the award this spring, he'll join a short list of players who have won back-to-back MVP honors — a group that includes Wilt Chamberlain, Kareem Abdul-Jabbar, Magic Johnson, Michael Jordan, LeBron James, Stephen Curry, and Nikola Jokic. That is the company he's keeping now.

Wemby's Historic DPOY Chase

Victor Wembanyama's defensive season was, by any objective measure, historic. He averaged 25 points, 11.5 rebounds, and 3.1 blocks per game — becoming the first player since Shaquille O'Neal in 1999-2000 to average at least 25, 11, and 3 blocks in a season. His impact on the defensive end is even more difficult to quantify, and the Spurs' 62-20 record exists in large part because opponents cannot score efficiently against a team anchored by Wembanyama.

The Defensive Player of the Year award appears to be Wembanyama's. But the more interesting question is whether it becomes a unanimous vote. No player in NBA history has ever received every first-place vote for DPOY. Wembanyama's odds suggest that this is one of the most dominant award races in league history, but unanimous recognition would require every single voter to agree — and voters have a way of finding reasons to differ on the margins.

Wembanyama himself said he would be "surprised" if the vote wasn't unanimous. Whether that confidence is warranted will be revealed when the award is officially announced.

The Rest of the Awards Landscape

Beyond the two headliners, the awards race includes a Rookie of the Year contest defined largely by Kon Knueppel's record-setting three-point shooting season — his 265 made three-pointers shattered the previous rookie mark of 206. Coach of the Year consideration has centered heavily on Mark Daigneault, who led the Thunder to a 64-16 finish. Sixth Man and Most Improved Player awards are expected in the coming weeks as well.

What's Next

The NBA has not yet announced official award winners — those announcements are expected in the weeks ahead. For now, both SGA and Wembanyama are shifting their focus from individual accolades to postseason performance: SGA with the No. 1 Thunder and Wemby with the No. 2 Spurs. Their teams are two of the three teams that won 60-plus games this season — and by the time the awards are announced, they'll already be deep into first-round series that could define their legacies as much as any regular-season numbers.
