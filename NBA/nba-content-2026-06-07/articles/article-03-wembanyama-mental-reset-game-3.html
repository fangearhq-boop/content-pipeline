Wembanyama's Mental Reset: "We Can't Change the Past" After Game 2 Heartbreak

By Marcus Cole | June 7, 2026 | Hoop Heroes

Victor Wembanyama put up 29 points and shot 11-of-21 from the floor in Game 2 of the NBA Finals. By almost any reasonable measure, he was one of the two or three best players on the court for 47 minutes and 47 seconds.

The other 13 seconds will define the narrative heading into Game 3 — unless Wembanyama doesn't let them.

What Happened in Those Final Seconds

With the Spurs down two and the game slipping away, Wembanyama grabbed a defensive rebound and looked to initiate the offense. He fired a pass toward Stephon Castle — but Castle wasn't facing him. The ball hit Castle in the back and caromed directly to Jalen Brunson.

Brunson, never more dangerous than when he senses blood, drove hard to the baseline, drew a foul as he went out of bounds, and stepped to the free-throw line with 105-103 on the scoreboard and the Knicks' lives on the line. He made one of two free throws. It was 105-104 with 2 seconds left.

The Spurs inbounded quickly. Wembanyama caught, turned, and launched a 20-foot jumper as time expired. It bounced away. New York 105, San Antonio 104.

Composure Beyond His Years

What Wembanyama said afterward reflected a maturity that is rare at any age, let alone at 22 in his first Finals appearance.

"We can't change the past," he said in the locker room after the loss. "We're already thinking about Game 3."

That is exactly the right answer — the only productive answer — but it is easier to say than to embody when you have just turned the ball over in the final seconds of an NBA Finals game. The fact that Wembanyama could articulate it clearly, and that the reporting suggests he genuinely meant it, speaks to something real in his character.

He was not hiding from the moment. He was not making excuses. He took responsibility in the most forward-looking way possible.

The Full Scope of His Performance

It would be a disservice to Wembanyama to reduce Game 2 to only those final 13 seconds. His 29 points came on efficient shooting. He generated multiple contested looks, hit mid-range jumpers, and attacked the rim against a team that was specifically game-planning around limiting him.

Over the two-game series, Wembanyama has averaged 27.5 points on strong shooting. He had 26 points and 12 rebounds with 3 blocks in Game 1. The Knicks have won both games not because Wembanyama failed, but because Jalen Brunson and Karl-Anthony Towns were remarkable and because New York's depth has been relentless.

The Spurs are a two-point loss away from elimination. But Wembanyama's individual performance has not been the reason. San Antonio's collective defensive breakdowns and missed free throws have cost them more than anything Wembanyama has done wrong.

What's Next

Game 3 is Monday, June 8 at 8:30 PM ET at Madison Square Garden on ABC. Wembanyama will walk into the building that beat him in 1999 — metaphorically speaking, he was not alive — and face a crowd that has been waiting 27 years for this moment.

What he does in that environment will define a significant chapter of his story. He is already exceptional. If he can respond in New York the way his postgame comments suggest he intends to, the Spurs might not be done yet. The series is 2-0, not 3-0. There is still time — but only barely.
