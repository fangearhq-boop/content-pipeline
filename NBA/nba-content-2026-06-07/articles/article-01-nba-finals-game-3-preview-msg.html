MSG Hosts Its First NBA Finals Game in 27 Years — And the Spurs Are Running Out of Time

By Damon Pierce | June 7, 2026 | Hoop Heroes

Madison Square Garden has not hosted an NBA Finals game since June 25, 1999 — a night that ended with San Antonio winning the championship and Knicks fans wondering when they'd get another chance. Twenty-seven years later, Monday night delivers that chance, and the city of New York is ready.

Game 3 of the 2026 NBA Finals tips off Monday, June 8 at 8:30 PM ET on ABC. The New York Knicks arrive at MSG carrying a 2-0 series lead over the San Antonio Spurs — the same franchise that broke their hearts in 1999. The stakes have never been higher. For the Spurs, they are now staring down the most daunting deficit in NBA Finals history.

The Weight of What Monday Means

No team in NBA Finals history has ever come back from a 3-0 series deficit. Not one. The league has existed for eight decades, and the 3-0 combination has always meant the same thing: it is over.

San Antonio needs to win Monday night at Madison Square Garden — one of the most hostile environments in professional sports — to avoid that fate. The Knicks will have a crowd that has been waiting 27 years for this moment. The Garden will be loud in a way that only championship moments can produce.

The Knicks opened this series as +164 underdogs to win the championship. After two convincing victories in San Antonio, they are now -490 series favorites. ESPN Analytics gives New York a 58.2% win probability in Game 3, even on the road in their own building, and the Spurs' odds of winning the series sit at a fragile +380.

How the Knicks Got Here

New York has won 13 consecutive playoff games heading into Game 3, compiling a point differential over +270 across this postseason run. They swept their Eastern Conference Finals opponent and then won the first two games of the Finals on the road — only the third team in NBA history to accomplish that feat.

Game 1 saw Jalen Brunson pour in 30 points while Karl-Anthony Towns dominated the interior with 18 points and 12 rebounds. Game 2 was messier — Brunson shot 7-for-25 from the field — but KAT stepped up with 21 points and 13 rebounds, and Brunson was clutch when it mattered, drawing a crucial foul on Victor Wembanyama's errant-pass turnover in the final 13 seconds.

Wembanyama responded with 29 points on 11-of-21 shooting, but his team's self-inflicted errors — defensive breakdowns, missed free throws, and that critical final turnover — cost the Spurs dearly. His 20-foot buzzer attempt fell long. New York 105, San Antonio 104.

What the Spurs Need

The Spurs must be sharper. They have been undone by moments of sloppiness in both games, and they cannot afford to gift the Knicks easy points at MSG. Wembanyama is capable of delivering a performance that changes a series — he was the Western Conference Finals MVP, averaging 27.3 points and 10.9 rebounds — but he needs support.

After the Game 2 loss, Wembanyama was measured. "We can't change the past," he said. "We're already thinking about Game 3." The 22-year-old's composure is remarkable for a player in his first Finals, and if the Spurs are going to pull off the impossible, his leadership will be essential.

San Antonio needs to take advantage of any Brunson shooting struggles, find open looks for their role players, and defend the Knicks' depth with more discipline than they managed in Games 1 and 2.

What's Next

Game 3 tips off Monday, June 8 at 8:30 PM ET at Madison Square Garden on ABC. A Knicks win puts them on the brink of their first championship since 1973. A Spurs win keeps a remarkable story alive.

For New York fans who were alive in 1999 and remember how that series ended, Monday is a chance at redemption against the same opponent. For the generation of Knicks fans who grew up in the drought years, it is simply the biggest game of their lives. Either way, the Garden will be ready. It always is.
