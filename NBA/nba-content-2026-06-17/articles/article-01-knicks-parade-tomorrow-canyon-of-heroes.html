Knicks Parade Tomorrow: The Canyon of Heroes Finally Gets Its First Knicks Celebration

By Jake Torres | Hoop Heroes | June 17, 2026

Fifty-three years of waiting ends tomorrow morning at 10:00 AM at Battery Park.

The New York Knicks championship parade rolls through the Canyon of Heroes on Thursday, June 18, traveling north along Broadway from Battery Park to City Hall Plaza — and for the first time in franchise history, New York's basketball team will be honored in the most storied stretch of pavement in American sports celebration.

That qualifier is important. The Knicks won NBA championships in 1970 and 1973, but neither title was celebrated with an official Canyon of Heroes ticker-tape parade. Thursday is not just the first Knicks championship parade since 1973. It is the first Canyon of Heroes parade the franchise has ever had.

Everything You Need to Know

The parade begins at 10:00 AM ET Thursday at Battery Park in Lower Manhattan. From there, the procession heads north along Broadway through the Canyon of Heroes — the legendary stretch flanked by skyscrapers that has hosted more than 200 ticker-tape celebrations for athletes, astronauts, and world leaders dating back to the 1880s.

The route ends at City Hall Plaza, where Mayor Mamdani will host a Key to the City ceremony immediately following the parade. City Hall and municipal buildings across New York City will be illuminated in Knicks blue and orange Thursday night.

Black granite plaques embedded in the sidewalk along Broadway commemorate each historic parade. By Thursday afternoon, preparations for a new plaque honoring the 2026 Knicks will already be in the works.

Why This Is Different

New York has hosted massive parades before — the Yankees alone have been celebrated on Broadway 27 times. The Giants, Rangers, and Islanders have all made the walk. But for Knicks fans who grew up hearing about the 1970 Willis Reed moment or the 1973 championship, there has always been a quiet sting: those title teams never got this.

Jalen Brunson's 2026 championship squad gets what those teams did not. And after a 53-year drought, the weight of that fact is not lost on anyone who has spent decades watching the Knicks rebuild, reset, and try again.

Brunson was the unanimous Finals MVP after averaging 32.6 points, 4.6 assists, and 4.2 rebounds through the series. His 45-point Game 5 performance at Frost Bank Center clinched the title, and his willingness to leave $113 million on the table to help the franchise build a championship roster is the defining narrative of this team's ascent.

The Scene Thursday

Crowd projections are staggering. NYPD already reported 63 arrests the night the Knicks clinched the championship — a number that suggests the scale of the city's reaction. For the parade itself, the entire Broadway corridor from Battery Park to City Hall will be effectively impassable.

If you are heading into Manhattan on Thursday, city officials recommend arriving hours early and being prepared for disruptions to all subway lines serving the Financial District and Lower Manhattan.

For those watching from home, local news stations and national outlets will provide full coverage beginning at 9:00 AM ET.

What's Next

After the confetti settles Thursday, the Knicks' front office pivots immediately to offseason business. Karl-Anthony Towns' extension timeline, OG Anunoby's re-signing priority, and whether the organization engages on the Giannis Antetokounmpo trade market — which has a June 23 deadline — are all on the table.

But Thursday is not for business. Thursday is for Broadway. The Knicks are coming home, and after 53 years, the Canyon of Heroes will finally know what it feels like to celebrate New York basketball.
