Ant Edwards Week-to-Week: Inside the Injury and How the Wolves Survived Without Him

By Damon Pierce | May 1, 2026 | Hoop Heroes

The Timberwolves are celebrating. But one of the biggest questions surrounding their second-round run hangs in the air: when will Anthony Edwards be back?

Edwards suffered a left knee hyperextension and bone bruise in Game 4 against the Denver Nuggets and did not play in Games 5 or 6. Minnesota went 2-0 in those games, eliminating Denver to advance to the Western Conference Semifinals. The official designation remains week-to-week, leaving his availability for the Spurs series an open question.

What the Injury Means

A bone bruise with hyperextension is one of the more unpredictable injuries in the NBA. The hyperextension component describes the joint bending beyond its normal range of motion — in Edwards' case, his left knee. The bone bruise is the result of impact to the surrounding bone tissue.

The encouraging element of this type of injury is the absence of structural ligament damage. A bone bruise and hyperextension, while painful and limiting, do not carry the same severity as a torn ACL or MCL. Rest, ice, and reduced activity typically allow the swelling and inflammation to subside within one to three weeks. The lingering soreness from a bone bruise, however, can persist and requires careful management.

Given the Timberwolves' second-round series against San Antonio is set to begin shortly, the window for Edwards is tight but potentially realistic for later games if the series runs long.

How the Wolves Responded

The depth required to survive without Edwards — and three other rotation players — was extraordinary. Jaden McDaniels answered with 32 points, 4 rebounds, and 5 assists in Game 6. Terrence Shannon Jr. added 24 points and 6 rebounds. Between the two, Minnesota had 56 combined points from players who were not expected to be the stars of this series.

McDaniels, the 6-foot-9 forward, used his length and quickness to find scoring opportunities in isolation and off the drive. Shannon proved his regular-season contributions were no fluke, converting in a high-pressure playoff environment on the road to eliminating a defending champion.

Looking Ahead to the Spurs

The Western Conference Semifinals will be a massive challenge regardless of Edwards' status. Victor Wembanyama, De'Aaron Fox, and the San Antonio Spurs just swept the Trail Blazers in the first round and are rested, healthy, and dangerous. Wembanyama's combination of size and mobility presents problems for every team in the league.

Minnesota with Edwards is arguably the best team in the West not named Oklahoma City. Without Edwards, they've proven they can compete — but winning a second-round series against a rested Spurs team will require a far different level of sustained effort.

The Wolves' medical staff and coaching staff will be managing Edwards' return carefully. Risk of re-injury is real with a bone bruise, and pushing him back too soon could cost Minnesota far more than a game or two in Round 2.

What's Next

The Timberwolves' second-round series against the Spurs is expected to begin in the coming days. Edwards will continue to be evaluated on a day-to-day basis within the week-to-week designation. The earliest realistic return would be Game 2 or Game 3 of the series, though the team will prioritize his long-term health over rushing him back. Watch for official practice reports as the series approaches.
