Donovan Mitchell Signs $273M Max Extension With Cavaliers, Leaves $80M on the Table

By Marcus Cole | July 8, 2026

NBA Free Agency

Donovan Mitchell made one of the most striking loyalty calls of the NBA offseason on Tuesday, agreeing to a four-year, $273 million max extension with the Cleveland Cavaliers — while knowingly walking away from roughly $80 million he could have earned by waiting.

The deal, confirmed by multiple outlets including Sports Illustrated and Yahoo Sports, does not begin until the 2027-28 season. Mitchell will earn $50.1 million this year under his existing contract. The extension includes a player option for 2030-31 and a full trade kicker — giving Mitchell both security and a measure of flexibility. But the headline number is the commitment: the 29-year-old guard chose Cleveland, chose now, and chose certainty over chasing the biggest possible number.

"Spida" is staying in Cleveland. And the Cavaliers' front office has to be exhaling right now.

The Math Behind the Decision

Had Mitchell opted to play out his current deal and become an unrestricted free agent next summer, he would have been eligible for a five-year, approximately $353 million maximum contract. That is roughly $80 million more than the deal he signed Tuesday. By committing now, Mitchell traded financial upside for the certainty of knowing his next decade is set — on his terms, in his city.

The math cuts a different way, too: Mitchell's new deal works out to approximately $24,000 per minute played, a figure that circulated widely on social media after the announcement. For a player who averaged 27.9 points, 5.7 assists, and 4.5 rebounds in 70 games during the 2025-26 season — his seventh consecutive All-Star campaign — the extension validates what he has delivered and what he is expected to keep delivering.

What It Means for the Cavaliers

Cleveland enters the 2026-27 season with Mitchell locked in as the face of the franchise through at least 2031. That clarity is meaningful — and the timing is significant. The Cavaliers were pursuing LeBron James in free agency, dangling the opportunity to return home to his native Northeast Ohio. Having Mitchell's long-term commitment in place makes the Cleveland pitch more credible: they are not a one-year stopover, they are building something.

The Cavaliers have been one of the Eastern Conference's most consistent teams over the past two seasons, even with LeBron sweepstakes dominating the headlines around them. Mitchell's 27.9-point scoring average last season ranked among the top eight in the NBA, and his playoff leadership has drawn league-wide respect.

The extension also signals organizational confidence. The Cavs are betting that their core is young enough, talented enough, and Mitchell is committed enough to compete for a championship in the near future. Mitchell's player option in 2030-31 gives him a decision point at 34 years old, still within the prime-to-veteran range for elite guards.

Setting the Stage

The Mitchell extension lands at a pivotal moment in the NBA calendar. July 6 brought an avalanche of trades and signings as the league moratorium ended, reshaping contenders across both conferences. In that environment — with stars moving to Miami (Giannis Antetokounmpo), Philadelphia (Jaylen Brown), and Portland (Ja Morant) — Mitchell's decision to stay represents a quiet but powerful counter-narrative.

Not everything is about the biggest move or the flashiest market. Sometimes the story is a star who looks at what he has, decides it is worth building on, and signs the papers.

Donovan Mitchell signed the papers.

What's Next

With Mitchell secured through 2031, the Cavaliers can now direct their full attention toward this summer's remaining questions — including whether LeBron James will add his name to what would be a formidable Cleveland core. The Cavs can only offer LeBron the veteran minimum (~$3.9 million), a financial gap that has been widely noted, but the basketball case has grown considerably stronger. Mitchell's extension deadline has passed. Now the league watches to see if the King follows.
