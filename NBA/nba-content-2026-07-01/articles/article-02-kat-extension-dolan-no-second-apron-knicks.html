KAT's Extension Ultimatum: Dolan Says No Second Apron, and the Brunson Blueprint Is the Ask

By Marcus Cole | Hoop Heroes | July 1, 2026

The Knicks won the championship. Now the most important offseason negotiation in franchise history is underway — and the owner has publicly drawn the line.

Knicks owner James Dolan announced that New York will not operate in the NBA's second tax apron in the 2026-27 season. The statement was not directed at the general public. It was directed at Karl-Anthony Towns.

The $273 Million Question

Towns is eligible for a four-year extension potentially worth $273 million or more. The full maximum would make him one of the highest-paid players in league history and cement his place in New York for the foreseeable future. It would also push the Knicks deep into second-apron territory — a threshold that carries severe roster-building restrictions, including limitations on trades and the use of exceptions.

The Knicks are asking Towns to accept $7 to $10 million less than the maximum. That haircut, combined with similar flexibility from Josh Hart — also extension-eligible this summer — would keep the championship core intact without triggering the second apron's punishing constraints.

The Brunson Blueprint

The comparison that hangs over every Towns negotiation is Jalen Brunson. The Knicks' Finals MVP took approximately $113 million less than his market value when he extended with New York — a sacrifice that was repaid with a championship, a city's undying love, and a legacy that goes beyond any contract number.

Brunson averaged 32.6 points per game in the 2026 Finals. He is the defining athlete in New York sports right now. The Knicks' front office is making a straightforward argument to Towns: Brunson did it. The result speaks for itself.

Towns has publicly stated he is "ready to make a financial sacrifice." Dolan's announcement transforms that statement into a test. The sacrifice is being named and priced.

The Numbers Behind the Tension

The Knicks' salary commitments are already enormous. OG Anunoby carries $212.5 million remaining on his deal. Mikal Bridges has $150 million committed. Add Towns's current owed salary of $57.1 million next season and his $61 million player option for 2027-28, and the second apron math becomes a real constraint, not just a theoretical one.

A max extension at $273 million pushes the Knicks into territory where they lose flexibility on future trades, cannot use bi-annual exceptions, and face additional restrictions on bringing in veterans. The haircut isn't about saving money — it's about preserving the ability to build around the championship core.

What's Next

Extension negotiations run alongside free agency, and the clock is ticking. If Towns accepts the terms New York is offering, the Knicks retain their best chance at a repeat. If he pushes for the full maximum, the franchise faces a harder set of choices about who stays and who goes. Dolan's public statement suggests the Knicks have determined that the line is drawn. The next move belongs to Karl-Anthony Towns.
