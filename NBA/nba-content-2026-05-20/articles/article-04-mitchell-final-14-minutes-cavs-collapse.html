Mitchell Scoreless in Final 14 Minutes: Breaking Down the Cavaliers' Historic Collapse

By Jake Torres | Hoop Heroes | May 20, 2026

Donovan Mitchell played one of the finest individual defensive performances of his career in ECF Game 1 at Madison Square Garden. A career-high 6 steals. Twenty-nine points. For three quarters and into the fourth, he was the best player on the floor and the reason the Cleveland Cavaliers led by 22 points with 7:52 remaining.

Then the Knicks' defense found him. And Mitchell went scoreless for the final 14 minutes of one of the most stunning collapses in Conference Finals history.

New York won 115-104 in overtime. The Cavaliers shot 22 percent as a team during the Knicks' 44-11 comeback run. A 22-point lead became a 11-point overtime loss in less than eight minutes of game clock.

What Changed

The Cavaliers' offensive drought was not exclusively Mitchell's responsibility, but as Cleveland's primary creator and scorer, the burden of breaking it fell on him. He did not. The reasons are worth examining.

New York's defense — helped by the collective defensive pressure of OG Anunoby and the Knicks' switching scheme — began attacking Mitchell's drive lanes and contesting his mid-range more aggressively in the final stretch. The initial defensive pressure that allowed him to accumulate 6 steals earlier in the game became a double-edged sword: his defensive intensity may have contributed to fatigue that affected his offensive output in the closing minutes.

That said, Mitchell has played through exhaustion before. What happened Tuesday in the final 14 minutes was more than fatigue. The Knicks made specific defensive reads — and Mitchell, in that critical window, did not find his shot.

The Team Context

Mitchell did not collapse alone. The Cavaliers as a team shot just 22 percent during the Knicks' comeback run. That is a team-wide offensive failure, not a one-man breakdown. Karl-Anthony Towns contributed 11 points, 10 rebounds, and 5 assists for the Cavaliers' opponents — no wait, Towns plays for the Knicks. For Cleveland, the role players who had contributed earlier in the game went cold simultaneously.

The Cavaliers arrived at MSG battle-tested from a 7-game series against the Detroit Pistons. That playoff mileage may be a factor. The Knicks were rested, having swept the Philadelphia 76ers, and brought full legs to the fourth quarter. Energy and freshness can be decisive in late-game moments.

What's Next

Game 2 is Thursday at Madison Square Garden. For Mitchell and the Cavaliers, the narrative is simple: they need to answer one loss with a definitive response. Mitchell has done that before — he is one of the league's best players at compartmentalizing a bad performance and coming back with a better one.

Cleveland's coaching staff will game-plan to get Mitchell better looks early in the fourth quarter rather than relying on him to manufacture them. The Knicks, meanwhile, will try to replicate the defensive intensity that froze Cleveland's offense during the comeback. Game 2 will reveal which version of Donovan Mitchell showed up Tuesday — an outlier or a warning sign.
