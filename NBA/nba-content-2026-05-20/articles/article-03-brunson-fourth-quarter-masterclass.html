Brunson's Fourth-Quarter Masterclass: Inside the 15-Point Takeover That Shocked Cleveland

By Damon Pierce | Hoop Heroes | May 20, 2026

Numbers can describe what Jalen Brunson did in the fourth quarter of ECF Game 1. They can not fully capture it. But let's start with the numbers anyway: 15 points, 7-of-9 shooting, in the final period of a game the Knicks trailed by 22 points.

That's the foundation. The full picture is more extraordinary. Brunson scored or assisted on 23 of New York's final 32 points in regulation and overtime as the Knicks pulled off the largest fourth-quarter comeback in Conference Finals history since 1997. The Knicks won 115-104 in overtime.

Possession by Possession

What made Brunson's fourth quarter remarkable was not just the volume of production but the quality and timing of each possession. When the Knicks were down 22, the natural instinct for any player is to play with urgency that borders on desperation. Brunson did the opposite.

He ran pick-and-rolls with patience, attacking the Cleveland defense at its seams rather than forcing shots against set defenders. When the mid-range opened up — and it repeatedly opened up against a Cavaliers defense that was increasingly rattled by the MSG crowd — Brunson pulled up and converted with the same efficiency he shows in practice.

Seven-of-nine shooting in the fourth quarter of a Conference Finals game, while your team is chasing a comeback from 22 down, under the brightest lights in basketball. That is a level of clutch performance that very few players in any era can deliver.

The Assist Dimension

Brunson's 38-point night (6 assists, 5 rebounds, 3 steals for the full game) might create the impression that this was a solo effort — that he dragged the Knicks over the finish line by himself. That would be an oversimplification.

The "23 of the final 32 points" figure includes his assists, and those assisted baskets were critical components of the comeback. Brunson's ability to draw defensive attention and deliver accurate passes in transition and in the half court created open looks that teammates converted. The Knicks won this as a team. But they won it because Brunson refused to let it slip away.

His 6 assists across the full game, combined with his 15 fourth-quarter points, reflect a player who understands that individual scoring and team offense are not competing priorities — they are the same thing when executed correctly.

What's Next

The Cavaliers now understand exactly what Brunson is capable of in a Conference Finals setting. Game 2 is Thursday at MSG. Cleveland will come with a designed defensive scheme aimed at disrupting Brunson's rhythm — more early pressure, different coverages on the pick-and-roll, attempts to tire him before the fourth quarter arrives.

Brunson has been through that kind of adjustments before. His response will define the series. If he can replicate even half of Tuesday's fourth-quarter efficiency, the Cavaliers are in serious trouble. The Garden is ready. The question is whether the Knicks can sustain what they started, and whether their No. 1 option can carry that load all the way to the Finals.
