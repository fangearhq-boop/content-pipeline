Pistons Beat Wolves 113-108 Without Cade as Duren and Jenkins Shine; Edwards and Cunningham Award-Ineligible

By Damon Pierce April 3, 2026

Detroit Wins Anyway

Cade Cunningham has been sidelined since March 17 with a collapsed left lung. On Thursday night against the Minnesota Timberwolves, that fact barely registered in the outcome. The Detroit Pistons won 113-108, moving their magic number for the No. 1 East seed down to 1 — and they did it the same way they have handled Cade's absence all season: by refusing to use it as an excuse.

Jalen Duren was the anchor, posting 22 points and 14 rebounds in a dominant interior performance. Daniss Jenkins provided 26 points from the perimeter, giving Detroit two reliable sources of scoring in a game where the Timberwolves threatened to claw back in the second half. When Minnesota got close, Detroit responded: an 11-0 run in the fourth quarter put the game away and removed any doubt about the final result.

The Award-Ineligibility Bombshell

The box score result was meaningful. But the larger story of Thursday's game was a confirmation that arrived before tip-off: both Anthony Edwards and Cade Cunningham have fallen below the 65-game games-played threshold required for NBA end-of-season award eligibility.

Edwards, who did not play for Minnesota on Thursday due to right knee patellofemoral syndrome, had been averaging a career-high 29.5 points per game and was widely considered a frontrunner for multiple All-NBA honors. Cunningham, who had been on the fringes of the MVP conversation earlier in the season before his collapsed lung ended his campaign, is also below the threshold and will not receive votes in any category. Neither player is expected to return before the regular season ends April 12.

The implications are significant. Two of the NBA's best young players — players many expected to anchor awards conversations for the next decade — are watching the 2025-26 awards season play out from the sideline. Their absences open the door for other players, and they add a melancholy footnote to what has otherwise been a historically compelling season.

Detroit's Unlikely Journey

There is a broader context to appreciate here: the Detroit Pistons were 14-68 just two seasons ago. They are now one win away from clinching the No. 1 overall seed in the Eastern Conference, and they are doing it while their franchise cornerstone recovers from a collapsed lung on the bench. Julius Randle scored 27 points for Minnesota, but it was not enough to slow a Detroit team that has become one of the NBA's most surprising sustained excellence stories.

Isaiah Stewart (left calf strain) was also absent for Detroit, and Tobias Harris left Thursday's game with a left knee contusion. The Pistons are winning with health issues piling up — a testament to a roster and coaching staff that has refused to let circumstances dictate results.

What's Next

Detroit needs just one more win — or a competitor's loss — to clinch the No. 1 East seed. That milestone, when it arrives, will be one of the most remarkable single-season turnarounds in recent NBA history. For Minnesota, the final stretch of the season brings the uncomfortable reality that their best player cannot help them, and the question of whether Edwards can return and restore some playoff momentum in time to matter will define the Timberwolves' postseason outlook. For the awards race, the landscape has permanently shifted: Edwards and Cunningham are out, and a generation of voters will have to make their choices without two of the season's most compelling individual performers.
