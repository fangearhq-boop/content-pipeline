Wembanyama Takes No. 1 on MVP Ladder With Once-in-a-Generation Statistical Run

By Marcus Cole April 3, 2026

A Voter Shift Nobody Saw Coming This Late

For most of the 2025-26 NBA season, the MVP race has felt settled. Shai Gilgeous-Alexander, the reigning award winner, has led the league in scoring at 31.5 points per game. Oklahoma City, his team, is 61-16. The case for a repeat seemed airtight.

Then Victor Wembanyama decided to put on the most historically extraordinary final stretch of the season any player has had in 50 years — and suddenly the race is a genuine conversation again. The NBA.com MVP Ladder, updated this week, now has Wembanyama at No. 1.

The Historic Context

The numbers behind Wembanyama's surge require historical context to fully appreciate. He has become the youngest player in NBA history to record back-to-back games of 40+ points and 15+ rebounds, accomplishing the feat at 22 years and 88 days old. More remarkably, he is the only player in 50 years to produce consecutive games with a stat line of 40+ points, 15+ rebounds, and 3+ blocks. These are not incremental improvements on existing records. They are performances that place Wembanyama in company that has not existed in the modern game.

Over his last 10 games, he is averaging 27.9 points and 12.3 rebounds. For the season, he has maintained lines of roughly 24.5 points, 11.4 rebounds, and 3.1 blocks per game. The blocks figure is a consistent reminder that Wembanyama's impact extends beyond offense — he changes the defensive calculus of every opponent's possession in ways that box scores can only partially capture.

The Two-Horse Race

Current MVP betting odds now place SGA as the -300 favorite, with Wembanyama at +220. Nikola Jokic sits at +600 and Luka Doncic at +1500. The gap between SGA and Wemby — while still meaningful — has compressed significantly from where it was a month ago.

The argument for SGA remains powerful: he leads the league in scoring, his team leads the Western Conference, and he has done it with extraordinary consistency across 77-plus games. The argument for Wembanyama centers on the scale of his performances when it matters most, the franchise significance of his run — San Antonio has gone 23-2 since February 1, and Wemby is the engine — and the historical uniqueness of what he is doing statistically.

Both arguments are legitimate. What the MVP Ladder movement reflects is that a growing number of observers believe the conversation has been reopened in the season's final week, and that voters who freeze their ballots in the next two weeks will need to grapple with what they have seen from a 22-year-old in San Antonio doing things nobody has done in decades.

What's Next

The San Antonio Spurs have 5 games remaining. Each one is now a dual stage for both a team chasing an improbable No. 1 seed and an individual player making the case for the most important individual honor in basketball. Victor Wembanyama is 22 years old. He is No. 1 on the MVP Ladder. And the most interesting question in the NBA right now is whether the voters who have watched SGA all season will have seen enough to change their minds. The final week will answer it.
