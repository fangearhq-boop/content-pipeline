Wembanyama After Historic Collapse: "It Just Hurts" — Can He Respond in Elimination Game 5?

By Jake Torres | June 11, 2026 | Hoop Heroes

Three words. That is all Victor Wembanyama offered after the most painful loss of his professional life.

"It just hurts."

He played 44 minutes on Wednesday night. He scored 24 points and grabbed 13 rebounds. He blocked three shots. And he watched his team take a 27-point lead into halftime, shoot 59.6 percent from the field in the first 24 minutes, and then collapse completely in the second half as the New York Knicks erased everything the Spurs had built and won 107-106 on a tip-in with 1.2 seconds remaining.

The series is 3-1 now. Saturday is an elimination game in San Antonio. And Wembanyama, 22 years old and in the first NBA Finals of his career, has to find a way to reset — again.

The Numbers Behind the Collapse

Wembanyama shot 9-of-25 from the field in Game 4. The Spurs as a team shot 8-of-39 in the second half — 20.5 percent from the field after going 28-of-47 in the first. That 46-point swing from one half to the next, per the Elias Sports Bureau, tied the largest in the shot clock era.

It is difficult to pinpoint exactly what changed at halftime. The Knicks made defensive adjustments. Jalen Brunson stopped deferring and started creating. OG Anunoby caught fire from three-point range, hitting four three-pointers in the second half alone. But the Spurs' offense — which had looked unstoppable — simply stopped working, and Wembanyama was unable to carry it alone.

He went 2-of-8 from three-point range and 4-of-7 from the free-throw line. His shot selection in the second half was imperfect. But he was also the Spurs' most consistent offensive presence in the half where nothing went right, and that counts for something.

The Pattern of Resilience

The word that has defined Wembanyama throughout this postseason is not talent — though that has never been in question. It is resilience.

After his errant pass with 13 seconds left in Game 2 gave the Knicks the win, he stood at the podium and said, "We can't change the past. We're already thinking about Game 3." He was not being diplomatic. He meant it. In Game 3, he delivered 32 points, eight rebounds, six assists, three blocks, and two steals at Madison Square Garden — the best performance of his Finals run.

The question Saturday is whether that reset has limits. Game 2 was one mistake in an otherwise strong performance. Game 4 was a team-wide collapse in which Wembanyama was a part for 44 minutes. The emotional and physical weight of those two things is different.

What the Spurs Need From Him

San Antonio's path to survival runs through Wembanyama being himself at full capacity — the player who posted 32 points in Game 3, who averaged over 27 points per game in the Western Conference Finals, who won the Defensive Player of the Year award unanimously. The Spurs cannot win an elimination game with the version who shot 36 percent in Game 4.

They also need the collective to show up. Stephon Castle was brilliant in Games 3, delivering the go-ahead shot in the final two minutes. De'Aaron Fox has been inconsistent but capable of explosive stretches. The Spurs have the talent to compete; Game 4's second half was historic in its anomaly.

What's Next

Game 5 is Saturday, June 13, at 8:30 PM ET on ABC at Frost Bank Center in San Antonio. The Spurs open as 5.5-point favorites in their own building, which speaks to how the market still values their home-court advantage despite facing elimination.

For Wembanyama, Saturday is the most important game of his career. He has shown once in this series that he can respond brilliantly to adversity. The question is whether he has a second act — and whether it arrives in time.
