Knicks 107, Spurs 106: OG Anunoby's Tip-In Caps the Largest Comeback in NBA Finals History

By Jake Torres | June 11, 2026 | Hoop Heroes

Nobody in Madison Square Garden believed it at halftime. The scoreboard read Spurs 76, Knicks 49, and San Antonio had just put together one of the most dominant 24 minutes any road team has ever played in an NBA Finals. The Spurs shot 59.6 percent from the field, made 14 three-pointers, and carried a 27-point lead into the locker room.

Twenty-four hours later, it is the first half that nobody remembers. What they remember is OG Anunoby's right hand stretching high, softly flicking Jalen Brunson's miss through the net with 1.2 seconds left — and the building erupting around it. Knicks 107, Spurs 106. The largest comeback in NBA Finals history. Series lead: 3-1.

A First Half for the Record Books

The Spurs came out executing at a level that seemed to make the Knicks' entire defensive scheme irrelevant. Victor Wembanyama was operating in space, San Antonio's ball movement was crisp, and the three-pointers kept dropping. They set a Finals record for three-pointers in a first half, hitting 14. By the break, the Knicks had been outscored by 27.

The previous largest comeback in NBA Finals history was 24 points. Entering the second half, the Knicks were in unprecedented territory — behind by more than any team in the championship round had ever come back from. The math said it was over.

The Second Half That Will Live Forever

Jalen Brunson does not acknowledge math. Neither does OG Anunoby.

Brunson finished with 36 points, finding every seam in San Antonio's defense and attacking the rim with the kind of relentless confidence that has defined his entire postseason run. The Spurs, who had been lights-out in the first half, simply stopped making shots. They went 8-for-39 from the field in the second half — a 20.5 percent clip that represented a 46-point swing from their first-half output. The Elias Sports Bureau reported that 46-point differential between halves tied the largest in the shot clock era.

Anunoby was the other half of the equation. He shot the ball with remarkable freedom, hitting seven three-pointers — both a playoff career-high — and finishing with 33 points, also a playoff career-high. Two massive three-pointers in the fourth quarter kept the Knicks' comeback mathematically alive when it had no business still breathing.

The Final Play

With 1.2 seconds left and the Knicks trailing 106-105, Brunson launched a three-pointer that rattled off the front of the rim. Anunoby, crashing the glass, soared for the rebound and softly tipped the ball through the basket. A heartbeat of silence — then the Garden shook.

Wembanyama, who played 44 minutes and finished with 24 points and 13 rebounds despite going 9-of-25 from the field, stood at center court as the celebration broke out around him. His post-game comment was three words: "It just hurts."

What It Means

The Knicks have not won an NBA championship since 1973. In five days, they can end that drought. Every team in NBA history that took a 3-1 lead in the Finals went on to win the championship — except one. The 2016 Golden State Warriors. That team won 73 regular season games and still squandered a 3-1 lead against LeBron James's Cavaliers.

The Spurs are not that team. But they are resilient, they play in San Antonio on Saturday, and they have the most talented young player in the world. That combination is not nothing.

What's Next

Game 5 is Saturday, June 13, at 8:30 PM ET on ABC at Frost Bank Center in San Antonio. A Knicks win ends it. The Spurs, 5.5-point favorites in their building, need to find a way to replicate their first half and sustain it for an entire game. Both things feel possible. Only one will happen.

The Knicks are one win from making history. And after what happened Wednesday night in New York, no one is counting them out — and no one is counting the Spurs out either.
