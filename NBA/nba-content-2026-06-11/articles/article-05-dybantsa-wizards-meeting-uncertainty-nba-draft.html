Dybantsa to No. 1 Is No Longer a Lock: Wizards Meeting Scheduled as Draft Uncertainty Rises

By Marcus Cole | June 11, 2026 | Hoop Heroes

Twelve days ago, the first pick in the 2026 NBA Draft felt like the most settled question in basketball. AJ Dybantsa was going to Washington with the No. 1 overall selection. The market said it at -450. Dybantsa himself said it — "100%" confident, he told reporters. Nearly every mock draft agreed.

Then the NBA Draft Combine happened. And now, with the draft 12 days away, a question that seemed answered has reopened.

What the Combine Revealed

CBS Sports reported this week that based on intel from the NBA Draft Combine, Dybantsa going No. 1 to Washington is "not the lock everyone assumed." The Wizards, the report noted, are still actively evaluating their options at the top of the board. Darryn Peterson — the Kansas guard who had been widely projected as the No. 2 pick to Utah — is reportedly still in the mix for Washington at No. 1.

Dybantsa himself performed well at the Combine. He posted a 42-inch vertical leap and a 7-foot, 0.5-inch wingspan — measurements that confirmed the athleticism behind his college production. At 6-foot-9 and 210 pounds, with elite scoring ability in the open floor and off the catch, he projects as exactly the kind of franchise cornerstone a 17-65 team needs.

But the Wizards plan to bring him in for a formal pre-draft visit in the coming days, per The Athletic. In draft circles, that meeting matters. It is an opportunity for the front office to look their most important decision in the eye.

The Case For Dybantsa

The numbers from BYU make an immediate argument. Dybantsa averaged a Division I-high 25.5 points per game on 51.0 percent shooting in his only college season. He is a versatile, two-way wing who can create off the dribble, attack mismatches, and defend multiple positions. The Combine confirmed his physical tools are legitimate.

Washington has not had a No. 1 overall pick since John Wall in 2010. That selection launched a run of expectations that never fully materialized. Dybantsa represents the same kind of franchise-reshaping opportunity — and at his age and production level, most evaluators consider him the safest high-ceiling pick in this class.

The Case for Peterson

Darryn Peterson's insertion into the No. 1 conversation reflects the front office's due diligence more than a genuine belief he is the better prospect. Peterson is a dynamic guard who projects as a starter immediately, with a scoring ceiling that some evaluators put at All-Star level. If the Wizards see him as a more position-specific fit for their roster construction, the argument is not irrational.

Most projections still have Peterson going No. 2 to the Utah Jazz if Washington takes Dybantsa. The fact that he is "in the mix" at the top suggests uncertainty rather than a genuine fork in the road — but in the NBA Draft, perception and uncertainty become self-fulfilling in unpredictable ways.

What's Next

The Washington Wizards will host Dybantsa for a pre-draft visit in the coming days. That meeting, combined with the remaining two weeks of front-office deliberation, will determine whether the odds-on favorite becomes the reality or whether an upset reshapes the entire draft board.

The 2026 NBA Draft takes place June 23-24 at Barclays Center in Brooklyn. Twelve days remain. For Dybantsa, the uncertainty adds a layer of pressure to what had felt like a done deal. For Peterson, it represents an opening. For basketball fans, it is the storyline to watch as the Finals wrap up and the offseason takes center stage.
