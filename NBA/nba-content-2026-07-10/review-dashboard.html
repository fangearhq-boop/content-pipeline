### Long-Form Post The most hyped Summer League opener in years lived up to every bit of the anticipation. AJ Dybantsa scored 27 points Thursday night at the Thomas and Mack Center, leading the Washington Wizards past the Utah Jazz 92-88. He added 7 rebounds, 2 assists, 2 steals, and a block in his professional debut. The No. 1 pick answered on the biggest stage Summer League offers. Darryn Peterson didn't go quietly. The No. 2 pick from Utah fought back with 24 points, but eight turnovers were too much to overcome. That turnover number — eight in his debut in Salt Lake City too — is becoming a real pattern for Peterson to clean up. The talent is obvious. The ball security has to improve. There was one storyline within the storyline: Dybantsa went 0-for-5 from three-point range. He got to the rim, drew fouls, and competed physically the way scouts expected. But the outside shot is still a work in progress. In the NBA, that matters. Peterson, for all his turnovers, is a better shooter right now. One Summer League game doesn't define careers. But round one of this rivalry went to Washington. The Jazz and Wizards play again before the Vegas schedule is done, and the next game is already must-watch. Who are you more excited about — Dybantsa or Peterson? ### Image Post Caption Dybantsa 27. Peterson 24. Wizards 92, Jazz 88. Round one of the rookie rivalry goes to Washington. 🏀

### Long-Form Post Draymond Green just made the most interesting move of LeBron James' free agency — and it wasn't LeBron's. Green declined his $27.7 million player option with the Golden State Warriors, making the move specifically to create financial flexibility for a potential LeBron pursuit. He and Warriors leadership have been in discussions for weeks about exactly this scenario: if Green takes a pay cut or restructured deal, Golden State can chase LeBron alongside Stephen Curry. That's the pitch. LeBron left the Lakers last week. He told the franchise he will not return for the 2026-27 season. Now six teams are chasing him — Warriors, Cavaliers, 76ers, Heat, Timberwolves, and Nuggets — and every one of them has a legitimate case. Cleveland's case: home, Donovan Mitchell, and a franchise that's been rebuilt into a contender. ESPN's Brian Windhorst describes the Cavs as the front-runner. Windhorst knows LeBron better than most. Golden State's case: Stephen Curry, Draymond, and one last championship run before both stars wind down. Draymond declining $27.7 million because he wants to play with LeBron is a powerful signal. Philadelphia's case: Tyrese Maxey (a Klutch Sports client like LeBron), Jaylen Brown, and a team that could threaten any conference rival immediately. Rich Paul, LeBron's agent, says a decision is not coming soon. Mid-July is the working timeline. Everyone is watching. Kawhi Leonard Trade to Toronto on Hold Pending NBA Investigation into Clippers
NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post Toronto and Los Angeles had a deal. Kawhi Leonard was going home. And then the NBA froze everything. The Raptors and Clippers agreed to a trade sending Kawhi back to Toronto, with the Raptors giving up Brandon Ingram, Gradey Dick, two first-round draft picks, two second-round picks, and pick swaps. It was a significant package for a 34-year-old coming off injury-filled seasons. But for the Raptors, it was a franchise moment — the champion coming home. Now that deal is stuck in place, and nobody knows for how long. The NBA is conducting an investigation into whether the Clippers circumvented salary cap rules to sign Kawhi in 2019. At the center of the probe is an alleged $28 million endorsement deal between Leonard and Aspiration Fund Adviser LLC — a company that has since filed for bankruptcy. If the Clippers improperly paid Leonard benefits to lure him from San Antonio, that could violate league rules, and the trade would need to be adjudicated before it can close. Outside counsel is handling the investigation. There is no timetable for its completion. The Clippers say they "did not funnel money to Kawhi Leonard." Until the NBA concludes otherwise, or clears them, the trade sits in limbo. Raptors fans who were starting to imagine the jersey retirement ceremonies and the roar of Scotiabank Arena have to keep waiting. This is the NBA offseason working at its messiest. ### Image Post Caption Kawhi's Toronto homecoming is on hold. The NBA investigation into the Clippers has no end date. 🚨

### Long-Form Post Tim Connelly does not forget who came through when it counted most. Last spring, the Timberwolves were in their first-round series against the Nuggets when Donte DiVincenzo went down with a torn Achilles. The game was Game 4. The season was on the line. And off the bench, Ayo Dosunmu scored 43 points to help Minnesota steal the win. That performance had a price tag. Connelly and the Timberwolves moved quickly to keep Dosunmu from ever reaching open market, and on Thursday they reached agreement on a five-year, $112 million deal with a player option in the fifth year. In 24 regular-season games with Minnesota, Dosunmu averaged 14.4 points, 4.2 rebounds, and 3.5 assists per game. In the playoffs he took it up a level — 15.6 points per game in 29.2 minutes across a 10-game run, including the career-defining 43-point game when his team needed him most. Several teams had interest in Dosunmu as a free agent. Connelly never gave them the chance. The Timberwolves' offseason has been busy — Julius Randle was shipped to Brooklyn, LaMelo Ball arrived via trade — and this signing gives them a proven scorer and two-way contributor who fits the contender timeline. Is $112M the right price for Dosunmu? The market says yes. The Game 4 tape says definitely. ### Image Post Caption 43 off the bench in the playoffs. $112 million to stay. Jokić Confirms He'll Wait for $359M Extension With Nuggets Next Summer
NOT VERIFIEDFACEBOOK
### Long-Form Post Nikola Jokić could have signed a $278 million contract extension with the Denver Nuggets this summer. He is going to wait instead. The three-time MVP told reporters Thursday that he plans to hold off on signing until after the 2026-27 season. By waiting one year, he becomes eligible for a five-year, $359.5 million maximum contract — the largest deal in NBA history — rather than the four-year, $278 million extension available now. The math is simple: patience is worth roughly $81 million. "My idea and desire is to stay in Denver. I'll probably sign next year," Jokić said. This is not about leverage, and it is not about uncertainty. Jokić has never asked out of Denver. He won three MVP awards and a championship there. He has repeatedly said he wants to finish his career in Colorado. The delay is purely financial strategy, and it is entirely rational. What it means for the Nuggets: they head into 2026-27 without a signed contract extension from their franchise player. That creates a small but real layer of roster-building risk. Teams cannot treat an unsigned Jokić exactly the same as a locked-in Jokić when planning long-term. What it means for the NBA: if and when Jokić signs next summer, the $359.5 million deal will reset every benchmark in the sport. Until then, the best player in basketball is playing out the year, committed to Denver, just not yet signed. ### Image Post Caption Jokić said it himself: "I'll probably sign next year." At $359M, can you blame him? 🐐
