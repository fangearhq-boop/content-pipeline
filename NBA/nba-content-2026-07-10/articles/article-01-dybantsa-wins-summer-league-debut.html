Dybantsa Wins Round One: 27 Points, Wizards Beat Jazz 92-88 in Vegas Debut

By Damon Pierce July 10, 2026

The Debut Delivered

AJ Dybantsa showed up to Las Vegas and played like No. 1 picks are supposed to. The Washington Wizards' first overall pick scored 27 points Thursday night at the Thomas and Mack Center, adding 7 rebounds, 2 assists, 2 steals, and a block as the Wizards defeated the Utah Jazz 92-88 in the most-watched Summer League opener in recent memory.

The NBA's top draft pick from the 2026 class went into the game carrying enormous expectations and a counter-narrative working against him. Darryn Peterson, the No. 2 pick, had already posted a 25-point, 12-assist double-double in Salt Lake City and followed it with 28 points and an overtime win against the Hawks. Dybantsa needed to answer. He did.

Where Dybantsa Was Sharp — and Where He Wasn't

Dybantsa's points came through the exact channels scouts expected: attacking the rim, drawing fouls, and converting in traffic. His physical tools translate immediately at this level. At 6-foot-9 with the strength to play through contact, he creates angles defenders struggle to stop without fouling.

The weakness that emerged is one Wizards fans will be watching closely all summer. Dybantsa went 0-for-5 from three-point range Thursday night. He took the shots — the opportunity was there — but none of them fell. At BYU, where he led all of Division I with 25.5 points per game, his three-point shooting was inconsistent. That pattern continued in his debut. Against an NBA defense that will eventually sag off him and dare him to shoot, Dybantsa has to answer that challenge before it becomes a ceiling.

Peterson's Case Remains Alive

Darryn Peterson scored 24 points Thursday and competed throughout the game, but eight turnovers were too costly to overcome. That is the same number he posted in his debut against the Grizzlies in Salt Lake City. For a playmaker with his level of vision and creation, the turnover volume is not a flaw to rationalize — it is the primary thing standing between Peterson and an elite ceiling.

The offense is not the concern. Peterson's ability to create for himself and others is evident on nearly every possession. His shot-making is already at a higher level than Dybantsa's from the perimeter. He shot 4-for-7 from three in the Salt Lake opener and has shown he can score from multiple levels. If the turnovers come down — and at his age, against his competition level, they should — Peterson has the tools to make this rivalry genuinely uncomfortable for Wizards fans over the next decade.

The Bigger Picture

One game in Summer League does not decide anything. Both players know that. The rosters around them are filled with players who won't be in the league by October, and the defensive intensity is a fraction of what either of them will face in November. But Summer League creates narrative, and Thursday night in Las Vegas the narrative went in Dybantsa's direction.

The Wizards won the game. Their top pick had the best individual performance on the floor. He left with a box score that validates the selection and a shooting line from three that gives him a clear development priority. Peterson, despite the loss, proved once again that the No. 2 pick argument is not going away.

What's Next

Both the Wizards and Jazz continue through the Vegas Summer League round-robin, with the top four teams advancing to a playoff bracket beginning July 18. Dybantsa and Peterson will likely cross paths again before the competition is over. For fans watching this rivalry develop, the first chapter delivered exactly what anyone could have hoped for — two legitimate talents, a competitive game, and no definitive resolution. That's the best possible outcome for a Summer League opener.
