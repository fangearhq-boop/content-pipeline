Home Court Reality: What the Knicks Face in the 2026 NBA Finals

By Damon Pierce | Hoop Heroes | May 30, 2026

The New York Knicks are going to the NBA Finals. They will also be the road team for most of it. That is the straightforward consequence of finishing the regular season with a 53-29 record in a Western Conference that produced two of the greatest records in recent NBA history.

Oklahoma City went 64-18. San Antonio went 62-20. Both of those records are superior to New York's, which means that whoever emerges from tonight's Game 7 will hold home-court advantage in the Finals — hosting Games 1, 2, 5, and 7. The Knicks get Games 3, 4, and 6 at Madison Square Garden.

The Home Court Numbers

Home-court advantage in the NBA Finals is meaningful but not determinative. Since 2000, the home-court team has won roughly two out of every three championship series. It is a real edge, built from familiar surroundings, crowd energy, sleeping in one's own bed, and reduced travel during the crucial middle portion of a series.

But there are notable exceptions. The 2011 Mavericks won the title on the road against Miami. The 2016 Cavaliers came back from 3-1 to win three straight on the road. The home-court advantage is a starting point, not a destiny.

For the Knicks, starting on the road means they need to steal at least one game in the Western city to put themselves in the driver's seat. If they can split the first two games, they return to Madison Square Garden with the series tied — and MSG in October would be one of the loudest sporting venues in the world.

OKC vs. SA: Two Different Road Challenges

The two potential Finals opponents present different road challenges for New York. Oklahoma City's Paycom Center, where the Thunder went 34-7 in the regular season and 6-1 in these playoffs, is an intense environment built around SGA worship and defensive energy. The Thunder run their system relentlessly at home, where the crowd noise disrupts opposing offensive communication.

San Antonio's AT&T Center carries the legacy of a Spurs franchise that has been to the Finals six times. While San Antonio finished with the second-best record in the NBA, their home record was strong but not quite OKC's level of dominance. The Spurs are also a road-tested team — they went 5-3 away from home in these playoffs, which suggests their quality travels.

Either way, the Knicks will open the Finals in a building built to be hostile. Coach Tom Thibodeau has emphasized preparation over the 13-day wait — his team knows both potential opponents in detail. The Knicks' defensive identity, built around physicality and rotation discipline, does not suddenly evaporate on the road.

Madison Square Garden as the Equalizer

When the Finals come to New York for Games 3 and 4, the equation may flip. Madison Square Garden has a history of overwhelming visiting teams — the noise, the tradition, the expectation. The Knicks have gone 53-29 at home this season and were dominant at MSG through the entire playoff run.

For the western team, playing Games 3 and 4 at MSG will be a different kind of pressure — a chance to close the series out early, or a chance to fall behind in a building that will be the loudest in the country. Either scenario produces high-stakes basketball. The Knicks' path to a title runs through the road, but it includes three potential home games at the most famous arena in sports.

What's Next

Game 7 between Oklahoma City and San Antonio tips tonight at 8:15 PM ET on NBC/Peacock. The Knicks will learn their Finals opponent before midnight. Game 1 of the NBA Finals begins Wednesday, June 3, at 8:30 PM ET on ABC. The Knicks' first home game is June 8 — Game 3 at Madison Square Garden.

The road disadvantage is real. So is the Knicks' talent, their 11-game win streak, and their 27 years of pent-up New York energy. The Finals will be compelling regardless of how the home-court equation plays out.
