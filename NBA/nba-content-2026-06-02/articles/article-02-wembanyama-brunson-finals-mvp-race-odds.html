Wembanyama vs. Brunson: The 2026 Finals MVP Race by the Numbers

By Jake Torres | Hoop Heroes | June 2, 2026

Before the first tip of the 2026 NBA Finals, the market has rendered a preliminary verdict: Victor Wembanyama is the -170 favorite to win Finals MVP. Jalen Brunson, the player who could make that verdict look foolish, sits at +265.

The spread is narrow enough to call this a coin flip dressed up as a favorite. But the numbers behind each case are worth examining before Game 1 on Wednesday.

The Case for Wembanyama

Wembanyama's regular season was historic. He averaged 26.8 points, 11.2 rebounds, 3.8 assists, 2.1 steals, and 3.4 blocks per game — all while shooting 48.7 percent from the field and 37.2 percent from three. He won the Kia Defensive Player of the Year award unanimously.

In the Western Conference Finals against the defending champion Oklahoma City Thunder, Wembanyama elevated further. He averaged 27.3 points, 10.9 rebounds, and 2.7 blocks per game over seven games, earning WCF MVP honors. The Spurs came back from a 3-2 deficit to win the series, with Wembanyama posting 28 points and 10 rebounds in the clinching Game 6 before the decisive Game 7 in Oklahoma City.

The offensive ceiling is extraordinary. The defensive floor is elite. And at 22 years old, he is playing without the burden of expectation — or so the narrative goes.

The Case for Brunson

Jalen Brunson averaged 28.4 points and 6.1 assists per game in the regular season, shooting 51.2 percent from the field and 41.8 percent from three. He was the most efficient point guard in the league over a full season.

His postseason has continued that form. Over his last 10 playoff games, Brunson has averaged 27.4 points on 51.8 percent shooting. In three regular season matchups against the Spurs this year, he averaged 26 points, 4 rebounds, and 8 assists. He knows this defensive scheme. He has beaten it.

ESPN's First Take debated Monday whether Brunson or Wembanyama faces greater Finals pressure. The argument for Brunson: the Knicks are in the Finals for the first time since 1999, and a city of 8 million people is depending on him to close it out. The counterargument: Brunson has been here all playoff long, and pressure has not changed his game one bit.

The Matchup Problem

The Spurs' defensive scheme for Brunson begins with Stephon Castle and Dylan Harper as the primary guards, designed to slow him before Wembanyama needs to rotate. It is a layered scheme built on the idea that Brunson will be fatigued by the time he reaches Wemby's orbit.

Through 14 playoff games, no team has successfully depleted Brunson. He has played between 36 and 40 minutes in most games and has not posted below 22 points in a single playoff contest. The Castle-and-Harper wall is the best plan the Spurs have — but it is an unproven one against this specific opponent.

What's Next

The Finals MVP is decided by the player who performs best in the games that matter most. Neither Wembanyama nor Brunson has ever played in the NBA Finals. Game 1 on Wednesday will tell us something. Games 4 through 7, if they happen, will tell us everything. Watch both closely.
