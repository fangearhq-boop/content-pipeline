Gregg Popovich's Shadow: How the Spurs Legend Still Drives the 2026 Finals Run

By Marcus Cole | Hoop Heroes | June 2, 2026

Gregg Popovich will not stand in the coaching box during the 2026 NBA Finals. He will not draw up plays in a timeout huddle or pace the sideline in the fourth quarter. But anyone who believes Pop is absent from this series has not been watching closely enough.

The San Antonio Spurs are in the NBA Finals because of what Gregg Popovich spent 29 years building. And he is still in the building.

What Happened to Pop

In November 2024, Popovich suffered a stroke that effectively ended his career as an active head coach. He is 77 years old. He has been in rehabilitation since, working to regain strength and function alongside — remarkably — Tim Duncan, who is now with the Spurs organization and frequently works out with Popovich during his rehab sessions.

Pop now holds the title of President of Spurs Basketball. In practice, he functions as a coach emeritus. He attends practices, sometimes with the assistance of a cane. He watches film. He advises head coach Mitch Johnson. He is, by multiple accounts, still a regular presence in the ears of the coaches and in the consciousness of the players.

What He Means to Wembanyama

Victor Wembanyama arrived in San Antonio in 2023 as the most anticipated prospect since LeBron James. Popovich was still the coach for that first season. What developed between them — a 77-year-old basketball philosopher and a 22-year-old with a once-in-a-generation skill set — became one of the more quietly remarkable relationships in the league.

Wembanyama has spoken about Popovich with visible emotion. That is not something Wembanyama does easily. The connection runs deep, and the motivation to win this championship for Pop is part of the story no box score captures.

Mitch Johnson's Role

Head coach Mitch Johnson did not create the Spurs' culture. He inherited it, was shaped by it during his own time in the organization, and has been trusted to carry it forward. That is itself a testament to what Popovich built — a system that produces coaches, not just players.

The Spurs' 62-20 regular season record and first Finals appearance since 2014 belong to Johnson's team. But the foundation — the principles of team defense, ball movement, and professional accountability — belong to the man who coached this franchise to five championships over three decades.

What's Next

The 2026 NBA Finals begin Wednesday, and Gregg Popovich will watch from somewhere inside the Frost Bank Center. If the Spurs win this championship, it will be the sixth banner in franchise history — and the one that belongs to a new generation. But it will also belong to the coach who made all of it possible. His shadow is not a burden on this team. It is a blueprint.
