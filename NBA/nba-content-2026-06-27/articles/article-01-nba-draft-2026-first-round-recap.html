2026 NBA Draft Recap: Michigan Makes Lottery History as Dybantsa Goes No. 1 to Washington

By Damon Pierce

June 27, 2026

Hoop Heroes

AJ Dybantsa had been the No. 1 pick in every mock draft for nine months. On Thursday night at Barclays Center, it became official. The Washington Wizards selected the BYU small forward with the first overall pick in the 2026 NBA Draft, making Dybantsa the franchise's first top selection since John Wall in 2010. But the story of draft night did not belong to any single player. It belonged to Michigan.

The Wolverines sent three players to the lottery in a single draft for the first time in school history — a milestone that arrived with fitting irony. Morez Johnson Jr. went No. 9 to the Dallas Mavericks, the team that had just hired Michigan head coach Dusty May weeks after May led the Wolverines to the national championship. Yaxel Lendeborg followed at No. 11 to the Golden State Warriors. Aday Mara, a 7-foot-3 center with international upside, rounded it out at No. 12 to Oklahoma City. Three lottery picks. One program. One night.

The Top of the Board

The consensus held at the top. Darryn Peterson, the Kansas guard, went No. 2 to the Utah Jazz — one of the few players all year who generated genuine debate about whether he might supplant Dybantsa at the top of the board. Cameron Boozer, the Duke forward who won the Naismith Award and averaged 22.5 points and 10.5 rebounds per game this season, landed at No. 3 in Memphis, where the Grizzlies need frontcourt depth after dealing Jaren Jackson Jr. in the off-season.

Caleb Wilson, a 6-foot-10 wing from North Carolina, went No. 4 to Chicago. Keaton Wagler, a guard from Illinois, followed at No. 5 to the Clippers. Mikel Brown Jr. from Louisville went No. 6 to Brooklyn. Darius Acuff Jr. from Arkansas landed at No. 7 in Sacramento. Kingston Flemings from Houston went No. 8 to Atlanta.

And then came the Michigan run that defined the night.

Michigan's Historic Lottery Sweep

Morez Johnson Jr. never played a minute under Dusty May's replacement in Ann Arbor. He left for the draft and landed in Dallas — directly under the coach who just got him a national championship ring and knows his game better than anyone in the league. Mavs general manager Nico Harrison called it a fortunate coincidence. May probably smiled when he saw the pick.

Lendeborg, the Wolverines' versatile forward, went to the Warriors at No. 11. Golden State has long prized the type of switchable, modern big that Lendeborg profiles as, and he fits comfortably into what Steve Kerr has spent years constructing.

Mara may be the most intriguing of the three. At 7'3" with a developing offensive game and legitimate defensive tools, he projects as the kind of long-term project that Oklahoma City, which already has one of the deepest young rosters in the league, can afford to develop. OKC acquired the No. 12 pick via the Clippers in a pre-draft trade — and then used it on a player who could be dominant by the time their current core reaches its peak.

Brayden Burries from Arizona went No. 10 to Milwaukee between the Lendeborg and Mara selections. The full lottery, picks 1-14, featured 10 players who had completed just one college season — the continuation of a trend that now defines how teams approach the top of the draft.

Other Notable Selections

The Big 12 had a night to remember beyond just the top picks. The conference sent 13 players to the NBA on Thursday, a new all-time conference record. Big 12 commissioner Brett Yormark called it validation of what the conference has built since expansion.

One of the night's more unexpected developments was the dramatic fall of Koa Peat, who had been projected as a consensus top-five selection heading into the week. Shooting concerns from pre-draft workouts — particularly the consistency of his release — spooked several teams, and Peat reportedly slid all the way to the late first round before Dallas selected him around No. 30 and immediately packaged him in a trade to the Phoenix Suns.

The second round was notable for its commerce: 29 of 30 picks were reportedly traded before or during the draft, a historically high figure that reflects how teams have learned to manufacture value from assets that are no longer reliably useful at their face value. The Grizzlies added Isaiah Stewart from Detroit in a second-round deal, giving Boozer a veteran presence alongside him from day one.

The Knicks executed a draft-night swap of their No. 31 pick to the Houston Rockets, a move that generated a smaller return but gave New York some cap clarity heading into free agency.

What's Next

The Summer League is where these names become real. Las Vegas opens July 9 with a marquee doubleheader: Cameron Boozer's Grizzlies take on Caleb Wilson's Bulls at 8 PM ET on Prime Video, and then the headline event — AJ Dybantsa's Wizards against Darryn Peterson's Jazz at 9 PM on ESPN. The No. 1 and No. 2 picks, facing each other in the desert on the same night they were drafted together on a Brooklyn stage. The NBA Summer League has never had higher-profile openers.

Free agency opens June 30, meaning teams that drafted players Thursday night are simultaneously in the final stages of recruiting veterans. The next 72 hours will be chaotic in every front office across the league — and by the time Summer League tips off, the rosters around these rookies will look very different from what they are today.

For Michigan, this draft night joins a short list of moments the program can point to as evidence of what it has become. Three lottery picks, one night, one draft class. It has never happened before for the Wolverines. It may never happen again.
