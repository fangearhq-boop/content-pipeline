Knicks Draft Night Done. Now Comes the Harder Part: KAT, Robinson, and Holding a Dynasty Together

By Damon Pierce

June 27, 2026

Hoop Heroes

The confetti from the championship parade is barely off Canyon of Heroes. The Knicks used their draft picks Thursday night, completed their draft-day transactions, and now face a more complicated problem than any team that just won a title should want to face: holding the whole thing together.

New York entered the 2026 draft with three selections — No. 24 in the first round, the No. 31 pick originally acquired from Washington (which was swapped on draft night to the Houston Rockets), and a second-round selection at No. 55. They made their picks, processed their transactions, and emerged from Barclays Center with a roster that is nominally intact and financially constrained in ways that will define how the next three years unfold.

The KAT Calculation

Karl-Anthony Towns is the most important decision the Knicks make this summer. He is eligible for a four-year extension worth up to approximately $272 million — roughly $70 million per year. He currently earns $57.1 million next season and holds a $61 million player option for 2027-28.

He has already said the right things. "I'm ready to make a financial sacrifice," Towns told reporters earlier this month. "If we can keep it all together, show me then. We all got to get in the room."

The Brunson precedent is impossible to ignore. Two years ago, Jalen Brunson took $113 million less than his maximum value when he signed his extension with New York. He then won the NBA championship, won Finals MVP — averaging 32.6 points in the series — and gave the franchise its first title since 1973. Brunson was willing to sacrifice. The city rewarded him with a legacy.

Now it is Towns's turn. The Knicks cannot go into the second luxury tax apron — James Dolan has made that explicit. OG Anunoby is locked at $212.5 million over five years. If KAT takes the full $272 million, the roster construction around him becomes almost impossible. If he takes $200 million, or $180 million, the math starts to work again. The negotiating window opens Monday. The real number will emerge in the coming weeks.

The Robinson Question

Mitchell Robinson is an unrestricted free agent. He is 28 years old, a rim protector who blocks shots and crashes boards at an elite level, and a player the Knicks have relied on selectively throughout the championship run. He played through injury during the playoff push and was not always able to give the team his best version.

The Knicks would prefer to retain him. But only at the right number. They know his value better than anyone — and they also know that their cap situation has no room for generosity. Robinson will test the market. He deserves to. What he finds there, and whether New York can match it, will tell us something about the limits of what this front office can actually sustain.

The Bigger Picture

What the Knicks have built is genuinely rare. They went 54 years without a championship. They have one now. The path to repeating runs directly through the decisions of the next three weeks — not just KAT and Robinson, but the free agency period that opens Monday and the rosters that Miami, Boston, and Cleveland are building to challenge them.

The defending champions always face this moment: the window is open, the team is intact, the expectations are enormous, and the salary cap constraints that come with success start to work against you. Every championship team navigates this transition. Most fail it.

New York has the blueprint. They have the players who bought into it. The question is whether KAT, and Robinson, and whoever else faces a pay decision this summer, looks at what Brunson built and decides that winning is worth the sacrifice.

What's Next

Free agency opens Monday. KAT's extension talks are expected to begin around the same time. Robinson will hear from multiple teams once the negotiating window is live. James Harden's player option decision deadline is Sunday — his expected return to Cleveland, on a reduced deal, would affect the Eastern Conference landscape the Knicks have to navigate.

The draft is done. The offseason is just beginning. This is when dynasties are preserved or dismantled — not on the floor, but in boardrooms and phone calls in the days between June 30 and July 6. The Knicks know what they need to do. The question is whether everyone in that room is willing to do it.
