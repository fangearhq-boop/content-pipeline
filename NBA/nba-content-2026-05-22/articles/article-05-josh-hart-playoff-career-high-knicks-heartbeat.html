Josh Hart's 26-Point Playoff Career-High Is the Knicks Story Nobody Saw Coming

By Jake Torres | Hoop Heroes | May 22, 2026

No one picked Josh Hart to be the leading scorer in an Eastern Conference Finals game.

On Thursday night at Madison Square Garden, with the Knicks needing to close out a 2-0 series lead and Cleveland scrambling for answers, Hart went out and scored a playoff career-high 26 points — 10-of-21 from the field, 5-of-11 from three — in New York's 109-93 Game 2 win over the Cavaliers. He added 7 assists, 4 rebounds, and 2 steals. He was the best player on the floor.

He usually isn't. That's part of what makes it remarkable.

How He Got Here

Hart was a first-round pick — selected 30th overall — who carved out a professional career through relentless effort rather than the elite athleticism or shot-creation ability that defined higher picks around him. He spent time with multiple franchises before landing in New York, where the Knicks' culture of toughness and accountability aligned perfectly with what he had always been.

The shooting was the development that made him dangerous. Earlier in his career, Hart was a physical presence and a hustle-stat accumulator — not someone defenses needed to respect from three-point range. This season, that changed. He shot at a high clip from deep during the regular season, and the Cavaliers have paid for underestimating that development through two ECF games.

The Floor-Spacing Effect

In ECF Game 2, Hart made 5-of-11 threes. For context: that is a level of three-point production that would stress any defense in any game. In the context of a New York offense that also features Jalen Brunson attacking the paint, Karl-Anthony Towns in the post, and Mikal Bridges creating off the catch, it becomes an unsolvable puzzle.

Cleveland had to make choices. They chose to shade toward Brunson's drives and Towns' post ups — and Hart kept making them pay from the arc. When they adjusted to run Hart off the line, Brunson found cutting lanes or drew fouls. The 14-assist night Brunson had is in part a testament to the space Hart created by being a legitimate shooting threat.

The Knicks have always been a team built on the idea that no single player needs to carry the load every night. Game 1 was Brunson's night. Game 2 was Hart's night. The system adapts to whoever is going, and it produced two wins.

The Unsung Hero Narrative

Hart will not be the focus of most national broadcast analysis of this series. That airtime belongs to Brunson and, on the other side, to Donovan Mitchell. The MVP narratives, the storylines about the Cavaliers' collapse — none of them center on Hart.

That is fine with him, and it is fine with the Knicks. In a league that often rewards individual stars with the credit for team success, New York has built something genuinely collective — and Hart is the clearest example of how that works. He logged 26 points and 7 assists in an ECF game on Thursday, and on Saturday he will go to Cleveland and try to do whatever the team needs: defend, set screens, crash boards, shoot threes if they're open.

The playoff career-high will be in the record books. The Knicks are two wins from the Finals. And Josh Hart is exactly the kind of player who won't think about either of those facts until the series is over.

What's Next
