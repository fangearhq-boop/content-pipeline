Summer League 2026: Five Rookies Who Will Define This Draft Class in Las Vegas

By Marcus Cole

June 26, 2026

Hoop Heroes

NBA Summer League tips off July 3 in Las Vegas and runs through July 19, giving the league its first extended look at a rookie class that includes eight freshmen in the first nine picks — tying the all-time record for early-entry dominance. The hype has been building since October. Now comes the part where it meets reality, one possession at a time.

Summer League performances do not determine careers, but they establish early impressions and tell scouts things no workout ever could. Here is what to watch.

The No. 1 Question: Can Dybantsa Back Up His Swagger?

AJ Dybantsa did not work out for a single NBA team before the draft. That was not an oversight — it was a statement. "I don't gotta work out," he said. "They know what I can do." The Washington Wizards agreed, taking him first overall without the customary pre-draft audition.

The confidence is not unfounded. The 6-foot-9 forward from BYU averaged 25.5 points per game on 51 percent shooting, combining elite scoring efficiency with the kind of shot creation scouts spend careers looking for.

Summer League is where that declaration meets its first test. Washington's supporting cast will be thin, letting defenses focus entirely on Dybantsa. How he handles that attention — whether he forces things or finds angles, whether he stays composed — will tell scouts something real. The whole basketball world will be watching.

The Big Board: Five Others to Watch

Darryn Peterson lands with the Utah Jazz at No. 2. The Kansas product averaged 20.2 points per game and brings scoring aggression that fits Utah's need for a go-to option. The Jazz have been patient rebuilders, and Peterson gives their timeline a genuine accelerant.

Cameron Boozer goes to the Memphis Grizzlies at No. 3. The Duke big man averaged 22.5 points and 10.5 rebounds per game and won the Naismith Award. He plays with physicality and poise beyond his age, and Memphis's up-tempo, physical system should bring out his best.

At No. 9, Morez Johnson Jr. heads to Dallas. He will spend his first season alongside Cooper Flagg, last year's No. 1 pick and the reigning Rookie of the Year. Johnson is long, switchable, and skilled enough to complement Flagg rather than compete with him.

Yaxel Lendeborg goes to Golden State at No. 11. The stretch-and-switch forward has range and IQ for a Warriors system that has historically developed skilled bigs well. At No. 12, Aday Mara arrives at Oklahoma City — a 7-foot-3 floor-spacer from Michigan by way of Spain who is the class's most physically unique prospect. OKC's development track record gives Mara an ideal landing spot.

That is three Michigan players in the top twelve — Lendeborg, Johnson, and Mara — making the Wolverines the first program in NBA history to achieve that in a single draft. It is a program-defining milestone.

What's Next

Summer League tips off July 3 and runs through July 19 at the Thomas and Mack Center and Cox Pavilion in Las Vegas. All thirty teams participate, giving fans and front offices the most concentrated burst of new NBA talent they will see until the regular season opens in October.

Beyond individual performances, watch for the team-building signals these games send. Which organizations look organized and purposeful in how they deploy their picks? Which rookies are being asked to do too much too soon? Those answers shape how front offices think about their rosters heading into training camp.

For Dybantsa, Boozer, Peterson, and the rest of the class, Summer League is the first chapter of a professional story that will be told for years. The swagger, the hype, the draft boards — they all give way to one thing starting July 3: can you play? Las Vegas is about to find out.
