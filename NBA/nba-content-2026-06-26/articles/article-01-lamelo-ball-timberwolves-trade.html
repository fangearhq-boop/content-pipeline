LaMelo Ball Traded to Timberwolves: Anthony Edwards Finally Has His Running Mate

By Jake Torres

June 26, 2026

Hoop Heroes

The move everyone in Minnesota had been waiting for finally happened. The Charlotte Hornets traded LaMelo Ball and Josh Green to the Minnesota Timberwolves on Thursday, delivering Anthony Edwards the co-star he has needed since his rookie year. For a franchise that has spent years searching for a second pillar alongside its franchise cornerstone, the deal represents the biggest swing of the Tim Connelly era.

The Timberwolves sent Naz Reid, an unprotected 2033 first-round pick, pick swaps in 2028, 2029, and 2030, and three second-round picks (2029, 2032, 2033) back to Charlotte to complete the transaction. It is a steep price — Reid had been one of the most dependable bench bigs in the Western Conference — but Minnesota decided the moment called for boldness.

The timing makes sense. Donte DiVincenzo, who had become a key piece of the Wolves' backcourt rotation, ruptured his Achilles in the playoffs this spring, leaving a glaring hole at the point guard position heading into next season. Rather than patch the wound with a stopgap signing, Connelly and the Timberwolves front office swung for the franchise-altering move.

What the Deal Means for Minnesota

Two first-overall picks from the same draft class rarely end up on the same team. Edwards went No. 1 in 2020; Ball went No. 3 in that same class. Now they share a locker room. The Ringer put it plainly after the trade broke: "The Wolves Bet the Anthony Edwards Era on the LaMelo Trade." That framing is accurate.

Ball arrives with plenty of evidence that he can handle the weight of that bet. In 72 regular-season games during the 2025-26 campaign, he averaged 20.1 points, 7.1 assists, and 4.8 rebounds per game while shooting 36.8 percent from three. His vision and passing creativity are precisely what Edwards' game has been missing alongside him. Edwards generates his best offense in transition and in isolation; Ball is one of the best in the league at pushing pace and finding cutters in the open floor.

Ball has already signaled his commitment to the new chapter. He intends to sign a five-year, $112 million extension with Minnesota, locking him in for the foreseeable future. For a fanbase that has suffered through years of close-but-not-quite playoff runs, the combination of Edwards and Ball represents the clearest path to a legitimate championship contender the franchise has seen in decades.

Connelly, the Wolves' president, has spoken publicly about his determination to build a legitimate co-star pairing around Edwards. Thursday's trade is the answer to that years-long search.

What Charlotte Gets

For the Hornets, this deal marks the beginning of a deliberate retooling. Ball was a former No. 3 overall pick who never quite fit Charlotte's trajectory as a team or market, and trading him now — while his value remains high after a strong statistical season — allows the organization to collect meaningful future capital and reset expectations.

Reid gives Charlotte an immediate starting-caliber center, a player who can anchor a young roster while the Hornets figure out their next direction. The haul of future picks, including an unprotected first-rounder in 2033 and multiple pick swaps, provides flexibility for years to come. Charlotte is not rebuilding in secret — this is an open acknowledgment that the franchise needs a new identity, and trading its most recognizable player is step one of finding it.

Green, who came over alongside Ball, adds another piece for Charlotte to evaluate. He is athletic and defends at a high level, the kind of versatile wing that rebuilding teams often covet as they develop their core.

What's Next

Ball will join the Timberwolves just as the league calendar picks up speed. NBA Summer League tips off in Las Vegas on July 3 and runs through July 19, giving Minnesota's youngest players a stage before the real business begins. Signings from the free agency negotiating period — which opens June 30 at 6 PM ET — can be made official starting July 6, meaning the Wolves will be simultaneously managing Ball's extension paperwork alongside whatever additional moves Connelly has in mind to round out the roster.

The big question surrounding Minnesota heading into next season is fit. How do Edwards and Ball share the ball on a night-to-night basis? How does head coach Chris Finch structure the offense to take advantage of two elite playmakers who both need the rock in their hands? Those are questions that training camp and preseason will begin to answer.

For now, the Timberwolves have made their declaration. The Anthony Edwards era just got a whole lot more interesting.
