Four Days Until Free Agency: KAT's Extension, LeBron's Decision, and the Stars Still Available

By Marcus Cole

June 26, 2026

Hoop Heroes

The negotiating period opens June 30 at 6 PM ET, and every front office in the league is already running the numbers. Actual contracts cannot be signed until July 6, but the next four days represent the quiet before the storm — a stretch where relationship calls get made, offers get floated, and the league's biggest decisions begin to take shape in private. By the time the calendar flips to July, the landscape of the NBA could look entirely different.

This offseason carries an unusual amount of weight. Extensions for foundational players are still unresolved. And at least one player at the tail end of one of the sport's all-time careers faces a decision the entire league is watching. Four days is a long time when the stakes are this high.

The Extensions That Define the Offseason

No extension situation looms larger than Karl-Anthony Towns and the New York Knicks. KAT is eligible for a four-year, $272 million deal or a three-year, $208 million extension, and everything about his public posture suggests he wants to stay in New York. "I'm ready to make a financial sacrifice," he said earlier this month — a remarkable statement from a player who could command maximum money on the open market.

The sacrifice framing is not accidental. Jalen Brunson set the template. Brunson took $113 million less than the maximum to re-sign with the Knicks, and that decision paid off in ways that transcended money: New York won the championship this spring, and Brunson took home Finals MVP. The Knicks have built a culture where stars are willing to leave money on the table for the chance to compete, and Towns is signaling he belongs to that same school of thought.

The complication is the roster math. Owner James Dolan has been explicit about his view of the second apron — he called it "suicidal" for a franchise's long-term competitive health. With the Knicks currently sitting $3.5 million below the first apron, the length and structure of a Towns extension carries enormous implications for what the team can do around him. A shorter deal at a lower number gives the front office more room to maneuver. A max-length deal at full value could box them in. The next four days will tell us how those competing priorities get resolved.

The UFAs That Will Move the Market

Beyond extensions, the unrestricted free agent pool includes names that can genuinely change the direction of franchises.

James Harden enters the market at 34 years old coming off what sources around the league describe as a strong bounce-back season. His resume still commands attention, and multiple teams have expressed interest. The question with Harden has always been fit — he needs the ball and the offense built around him — but for contenders with a specific hole at the two-guard or point-forward spot, he remains a legitimate target.

Trae Young is perhaps the most polarizing name on the board. Young wants big money and believes he has earned it. The Atlanta Hawks, who hold his Bird rights, face a genuine decision about whether his value to a rebuilding franchise justifies the kind of contract he is seeking. If Atlanta balks, Young will have no shortage of suitors, and the market he creates for himself in those first hours of the negotiating period will set the tone for the entire free agency window.

Then there is LeBron James. At 41, one of the greatest players in the history of the sport has not yet announced his intentions for next season. His decision — whether to return, whether to change teams, or finally call it a career — stands as the offseason's single most anticipated piece of news. Every team with cap space is quietly accounting for the possibility that LeBron becomes available.

What's Next

The next seventy-two hours will be filled with background noise — unnamed sources, reported interest, cap sheet breakdowns posted on social media at midnight. Most of it will be positioning. The real decisions happen in conversations that never become public until a deal is done.

NBA Summer League opens in Las Vegas on July 3, right as the negotiating period kicks off. Signings become official July 6, meaning the first genuine announcements are still ten days away.

In the meantime, watch KAT. Watch Harden. Watch Trae Young's Instagram. And watch whatever LeBron does or does not say over the weekend. This is the quiet before the storm, and the storm is going to be loud.
