KAT's Turn: How the Knicks' Dynasty Hinges on One Financial Decision

By Jake Torres

June 26, 2026

Hoop Heroes

It started with Jalen Brunson. Four years, $156.5 million. Not the $269 million max he could have waited for. Not the figure his talent commanded on an open market. Just a team-friendly deal from a franchise player who understood exactly what was at stake. "I don't really care about the money," Brunson said at the time, a line that drew some skepticism and a lot of admiration. This past June, after he delivered 32.6 points and 4.6 assists per game in the Finals to lead New York to its first championship in 53 years and walked off the court as Finals MVP, nobody was questioning the decision anymore.

Now it is Karl-Anthony Towns' turn to answer the same question.

With free agency opening June 30 and the extension negotiating window beginning the same day, the Knicks and KAT will soon sit down to discuss the shape of New York's next chapter. He can sign a four-year extension worth $272 million — the maximum available to him — or a three-year deal at $208 million. Both are life-altering money. Only one of them gives the Knicks room to keep their championship core together.

Towns has already signaled where his head is. "I'm ready to make a financial sacrifice," he said recently. The question is how big that sacrifice actually needs to be, and whether the math works out on both sides.

What the Numbers Look Like

The Knicks sit roughly $3.5 million below the first apron. That margin tightens fast depending on what happens in free agency. Mitchell Robinson is an unrestricted free agent, and his decision matters enormously — if Robinson returns on a team-friendly deal, some flexibility is preserved; if he gets paid elsewhere, the replacement cost eats into the room KAT's discount is meant to create.

The second apron is the line New York cannot cross. Owner James Dolan has been direct: "There's certain things in the NBA that you'd have to be suicidal to do. One of them is the second apron." Its restrictions — no traded player exceptions, no MLE — would effectively freeze the roster and prevent the in-season flexibility that contenders depend on.

OG Anunoby is locked at $212.5 million over five years through 2028-29. Brunson's deal runs out. The bones of the championship team are in place. What happens with KAT determines whether there is enough around them to repeat.

Why It's Harder for KAT Than It Was for Brunson

Brunson's sacrifice came before the championship, before the Finals MVP, before the validation. He bet on himself and the organization before the evidence was complete. That bet paid off in the form of a trophy and a legacy.

KAT faces similar pressure under different conditions. He is one of the best offensive bigs in the league and already gave something by uprooting from Minnesota. Now the organization is asking for more. Brunson's team-friendly deal set a cultural expectation in the Knicks locker room: stars here prioritize winning. That norm carries weight, but it also carries pressure when it is your turn at the table.

A discount of roughly $50 million — similar to what Brunson left on the table — would give the Knicks room to work. A smaller discount buys goodwill without fully solving the puzzle. No discount at all likely means difficult decisions about who else stays.

What's Next

Free agency opens June 30, and the Robinson decision will likely move quickly one way or another. His market will be active, and the Knicks will need to make an early call on whether matching an outside offer makes cap sense.

The KAT extension window opens the same day, but there is no requirement to move fast. These conversations can run through the summer and into training camp. Expect both sides to use the time. The Knicks will want to see how the rest of free agency shapes up before locking in the number; KAT's camp will want to understand exactly what the roster looks like around him before agreeing to a discount.

What is not in doubt is the goal. New York just ended a 53-year championship drought. The city is not interested in a one-and-done. Whether the Knicks become a dynasty depends on decisions made in offices and at negotiating tables over the next few weeks. KAT has a chance to be the next piece of that legacy. Brunson showed him how it's done.
