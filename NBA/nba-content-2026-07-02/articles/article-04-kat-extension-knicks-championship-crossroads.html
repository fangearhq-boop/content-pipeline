KAT Extension Watch: Can the Knicks Keep Their Championship Core Together?

By Damon Pierce | July 2, 2026 | Hoop Heroes

The New York Knicks won the NBA championship. They're the best team in basketball. And now comes the part nobody warned them about — figuring out how to pay everyone.

Karl-Anthony Towns is the key piece in a complex offseason puzzle. The Knicks center is owed $57 million in 2026-27 and holds a $61 million player option for 2027-28. He is also eligible for a new four-year, $272 million extension — if he declines that option — or a three-year, $208 million deal if he exercises it. But owner James Dolan has made the team's position crystal clear: the Knicks will not operate in the second apron under any circumstances.

That means KAT faces a choice: take a haircut of roughly $7 to $10 million per year below his maximum value, or risk a messy standoff that fractures the championship roster before the defense begins.

The Brunson Blueprint

Jalen Brunson already showed the way. The Knicks' Finals MVP famously accepted approximately $113 million less than his market value when he signed his extension, betting on team success over individual salary maximization. That bet paid off spectacularly: Brunson became a champion and earned the Finals MVP trophy.

KAT has publicly gestured in the same direction. "Ready to make a financial sacrifice" were his words earlier this offseason — a carefully chosen phrase that acknowledged what would need to happen. The question now is whether those words translate into a signed contract.

Josh Hart is in a similar position. The heart-and-hustle wing is also extension-eligible and expected to take a below-market deal to stay in New York. If both KAT and Hart follow the Brunson Blueprint, the Knicks have a real chance at running back their championship team with their core intact.

The Second Apron Problem

The NBA's second apron — a luxury tax threshold that triggers severe roster construction restrictions — is the villain of this story. Teams over the second apron cannot aggregate contracts in trades, cannot use the Mid-Level Exception, and face other significant penalties. Dolan's refusal to cross it isn't mere cost-cutting; it's a strategic decision about competitive flexibility.

OG Anunoby and Mikal Bridges are also locked up on significant contracts. The Knicks' payroll is already substantial, and fitting KAT's full maximum value on top would send them deep into apron territory for years. Something has to give, and that something is Towns' asking price.

KAT's Leverage — and His History

Towns won the championship. He earned every dollar he's being asked to leave on the table. A different player, on a different team, might test the open market and see what other franchises would offer. But KAT has found his home in New York. He has spoken repeatedly about his love for the city, the fans, and the Knicks organization.

No other team can offer him what the Knicks can: a championship environment, a city that adores him, and teammates who are already proven winners. The money left on the table is real. The value of what he'd be keeping is harder to quantify but arguably just as large.

What's Next

No deal has been signed, and negotiations have their own timeline. The Knicks have until July 6 and beyond to work through the details. Both sides appear motivated to find a solution. But motivation and finalized paperwork are different things, and the gap between KAT's market value and Dolan's ceiling is real.

The next few weeks will define whether the 2026 Knicks are a one-year wonder or the start of something the Garden hasn't seen in decades. The ball is in KAT's court.
