Knicks Parade Day: The Canyon of Heroes Gets Its First New York Basketball Celebration

By Damon Pierce | Hoop Heroes | June 18, 2026

The parade is here. After 53 years, after two championship teams that never quite got this moment, the New York Knicks are marching through the Canyon of Heroes on Thursday morning — and every step down Broadway is a first.

The 2026 championship parade begins at 10:00 AM ET from Battery Park, traveling north along Broadway through the Canyon of Heroes and ending at City Hall Plaza. Mayor Zohran Mamdani will present the team with the Key to the City at the ceremony, with Knicks legends Walt "Clyde" Frazier and Patrick Ewing among the participants.

The number that tells you everything about what this moment means to this city: 347,000 people applied for 600 available lottery seats at the City Hall ceremony. That's more than one in 24 New Yorkers who tried to be in that specific room for this specific moment.

A Franchise First — Yes, Really

Here is the fact that has surprised even longtime Knicks fans: Thursday is the first Canyon of Heroes ticker-tape parade in the history of the New York Knicks franchise.

The Knicks won NBA championships in 1970 and 1973. Those were genuine title runs — the Willis Reed tunnel walk in Game 7, the Frazier-led dynasty, the city going wild in both instances. But neither championship was celebrated with an official Canyon of Heroes ticker-tape parade through Lower Manhattan. The city celebrated, but not in this way.

The Canyon of Heroes has welcomed the 1969 Mets, multiple Yankees championships, the 2008 Giants, the 2012 Giants, the 1994 Rangers, the 1986 Mets, and dozens of other champions across more than a century. Today, for the first time, the Knicks are getting their own plaque on Broadway.

The Man at the Center

Jalen Brunson earned the Bill Russell Trophy as Finals MVP in unanimous fashion. His 45-point Game 5 performance at Frost Bank Center in San Antonio — delivering 13 consecutive points in the fourth quarter when the Knicks needed it most — clinched the franchise's third NBA title and first since 1973.

Brunson averaged 32.6 points, 4.6 assists, and 4.2 rebounds across the 2026 Finals. His path to this parade, however, started long before June 14. When Brunson restructured his contract before the 2023-24 season, leaving approximately $113 million on the table to give the Knicks cap flexibility, he made a bet on himself and on New York. Thursday is that bet paying off in full.

Karl-Anthony Towns, OG Anunoby, Josh Hart, and the supporting cast earned their place on those floats as well. But Brunson is the reason those floats exist.

A 40% Chance of Rain, and Nobody Cares

The forecast for Thursday in Manhattan shows a 40% chance of showers with temperatures in the low-to-mid 70s. The entire Broadway corridor from Battery Park to City Hall is expected to be effectively inaccessible by the time the parade begins, with NYPD coordinating one of the largest crowd management operations in recent city history.

For those watching from home, local broadcasts and national outlets have full coverage beginning at 9:00 AM ET. The City Hall ceremony is expected to air live as well.

City Hall and municipal buildings across New York City will be lit in Knicks blue and orange Thursday night — the same lights that were turned on after the championship clincher on June 14, now burning for the celebration that follows.

What's Next

After the confetti settles, the Knicks front office turns its attention immediately to one of the most consequential offseason periods in recent memory. The Giannis Antetokounmpo trade market has a June 23 deadline — just five days from Thursday. The NBA Draft follows on June 23-24. Free agency opens June 30, with Austin Reaves and others among the significant names in play.

But Thursday is not for business. Thursday is for Broadway. For Walt Frazier and Patrick Ewing watching the next generation get what the legends of 1970 and 1973 never had. For the 347,000 people who tried to get 600 seats and the hundreds of thousands more who will line the streets of Lower Manhattan regardless.

The Canyon of Heroes finally has its first New York Knicks parade. Fifty-three years is a long time to wait.
