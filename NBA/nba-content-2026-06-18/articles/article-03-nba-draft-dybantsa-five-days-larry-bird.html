NBA Draft 5 Days Away: Dybantsa's Larry Bird Numbers Make Him the Odds-On No. 1

By Marcus Cole | Hoop Heroes | June 18, 2026

Five days from now, the 2026 NBA Draft begins at Barclays Center in Brooklyn. The Washington Wizards hold the No. 1 pick. AJ Dybantsa is the heavy favorite to hear his name called first. And the comparisons being drawn to previous top picks have landed in a very specific place.

Larry Bird. Indiana State. 1978-79.

Dybantsa averaged 25.5 points, 6.8 rebounds, and 3.7 assists per game at BYU while shooting 51.0 percent from the field and earning consensus All-American honors. According to research circulating in draft circles, he is the first player since Bird to put up those statistical marks and receive consensus All-American recognition in a single college season.

The Case for No. 1

At 6-foot-9, Dybantsa is the kind of prospect teams build franchises around. His three-level scoring ability — the efficiency at the rim, the pull-up mid-range game, the willingness to initiate from the perimeter — reads as polished beyond a freshman year. The 51.0 percent field goal shooting on high volume is the number that most impresses scouts. It suggests his shot selection, athleticism, and finishing package are all above standard.

The free throw percentage — 77.4 percent — is a minor question mark for a player who projects as a primary scorer, but at 18, there is significant room for development.

Washington has narrowed its focus to Dybantsa and Darryn Peterson. Both players made visits — Dybantsa to Washington and Utah, Peterson to Washington only. The Jazz hold the No. 2 pick, giving them a natural landing spot if Peterson falls from No. 1.

Peterson's Push

Darryn Peterson is not going quietly. Multiple reports indicate the competition between the two prospects for No. 1 is genuine, even if Dybantsa holds the -450 odds edge.

Peterson's athleticism and defensive versatility have drawn strong marks from teams. His offensive game is further along in some evaluators' opinions on shot creation in isolation. The concern — shared by some front offices — is that his efficiency numbers in college didn't match Dybantsa's production level.

If Peterson does not go No. 1 to Washington, the Utah Jazz at No. 2 represent a near-certain soft landing. Utah has the roster context to develop either player, and Peterson's style of play may fit their structure slightly better.

The Wizards' Situation

Washington enters the draft with a compelling pitch for any No. 1 prospect: Trae Young and Anthony Davis are already on the roster, giving Dybantsa or Peterson immediate All-Star-caliber teammates and a franchise that has explicitly committed to winning as quickly as possible.

The Wizards made a "playoff promise" in pre-draft meetings, per reports — a message that this organization intends to compete, not just develop. For a prospect who has talked openly about his admiration for players who won early in their careers, the pitch may resonate.

What's Next

Draft night is June 23-24 at Barclays Center in Brooklyn, beginning at 8:00 PM ET on ABC/ESPN. The Green Room guests — Dybantsa, Peterson, and Cooper Flagg's successor class — have been confirmed.

If Dybantsa goes No. 1 as expected, the conversation immediately turns to how quickly Washington can build around him. If there's a surprise and Peterson goes first, the storylines for both players' rookie seasons become even more compelling.

Five days. One pick. The No. 1 conversation ends soon.
