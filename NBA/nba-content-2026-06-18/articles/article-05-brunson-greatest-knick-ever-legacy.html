On Parade Day, the Greatest Knick Ever Debate Has a Clear Answer

By Jake Torres | Hoop Heroes | June 18, 2026

Parade day is the right day for this conversation. While Jalen Brunson rides down Broadway through the Canyon of Heroes — the first such parade in Knicks franchise history — the question that has been building since Game 5 deserves an honest answer.

Is Jalen Brunson the greatest New York Knick of all time?

Yes. And the case is stronger than you might expect.

The Standard to Beat

Before the argument, acknowledge what came before. Walt "Clyde" Frazier remains one of the most stylish, complete guards in NBA history. His 1970 Game 7 performance — 36 points and 19 assists against the Los Angeles Lakers — is one of the great individual playoff games ever played. He won two championships in New York, is beloved by generations of fans, and remains an ambassador for the franchise more than 50 years later.

Willis Reed walked through the tunnel on two separate Finals occasions. The 1970 moment, when Reed emerged injured from the locker room before Game 7 and inspired a Knicks blowout, is arguably the most iconic single image in franchise history.

These are not small legacies to eclipse.

What Brunson Did

And yet. Jalen Brunson did something neither Frazier nor Reed ever did: he delivered a championship as the unambiguous No. 1 option on the team.

Frazier was the system's engine, but the Knicks of 1970 and 1973 were genuine teams where no single player carried the full weight. Reed was the symbolic center of gravity and an elite center, but Frazier's playmaking defined those teams just as much.

Brunson carried this team. He averaged 32.6 points, 4.6 assists, and 4.2 rebounds in the 2026 Finals against San Antonio. His 45-point Game 5 — including 13 consecutive points in the fourth quarter when the Knicks needed every one of them — is the highest-scoring individual performance in Knicks Finals history. He won the Bill Russell Trophy unanimously.

And he did it while having previously left approximately $113 million on the table in a contract restructure specifically to help the Knicks build a championship roster. That is an act of self-sacrifice that most NBA stars — fairly paid at their market rate — never face as a decision. Brunson faced it, made the choice, and then delivered the championship that justified it.

The Counterargument, Addressed

The standard counterargument for any "greatest ever" claim about a current player is era. Different competition. Longer seasons. More available talent everywhere on the roster.

Fair point. The 1970 Knicks played in a 14-team league against a narrower talent pool. Direct statistical comparisons across eras are genuinely problematic.

But era-adjusted arguments ultimately concede the ground. The question is not who would beat whom in a hypothetical matchup. It is who had the most significant impact on the franchise in the context of their time. Brunson ended a 53-year title drought. He did it as the alpha. He did it with a pay cut that directly enabled the roster construction around him.

The Knicks of 1970 and 1973 were great teams. They also never got a Canyon of Heroes parade. The symbolic gap in what those two titles received versus what Brunson's title is receiving today reflects something real about what the city waited for.

What's Next

Frazier, who is participating in Thursday's parade, will likely always occupy a singular place in Knicks lore — the icon who defined the franchise's only previous championship era. That is not diminished by what Brunson accomplished.

But when this organization's history is written, the 53-year drought and the player who ended it will be the defining arc. Brunson's chapter is the one that closes a half-century of waiting. That belongs at the top.

Today he rides down Broadway. Today New York gives him its first Canyon of Heroes parade. The greatest Knick ever debate is not closed because the argument is finished — it is closed because the evidence is in.
