KAT's $272M Decision and Dolan's Hard Line: The Knicks' Championship Is Already Under Threat

By Damon Pierce June 25, 2026

The Morning After the Parade

The confetti has barely settled and the Knicks already face the hardest summer of the James Dolan era. On June 11, New York won its first NBA championship since 1973. On June 30, free agency opens — and with it comes a series of financial decisions that will determine whether the Knicks can defend their title or begin an unraveling they cannot control.

At the center of it: Karl-Anthony Towns. The center who anchored the Knicks' championship run is eligible for either a 4-year, $272 million extension or a 3-year, $208 million deal. He has said publicly that he is willing to take a financial sacrifice to keep the team together. The question is how big that sacrifice needs to be.

The Math That Makes Everything Hard

James Dolan, the Knicks owner, has drawn a public line. On WFAN's The Carton Show, he said: "There's certain things in the NBA that you'd have to be suicidal to do. One of them is the second apron. Cannot go into the second apron." The Knicks currently sit approximately $13 to $16 million below that threshold.

The numbers on the existing core make the math brutal. Jalen Brunson — the Finals MVP who averaged 32.6 points per game in the series and scored 45 in the clinching game — signed for $156.5 million, taking roughly $113 million less than the maximum he could have eventually commanded. OG Anunoby is locked in at $212.5 million over five years through 2028-29. Mikal Bridges is under contract at $150 million over four years. Josh Hart is under contract.

The Brunson sacrifice created room for the Knicks to operate. Towns has watched all of this. He watched Brunson take less, win a championship, win Finals MVP, and build a legacy in New York that will outlast any contract. Now the Knicks need Towns to look at that blueprint and decide he wants to replicate it.

What KAT Actually Said

"I'm ready to make a financial sacrifice," Towns said publicly heading into the summer. The words are right. The question is how many digits that sacrifice involves. A $10 million discount is a gesture. A $100 million discount is a choice that defines a career. Somewhere between those numbers is the deal that either keeps the Knicks together or fractures them.

The outstanding decisions before June 30 extend beyond Towns. Mitchell Robinson, the center who provided interior depth throughout the championship run, is an unrestricted free agent. Per Hoops Rumors, he is "very open" to re-signing — but bringing him back risks pushing New York closer to the second apron Dolan has explicitly refused to breach. Guard decisions around Landry Shamet, Jose Alvarado, and Jordan Clarkson also remain pending.

What's Next

Free agency opens June 30, and the clock starts immediately. The Knicks enter that date with a clear priority list: lock in KAT at a number that keeps them under the second apron, make a decision on Robinson, and address the guard depth that helped carry them through a playoff run. Brunson set the tone in 2024 when he sacrificed. He won a championship. He earned Finals MVP. He has a Hall of Fame legacy building in New York. Whether Towns follows that example — and whether the number he accepts is one the Knicks can actually make work within Dolan's constraints — defines whether this championship is the beginning of a dynasty or the high-water mark of a one-year run.
