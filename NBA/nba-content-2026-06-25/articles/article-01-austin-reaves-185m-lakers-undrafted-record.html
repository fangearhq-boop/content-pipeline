Austin Reaves Signs $185M Max Deal: The Richest Contract Ever for an Undrafted Player

By Marcus Cole June 25, 2026

A Record That Redefines the Undrafted Journey

Austin Reaves was on a golf course when it happened. The deal was done. He fell to the ground on the grass beside a fairway bunker, hands on his head, and his girlfriend posted the moment on Instagram with a simple caption: "Behind the scenes." That image — the most coveted free agent guard of this offseason in full emotional collapse on a manicured fairway — encapsulates what this moment means.

On June 24, 2026, Reaves agreed to a 4-year, $185 million maximum contract to remain with the Los Angeles Lakers, declining his $14.9 million player option to sign the deal that makes him the highest-paid undrafted player in NBA history. The contract breaks the prior record that had stood for three years. The year-by-year breakdown: $41.3 million in 2026-27, $44.6 million in 2027-28, $47.9 million in 2028-29, and a player option worth $51.2 million in 2029-30.

What the Deal Means in Context

Reaves went undrafted out of Oklahoma in 2021. Sixty picks came and went. No one called his name. The Lakers signed him as an undrafted free agent. Five years later, he is the richest of his kind in league history.

The Brooklyn Nets were prepared to offer him $178.5 million over four years. The Detroit Pistons, per reports from Silver Screen and Roll, were "planning a real run" at Reaves in free agency — they had been maneuvering cap space specifically with him in mind. The Lakers accelerated their timeline when they got wind of the Pistons' seriousness. The result was a deal agreed to before free agency officially opened on June 30.

Reaves averaged 23.3 points, 5.5 assists, and 4.7 rebounds per game in 2025-26 on 49% shooting in 51 games. He was, by any measurement, one of the most productive shooting guards in the league last season.

The Human Side: 'A Couple of Times' He Thought He'd Leave

In an interview with The Athletic's Dan Woike, Reaves opened up about the tension of the negotiation in a way that surprised people who assumed this was always a foregone conclusion. When asked if he ever thought he might not remain a Laker, he answered: "A couple of times actually."

"My heart's been in LA," he said. "Every morning I wake up, I'm just like, 'I hope we can get this done today.' And even if I was frustrated through the process when we weren't getting the numbers we wanted, the next morning I woke up, it was still the same thing: 'Hopefully we can get this done today.'"

Reaves described the relief when the deal closed as "a breath of fresh air." He added he had not stopped thinking about where he was going to be. "It's just like a sigh of relief," he said.

Lakers president Rob Pelinka framed it the way the organization felt about the outcome: "He started his journey here as a Laker, and has made it very clear to us that he wants his journey to continue as a Laker. And we feel the same way."

What's Next

With Reaves locked in on a max deal through 2028-29 (with an option for 2029-30), the Lakers head into June 30 free agency with their most important piece secured. The Detroit Pistons, who came up empty in their pursuit, have reportedly pivoted to other guard targets. For Reaves, the work now shifts from negotiations to basketball: the 2026-27 Lakers season begins in October, and he will enter it as a franchise cornerstone under the largest undrafted contract in the history of the sport.
