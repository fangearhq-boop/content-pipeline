Cade Cunningham's Breakout Playoff Run Has Redefined Detroit's Future

By Marcus Cole | Hoop Heroes | May 15, 2026

Before the 2026 NBA playoffs began, the conversation about the Detroit Pistons was cautiously optimistic at best. A young team, a good-not-great regular season, a first-round matchup against an established Cleveland Cavaliers team — expectations were measured. Get some experience. Learn from it. Come back better next year.

Then Cade Cunningham happened.

Through five games of an Eastern Conference second-round series, Cunningham has delivered performances that belong in discussions about the best individual playoff showings of the 2026 postseason. His 39-point, 9-assist effort in Game 5 — in a road overtime game, while trailing for stretches, against a team with James Harden and Donovan Mitchell — was as complete a performance as anyone in the playoffs has managed.

Taking the Series Over

The Pistons jumped out to a 2-0 series lead by winning both of their first two games on the road in Cleveland. That alone was a statement. Detroit built leads in Games 3 and 4 at home before Cleveland's experience and depth took over, but the Pistons never folded. They competed in every quarter of every game.

And it was Cunningham leading the way throughout. He has been the primary ball-handler, the primary scorer, and the primary decision-maker — carrying the load that experienced veterans typically handle in playoff situations. At 26 years old, he is doing it for the first time on this stage, and it looks entirely natural.

His three-quarter court vision, the ability to pull up from mid-range at the end of shot clocks, and his free throw drawing — Cade posted that 11-of-14 FT performance himself in the series, showing the complete scorer's toolkit — marks him as a player who has figured out how to hurt you in multiple ways in multiple situations.

The No-Call and the Resilience

The Game 5 controversy — Ausar Thompson appearing to be tripped by Jarrett Allen as the clock expired, with no foul called — gave Detroit legitimate reason for grievance. Cunningham's postgame comments were direct without being inflammatory. He saw what happened, said so clearly, and then made sure the entire basketball world noticed that his team belongs among the elite.

That's a franchise player's response. Not a whine. A declaration.

The Pistons bench and supporting cast have also contributed. Ausar Thompson brings athletic versatility. Jalen Duren provides interior physicality. The youth of this core — Cunningham at 26, Thompson at 22, Duren at 21 — means this group has years of playoff experience ahead of them. Tonight's potential elimination game, as painful as it might be if Detroit loses, is simply part of the education.

Whatever Happens Tonight

If the Pistons win Game 6 tonight and force a decisive Game 7 in Detroit, it will be remembered as one of the best second-round series in recent memory. If Cleveland closes it out, Detroit's 2026 run will still be remembered as the first real sign that this franchise's rebuild has produced something genuine and lasting.

Cade Cunningham came into this series as a highly regarded young player. He leaves it — whenever the series ends — as a certified playoff performer. That distinction matters in this league. Teams around the league now know what they're dealing with when they face Detroit.

The star has arrived. The Pistons' best years are still ahead.

What's Next

Game 6 tips off tonight at 7:00 PM ET at Rocket Mortgage FieldHouse in Cleveland, broadcast on Prime Video. A Pistons win extends the series to Game 7 in Detroit. A Cavaliers win eliminates Detroit and sets up an ECF matchup with the New York Knicks. Either way, Cade Cunningham's 2026 postseason has already told us everything we need to know about what the Detroit Pistons will look like in the years to come.
