Lendeborg Makes History: Only Second Player Ever to Win NCAA Title and Summer League Championship in Same Year

By Marcus Cole

July 21, 2026

When Yaxel Lendeborg walked off the court at Thomas & Mack Center on Sunday night, he had just added his name to a list that only one other player had ever appeared on.

By rallying the Golden State Warriors from 12 points down in the fourth quarter to a 94-90 victory over the Memphis Grizzlies, Lendeborg became only the second player in NBA history to win both an NCAA national championship and an NBA Summer League championship in the same calendar year. The only other player to accomplish it: Davion Mitchell, who won the national title with Baylor in 2021 before leading the Sacramento Kings to the Summer League crown that same summer.

That is the company Lendeborg now keeps — before he has played a single regular-season NBA minute.

A Championship Defined by the Fourth Quarter

The game itself was a showcase for what the Warriors believe they drafted when they selected Lendeborg at No. 11 overall in June. Memphis led by 12 entering the final period, and everything pointed toward a Grizzlies championship. Then Lendeborg took over.

He finished the championship game with 21 points, 10 rebounds, 2 assists, and 2 steals in just 23 minutes of action. The Championship MVP award was unanimous. Off the bench, Deivon Smith matched Lendeborg's 21-point output on 9-for-14 shooting, while LJ Cryer added 15 points and four three-pointers as the Warriors mounted a 28-12 fourth-quarter surge to close the game.

It was Golden State's first Summer League title since 2013. They won it in the most Golden State way possible: through a depth-driven, fourth-quarter run when the game was on the line.

Context: The Davion Mitchell Comparison

Mitchell's 2021 double is the benchmark. He won the NCAA title with Baylor in the spring, was selected by Sacramento in the draft, and then won Summer League MVP before going on to become one of the league's elite defensive guards in his first several seasons. The Kings, long rebuilding, used Mitchell as a cornerstone of their rise.

The parallels to Lendeborg's situation are notable. Golden State, like Sacramento five years ago, is in a state of transition — experienced stars aging, franchise identity in flux, free agency uncertainty swirling around LeBron James and the league's broader landscape. And now they have a No. 11 pick who just showed he can handle the biggest moments in the biggest settings.

The skeptics who questioned the pick at No. 11 — draft analysts who had Lendeborg outside the top 15 — are now watching him claim a championship trophy and a historic footnote in the same weekend.

More Than the Numbers

Lendeborg's Summer League production went beyond the stat line. His two steals alongside the 21-point, 10-rebound output reflected the two-way impact the Warriors prize in their big men. His 23 minutes of action demonstrated a capacity to stay on the floor in big moments — no foul trouble, no defensive breakdowns in the crucial fourth quarter when Memphis needed stops to protect its lead.

The Warriors have consistently turned first-round picks into rotation contributors. Draymond Green was a second-round pick who became a Defensive Player of the Year. The organization's ability to identify and develop the right kinds of players is as much its identity as the Splash Brothers era itself.

If the pattern holds, Lendeborg may be exactly the kind of player that turns a rebuilding team into a contending one — not through individual star power, but through the kind of winning basketball that brings championships.

What's Next

Lendeborg heads into his first NBA training camp with a Summer League MVP, a championship, and a historic footnote already secured. The Warriors will now look to build their regular-season roster around his development — and potentially around LeBron James, whose decision is expected any day.

For now, the Summer League trophy sits with Golden State. And Yaxel Lendeborg's name sits alongside Davion Mitchell's in the record books. That is not a bad place to start a career.
