KAT Extension Talks Stalled — Defending Champion Knicks Face Brutal Second Apron Math

By Marcus Cole | July 12, 2026

NBA Offseason

The New York Knicks won the 2026 NBA championship. Now comes the hard part.

Karl-Anthony Towns is owed $57 million in 2026-27, with a $61 million player option for 2027-28. He is extension-eligible this summer, and the Knicks are facing a decision that carries enormous consequences for the franchise's ability to defend its title: commit to a long-term deal under complicated financial constraints, or navigate the uncertainty of Towns reaching the open market.

The problem is the second apron. Under current collective bargaining rules, teams spending at the luxury tax level New York occupies face significant restrictions on trades, free agent signings, and the use of certain roster-building tools. A full KAT extension at market value essentially locks the Knicks into those constraints for years — with limited ability to adjust around the star player they're paying.

The Extension Math

Towns has two extension options available to him. If he declines his 2027-28 player option, he becomes eligible for a four-year, $272 million deal — a massive commitment that would represent one of the largest contracts in NBA history. If he exercises the option, his path leads to a three-year, $208 million extension.

Either number creates complexity for the Knicks under owner James Dolan. The second apron limits what teams can receive in trades, restricts sign-and-trade transactions, and caps the dollar amount teams can add via the mid-level exception. For a franchise that might need to maneuver to keep the championship window open, those restrictions matter enormously.

The precedent that looms over all of this is Jalen Brunson's sacrifice. Brunson took roughly $113 million less than his market value on his most recent contract extension specifically to give the Knicks roster flexibility. He then won Finals MVP and the organization's first championship in over five decades. The ask implicit in the KAT negotiation is whether Towns will make a similar kind of commitment.

Hart Also in Play

Josh Hart faces an analogous situation on a smaller scale. Hart is also extension-eligible this summer, and the Knicks need to make decisions on both players with the same second apron math in the background.

Hart's role on the championship team was essential — his energy, defense, and late-game composure were part of what made New York's run possible. Losing him to a more financially flexible team would represent a meaningful roster subtraction going into the 2026-27 season.

The Knicks have already added some depth — Jordan Clarkson re-signed as a backup guard, and Landry Shamet (four years, $24 million) and Jose Alvarado (three years, $14 million) also returned. But the core question is whether Towns and Hart both remain on the roster when the season opens.

A Championship Window With an Expiration Date

Championship windows in the modern NBA are shorter than fans typically anticipate. Rosters age, contracts expire, and the league's draft structure means young talent continuously refreshes competing teams. The Knicks have a genuine window right now — the question is how many more years it stays open.

Committing to a full KAT extension at market value suggests the Knicks are prioritizing roster stability over flexibility. Asking for a Brunson-style discount suggests they want both — but that ask requires a level of sacrifice that not every star player is willing to make.

The resolution of the KAT extension talks will say a lot about the Knicks' championship ambitions beyond 2027. A signed and locked-in Towns means New York is betting on continuity. A Towns departure would force a rebuild conversation no franchise wants to have the summer after winning it all.

What's Next

Extension talks tend to intensify as training camp approaches in September. Neither side has public leverage over the other right now — Towns cannot be traded without his cooperation, and the Knicks hold significant roster control over his current contract structure.

The resolution, whenever it comes, will define the Knicks' competitive trajectory for the next half-decade. The math is hard. The stakes are enormous. And the defending champions are working through it all while every other team in the league watches to see what the blueprint for their dynasty actually looks like.
