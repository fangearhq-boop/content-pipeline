The Celtics Didn't Get Giannis. Their Pivot May Have Been Better.

By Marcus Cole | Hoop Heroes | July 5, 2026

The Boston Celtics wanted Giannis Antetokounmpo. They offered Jaylen Brown — their 2024 Finals MVP — and two unprotected first-round picks. Milwaukee said no, choosing the Miami Heat's offer instead. By most accounts, the Celtics had just lost the biggest sweepstakes of the summer.

Within 48 hours, Boston may have turned a near-miss into something better.

The Paul George Deal

Rather than retaining Brown and missing out on Giannis entirely, the Celtics pivoted immediately to the Philadelphia 76ers. The trade: Brown to Philly, Paul George to Boston — along with the 2028 first-round pick from Philadelphia, the 2031 first-round pick from Philadelphia, the best of the 2028 seconds among Golden State, Oklahoma City and Milwaukee, and the best of the 2030 seconds among Washington, Portland and Phoenix.

George is a three-time All-Star with NBA Finals experience who gives Jayson Tatum a proven co-star. He is not Giannis, but he is a legitimate second option on a championship-caliber roster. And he comes with a collection of future picks that gives the Celtics ammunition to improve further over the next several years.

One anonymous general manager called the Brown-for-George swap "highway robbery" in Philly's favor. But looking at what Boston received in return — George plus a draft pick war chest — the Celtics are not complaining.

Frontcourt Reinforcements

Boston did not stop with the George trade. They also signed Mitchell Robinson to a 3-year, $47 million deal. Robinson is one of the most efficient rim protectors in the NBA, and his shot-blocking presence addresses a genuine defensive need in the paint. Alongside Robinson, the Celtics signed Neemias Queta to a 4-year, $56 million contract for reliable center depth.

The Celtics now head into 2026-27 with Tatum, George, Robinson, Queta, and the rest of their core intact — plus a significantly enhanced pick portfolio for future flexibility.

What the Brown Trade Really Means

Brown, 29, had spent 10 seasons in Boston and won a Finals MVP in 2024. His departure stings for long-time Celtics fans. But the front office recognized a reality that the Boston Globe reported on this week: the long-term cost of a Brown re-extension under the current cap structure was untenable. Moving Brown now, while his value was at its peak, allowed Boston to maximize the return and reshape the roster for sustained contention rather than one more run before a forced rebuild.

What's Next

All of these moves become official on July 6 when the moratorium lifts. Boston heads into the 2026-27 season with a retooled roster, a new-look frontcourt, and arguably better long-term positioning than if they had simply retained Brown and moved on from the Giannis miss. The Eastern Conference is more competitive than ever, but the Celtics did not cede their place in the conversation. They just changed the terms of it.
