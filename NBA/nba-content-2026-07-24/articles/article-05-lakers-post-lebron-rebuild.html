Life After LeBron: How the Lakers Are Already Moving On

By Marcus Cole | July 24, 2026

While the world waits for LeBron's free agency decision, the Los Angeles Lakers have signed Matisse Thybulle, Ziaire Williams, and traded Deandre Ayton for Jaden Hardy. A new identity is taking shape.

The Los Angeles Lakers have not waited for LeBron James to decide their future.

While the NBA world holds its collective breath — betting markets updating every time LeBron exhales near a microphone — the Lakers have been busy. They've added defensive wing Matisse Thybulle on a one-year deal worth $3.29 million. They've signed Ziaire Williams. They've traded center Deandre Ayton to the Washington Wizards in exchange for guard Jaden Hardy and two second-round picks.

These aren't splashy moves. They don't signal a championship window opening. But they tell a coherent story about what the Lakers intend to be in the era after No. 23.

Defense First, Youth Second

Thybulle's signing is the clearest statement of intent. He is one of the most disruptive perimeter defenders in basketball — a five-time All-Defense candidate who has never found a consistent offensive game but has made multiple organizations better through his defensive presence alone. For a team built around Anthony Davis — a player whose value on both ends of the court is undeniable — adding Thybulle makes structural sense.

Ziaire Williams adds length and youth to a wing rotation that needed both. Still only in his mid-twenties, Williams has shown enough of an offensive skill set that he can contribute meaningfully without demanding the ball at the expense of everyone else.

The Ayton deal is interesting in a different way. Trading away a big in exchange for Jaden Hardy — who slipped to No. 37 in the 2022 draft but has shown real scoring instincts — and two second-round picks suggests the Lakers are comfortable with Davis as their lone frontcourt anchor while building out a younger, more athletic perimeter.

The End of the LeBron Era in Los Angeles

LeBron James arrived in Los Angeles in 2018. He led the Lakers to the 2020 NBA championship in the Orlando bubble. He delivered memories — some triumphant, some painful — that the fanbase will carry for decades.

He has since departed as a free agent, and the Lakers are constructing their roster as though he is not coming back. That is the practical reality of building a team while a superstar's destination remains undecided. You cannot hold 12 roster spots in limbo indefinitely. LA's front office is sending a clear message with every signing: Anthony Davis is the foundation, and these moves are designed to complement him.

What This Team Could Look Like

A Davis-centered team with Thybulle's defense, Williams's wing versatility, and Hardy's scoring instincts at guard would not be a championship contender in the Western Conference's current form. The Thunder, Warriors, Nuggets, and Spurs are all better on paper. But the Lakers would not be irrelevant either — they would be a team competing for the playoffs, building toward something, and doing it without a superstar whose next chapter belongs somewhere else.

That's a different kind of Lakers team than the one most fans have known for the past eight years. It's smaller in ambition in the short term. But it's also more honest — a roster built for what they have, not shaped by what they're hoping to get.

What's Next

The Lakers will continue filling out their roster through the rest of free agency and potentially additional trades before training camp. If LeBron signs somewhere else — which his departing the franchise already signals — Los Angeles will have the choice of whether to swing for another star or trust the patient build they've started.

For now, they're not frozen. They're moving. And in an offseason where standing still has been the condition of too many teams waiting on one man's decision, that matters.
