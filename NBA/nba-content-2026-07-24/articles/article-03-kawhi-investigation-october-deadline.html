Kawhi Investigation: Adam Silver's October Deadline Creates New Pressure for Toronto

By Damon Pierce | July 24, 2026

The NBA's probe into Kawhi Leonard and the Clippers is approaching 11 months with no end in sight. Commissioner Silver says it must conclude before next season. The Raptors are stuck in the middle.

Somewhere between the Aspiration endorsement deal, a second unnamed company arrangement, a convicted fintech CEO, and a star player who is now 35 years old, the NBA's investigation into Kawhi Leonard and the Los Angeles Clippers has become one of the most consequential stalls in league history.

It has been nearly 11 months. There is still no verdict.

Commissioner Adam Silver has been consistent on one point: the investigation — conducted by the Wachtell Lipton law firm on behalf of the league — must be completed before the 2026-27 season begins. Training camps open in late September. The math is not favorable to a thorough resolution.

What the Investigation Is About

The original question was whether the Los Angeles Clippers coordinated with Aspiration, a fintech company, to funnel money to Leonard outside the salary cap. The arrangement looked like this: Aspiration announced a $300 million arena naming rights deal with the Clippers while simultaneously signing Leonard to a personal endorsement deal worth $28 million. Clippers owner Steve Ballmer made a $60 million personal investment in the company.

Joe Sanberg, Aspiration's CEO, was convicted and sentenced to 14 years in federal prison on unrelated fraud charges. The NBA seized on the case as an opportunity to examine the full scope of Leonard's relationship with Clippers-connected entities.

The investigation has since expanded to a second undisclosed endorsement deal and questions about personal expenses. What began as a focused inquiry into one contract has become something broader and more complicated — which is at least part of why it has taken this long.

Toronto in Purgatory

A trade had been arranged: Leonard to the Toronto Raptors for Brandon Ingram, Gradey Dick, and a package of draft picks. It made sense on paper for both sides. Toronto would get a star (albeit an aging one), and the Clippers would restart around younger pieces.

But the Raptors, when asked by the league and the Clippers to assume in writing any penalty risk that Leonard might face as a result of the investigation, declined. They did not want to be liable for sanctions tied to something that happened while Leonard was in Los Angeles. The trade froze.

That freeze has now lasted long enough that Toronto's entire roster planning is distorted. They cannot make decisions around Leonard — they don't know if they'll have him. They cannot fully move on from Leonard — the trade is technically still alive. They exist in a state of roster paralysis that has real competitive costs.

The Clock and the Cost

Kawhi Leonard is 35 years old. That is not an age at which a year of roster limbo is easily absorbed. Every week the investigation drags is a week in which the window for a Leonard-centered rebuild in Toronto gets smaller. Ingram, Dick, and the draft picks sitting on the Clippers' side of the frozen deal also have opportunity cost — players and assets that could be used elsewhere but cannot be moved while the trade is pending.

Silver's October deadline is the first hard constraint anyone has placed on this process. Whether the investigators can meet it depends on factors outside the league's direct control — the scope of what Wachtell Lipton is examining continues to expand, and legal investigations rarely respect arbitrary deadlines imposed by outside parties.

If the investigation is not complete before training camps open, the NBA will face a difficult choice: delay the camps (unlikely) or allow the trade to proceed under conditional terms (unprecedented) or formally void the arrangement and let both sides restart (painful for everyone).

What's Next

Silver has made it clear the league will not rush its judgment. But not rushing and allowing a year-long investigation to slide into a second season are two different things. The pressure on Wachtell Lipton to produce findings that the league can act on has never been higher.

For Toronto fans, the outcome of this investigation will define what the next two or three years of Raptors basketball look like. If the trade goes through and Leonard arrives healthy, they have a competitive core. If it falls apart, they restart around Ingram and Dick and a pile of picks — younger, but further from contending.

Either outcome has value. The purgatory has none.
