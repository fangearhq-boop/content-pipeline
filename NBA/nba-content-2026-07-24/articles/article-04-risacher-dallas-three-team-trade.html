From No. 1 Pick to Dallas Maverick: What the Risacher Trade Signals About the NBA's Rebuilding Wave

By Jake Torres | July 24, 2026

Zaccharie Risacher — the 2024 NBA Draft's top pick — is a Dallas Maverick now. The three-team trade with Atlanta and OKC says something about where both franchises are headed.

When Zaccharie Risacher went No. 1 overall to the Atlanta Hawks in the 2024 NBA Draft, he carried the weight of a franchise's immediate future on his shoulders. Two years later, he's a Dallas Maverick — and the move makes a lot of sense for everyone involved.

On July 19, the Dallas Mavericks, Atlanta Hawks, and Oklahoma City Thunder executed a three-team trade: Ryan Nembhard went from Dallas to Atlanta, Luguentz Dort went from Oklahoma City to Atlanta, and Risacher came to Dallas with a mix of second-round picks changing hands across all three teams.

What Dallas Gets

Risacher is 21 years old, a 6-foot-8 wing with the shooting, playmaking, and defensive tools that contending teams spend years trying to acquire through the draft. His first two professional seasons in Atlanta were mixed — flashes of real upside, stretches of inconsistency that are normal for any player making the jump from European basketball to the NBA at a young age.

The Mavericks are in an explicit rebuild. They traded Luka Dončić in a prior blockbuster and have been methodically stacking high-ceiling youth. Risacher fits exactly that blueprint: young, already at the NBA level, with a developmental track that should peak in his mid-twenties when Dallas hopes to be competitive again.

Adding a player of this pedigree — a No. 1 overall pick, regardless of how his early career has unfolded — in exchange for Ryan Nembhard and picks is the kind of favorable acquisition that rebuilding teams spend years trying to engineer. Dallas made it happen by identifying value the Hawks no longer prioritized.

What Atlanta Gets

The Hawks, in pivoting away from Risacher, are sending a clear signal about their own direction. Luguentz Dort from Oklahoma City brings something Risacher does not: established NBA-level defensive intensity and a contract that fits timeline-wise. Ryan Nembhard adds playmaking depth. Atlanta is moving away from full rebuild mode and toward assembling a functional, experienced roster.

That does not mean the Dort-Nembhard pairing makes the Hawks a contender. It means they are trying to be competitive rather than purely developmental — a distinction that becomes important when evaluating their upcoming roster decisions.

What OKC Gets

For Oklahoma City, moving Dort — an excellent defender who has been a role player on their rise — was likely about cap management and roster flexibility. The Thunder, who have championship aspirations after years of patient building, may have simply outgrown what Dort could offer relative to what his salary cost them. The second-round picks they received help keep their draft pipeline stocked.

What's Next

For Risacher, this is effectively a fresh start. A new city, a new coaching staff, and a Mavericks organization that will build around him and his peers rather than around an established superstar. That can be either liberating or daunting for a 21-year-old — the answer will emerge once training camp opens and the real evaluation begins.

For the Mavericks' fanbase, adding Risacher gives them something legitimate to watch for in what is otherwise a patience-required rebuild. He is not a finished product. He is the kind of unfinished piece that, if it develops correctly, becomes the foundation of a future contender. Dallas is betting on exactly that.
