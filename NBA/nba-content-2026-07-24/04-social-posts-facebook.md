### STORY 3: Kawhi Investigation

#### Long-Form Post — 5:00 PM ET

The NBA's investigation into Kawhi Leonard and the Los Angeles Clippers has been running for nearly 11 months. It's still not over.

Here's the background: After Leonard signed a 4-year extension with the Clippers, Aspiration — a fintech company — announced a $300 million arena naming rights deal with the team while simultaneously signing Leonard to a $28 million personal endorsement deal. Clippers owner Steve Ballmer had also made a $60 million personal investment in Aspiration. The NBA began investigating whether those arrangements were coordinated to pay Leonard outside the salary cap.

Joe Sanberg, Aspiration's CEO, was convicted and sentenced to 14 years in federal prison on unrelated fraud charges. The investigation, conducted by the Wachtell Lipton law firm, has since expanded to a second undisclosed endorsement deal and questions about personal expenses.

Commissioner Adam Silver has been clear: this must be wrapped up before the 2026-27 season begins.

Meanwhile, Toronto agreed to receive Kawhi in a trade for Brandon Ingram, Gradey Dick, and draft picks. But when asked to assume potential penalty risk in writing, the Raptors said no. The trade froze. Toronto has been in roster purgatory ever since — unable to plan around a player they may or may not receive, unable to move on from a deal still technically alive.

Kawhi is 35. Training camps open in late September. Every day without a resolution costs the Raptors real competitive ground.

Silver's October deadline is now the only hard constraint on a process that has dragged past what anyone anticipated.

### STORY 4: Dallas 3-Team Trade

#### Long-Form Post — 7:00 PM ET

The Dallas Mavericks quietly made one of the more interesting moves of the offseason on July 19.

In a three-team deal with the Atlanta Hawks and Oklahoma City Thunder, Dallas acquired Zaccharie Risacher — the No. 1 overall pick in the 2024 NBA Draft. Ryan Nembhard went from Dallas to Atlanta. Luguentz Dort went from Oklahoma City to Atlanta. Risacher came to Dallas with multiple second-round picks changing hands across the three teams.

Risacher is 21 years old, a 6-foot-8 wing with shooting and playmaking tools. His first two seasons in Atlanta were uneven — some bright flashes, some normal-for-his-age inconsistency. But he is exactly the kind of upside piece a rebuilding team like Dallas covets.

The Mavericks, who are rebuilding after trading Luka Dončić, are stacking their roster with high-ceiling young talent. Risacher fits that blueprint. A former No. 1 pick who is still only 21 and hasn't yet hit his development ceiling is the exact kind of asset that makes rebuilds exciting.

Atlanta gets Dort and Nembhard — established veterans who give them more competitive depth as they pivot away from a pure rebuilding posture.

This isn't a headline trade. But for Mavericks fans tracking what the franchise looks like in two or three years, Risacher joining the roster is worth paying serious attention to.

### STORY 5: Lakers Post-LeBron Rebuild

#### Long-Form Post — 9:30 PM ET

The Los Angeles Lakers aren't waiting for LeBron James to define what comes next.

Since LeBron departed as a free agent, LA has been moving decisively. They signed defensive wing Matisse Thybulle to a one-year, $3.29 million deal. They added Ziaire Williams to their roster. They traded center Deandre Ayton to the Washington Wizards for guard Jaden Hardy — who slipped to No. 37 in the 2022 draft but has shown scoring instincts — and two second-round picks.

Anthony Davis remains the centerpiece of the roster. The additions around him tell a coherent story: LA is building toward a younger, more athletic, defense-first identity. Thybulle is one of the best perimeter defenders in basketball. Williams adds wing length and development upside. Hardy adds a scoring option at guard who's still finding his ceiling.

This isn't a championship roster. It isn't meant to be — not yet. It's a franchise charting a realistic course for its next chapter rather than waiting in suspended animation for a star who may not be coming back.

For Lakers fans who experienced the LeBron years — the 2020 championship, the playoffs, the moments — watching LA move forward without him will feel strange. But the front office is doing what good front offices do: making thoughtful moves with the assets they have, building toward something real rather than banking on something uncertain.

A new era in Los Angeles has already quietly begun.
