Cade Cunningham Returns From Collapsed Lung, Pistons Rout Bucks 137-111

By Marcus Cole | Hoop Heroes

April 9, 2026

Cade Cunningham's first basket back was an alley-oop. Ausar Thompson floated it up, Cunningham caught it in stride and hammered it home — and just like that, after 11 games away with a collapsed lung, Detroit's franchise point guard was back.

Wednesday night's 137-111 destruction of the Milwaukee Bucks was about more than a box score. Cunningham played 26 minutes, logged 13 points on 6-of-11 shooting, and finished with 10 assists and 5 rebounds. The production was clean for a player returning from a serious injury. More importantly, the Pistons didn't miss a beat — they rolled by 26 points and served notice to the Eastern Conference that their No. 1 seed is built to last through April 18 and beyond.

Eleven Games That Tested Detroit's Depth

Cunningham went down March 17. He was driving for a loose ball in a win over the Washington Wizards when he collided with Tre Johnson. The diagnosis — a collapsed lung — immediately raised questions about his timeline. Collapsed lungs are not minor injuries. They're not the kind of thing you rush back from. The Pistons, to their credit, took the necessary time.

Detroit was already the No. 1 seed before Cunningham got hurt. What happened over the next 11 games was a test: could the Pistons hold their position without the player who drives everything? The answer, with Ausar Thompson, Jalen Duren, and company stepping up, was yes. Detroit stayed put at the top of the East standings throughout his absence.

That resilience speaks to how well head coach J.B. Bickerstaff has built the culture in this locker room. The Pistons came in as a 14-68 team just two years ago. This version doesn't flinch.

A Season to Remember

Before the injury, Cunningham had been having one of the finest seasons any Piston has ever put together. He's averaging 24.5 points, 9.9 assists, and 5.6 rebounds per game in 61 appearances — numbers that would put him in the MVP conversation if the award eligibility rules didn't require 65 games played.

The collapsed lung cost him not just games, but potentially his shot at a first-ever All-NBA selection under those rules. That's a painful footnote to what has been a remarkable year. But what Cunningham can control — his health, his readiness, his impact on a playoff roster — that's what Wednesday was about.

Seven minutes in the first quarter. Four quick points. Ten assists overall. He looked like himself.

What's Next

The Detroit Pistons (57-22) have three games remaining in the regular season before the playoffs begin April 18. The primary objective over those games: get Cunningham's legs under him, let him feel his way back to game speed, and avoid anything that could complicate his recovery timeline.

Detroit drew the No. 1 seed for a reason. They're going to need Cunningham operating at full capacity once the real games start. Wednesday's return was a strong first step. The next three regular-season contests are tune-up reps. The Pistons are thinking bigger.

A championship roster has been assembled in Detroit. The city that was 14-68 two years ago is now the top seed in the Eastern Conference playoffs. And its best player is healthy. Good timing.
