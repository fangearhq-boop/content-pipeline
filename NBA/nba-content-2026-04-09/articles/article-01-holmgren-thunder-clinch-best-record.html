Chet Holmgren Drops 30/14/4 Blocks as Thunder Clinch NBA's Best Record at 64-16

By Jake Torres | Hoop Heroes

April 9, 2026

The Oklahoma City Thunder are the best team in basketball — officially, mathematically, undeniably. Wednesday night's 128-110 victory over the Los Angeles Clippers put that on the board, as OKC clinched the NBA's top regular-season record at 64-16 and locked in homecourt advantage for every series they could possibly play this postseason.

The man responsible for the celebratory mood in Oklahoma City? Chet Holmgren. The second-year center poured in 30 points and hauled down 14 rebounds, adding 5 assists, 2 steals, and 4 blocks while shooting an absurd 10-of-13 from the field and 3-of-4 from three-point range. He scored 14 points in the first quarter alone, opening a gap the Clippers spent the rest of the night trying and failing to close.

Holmgren's Most Complete Night

There's a version of this story that leads with Shai Gilgeous-Alexander — the reigning MVP candidate, the -400 favorite, the face of this franchise. But on Wednesday, SGA's 20 points and 11 assists were almost a supporting act. Holmgren was the headliner.

He shot 83.3% in the first quarter. He blocked shots, he ran the floor, he hit pull-up threes. The Clippers tried size, they tried speed, they tried switching. Nothing worked. Kawhi Leonard had 20 points for LA and Brook Lopez contributed 16, but OKC's defense was airtight — no fast-break field goals allowed, 23 points scored off 13 Clipper turnovers, nine of those forced by Thunder steals. The Thunder shot 58.1% from the field.

Jalen Williams added 18 points to round out OKC's top three. This is a team built with depth and defended with ferocity — a blueprint the rest of the league has been staring at all season without finding a counter.

What Clinching the No. 1 Seed Means

Oklahoma City's best record means something concrete: every playoff series, in every round, starts in the Paycom Center. The Thunder went 38-3 at home this season. Their crowd is one of the most energized in the NBA, and their arena has become one of the most difficult buildings for visiting teams to win in.

The San Antonio Spurs (61-19) had mounted a remarkable challenge — an 18-2 run since February that brought them within two games of OKC. Victor Wembanyama's brilliance made this a legitimate race. Wednesday's result ended the suspense. The Spurs secured the No. 2 seed and a first-round matchup of their own, but home court in a potential conference semifinals showdown with OKC belongs to the Thunder.

It's a meaningful edge. Playoff basketball is different, and homecourt isn't everything — but when two elite teams meet, Game 7 at home instead of on the road is the kind of advantage championships are built on.

What's Next

Oklahoma City enters the final three games of the regular season with nothing left to prove in the standings and everything to prepare for in the postseason. Expect the Thunder to manage playing time for SGA and Holmgren as the team tunes up for the playoffs beginning April 18.

The Western Conference bracket is nearly set. OKC will face the No. 8 or No. 9 play-in team in the first round. The Spurs at No. 2 could meet them later. If both teams stay healthy, an OKC-San Antonio Western Conference Finals would be among the most anticipated matchups the NBA has staged in years.

The Thunder are the defending champion, they're the No. 1 seed, and they're playing their best basketball heading into the stretch. Everything the rest of the West is working toward — they're already there.
