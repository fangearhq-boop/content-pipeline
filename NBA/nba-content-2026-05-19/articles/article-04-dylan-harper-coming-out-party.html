24 Points, 7 Steals, a Team Record: Dylan Harper's WCF Game 1 Was the Night He Announced Himself

By Damon Pierce | Hoop Heroes | May 19, 2026

By the time the final buzzer sounded Monday night at Paycom Center, the conversation had already started forming around Victor Wembanyama. Forty-one points. Twenty-four rebounds. A logo 3-pointer. An and-one dunk to close it out. That conversation is entirely justified.

But there is another Spur who played one of the best games of his young career Monday night, and he deserves his own reckoning. Dylan Harper: 24 points, 7 steals, and a performance that proved the San Antonio Spurs are not a one-man team — they are a two-headed threat that just introduced itself to the entire basketball world.

Seven Steals That Changed the Game

Seven steals is a number that requires context. It is a Spurs team playoff record, surpassing steal totals recorded by San Antonio teams throughout their dynasty years. It is the kind of total that only happens when a defender is playing at an impossible level — anticipating passing lanes, jumping routes, using length and quickness to disrupt rhythm at every turn.

Harper's steal totals were not flukes of positioning or lucky tipped balls. They were the product of elite anticipation and relentless defensive engagement, applied to an Oklahoma City offense that had gone 8-0 in the playoffs before Monday and had every reason to trust its ball movement. Harper made them second-guess every pass, every handoff, every decision at the point of attack.

The ripple effects were enormous. Each steal either led to a fast-break opportunity for San Antonio or denied OKC a possession at a moment when one more basket might have swung the game. In a contest decided by double overtime, those moments were the margin.

The Pass That Won the Game

Statistics tell part of the story. Moments tell the rest.

Late in the second overtime, with the score close and every possession carrying championship-level weight, Harper caught the ball in transition, assessed the defense in a split second, and threw the lob to Wembanyama cutting toward the basket. Wembanyama caught it above the rim, absorbed Chet Holmgren's contest, and converted the and-one slam that put the Spurs up by a margin they would not relinquish.

The pass required Harper to understand exactly where his teammate would be, what the defense had conceded, and how quickly the play would develop. In the moment it counted most, a young player made the right read.

The Spurs' Design

When San Antonio drafted Harper, the organizational thinking was straightforward: they had a generational talent in Wembanyama. They needed a complementary player who could elevate the team without requiring the ball to run through him constantly — someone whose defensive instincts and offensive versatility would make Wembanyama better while being made better by him in return.

That partnership is working. The Spurs' willingness to trust two young players — both under 23 — with Conference Finals minutes against the league's most complete team is a testament to how San Antonio's front office has evaluated the talent they have.

What's Next

Harper and the Spurs face the Thunder again tonight in Game 2 of the Western Conference Finals at 8:30 PM ET on NBC/Peacock, back at Paycom Center in Oklahoma City. The Thunder will make defensive adjustments and scheme specifically to limit both Wembanyama and Harper. How Harper responds to those adjustments — and whether he can continue to disrupt OKC's flow — will go a long way toward determining how many games this series runs.

For now, though, Monday night belongs to him. The Spurs have a second player. His name is Dylan Harper, and he just had one of the best defensive games in Spurs playoff history.
