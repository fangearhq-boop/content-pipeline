Only the Second Wilt: Wembanyama's 2026 Season Is Already Unlike Anything We've Seen

By Marcus Cole | Hoop Heroes | May 19, 2026

Every few decades, a player arrives who makes previous comparisons feel inadequate. The comparisons always come — it is how we make sense of greatness, by referencing the greatness that came before. But Victor Wembanyama keeps rendering those comparisons incomplete. He is 22 years old, and he is already doing things that only Wilt Chamberlain has ever done.

Let's put his 2026 season in full context, because the accumulation of what he has accomplished this year is staggering regardless of any single game or statistic.

The Unanimous DPOY

When the NBA announced its end-of-season awards, Wembanyama won the Defensive Player of the Year award with all 100 first-place votes. Every single voter chose him first. That had never happened in the history of the award. Every other unanimous award winner in NBA history — MVP, Finals MVP, Rookie of the Year — has had at least one dissenting vote somewhere. Not Wembanyama. Not this year.

He was also the youngest DPOY winner in NBA history, having turned 22 in January. He led the league in blocks for the second consecutive season with 197 total, averaging 3.1 per game. He protected the paint, defended the perimeter, and guarded multiple positions at an elite level in ways no player his size — 7-foot-4, with a wingspan that seems to occupy an entire lane — should be able to do.

The Conference Finals Debut

Monday night in Oklahoma City took Wembanyama's 2026 season into entirely new territory. His 41-point, 24-rebound performance in the Spurs' 122-115 double-overtime victory over the Thunder placed him alongside Wilt Chamberlain as the only players in NBA history to record 40 or more points and 20 or more rebounds in a Conference Finals debut.

The achievement is not just historical — it is contextual. Chamberlain accomplished it in an era when teams played at a dramatically faster pace, rebounding totals were inflated by missed shots in a league with lower shooting percentages, and the competition, while fierce, was distributed across far fewer teams. Wembanyama did it against a Thunder team that had the best record in the Western Conference, led by a two-time Most Valuable Player, after going 8-0 in the playoffs.

He also did it in 49 minutes. He did not come out. He did not slow down. He hit a 27-foot 3-pointer from logo range to tie the game in the first overtime, then threw down an and-one dunk in the second overtime to ice the victory. His endurance matched his skill.

A Player Without a Template

The usual approach to evaluating young stars involves asking which established legend they resemble. People have tried that with Wembanyama — and found the exercise difficult. He is too tall for the comparisons to guards. Too skilled offensively for the comparisons to traditional centers. Too defensively dominant for the comparisons to offensive-first big men. Too multidimensional for any one template to hold.

The Wilt comparison is not perfect either, but it is closer than most. Chamberlain combined size, strength, and scoring in ways no center before or since has matched. Wembanyama adds something Wilt did not have in the same measure: elite three-point range and shot-blocking instincts that extend far beyond the paint. He is a different kind of player — a 21st-century archetype that the sport had not seen before he arrived.

What's Next

The Western Conference Finals continue with Game 2 tonight at 8:30 PM ET on NBC/Peacock in Oklahoma City. The Thunder, a team built around one of basketball's best players in SGA, will search for answers to the questions Wembanyama raised Monday night. It will be fascinating to watch their adjustments — and even more fascinating to watch how Wembanyama responds to a team that has now seen him up close once and will be desperate not to see the same performance again.
