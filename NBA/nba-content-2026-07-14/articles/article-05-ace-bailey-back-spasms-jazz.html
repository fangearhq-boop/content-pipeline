Ace Bailey Exits Summer League Game with Back Spasms, Status Uncertain for Jazz

By Damon Pierce | July 14, 2026

The No. 5 overall pick in the 2026 draft left his July 12 game at halftime and did not return. Utah is monitoring the situation as Summer League continues in Las Vegas.

The Utah Jazz drafted Ace Bailey with the No. 5 pick in 2026 because they believe he is a cornerstone of their future. Right now, that cornerstone is sitting out with back spasms and an uncertain timeline for return.

Bailey left Utah's Summer League game on July 12 at halftime, visibly stiff and unable to continue. The diagnosis: back spasms. He did not return to the game. His availability for the remainder of Summer League play is uncertain, with the Jazz monitoring his condition and not providing a public timetable for a return.

It is the kind of update that arrives without drama and departs with a knot in the stomach — not because back spasms in young players typically signal serious long-term concerns, but because any time a top-five pick exits a game at halftime, the questions start.

Context Matters

Back spasms are a common, frequently non-serious condition — the result of overexertion, dehydration, muscle fatigue, or a combination of all three. Summer League schedules can be compressed and physically demanding for players who have spent months training at elevated intensity. Young athletes experience them more often than most fans realize.

But the NBA has also seen its share of situations where an initially minor-seeming back issue revealed itself to be something that required more careful management. The Jazz, understandably, are being cautious. This is not the regular season. The goal is not to win Summer League games at the cost of a franchise player's health.

What the Jazz Need From Bailey

Utah selected Bailey because of what they saw in his game: a combination of scoring versatility, defensive potential, and positional size that fits the modern NBA forward profile. The Jazz are in a building phase, and Bailey's development over the next two to three seasons is central to how that phase unfolds.

Summer League was supposed to be the first chance to see that development on an NBA court. Instead, he's managing a health issue that, while expected to resolve, is a reminder that every development path has bumps in it.

What's Next

The Jazz have not made an official statement on Bailey's status beyond the initial report of back spasms. The expectation within the league is that he will be evaluated day-to-day and cleared when the medical staff is satisfied it is safe to return.

The more important calendar is the regular season. Summer League is four weeks of evaluation and development; the regular season is where careers are built. If Bailey is healthy and ready when training camp begins in September, Tuesday's update will be a footnote. That, at least, is the most likely outcome.
