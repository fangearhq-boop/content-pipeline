The Sixers Are Making Their LeBron Pitch — And Philadelphia Is Playing Smart

By Damon Pierce | July 14, 2026

With Jaylen Brown now in Philadelphia and Bob Myers showing up on Rich Paul's podcast, the 76ers are working every angle in the chase for LeBron James.

When the president of basketball operations for a team competing for a superstar's services shows up on that superstar's agent's podcast, it is not an accident. Nothing in the LeBron James recruitment is ever an accident.

Philadelphia 76ers HBSE president Bob Myers — the same executive who built the Golden State Warriors dynasty — appeared on Rich Paul's "Game Over" podcast this week, and the message was unmistakable: Philadelphia is serious, organized, and willing to do whatever it takes to close the LeBron sweepstakes in their favor.

The Cavaliers remain the frontrunner. The Heat are still very much in the picture. But the 76ers have done enough work in recent weeks to establish themselves as a genuine third option — and they're not done building their case.

What Philadelphia Is Selling

The Sixers' pitch has sharpened considerably since the offseason began. Philadelphia acquired Jaylen Brown from the Boston Celtics, giving them a legitimate two-way wing who pairs with Tyrese Maxey to form the kind of young, proven core that championship contenders are built on.

Add LeBron James — even at 41 — to that group, and you have a trio that changes conversations. Myers on Rich Paul's podcast was a deliberate signal that the Sixers understand what it takes to get into this room, and they're not just waiting for a phone call.

Rich Paul has reportedly spoken to 27 different teams during LeBron's free agency process. Three have consistently come up in conversations: Cleveland, Miami, and Philadelphia. That's a short list, and the Sixers are on it.

The Rest of the Field

Cleveland's case rests on familiarity. Donovan Mitchell is there, the organization gave him a $273 million extension, and the Cavaliers have quietly positioned themselves as the place where LeBron can win right now without having to rebuild anything. Sources close to the process continue to describe Cleveland as the frontrunner.

Miami's pitch leans into legacy. The Heat are where LeBron won his first two championships, and the possibility of a lineup featuring LeBron, Giannis Antetokounmpo, and Bam Adebayo is the kind of formation that makes anyone sit up and take notice. But translating that vision into an actual roster under salary cap rules is complicated.

LeBron is 41 years old. He has been specific, according to reports, about wanting to join a championship contender where he also feels the cultural fit is right. He is not interested in a rebuilding situation. Every conversation is being filtered through that lens.

What's Next

Mid-July has been floated as a realistic target for LeBron to make a decision, though no formal deadline exists. Teams are managing their rosters around the uncertainty — unable to fully commit to other moves while LeBron's destination remains open.

The 76ers appear to have done what they can to be ready when the call comes. Whether Bob Myers and Rich Paul's podcast conversation translates into something more concrete is the question hovering over the entire free agency market right now.
