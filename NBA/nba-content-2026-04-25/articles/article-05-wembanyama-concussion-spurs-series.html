Wembanyama in Concussion Protocol: What His Absence Means for the Spurs' Playoff Run

By Damon Pierce | Hoop Heroes | April 25, 2026

The moment Victor Wembanyama fell to the floor in Game 2 against Portland, jaw striking the hardwood after contact from Jrue Holiday, the arena understood it was witnessing something more serious than a routine hard foul. Wembanyama was diagnosed with a concussion and entered the NBA's return-to-participation protocol, missing Game 3 on Friday night. The Spurs won anyway, 120-108, but the question looming over San Antonio's entire postseason is simple: how long will their franchise superstar be out?

What Concussion Protocol Means

The NBA's concussion protocol requires players to progress through a series of stages before they can return to game action. The process includes rest, light cardio, non-contact basketball activity, full practice, and ultimately game clearance — and players cannot skip steps or accelerate the timeline regardless of the stakes. Wembanyama completed some cardio work and took a few shots at San Antonio's facility on Thursday, before traveling with the team to Portland. That he could participate in limited activity is an encouraging sign, but it does not mean he was close to game-ready.

Game 4 is Sunday afternoon in San Antonio. Whether Wembanyama clears the protocol in time will depend on how his symptoms have progressed and how he responds to increasing levels of physical activity. The Spurs have not provided a specific timeline, and those close to the team suggest the focus is on being cautious with a 22-year-old who has his entire career ahead of him.

What Wembanyama Means to the Spurs

It is easy to state the statistical case: Wembanyama averaged 25 points, 11.5 rebounds, and 3.1 blocks per game this season. He shot 51.2 percent from the field. He was the near-unanimous choice for Defensive Player of the Year and the most transformative young big man the league has seen in a generation. San Antonio went 24-3 after the All-Star break with him healthy. The franchise has been built around him.

But the intangible case is just as important. Wembanyama changes what defenses must prepare for. His length and shot-blocking alter opponent shot selection before he even leaves his feet. His ability to generate offense from the post, the mid-range, and behind the arc gives the Spurs options that simply do not exist when he is not on the floor. Friday night's 120-108 win was remarkable precisely because it happened without him — and it was not a sign that he is expendable, but rather a testament to the depth of the roster his presence has helped build.

Castle and Harper: The Unlikely Insurance Policy

Stephon Castle (2024, No. 4 pick) and Dylan Harper (2025, No. 1 pick) combined for 60 points in Game 3 — a performance that announced both players as legitimate postseason contributors, not just prospects. Castle's 33-point explosion showcased the scoring instincts and composure that made him a lottery pick, while Harper's 27 points and 10 rebounds demonstrated the kind of versatility that translated immediately from the college level.

These two were always part of the plan in San Antonio. The plan was just supposed to unfold over years, with Wembanyama as the anchor and the young pieces developing around him. Instead, Game 3 against Portland turned into something of an audition — and they passed with flying colors.

What's Next

If Wembanyama returns for Game 4, the Spurs will be a strong favorite to close out the Trail Blazers and advance. If he misses Sunday's game, San Antonio will rely on their young core again — and will know, from Game 3, that they are capable of winning that way. Game 4 tips off Sunday afternoon in San Antonio. The Spurs lead the series 2-1. Whatever happens next, Wembanyama's health is the story that will define San Antonio's postseason ceiling.
