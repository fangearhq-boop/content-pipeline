LeBron's Tying 3, Smart's Historic Line Lift Lakers Past Rockets in OT — Lakers Lead 3-0

By Damon Pierce | Hoop Heroes | April 25, 2026

There are moments in sports that defy simple explanation, moments where you set aside the analytics and the salary sheets and just watch something remarkable unfold. Friday night in Houston was one of those moments. LeBron James — 41 years old, 23 seasons deep into his NBA career, already past the 45-minute mark — stepped back and buried a contested 3-pointer with 13 seconds remaining in regulation to tie the game and send it to overtime. The Los Angeles Lakers then rode Marcus Smart's historic performance to a 112-108 overtime win and a commanding 3-0 series lead over the Houston Rockets.

Smart's line belonged in a time capsule. He finished with 21 points, 10 assists, 5 steals, and 2 blocks — the first 20-point, 10-assist, 5-steal, 2-block performance in NBA Playoffs history since Michael Jordan accomplished it in 1991. He scored 8 of the Lakers' 11 overtime points, including a go-ahead 3-pointer that put Los Angeles up 107-103 and effectively ended the Rockets' chances. Smart had also recorded five steals in Game 2, making him the relentless engine driving this series.

The Comeback Was Pure LeBron

The script for the final 30 seconds of regulation should not have worked. Houston led by six with the clock winding down and the partisan Toyota Center crowd sensing a chance to stay alive in the series. The Rockets needed one stop.

They didn't get it. LeBron received the ball on the wing, created just enough space against the closing defender, and launched a 3-pointer that found the bottom of the net with 13 seconds on the clock. It was the kind of shot that requires not just skill but nerve — the nerve of someone who has been in these moments so many times that they no longer feel like moments at all. LeBron finished with 29 points, 13 rebounds, and 6 assists, logging more than 45 minutes in a performance that once again raised the question of when, exactly, this man is supposed to slow down.

Rui Hachimura added 22 points, providing steady secondary scoring throughout the night. The Lakers' ability to generate offense from multiple players against a shorthanded Rockets team made the eventual result feel, in retrospect, inevitable.

Rockets Without Durant Again

Kevin Durant did not play in Game 3 due to a left ankle sprain he suffered late in Game 2, an injury sustained while chasing down a loose ball near Luke Kennard. It was the third time in four games this series that Houston's best player was unavailable — Durant also missed Game 1 with a bruised right knee. In his absence, the Rockets have been unable to generate the offensive firepower needed to consistently threaten a team as experienced as the Lakers.

Houston showed fight, leading by six with under 30 seconds in regulation and coming agonizingly close to forcing a must-win situation for Los Angeles. But the Rockets now find themselves one game from elimination with no certainty about Durant's availability and no realistic path to beating the Lakers three consecutive times. A sweep has become the most likely outcome.

What's Next

Game 4 is Monday in Houston, and the Rockets' season is on the line. Kevin Durant's ankle will be evaluated in the coming days; if he can return, Houston has a fighting chance to extend the series. But the Lakers have shown an ability to win in a variety of ways — through LeBron's heroics, through Smart's two-way dominance, through the steady production of role players — that suggests they are a different caliber team than what the Rockets can currently match. A sweep would advance Los Angeles to the second round and give their aging stars much-needed rest before a deeper playoff push.
